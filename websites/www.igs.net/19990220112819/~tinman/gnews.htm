Wednesday, June 10, 1998

The team has been pushing hard lately finishing up the AI library for the Gothica engine. We've managed to put together an alpha version of many of the low-level AI commands used to build the advanced AI that you will find in Gothica. We've been able to add collision detection right down to the polygon level. This allows for combat that will approach the excitement and complexity of a fighting game. We don't want Gothica to be a point-and-click game!

We've updated the visual quality of Gothica to full 24 bit color textures. Many of the new 3D cards just on the horizon will support 24 bit rendering. This looks really nice and allows for rich looking environments! For older 3D cards, the textures will be automatically dithered down to 16 bit color. The current screen shots of Gothica represent the 16 bit color version -- so you will notice a slight visual improvement when we start showing the new 24 bit screenshots. We are also adding many new visual effects such as specular lighting, fog, and material based textures. The visual technology of Gothica will be fully scaleable depending on your computer and 3D card.

We've recently been sent several new Voodoo2 cards from 3DFX. These are awesome pieces of hardware. I can recommend the Voodoo2 to anyone without hesitation. They have fast multi-texturing capabilities that allow for cool effects such as bump mapping. Bump mapping is an effect that we will be taking full advantage of in Gothica.

We have added a new front page: http://www.igs.net/~tinman/tinman.htm

On the new Tinman Studios front page you'll notice the logo of another Tinman product -- Drone. Drone is a game being created using our engine and toolset. It's an action oriented title with lot's of environmental interaction. Drone will be released before Gothica -- and will hopefully be released soon! We'll be adding lot's more information about Drone in the near future.

Finally, we've started work on the Internet play aspects of Gothica. Internet multi-player is just plain fun! More on this as we progress!

Wednesday, March 4, 1998

We've been working hard on the Necromancer area in Gothica. This is a very large dungeon that is strewn across an ancient, ruined landscape. The action takes place both inside and outside of these ruins. Dilapidated rope bridges made of bone shakily span pools of stagnant sludge. This is the home of the Undead.

This new area includes a lot of cool interactive features. The rope bridges actually stress under the weight of your character. Skiffs can be used to float along some of the larger water areas. We've been working on a healthy set of monsters for this area as well. Right now, we will have zombies, skeletons, mummies, flying bat skulls, undead knights, and skeleton spiders.

All of these new changes in Gothica are very extensive. The Necromancer area looks so good and so cohesive that all of our current screen shots have been put to shame. Along with a new user interface system, we've been able to bring the game up another notch in quality. We are so excited about these changes that we are preparing a big update to the web site with new screen shots. I'd love to post a shot now, but we want to wait another short period of time so we can show lot's of new stuff all at once! Stay tuned because this coming update will be one not to miss!

We have been progressing on many fronts lately. Right now, our art team is building a village area and finishing some of the character/monster meshes required for a complete test of the Gothica game system. The technical team has moved forward on artificial intelligence, combat, and the role-playing engine. We plan to merge all of the work into one large microcosm of Gothica in the near future. Our development team always creates stand alone versions of the game every few weeks. These versions contain only those features that are at the alpha testing stage. In this manner, we are able to evaluate features without the excess baggage of work in progress. For the past year, we've been designing Gothica to be as open ended as possible. We want the game to be simple to play, but so complex that it will take months to figure every little detail out. Ideas have flown around the design table by the thousands. We have literally hundreds of design sketches. With all of the ideas and sketches, one piece of the puzzle was missing -- how do we bring all this complexity together in a simple, cohesive, easy to understand system. Well, a breakthrough at our last few design sessions has brought a perfect solution that we are extremely excited about. We'll leak out bits and pieces over the coming months!

To create an open-ended, complex game system, everything must be designed to be analog. By analog, we mean the ability to have infinite steps within a given constraint. This affects aspects in the game such as: character progression, combat, monsters, quests, and the magic system. Analog has been one of our major design goals in Gothica.

We have applied this analog system to the type of character that you play. The game plays drastically differently depending on the type of the character that you are playing. There are no character types that are chosen at the beginning of the game. For example, you don't choose to be a mage or a warrior. Your decisions and actions throughout the game bring the type of character you are playing to life. This is a very analog system where you could have a character that is 5/8 mage, and 3/8 warrior. This is a very simple example because there are many unique paths that can be followed in Gothica.

Monday, February 2, 1998

There is a nice preview of Gothica at the Adrenaline Vault. Check it out: www.avault.com

This week, we have been building a large graveyard. The graveyard is divided by a deep ravine into an old area and a new area. A stone bridge crosses the ravine and brings the player into the ancient part of the graveyard. After years of neglect, the graves and tombs are withered and dilapidated. Zombies and other creatures of the night find sanctuary in this abandoned place. The new area contains a large, central church surrounded by patches of grave sites and tombs. Beneath the graveyard, passageways of an ancient catacomb wind their way through the underground like roots from a tree.

In Gothica, we have placed a premium on character interaction with the environment. Our new fire engine is extremely powerful and allows for burning of any surface in the game. You can burn simple objects such as wooden doors, barrels, and crates as well as complex objects such as monsters. The fire is based on real physics so it actually burns an object with uncanny realism. Check out the screen shot excerpts. The first shows the smashing of a barrel with your weapon. Fragments of the barrel are blown all around. The second shows the after effects of a fireball hitting the barrels. The wood is ignited and burns until the barrels dissipate into ashes.

Keep watching this site because we are planning on releasing some new shots and information in the near future. We are also planning on adding a cool new section to the site! We’ll keep you posted.

Saturday, January 24, 1998

The major news of today comes from the creation of this new Gothica website. This site will be dedicated to bringing forth up to date information about our new role-playing game. Watch for news and added sections to appear on this site.

In the last week, we've been pulling all of our various technologies, gameplay, and artwork together in a preliminary demo. We will be showing this demo to publishing houses over the next month or so in order to secure a publisher for Gothica.

As far as progress on the game, we've added a new mesh deformation system that will allow more fluid and advanced animation sequences. You can now swing your weapon while running, jumping, or just about any special character action. This adds another dimension to the strategy required in hand to hand combat.
