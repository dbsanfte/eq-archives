How is playing Shadowrun on a MUX different than playing a TT game?

The most obvious difference is that there are many, many more people involved on the MUX than in your average TT game. Most TT games have around three or four PCs and one GM.

On Seattle MUX, there are often more than thirty or even fourty PCs online at once! Another obvious difference is that there is no single GM in charge of what happens to your character. RPS is responsible for guiding the actions in the world of Seattle MUX; they fulfill all the duties that a GM would handle in a TT Game.

Another fundamental difference: in a TT game, you and a few other PCs are usually the stars. If your game was an action movie, one of you would be Arnold Schwarzenegger, another would be Sylvester Stallone, a third might be Van Damme (if he wanted to play a total sissy). On the MUX, everyone has their own agenda, every PC has his or her own goals, and sometimes the goals of MUX players are opposed. Nobody is the star on Seattle MUX, everyone holds a place in the game. Sometimes you'll come into conflict with another player on Seattle MUX; in some cases, you may even enter into a life-and-death situation, and one of your characters might bite the dust. You rarely see a PvP conflict in a TT game.

Along this line of reasoning, another difference you may notice is that your character won't have a dozen skills at rating 6 or higher. On Seattle MUX, you won't be playing an action star who doesn't need to reload, racks up a triple-digit body count every weekend and can use any type of weapon he stumbles upon. If your character has combat experience, it will be easy to justify him knowing how to fire a pistol, how to wield a katana or a submachine gun, but everything is in moderation.

Read the skill ratings table at the end of the Skills chapter in the SR3 sourcebook for a good measure of how hard it might be to reach a level of mastery in a field that
can be represented with a rating 6 skill, and you'll understand why it would take some serious justification for your character to have Pistols, Rifles, Submachine Guns, Assault Rifles, Heavy Weapons, Launch Weapons, Edged Weapons, Unarmed Combat, and Gunnery all at Rating 6. It would take many, many years to develop such a mastery of all these types of weapons; perhaps a grizzled Green Beret veteran who continues to sign up for more combat tours despite his failing health and advancing age might be at this level, but he would be one of a truly elite few in the world.

Why do I have to get so much stuff approved before I can play?

Some say that Seattle MUX is an elitist place, that the people there don't
want you to play with them. Why do they say this? The chargen process has a
few hoops you have to jump through.

Now, let me be the first to tell you, anyone who says that I don't want you
to come play on Seattle with me is a liar! The chargen process isn't in
place to intimidate you or scare you off, it's there for another reason: to
make sure that every PC who hits the IC grid is a fully balanced,
believable, fleshed-out and ready-to-go character.

Why wasn't my background approved?

Before you get an answer to this question, you have to understand why you write a background in the first place. In some TT games, an emphasis is placed on "rollplaying", or succeeding against something by using the numbers on your character sheet and rolling dice. On Seattle MUX, roleplaying is more important than rollplaying, and while a character sheet may give you some idea of what your character looks like, what he can do, how strong or intelligent he is, it doesn't offer up much more information than that.

A background will give you (and any RPA's that want some information about your character) a good starting point to begin roleplaying intelligently. It's important to know where your character has been, why he's in Seattle, what his motivations are, and, if he's a criminal, why (this is a particularly important thing to know). If you don't know this information, you're not going to have very much fun trying to make it all up on the fly, and those you roleplay with won't have much fun either. The
background approval process ensures that when you hit the grid you won't have to worry about any of that.

Now, on to the vital question: what makes a background "good" or "bad"? First of all, it has to be readable. If a PHA loads up your BG and can't make sense of it due to poor grammar, spelling and editing errors, or lack of coherence, there's little chance that he's going to make it all the way through it. Make sure your BG is easy to read and understand! Don't switch tenses like they don't matter or use pronouns that are unclear.

Another important point is that your BG is consistent, or more specifically, that your character's actions are consistent. The actions your character takes in his lifetime need to make sense; if he was raised in an affluent family and happy all through his childhood, why would he suddenly become a criminal, rather than following in his parents' footsteps and becoming a corporate lawyer? Things need to be explained clearly and sensibly.

Perhaps the most important thing to explain: why is your character a criminal? If he is a member of the Yakuza, the Humanis Policlub, the Mafia, an independent shadowrunner, or even a gang member, he lives outside the law, and it's not enough to simply gloss over your character's descent into a lawless
lifestyle. One decker might run the shadows because he grew up in a poor neighborhood and couldn't make a break in life; he was seduced into crime because it offered him an easy way to make a buck. Another decker might run because he saw a friend murdered at the hands of a Mafia don and turned to the Matrix to uncover enough dirt to send the don to sleep with the fishes. These two characters could have the exact same character sheet, but they're very, very different people, with different motivations and experiences.

One more thing to keep in mind: you're not the only person who can see your BG. Even six months after you start playing, an RPA who wants to know how to hook your character into a plot might load up your BG and check our your character's history, personality and motivations. It's possible to create a BG that will pass through the approval process and not offer anything to you or RPStaff, but it's not going to benefit anyone in the long run.

A final hint: don't just make your background a laundry list of justifications for your cyberware and skills. It's nice (and required) to know why your character has certain abilities and equipment, but the background isn't just that: it defines who and what your character is in every way. The more information you include, the more believable and defined your character will be in the long run.

Why wasn't my application approved?

Once you get through the background submission/approval process, you'll go through the automated chargen process and create a character sheet, complete with attributes, skills, cyberware, gear, and contacts. After this is all complete and your application is submitted to the appropriate queue, an RPA will load up your background and +sheet and check them both out.

The RPA is really looking for one thing in particular: does your +sheet match your
background? If your character has wired reflexes 3, does your background explain why he has such expensive, uncommon cyberware? If your character has the Pistols skill at rating 6, does your BG explain why he can use a handgun better than most SWAT team members? If so, everything's cool and you're good to go.

Frankly, most applications that are denied fall into one of two categories: they don't make sense, or they're just plain overpowered. In the first case, the RPA may find glaring inconsistencies between the background and the +sheet: expensive cyberware isn't mentioned at all in the background (he's got a VCR 2? why? when did this happen?) and/or skills that are conspicuously not justified in the background (why does a former television reporter have Assault Rifles 5?).

In the second case, the character is just too combat-oriented. I'll be the first to admit, I groan audibly when I grab an application, load up a background that reads well and defines a well-rounded character, and then look at a +sheet with five rating 6 skills. There's a news files (news rules skills levels) that should give you a good idea how the rating number's translate to ability levels. If a street cop can use his Pistol at rating 3, then there's just no good reason that a teenage gang member should have Pistols at Rating 5. It's going to be hard to get me to believe that a teenage gang member can handle a pistol as well as a 20 year veteran policeman. Perhaps if his life has been a nightmarish whirlwind of constant gunfights... but individuals fitting this profile are few and far between.

Having too many high-level skills is just as bad as having unjustified skills: even a SWAT team member won't have Pistols, Submachine Guns, Shotguns, Rifles and Assault Rifles at rating 5 or 6, or even have every one of these skills at all. Rating 5 and 6 skills represent a near-mastery of the skill. Just how much time does your character spend training? Enough to master five different classifications of firearms? I sincerely doubt it. Even a SWAT member might only have Submachine Guns at 5, Pistols at 4, and perhaps Assault Rifles at 3, and those guys spend several hours a week training, training, training.

In short, if you want to play a character who's defined by his character sheet, go play a tabletop game! Nobody's going to hold it against you. I enjoy TT games very much myself; it's a different experience altogether. If you want to play on Seattle MUX, be willing to conform to the standards that have been set up there. With this in mind, submit a +sheet that meshes well with your background and doesn't force the RPA to reject it with the "Not Another Cookie-Cutter Combat Monster" stamp of disapproval.

What am I doing here? Why can't I find anyone to roleplay with?

First of all, the commands +ho, +map and +directory are going to prove very useful to a new player. +ho lists bars, restaurants and hangouts that have players in them, and you can head there to look for someone to roleplay with. +map will show you where you are and where you can get to, and +directory will help you find the establishments you're looking for.

Why isn't anyone looking for you? Simply put, this isn't a tabletop game. On Seattle MUX, there's dozens of players, not three or four, and you're not the only important person. Everyone else is already out there, they've already made friends, established contacts, gone on shadowruns and gained a street rep. You're the new guy, nobody knows you or even cares about you.

But wait! It's not so bad as all that, that's just in the game! While your character doesn't matter to anyone yet, you do! We want you to enjoy yourself, we want to roleplay with you. Get out there and meet people. Put your name out on the streets, maybe a fixer or a Johnson will pick it out of a hat and hire you for a job.

If you're completely unable to find anyone to roleplay with, page someone for advice. Seattle MUX is often daunting to new players because it can be difficult to find places to RP if you don't know where everyone tends to be. The worst thing you can do is idle in your apartment and wait for someone to come find you--be proactive! Go grab the bull by the horns and show Seattle what they've been missing by not having you around!

How do I buy a lifestyle?

On Seattle MUX, your lifestyle is determined by what apartments you live in. If you type "+check housing" you'll get a list of coded apartments where you can rent rooms. Using the +directory will help you locate these apartments, and there should be a help file or OOC sign to explain the commands to rent a room.

How can I get a new car (armor, silencer, laser sight, suprathyroid, cellular phone)?

This depends a lot on whether your character has a SIN (real or fake). If you're a SINless character, tough titties, pal. You're going to have to go through a fixer to get anything you want illegally, no matter what it is. There are PC fixers out there on the grid, you just have to go find them. This can be exciting in and of itself.

If you have a SIN, there's options available to you. Vehicles, guns, cyberware, computers and all kinds of things can be obtained legally. There's a coded car dealership for vehicles and a coded electronics shop for computers. Guns and armor, you'll have to get an admin to create those for you. Legal purchases, however, can be easily traced, and you'll have to get a permit for every gun you want to own or carry, as well as every piece of legal cyberware you want installed. Your legal car will be registered with the DMV.

And then there's always stuff that's just plain illegal... so, even a character with a SIN might decide to go through an illegitimate source for certain things.

How do you get a SIN? If your background calls for it, the RPA that approves your application should set you up with one when you go IC from the appropriate country. If you start out as a SINless character, you can walk right up to any Lone Star station, pay a fine for being SINless, and get yourself a brand spankin' new SIN. If you want a fake SIN, you're going to have to find some 'trix jockey to create it for you.

How can I earn money?

How do you think? This question gets asked a lot, and frankly, the answers are just what you'd expect. If you're a shadowrunner, find a shadowrun, go on it and get paid. If you're a ganger, go beat somebody up and take his credstick. If you're in an org (legal or illegal) or running a smallbiz (heh, legal or illegal), you're probably earning money anyway.

How do you earn money in a tabletop game? Apply this to the MUX. It's one concept that transfers pretty well.

How can I earn karma?

In a TT game, the GM hands out karma after each shadowrun. On Seattle MUX, an RPA hands out karma for good roleplaying. Remember that background you wrote? It defines your character's personality and motivation: stick with it! In a TT game you don't get the chance to act out your character's daily life, to meet new people, to chill in a bar or restaurant and just relax, to call someone up on the phone, to work out in a gym.

These and more are all things you might do on Seattle MUX, and if you stay in character while doing them, you're going to impress RPS. They'll hand out the rewards you deserve. Here's a hint: if you want to know if a RPA is watching your scene, use +edcheck and see.

If there's nobody watching your cool, cool RP, how can you earn karma for it? Log it! Most MU* client programs support logging, and if yours doesn't, get another one! Log the good stuff, edit out all the unnecessary OOC babble, and mail your log to an RPA. Check +staff/all to see who might want it, and look in their +finger to see if they accept RP logs. If you participate in a scene that you think is RPed well enough to deserve karma, chances are a RPA might think so too. Don't let it go to waste, log away!

How can I spend karma?

Pretty much the same ways you spend it in a tabletop game. Raising attributes, raising skills, learning new spells, bonding spirits. The only differences, and these are only differences if you happen to do it differently in your TT games, are:

1) You need some kind of justification to raise a skill or attribute. If you go to the gym four nights a week and pump mega iron, then you can justify raising your strength from 3 to 4. To raise your strength from 6 to 7 might require heavy steroid use and several hours a day at a fitness center. For raising skills, similar justification is required. It's not enough to just say, "My character is getting better with a gun," and raise your Pistols skill from 1 to 2. Is he actually practicing with the weapon? Is he getting real combat experience with it? If so, then your request is justified. If not, go do something about it.

2) "Training" takes time. After you raise a skill or attribute, the very soonest you can raise it again is a number of weeks equal to the new skill rating. For instance, after raising your Pistols skill from 2 to 3, it will be, at the very least, three weeks before you can raise that skill again. Generally, it's going to take longer than that, unless your character devotes a truly significant amount of time to improving his abilities in
that one area.

What exactly should you do to spend karma? @mail Roleplaying (which is a queue, not a character) with your request. If you want the process to go smoothly and quickly, include the skill or attribute name, its current level, the desired level, the linked attribute (for skills) the calculated good karma cost, and a justification for the raise. In some cases it may be necessary to provide an actual RP log to back up your claims that a skill raise is justified (e.g. your Pistols skill isn't going to raise from 6 to 7 just because you go to the firing range every other weekend).

How come I can't just apply for a mage?

Magic characters on Seattle are subject to more stringent regulations, because there is a high demand for these PCs, but realistically, you can only have so many of them on the grid at once.

As a result, Magic PCs are only open for applications when the RPC/Magic (that's Roleplaying Chief/Magic) posts openings, and those openings will generally be for a specific type of magically-active character. Look on bb1 for current openings.

What do all those three-letter acronyms by the staffers' names mean?

The first two letters of the code denote the department, and the third denotes the position within the department.

There are three staff departments here on SeattleMUX:

BCS - Build/Code Staff: BCS is the branch of staff that makes the grid exist. The BCS folks build all the places you love to hang out in, all the objects your PC owns, the special MUX-specific commands you use, and much, much more.

PHS - Player Help Staff: PHS folks are in place to help new players. They approve character backgrounds, answer questions, and are, in essence, the Welcome Wagon of Seattle.

RPS - Roleplaying Staff: RPS is the group in charge of the roleplaying on the MUX. They run plots, approve final character sheets, make rules decisions, and provide karma, among many, many other things.

There are three staff departments here on SeattleMUX:

A D denotes a Director, a person in charge of one of the three departments.

A C denotes a Chief within one of the departments, who heads up a subdivision of the department. RPS, for example, has Chiefs devoted to Magic, Organized Crime, Gangs, and more.

An A as the third letter signifies an Admin who works under a Chief.

Occasionally you will also see a four-letter code prefaced with A. This A denotes an Assistant. The ARPD, for example, is the Assistant Roleplaying Director.

Please note that staffers in one area cannot do things that are the province of another. A BCA (Build/Code Admin) cannot approve your character. A RPA (Roleplaying Admin) cannot implement code changes. And so forth...

Can MUX staff play characters?

All divisions of staff except RPS can, and often do, have player characters in addition to their Admin logins. RPS cannot have active PCs, as this is regarded as a conflict of interest.
