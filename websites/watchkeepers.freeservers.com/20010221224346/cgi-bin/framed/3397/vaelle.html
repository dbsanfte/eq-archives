VAELLE PIXIEBANE

Character Name:

Vaelle Pixiebane

Class:

Ranger

ICQ:

NA

Background:

The greatest question that we ever ask ourselves in this life is “Who am I?’ Some of us seek to find the answers in the words of gods, others in the workings of nature. Yet many more never stop to even ask this question, so great is their fear that they might find an answer, and that that answer would destroy what little security they have in this world.

Who am I? I am Vaelle Pixiebane, ranger of Kithcor, mistress of the whip and Ogre therapist. But there is a more banal truth to my existence than that. I am a creature that was born, and a creature that will die. Eventually, the world will not remember that I existed. That is the great horrible truth that we all face in our lives. I have chosen to accept it not with the fatality displayed by so many of my kindred, but with a loving embrace of that truth.

Now, don’t misunderstand, I am no worshiper of Innoruk who sees death as a blessing, rather, I use my acceptance of my mortality to drive me to greater deeds while I am still alive. Truth be told though, life is not always easy to live.

As a child, I was plagued by pixies in my birth city of Kelethin. The vile misfits would berate me at all hours of the day, pouring honey into my hair, putting pine needles into my pillow and mattress and other minor yet maddening annoyances. I never understood then why they hated me so much, that knowledge came much later.

When I was but five summers old, several pixies kidnapped me from my bed and tied me down to the top of the wizard’s pyramid on the road between Felwithe and the Butcherblock mountains. I was trapped up there for nearly two days before the search party of rangers found and rescued me. My parents took me home then, but I was much changed. I neither spoke nor slept, ever fearful that the pixies would return as soon as I shut my eyes. Several times, in my madness, I insisted that I could fly away and tried to hurl myself off of the platforms of the City.

There was nothing that the Druids could do for me, the sickness was not in my body but in my mind, and so they counciled my parents to send me to the far away island of Odus, where hopefully the learned clerics there could save me from myself. That same night, a great spell was cast on my mother and me, and we were instantaneously transported into Toxxulia Forest. We were brought before the clerics of Prexus and Quellious, who examined me carefully before bearing any news to my mother. They could cure me, yes, but it may take years. If the job were to be done right, it would be best for her to leave me in their care until I was fit to return home. With a heavy heart, my mother agreed, and began the long journey home to Kelethin.

My years in Erudin where hard, the clerics trained me as they would one of their own children, expecting nothing less than perfection in a variety of disciplines. By the time I left the hallowed halls of the great Erudin Library nearly a decade later, I spoke dozens of languages, and had a great understanding of the nature of the world. I adored the cold civilized walls of that city, and part of me yearns for them still, but times goes by and life must be lived. I long for the fireside debates of philosophy that I participated in during my youth there. It was in the homes of these stern yet loving clerics that I found my purpose of life.

I heard tales of truly uncivilized races that knew only bloodshed and fury. The Erudites often debated if it was by birth or by the society that such creatures were raised in that they became so twisted. There was never any doubt in my mind though, and my own life among the people of Odus was proof enough for me. Through their nurture, I had become vastly different than any wood elf of Norrath. I used science and not faith to make life work for me. The Ogres of Norrath could be changed through this path as well, I decided and I was just the lass to do it. I read everything I could on their history and culture, and explaining my plan to my masters, I set off to prove my theory correct.

Most Ogres, I have found, actually want to be good people. They do bad things simply because that is all that they have ever known. Through great kindness, and much discussion (using small words of course) they can come to understand the error of their ways. The most difficult task, however, is convincing the rest of the world that this change is possible. But I help do it, one ogre at a time.

I never made it back to Kelethin, and I now call Rivervale and its Kithcor Forest home. The great ranger, Morin Shadowbane, taught me to use the skills of my birth as well as the ones attained in my years on Odus to become a master ranger and find a greater sense of peace with my two lives. Nature is very comforting, but it still is lackluster in comparison to the walls of Erudin for me.

It was in the woods of Kithcor that I learned of my talent with the whip. An odd weapon for a ranger, but I found its subtleties perfectly suited to my inner desire to act as a tamer of the world. I soon displayed a great prowess for it, and it is still my favored hunting weapon, when I seek to relax from the day’s work with the Ogres of Norrath.

I still bear a great hatred for the pixies. No amount of reasoning with my erudite masters could cure me of that. In letters from my mother, I learned finally why the pixies plagued me so in my youth. My father was not my father by birth, it seems. Rather, my mother had been led astray of her marriage vows by a wooing pixie man, and soon fell into his arms. I was the issue of that tryst. I am a changeling child, born not of one race, but two. When the mistress of that pixie man learned of his indiscretion and of my birth, she made it her goal to destroy me and the sin that I represented. So far, she has failed. I thrive, I live and I learn. That is the nature of the person that I am. It is all that I know how to do, and it is more than enough to keep me busy until my dying day.

And until that day comes, I plan to live my life to its greatest potential, my masters would expect nothing less of me. And I expect nothing less of myself.
