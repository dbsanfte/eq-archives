IN GAMING TODAY.....
2001.06.13| Are you shady enough? |by Sharac and Cyberdragon

Are you shady enough to be part of this Web game's conspiracy?

"Who the hell do you think you are?"
Click.

You too can be hounded by those kind of scary phone calls, from people who know too much -- just like in the movies.

In the 1997 film, The Game, Michael Douglas plays a man whose life is taken over by a game that smacks of some greater conspiracy.

Enter Majestic: a new Internet-based game of suspense where you play the role of the man at the centre of the mystery and intrigue.

Once you've signed up for the game online, a series of clues will be revealed to you via the Electronic Arts Web site. Each time you log on, a little bit more of the plot will be revealed.

Your objective is to wade through all the information and solve puzzles along the way that could point to the heart of a conspiracy.

Developed by Electronic Arts, Majestic exploits all the methods of the modern stalker. Each player is guaranteed to be harassed with creepy email, online video clips, faxes and phone messages from characters in the game.

"We use the unique canvas that the Internet provides us with to paint a picture that's difficult to discern from reality," said Neil Young, the game's creator. "I wanted to create what I imagined online entertainment would be like, a sort of blend of interactivity, storytelling and communications...over the Internet."

This game, he said, is for people who "wanted to be moved by their entertainment, not just run, jump and shoot."

Young, who now runs Synthetic Studios at Electronic Arts, can still remember a magazine ad for the company that ran back in 1983. The doublepage ad posed the question, "Can a computer make you cry?"

"I felt like that was the right objective for an entertainment experience," Young said. "It's something that we have to achieve if we want to graduate from being game-makers to being people who make interactive entertainment."

The game is still a few weeks from being released, but already there is a groundswell of interest in the online community. More than 100,000 people have already pre-registered for the game. Hundreds of fan Web sites have been made, inspired by the myriad web sites for fictional companies involved in the story.

Young said the game's name is taken from a real world conspiracy theory that ponders the existence of a handful of high ranking U.S. officials charged with dealing with "downed aircraft."

"The seed at the centre of the story starts with the majestic conspiracy," he said. It uses that seed to probe the big questions, that "the world that we live in today is not really what we think it is."

Majestic unfolds at the pace of the real world, which means eager players will have to learn the art of waiting. If a character in the game says they'll get back to you tomorrow, they mean tomorrow.

Younge, who has been testing the game for months, said it's the unexpected phone calls that are his favorite. Young was at an airport restaurant, trying to unwind after a long day of presentations when his cellphone rang.

"Who the hell do you think you are?" shouted a voice on the other end of the line. "Why do you give a shit about these people?"

Young was spooked.

It took him a moment to realize it was just a character from Majestic.

"That is still thrilling for me," he said. "Even though I've received thousands of phone calls from the game, it still catches me off guard sometimes."

With testing on the game just wrapping up, Young expects that those who have already registered will be downloading the first episode of the game later this month.

The rest of us will have to wait until mid-July to try a free pilot episode. After that, you'll need a platinum membership for EA.com which costs $9.99 (US) a month. A new episode will be created about every four weeks.

For those who don't think they have the time to be swept up in all the intrigue, Younge said the game is as time-consuming as you need it to be.

If you have 30 minutes a day to spare, you've got time to scare yourself silly.

Check out the Game's Web Site at http://www.majestic.ea.com

 

 

(excerpted from Rachel Ross, Toronto Star, June 13, 2001)
