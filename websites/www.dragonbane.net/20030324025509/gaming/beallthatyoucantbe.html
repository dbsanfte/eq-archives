IN GAMING TODAY.....
2001.08.25| Be All That You Can't Be ... Dragons and Ogres and Elves, Oh My

You're deep in enemy territory, your ears are melting and you're running out of birdseed. The Necromancers are bearing down on you, and there's nothing left to do but ask yourself: "Do I feel lucky?"

Afraid? You should be. This is what can happen when you take role-playing games into another realm - live action. Here, anything you could have conjured while playing Dungeons & Dragons in your parents' rec room can come to life with a little makeup, some faux-fur and plenty of duct tape.

The game is called Nero. It is, simply, a group of adults - ages range from 16 to 55 - wearing elaborate costumes and beating each other with foam weapons. It's motto: "Be all that you can't be." They are the largest Live Action Role Playing organization in the United States and Canada today. In Live Action Role-playing, you become the character of your fantasies, acting out the action you used to describe as you sat around your gaming table. Instead of dice, you use skills that your character has earned, as well as the skills the player brings to the game.

Since its creation in 1989, more than 40 chapters have sprung up all over North America. Nero is time consuming and players often devote most of their summer weekends to the game. Many of the organizers spend even more of their time, writing the plots and playing minor characters at events (usually monsters), arranging and collecting props and costumes, as well as handling the general administration.

Although it may seem just a bit over the top, Nero is just an extension of many other, by now more traditional escapist pursuits. Beyond live action role playing games (and there are many - from fantasy D&D based games such as Nero to medieval games like the Society for Creative Anachronists), role-playing takes place online.

There is Ultima Online and Everquest. In mainstream circles, escapism comes in the form of paint-0ball or laser-tag games, Medieval Times dinner theatre, and murder mystery weekends.

The weekend in Nero begins Friday night and, as the players begin to arrive, the weapons - hatchets, broad swords, daggers and lances made from foam padded PVC pipe and duct tape - are checked for safety and given stickers, once approved. Characters don their costumes, affix elfin ears or ogre's horns and final plot revisions are made.

The fictional town of Thrush Peak is the setting for the action, and it's hardly a peaceful place, having been besieged by the Necromantic Wars for 1,000 years now. It can be hard to keep up with the plot if you're a new player or if you've missed an event, but the Haylem's Herald, Thrush Peak's newspaper helps keep everyone current.

It's 1:00 a.m. Saturday before play gets under way, but no one is willing to sacrifice game time to sleep. Sleep means leaving yourself vulnerable to attack and missing out on the action, because the game doesn't stop for weary adventurers; it runs 24 hours a day. There's magic - and bug spray - in the air.

Some NPC's hide among the trees to stage "random encounters" with adventurers and skirmishes often ensue.

The impromptu meetings offer NPC's the opportunity to leak plot clues, and for players, it's a chance to hone their fighting skills, By casting small birdseed packets, which represent spells, or dueling and scoring points for each hit, the random encounter usually ends in victory for the playing character.

Full scale battles happen when a group of players set off together and encounter a collective of Necromancers or Elementals who've been plaguing local villagers.

NERO, an acronym that stands for the New England Role-playing Organization, is a new concept in game playing. Under NERO's unique rule system an entire fantasy medieval town or world can be created.

In the NERO game, you create character concept for a fantasy story and actually play the character. It is much like improvisational theater in that you have a framework created by the props and supporting characters and must develop your part as you progress through the storyline. Your character's attitudes, ambitions, and history are yours to define.

If the adventurous life is your calling, you may play the part of a wanderer, meeting with some friends in the tavern. While swapping tales, your party could get hired by a poor farmer in desperate straits, and spend the afternoon slaying a foul necromancer and his undead abominations.

If the epic life is not your style, then you can play the part of a town merchant. You can make your money selling your wares-and information-for the right price.

Or perhaps you wish to be one who investigates the magical arts, selling your scrolls and potions to earn the money you need to continue your studies.

Or would you rather try and become leader of the Mages' Guild? The choice is yours.

NERO is dedicated to the legendary days of high fantasy, while running a fun (and above all safe) game.

They have fierce warriors, crafty scouts, powerful mages, noble knights, and benevolent healers. There are smelly goblins, blood-chilling spectres, evil necromancers, villainous spies to fight, and your very own legends to create.

For more information on Nero chapters, see http://www.nerolarp.com

(source Jennifer Paige, Toronto Star, August 21, 2001)
