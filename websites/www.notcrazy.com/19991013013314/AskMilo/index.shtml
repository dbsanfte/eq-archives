Milo D. Cooper is the Man that Puts the 3D in Everquest. Without Milo There would be no monks, no dwarves, no uhh.. well, 3d characters. Okay, maybe there would be, but they would not be as we know them now.

Milo is the 3d Professional artist that made most of the characters for Everquest. Ranging from the player characters everyone knows, to the werewolves of the commonlands, to the rare, powerful, and majestic dragons.

He has also allowed us to bombard him with questions about what he does, but doesn't want to be answering questions about Everquest all day.

This means, for those out there not bright enough (or stupid enough to try anyways) don't send him questions about everquest unless it is directly related to 3D modeling.

  • Milo will *not* respond to any questions regarding employment at Verant or idea submissions!
  • Off the wall questions are a big bonus (After all, we're NotCrazy, remember?)
  • If your questions can be found easily elsewhere on the net, don't expect your question to be answered.
  • And make sure that you message contains a question, not just a statement or an opinion.

Now, for those of you just drooling to ask him [insert question here], email it to him at askmilo@notcrazy.com.

Milo Says:
We don't map our characters like Quake characters; we don't have a single bitmap that's applied to an entire model (in most cases). Rather, we have individual images for various sections of a character's geometry (a map for the head, a map for the foot, a map for the inside of the mouth, etc.). This makes it easier for me to apply mapping coordinates, and it also makes image swapping (e.g. changing the face of a character during the character creation phase) more efficient and more practical. The sizes of the bitmap images varies greatly, depending on the size of the geometry that a bitmap is meant to cover. I am not allowed to publicize the images themselves.

Monday, September 13

The main question: Will DX7 hardware accelerated transform and lighting create a back-breaking workload for artists?

One reason for using DX7 transform features would be to put more polygons on the screen (in each frame) thus creating an even better looking scene. That means higher level-of-detail in the models. Are current "master" models already being made with say 8 times more polys than are actually used in a final game model? Will artists be required to make even higher LOD models for DX7 games, and more importantly do you think they can keep up with the workload that might entail? Or are today's models ready for tommorow's games?

Thanks,
David Davis

 

Milo Says:
It's definitely going to make for a lot more work for us artists, not only because we'll need to devote more time to geometric details, but also because we'll have a much greater number of moving parts to take into account as we construct the models. It's the difference between the blocky shape that represents a playable EQ character's fist, and the giant, disembodied hand that lurks in some regions of the game. There is more to the construction of the latter than making it look much more like a real hand; not only do the textures take more than twice as long to apply, but all of the digits have to animate as well, which means that they have to be built in such a way that they move properly. All of this is before skeletal information is applied to the model, which process is significantly more difficult and time-consuming thanks to the increases in both the number of moving parts in a model and the number of its vertices (sorting out which vertices move with which bones can be a real pain with dense collections of vertices). When things like proportional vertex movement (e.g. skin over a ball-and-socket joint like the shoulder) and vertex bulging (e.g. bicep flexing) become more commonly implemented, things will become even more complicated. I foresee art teams becoming more like they are at film-quality special effects studios, where responsibilities in the art production process are more subject subdivision than they are in the gaming industry. As for the feasibility of today's models, they're destined for the desktop recycle bins.

Milo Says:
I'm not a hardware guru, so I wouldn't bet my life on the accuracy of this answer, but I'm pretty sure that swapping colors on a bitmap before dropping it into the accelerator would defeat the efficiency that makes palette swapping practical in the first place. It would result in a bunch of copies of the same texture, all with different color schemes, all taking up precious memory at the cost of the rest of the textures in the game (UO isn't a 3D-accelerated game, so that program doesn't have this issue). If there's a way for us to palette swap automatically-organized 16-bit textures, I'm sure that one or more of our programmers will come through. Whether the male barbarian shield problem will be rectified some time is not my decision. There's a chance that the fix will come with the expansion pack, but it'll be up to Brad in the end.
