For those of us who don't know exactly what Shadowbane is, could you please give us a brief summary of what it's all about?

Shadowbane is a massively multi-user RPG that we're producing for the PC and Macintosh platforms, which we hope to release in late summer of 2000.
Unlike the other titles in this area, Shadowbane has more of a "dark fantasy" feel -- we're trying to build a game that has a lot more detail, storyline, and atmosphere than anyone has attempted in a 3d virtual world before.

It almost seems like with some of the other games that player interactions are almost tossed in as afterthoughts. What we're working toward is a system that makes those interactions the primary focus of the game.

Shadowbane seems to be about power, who has some and who has more. From playing games such as Everquest and Ultima Online, it always seemed that for most gamers the emphasis was on gaining levels quickly and becoming more powerful even though the mantra for the game as a whole differed. With Shadowbane, you're essentially taping into the base of what current online RPG games try to deny. First, what drove you towards what others before you attempted to quell (i.e. direct competition, PvP) in some form or fashion? Second, can you explain some of the methods of acquiring power, such as Holy Intervention, constructs and declaring an empire? Third, will there be any possibility of attaining true power without succumbing to a darker mistress?
You're exactly on target here. If our players were merely interested in meeting and making new friends, they wouldn't be playing our game- after all, there are plenty of chat rooms who offer environments much more conducive to that sort of behavior.

People who log into an immersive, competitive World like ours are looking for more than a casual talk session. Shadowbane taps into the more 'primal' force beneath RPG's - not that the game won't foster role-playing, mind you. We just give them the Power to back up the persona they are building.

Lets also talk for a moment, then, about the roads to Power. Another of the failings of some of the Game's we've seen is that they treat Power as if it was a flip of the coin, rather than the multi-faceted jewel that it is. We decided that to make a deeper, more (and I hesitate to use this word here) 'realistic' World, we would have to present Power in a number of different forms. Rather than simply saying 'go here and do this,' we choose to present the players with a number of tough choices that will determine their overall effectiveness within our World. For example, it's not enough to say 'I'm going to be the greatest Warrior who ever lived.' What kind of Warrior will you be? Fast and agile? Strong, but dim? Tough as nails? Our game is designed such that no one character can reach mastery in every area of the game - it's all about choices, and striking the "perfect balance."

"Holy Intervention" refers to the fact that we decided that, all in all, none of our team was really into automated Quests. Typical quests seem to fall along the lines of either being told by some NPC to 'go fetch this item' or 'go kill this bad guy.' So we decided to take a different route altogether - we're recruiting a number of individuals (based on role-playing ability) to play what we call 'Feature Characters.' Some mortal, some immortal, it's the job of these Feature Characters to keep things 'stirred up' on the game. Each of them has a role to play, and a hidden agenda. They'll interact with Guilds, help (or hinder) players, and build Quests in a more interactive and (we think) engaging method than could possibly be automated in a single user game.

Lastly, you asked if the 'Dark Road' was the only way to power. To give you a quick answer, it's not the only way - it's just often the fastest.

There are a lot of PvP issues out there: Newbie killing, Camping, and stuff like that. Why won't this be another EQ or UO?

First off, our game doesn't work like the ones you've seen before. Lower level characters have little to fear from higher levels -- not that they can't be killed by them, if they're stupid enough to go wandering off into another guild's territory alone -- but rather the system is designed such that the amount you lose depends in large part on what the challenge was that you faced. An 80th level player killing a 5th level, for instance, would cause the 80th level to gain almost nothing, and the 5th leveler to lose even less. Will this mean that it won't happen? Of course not! But it removes the reward as an added enticement.

Second, protected areas. You're assuming that "newbies" will immediately be thrown in with the Wolves. This isn't the case. We're building the game with a "weaning" process -- at first, players are given a protected area to fight in. This should serve until they can find a guild to join, at which point they'll find the guild territory is (more or less) a safe area.
Also, after death, you'll pop back up dead-center within your Guild hall. To die multiple times to this hypothetical PKer, you'd have to consciously make the decision to walk back out and face that 80th leveler again on his terms, over and over again. If, knowing this, you still have the problem -- well, I don't know what to tell you.

As for camping, you've assuming that items (and monsters) will be loaded statically. This isn't the case, so the problem resolves itself. It's also just a symptom, I believe, of a bigger problem -- scarcity of equipment and monsters. Hopefully we can address both the problem and the symptom, and the whole issue will go away.
