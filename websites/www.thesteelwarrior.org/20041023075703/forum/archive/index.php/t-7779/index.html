/salute Ariene. Pretty much my view on this matter.

Also, unless you are routinely roleplaying in your descriptions of your activities in EQ, stop arguing that in any substantial way the contemporary semantic field of the word "rape" involves non-sexual destruction. In the 1700s, yes. In the 21st Century, no. The only semantic weight the word "rape" has in contemporary English outside of sexual assault is as a specific grain: rape seed.

Also, stop arguing that only people who have been sexually assaulted might find this term used this way offensive. Such arguments are untrue.

There are only two arguments for using this word as a description of your character meeting an unexpectedly swift demise at the hands of a mob: 1) the other common term ("killed") is scarcely more accurate, does not communicate the suddenness of your character's demise, and is not judged offensive; 2) you have the right to say what you like, and you are not compelled to worry about whether your description offends others.

Strictly speaking, number two is true. History has a way of making people who attempt to control the speech of others look very bad indeed, and, I think, rightly so. Such people either end up attempting to prevent others from expressing certain ideas, or (somewhat more rarely, but also more accurately in this case) prevent them from using certain words to describe otherwise acceptable ideas. Niether attempt is admirable, and both open themselves up to appropriately scathing satire (cf. Swift's "A Modest Proposal" or George Carlin's routine about words you can't say on the radio).

Nor can anyone be expected to avoid affending *everyone* else, if for no other reason than someone somewhere will always be offended by something.

For these reasons, I don't bother with asking anyone to stop using the term.

I would point out, however, that having the right to say a thing does not necessitate you saying it. By using the term "rape" in this context you are enjoying the benefits of a rhetorically direct and easy means of communicating the suddenness/violence/unexpectness/unpleasantness of your character being decisively crushed by a mob. Against this advantage, you weigh the possibility that, by using this particular rhetorical strategy rather than some of the many others available to you (and there are many many others), you may offend some number of other people.

So, you choose.
