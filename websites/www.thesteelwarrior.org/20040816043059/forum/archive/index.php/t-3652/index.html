Mynka
Warriors need to be fixed. I know this, you know this.

I've read some ideas on this and other boards, and some of them are pretty good, some are just nuts... But if Warriors are going to be fixed Sony needs to look beyond just fixing Warriors. They are going to have to look at the Knight's as well, how will changes made to Warriors effect Knights, how will changes made to Knights effect Warriors.

Below are my ideas...

1) Taunt needs to work. IMHO the whole taunt system needs to be redone. Taunt needs to work to the point that when the tank presses the taunt button he/she can be reasonably sure that he/she is going to get aggro. I do not think this should be 100%, but as the tank gains levels their success rate should climb (my opinion is by the mid 50's a tank should have ~80% chance of a successful taunt each time the button is pressed). 1b) Taunt needs to have more range, perhaps the range increases as the tank levels, but the tank needs to be able to at least have a freakin chance to draw mob aggro on incomming with taunt. Granted all classes that can taunt would benefit from a Taunt revamp, but please keep reading...

2) I'm going to be hated by every Paladin on Norrath for this but... Aggro generated by Paladin stuns needs to be reduced. Now, I've never been one to call for a Nerf, but I honestly do not think that Paladin's stunning to lock aggro is how the spells were intended to be used. Paladin's would benefit from the Taunt revamp so they could continue to hold aggro reasonably well. IMHO, Paladin's and Warrior's should be pretty much equal with each other (Warriors being slightly better) as far as aggro is concerned.

3) This seems to be a pretty unpopular idea, but... Warriors need their innate HP/AC bonus upped. If the difference at the end game between Knights and Warriors is so minimal as to not make a difference, then this needs to be fixed. Warrior's should have a fairly large lead over Knights in the HP/AC department.

4) Shadow Knights... Shadow Knights have a line of spells that they can use to draw aggro. The opinion of most Shadow Knights that I've seen is these spells do not work and that Clinging Darkness generates more aggro than their aggro spells. This is lame. Shadow Knights from just about the onset of EQ have been aggro drawing machines. Their aggro spells should work as intended. Yes, I mean lower the aggro generated by Clinging Darkness and up the aggro generated by the spells that are supposed to draw aggro... IMHO the ability for an SK to draw aggro should be far superior to a Paladin or a Warror (boy is that going to be an unpopular statement on this board...). The balancing act here is, if you do have a Warrior and an SK in the group you would certainly want the Warrior tanking because their HP/AC are so much better than the SK's, the Warrior would be able to hold aggro fairly well cause Taunt has been revamped, and the SK's aggro spells could be saved for pulling aggro in the case of an emergency.

These are my ideas, love them or hate them. :)

-M

Graal the Dorf
This was a quote from another thread that I think is appropriate:
Second, of course Akilae and Akira are not the same thing. But outside of a couple of GLARING exceptions (Harmony being one) where it's more practical to use a lower level spell over a higher one. If Stuns follow the same logical progression, there's no harm to the Paladin class except it's not as easy as it used to be.

The thing about higher level spells is that generally they have a greater effect. Nukes do more damage, buffs give more hp, heals heal more hp etc. With stuns, it really doesn't work that way except for that magical threshold when mobs go from level 55 to level 56 and suddenly become immune to normal stuns. The stun spells available to paladins were not designed so that there a better return on mana used for the higher level stuns. As wizards gain levels, their nukes not only get bigger, but they get more efficient. As clerics gain levels, their heals not only get bigger, but they get more efficient as well. There really isn't any reason that a cleric would load up minor healing at level 60 because they would get much less return for the time and mana spent casting the spell. They use the newest spell because it is clearly superior than the lower level version. Paladin stuns spells weren't designed nearly as well, for the most part.

There are 3 main things a paladin will use stuns for: spell interruption, stopping melee/movement, and aggro. A good spell interruption stun is fast casting and refreshes quickly. The duration doesn't matter. A good melee/movement stopper lasts a long time. Fast cast times are nice, but not as critical as they are for spell interruption stuns. A good aggro stun is cheap and refreshes quickly...that's it.

As an interruption stun, cease is better than "stun". It has the same cast time, a shorter recast time, and lesser mana cost. Those are the primary concerns when using stuns to interrupt. Desist isn't nearly as good at this role as Cease is, the spell it is supposedly an upgrade to.

I can see why SOE put in cease and desist when luclin came out, but I think they made a mistake. One of the main reasons for grouping a paladin, historically, was a paladin's ability to interrupt casting NPCs. Adding cease and desist were meant to expand on that role to lower level paladins, but I don't think it was really needed that early in levels. Up until the point mobs start nuking for significant amounts of health and/or complete healing themselves, interruption isn't nearly as important.

The reason cease is so fast casting is because lower level spells are generally faster casting. To interrupt, the stun has to be faster than the spell it is to interrupt, and most heals and nukes from level 24 and below have generally a 2-3 second cast time, with 3 seconds being the high end. The low mana cost is due to the level of the spell. 15 mana is appropriate for a level 9 spell...assuming the spell itself is appropriate at level 9 for a hybrid. These same design issues hold true for desist as well. The problem is that this doesn't leave many ways for "upgrades" to these spells to be better in the role of spell interruption. What they really should have done is only add in 1 stun before "Stun" at most, and it should have been designed so that as soon as "Stun" was available it would be an upgrade in every way over the previous stun.

Another reasons paladins will use cease is due to the level 55 cap on stuns. If you have 3 spell gem slots (mainly because there really isn't anything to put there that needs to be used often) a paladin will load up 3 stuns. We don't have nukes to load those spots up with. If you only have 2 stuns that will actually land on the mobs you are fighting because of their level, you will simply load the cheapest stun you can in that extra slot. Since you aren't getting any effect from that stun other than aggro, you want it be the lowest mana cost possible. SOE has addressed this to some extent by adding in Force of Akera recently. It is a fast casting, non damaging stun that will hit mobs up to level 61. This has replaced cease for me, even though it costs 6 times the mana. The caveat to that is if I am fighting mobs that are level 62 or higher. There is then no benefit for me to use Force of Akera over cease since neither one do anything but give aggro at that point, so I will switch out Force of Akera for Cease. If I am fighting mobs that are both above and below that level 61 threshold, I will keep Force of Akera loaded. The only time I actually want a stun to NOT have an effect is when I'm pulling. Stunning a mob with Force of Akilae adds 4 seconds to the pull time. That is 4 seconds during which a wanderer can walk into assist range of the mob I just aggroed. That is the only situation in which I prefer to use cease BECAUSE of the lack of effect. Generally I (and most paladins) want our stuns to actually DO something.

Even if there wasn't a problem with warrior aggro, I think that changes similar to those above would be warranted. That being said, I don't think that these changes would be a fix for warriors, it would basically be a fix for the paladin stun spell line and would make it so that a paladin has much less reason to use the lowest level stuns. The lowest level stuns are just too cheap, too fast casting, and refresh too quickly for the mana cost. Changing the lower level spells so that they are less attractive would allow a more natural spell progression where newer stuns are real upgrades comparitively over the older stuns. Using a level 9 spell at level 65 is cheesy, but if it is the best choice, why would you not? It shouldn't BE the best choice. With the changes outlined above, paladins would have much more incentive to use higher level stuns, which would up the mana cost. At the very least, it would dramatically increase the mana cost and reduce effectiveness (by increasing recast time) when a paladin uses the lowest level stuns for pure aggro.

In regards to warriors, I've mentioned what I think needs to be done many times: Taunt needs to work at a minimum of 75-80% of the time against even cons and below at the skill level equalling a warrior's taunt cap. A taunt success rate of 75% would give a 6% chance of having 2 failed taunts in a row, as opposed to the current 25% chance. A taunt success rate of 80% would give a 4% of 2 successive failures. An 85% success rate would give a 2% chance of 2 successive failures. I also think taunt should probably work just a touch better against raid level mobs too, maybe the following suggestion would help in that regard. Taunt should also add a bit more +hate when moving a tank to the top of the hate list; instead of top of the hatelist+x (whatever x is currently) the added aggro margin from a successful taunt should be tripled (x*3). The range on taunt could also use a small boost, maybe 25% increased range. It shouldn't be "ranged aggro" but the range on taunt is TOO small. I don't like the innate hate idea because it makes it harder for warriors who don't want aggro to do good DPS and it lessens the warriors ability to control whether he has aggro or not. We have warriors who get aggro when they don't want it already. It would simply make their lives harder to make that situation worse.

I think changing taunt to work at a much higher rate (75-90%) against even cons and below and increasing the added margin of hate from a successful taunt would probably be enough to make warriors as viable a choice for xp groups as knights are. The biggest reason many groups don't want a warrior (as opposed to prefering a knight) is due to lack of reliable aggro.

Ok. Seen the word "portion"? Re-read above if ya need to. As you can see, SOE coded stuns as spells with different parts. The spell can be resisted as a whole (one resist check), and if it's not the stun part of it has been designed to have no effect if the mob is immune while the other parts do affect it.

Some of the higher level stuns have 3 parts: stun, damage, pushback. SOE has recognized a while ago that calling such spells "stuns" while stun is in fact just one of many components will confuse simpletons. This is why they changed the message when a mob is stunned from "target is stunned" to "target has been struck by a sudden force" in a patch from around a year ago, in Luclin era. However, the spell line has always worked this way, with a mob either being affected by some or all portions of the spell.

This is what Graal has been trying to explain to you. A stun spell pushing back a stun-immune mob is not an exploit, it's design. Hope you're clear on this now ;)
