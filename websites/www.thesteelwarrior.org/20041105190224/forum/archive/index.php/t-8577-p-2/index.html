When a warrior is in combat, attacks can effectively be a miss, or one of 19 different values from the mobs standard attack. They avoid fairly well, but when they get hit, they have a distributed range of values they can be hit for. By maximizing AC, they can move the probability of a max hit lower in a predictable way.

Monks, because of their low AC either get missed or hit for max damage. It's a binary function. Instead of having a dependable system in place to protect them when avoidance fails they have a system setup to essentially give only 2 values out in combat: miss or max. They effectively have only one defensive avenue.

Lets look back at warriors and suppose for a minute that there were only 3 combat states: miss, min, or max. Avoidance all the same, they get hit the same amount over time as they normally do. AC as well continues to function the same way and reduces the chance to be hit for max at the same rate. However, since instead of 19 hit values we only have 2 min and max, the short term effect is much more profound. Its easy to see that such a change would not be preferable. In either of the two systems you could have a situation in which the average hit was for 500 damage. However, in the binary system it is likely for you to get hit for max 50% of the time than a system that would otherwise allow you to get hit for near 500 most of the time instead, which is much more manageable. Mitigation functions this way, but avoidance due to its binary nature provides no significant real-time advantages. it's effects are only seen in the long term, whereas, mitigation allows for some more immediate benefit.

In short, warriors would be outraged and the game very nearly unplayable if combat hit had only min and max values. This is effectively what the monks face today. Short delays had that damage come in smaller chunks than Large weapons and blunt weapons. mobs and players have limited hitpoints. The longer the combat, they more hitpoints a player/mob had, the closer the 2 different delay came togather until they exacly matched each other. But for those first few swings, which most combat only consisted of a few attacks, the slower weapon sometimes had an effective DPS of over twice that of a faster weapon. Those chunks evened out over time, but that was irrelevent because time wasn't unlimited. This is essentially the same issue from DAoC iterated in EQ.

Depends on the mob's DB/DI. The larger the mob's DB and the smaller the mob's DI, the better avoidance is relative to shielding.

Let's run some numbers here:

- I'm assuming that 10 avoidance = 1% more misses.
- to make the equations look simpler I'm defining x=1d20(DI)

10 avoidance = 0.01(DB + x)
1% shielding = 0.01DB

For 10 avoidance to equal 1% shielding you have 0.01(DB +x) = 0.01DB so x has to equal zero. So we know that 10 avoidance is always better than 1% shielding because for them to be equal, DI would have to be zero.

The most shielding I've ever seen on an item is 5%.

10 avoidance = 0.01(DB + x)
5% shielding = 0.05DB

For those to be equal, x must be 4 times DB so DB has to be 20% of the total damage. So any time a mob's DB is less than 20% of it's total damage 10 avoidance is superior to 5% shielding.

To me that makes it fairly obvious that avoidance is almost always superior to shielding.
