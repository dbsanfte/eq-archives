Deadalus
As far as I recall, the way to make a japanese sword so it's both hard to break AND sharp was to combine two types of steel.

See, steel grades vary based on carbon content. Low carbon steel is difficult to break and flexible, making blades made from it sure not to break when struck with other blades in combat. However, the flexibility means a sword made from it won't hold an edge and will go dull fast. High carbon steel, on the other hand, is hard and will hold a sharp edge very well, but is brittle.

What the japanese swordsmith would do would be combine the two types when making a blade. The japanese sword is, simply put, a high-carbon, hard steel core wrapped inside a low-carbon, flexible steel jacket. Wrapped on 3 sides, of course; 1 side has to be exposed to form an edge which would be sharpened. A sword like this was strong, flexible, and difficult to dull.

The hamon is where you can see the two metals meet; it's a line that runs down the sides of the sword and marks the point at which the hard core peeks out from under the soft steel jacket. It becomes prominent and easy to see during the heat tempering of the sword.

Since the two grades of steel used have different properties, they can't be tempered the same way; the core needs to cool at a different rate than the jacket. The swordsmith would wrap the blade in a special mix of clay before heating and then cooling it, leaving the edge exposed. That way, when he dunked the heated sword in a water bucket (some sick bastards used blood of their enemies, thinking it would give the sword added strength) the edge cooled fast, while the clay-wrapped outer layer cooled slower.

Anyway, that's it for katana making. A bit off topic, but this is a warrior forum, I figured some warriors would be interested in swordsmithing techniques ;)
