Bush: Let illegal aliens work in U.S.

January 8, 2004

BY JENNIFER LOVEN Advertisement








WASHINGTON -- Promoting a plan that could brighten his election year prospects with Hispanic voters, President Bush on Wednesday proposed legal status -- at least temporarily -- for millions of illegal immigrants working in the United States.

But the sweeping policy overhaul, offered with few specifics, also angered many in the president's conservative Republican base of support and drew criticism from advocacy groups who questioned whether it would do much to help immigrants.

Democrats were united in calling the plan a political ploy that offers a false promise of legitimacy for the undocumented workers.

Decrying a system that now has ''millions of hard-working men and women condemned to fear and insecurity in a massive undocumented economy,'' Bush urged Congress to approve a temporary worker program.

The program would be open to all undocumented workers now in the United States. Applicants who can show they have a job -- or for those still in their home countries, a job offer -- would get an initial three-year work permit that would be renewable for an unspecified period.

"We should have immigration laws that work and make us proud. Yet today we do not,'' Bush said in the White House's East Room, which he entered to loud cheers from dozens of representatives from Hispanic organizations and immigration groups.

Senate Judiciary Chairman Orrin Hatch (R-Utah) praised Bush for opening the debate and said a new immigration policy should ''extend a welcoming hand to those whose presence will benefit our nation and our economy."

Allowing undocumented workers, who make up an unknown percentage of the around 8 million illegal immigrants now in the United States, to work legally here would benefit all Americans, Bush argued. He said it would make the nation's borders more secure by allowing officials to focus more on the real threats to the country and would meet U.S. employers' dire need for workers willing to take the low-wage, low-skill jobs unwanted by many Americans.

It also is the right thing to do, Bush said, to pull immigrants who now live in the shadows of American society under the protection of U.S. labor laws, allow them to travel freely back and forth to their home countries, bring dependents they can support here with them and grant them the confidence to talk openly to authorities about crimes and exploitation on the job.

"One of the primary reasons America became a great power in the 20th century is because we welcomed the talent and the character and the patriotism of immigrant families,'' the president said. ''We must make our immigration laws more rational and more humane. And I believe we can do so without jeopardizing the livelihoods of American citizens."

But even as Bush made the announcement, the tough sales job ahead for the White House was apparent as the president's plan drew heated criticism from both the right and the left.

Many conservatives balked at the idea of any reward for people who broke the law by coming to the United States.

''I'm not for allowing illegals to stay in this country,'' said Rep. Virgil Goode (R-Va.). ''I think they should have to go back to their home countries ... and get in line with Jack, Suzy and John and apply for a guest worker position.''

With about half of the illegal immigrants estimated to be from Mexico, the program was designed in part to win Bush increased support among the powerful Hispanic voting bloc in the November presidential election. He won just over one-third of that constituency in 2000.

AP


Proposal falls short, activists here complain

BY LUCIO GUERRERO Staff Reporter




President Bush's plan to overhaul the country's immigration laws would have a major impact in Chicago, home to an estimated 250,000 undocumented workers, or about 5 percent of the local work force.

But local immigration advocates said the proposal doesn't go far enough to really make a difference. They are angered that the plan does not give illegal immigrants living in Chicago any assurances of gaining U.S. citizenship.

"President Bush is starting to look like all cowboy hat and no beef," said Juan Salgado, president of the board of the Illinois Coalition for Immigrant and Refugee Rights. "We want to send a clear message to President Bush: We've heard the campaign rhetoric before, and it is not enough."

The advocacy group is concerned that the proposal will only expose and exploit undocumented workers by giving them legal status to work for a short period and then demanding they go back to their country of origin.

"It's saying, 'Give us your sweat and your taxes,' but then gives them nothing back in return," said Joshua Hoyt, executive director of the ICIRR. "This won't be acceptable to anyone who cares about immigrant rights."

The program would be open to all undocumented workers now in the United States. Applicants who can show they have a job -- or for those in their home countries, a job offer -- would get an initial three-year work permit, renewable for an unspecified period.

The plan would be designed so the undocumented workers would eventually go back to their home countries. Bush proposed letting those immigrants have a tax-free savings account for money to use once they go back to their countries of origin.

"This new system will be more compassionate," Bush said. "Decent, hardworking people will now be protected by labor laws, with the right to change jobs, earn fair wages, and enjoy the same working conditions that the law requires for American workers."

In Chicago's Latino community, news of the proposal was met with some skepticism. Many scoffed at the idea of coming forward to only be given temporary status, but others like the idea of being able to travel back and forth to Mexico without fear of deportation.

"We can come and go like any other American citizen," said Alberto Sanchez, 28, an undocumented worker. But Sanchez said he did not like the idea of having to go back to Mexico once the guest worker status expired.

"This is my home now, this is where I want to be," he said. "Mexico is my past; this is my future."





Business groups in favor but have questions

BY DAVID ROEDER Business Reporter





Business groups in the Chicago area voiced qualified support Wednesday for President Bush's proposal to confer legal status on undocumented immigrants who hold jobs in the United States, while the leading voice of labor attacked the plan for not going far enough.

Liberalizing immigration laws could help companies address chronic labor shortages, business leaders said. But they said they want to see the details first.

Among their concerns is whether the Bush plan will enforce new record-keeping requirements on employers. They also wondered if by simply applying for amnesty, illegals could implicate their employers for violations of an immigration law dating from 1986.

The so-called Immigration Control and Reform Act conferred amnesty on undocumented workers then in the United States but imposed strict penalties on employers for knowingly hiring illegals.

"This raises a lot of issues from an employer's perspective,'' said Gerald Skoning, senior partner at the law firm Seyfarth Shaw LLP, which handles employment issues from management's side.

One intent of Bush's plan is to match foreign workers with jobs in the United States that people here don't want. That's laudable, said Gerald Roper, president of the Chicagoland Chamber of Commerce, but it could pit that group against native-born Americans.

As the economy improves, businesses should rehire those downsized out of a job or making do with reduced wages and benefits, he said.

For years, labor unions opposed increases in immigration, arguing the newcomers held down wages. But they've adopted a different position in recent years as immigrants have come to dominate job ranks in restaurants, hotels and other service sectors that unions are trying to organize.

AFL-CIO President John Sweeney criticized Bush for not backing a proposal that would lead to citizenship for illegals. "The plan deepens the potential for abuse and exploitation of these workers while undermining wages and labor protections for all workers,'' he said.

Critics of U.S. immigration policies contend it's a myth that Americans don't want low-wage jobs in restaurants, housekeeping or landscaping. Business leaders said their experience shows otherwise.

Competition for kitchen help has raised those wages, yet the jobs are hard to fill, said Colleen McShane, president of the Illinois Restaurant Association. She said Bush's proposal would improve the lives of today's workers without encouraging more illegal immigration.
