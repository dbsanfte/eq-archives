Just to put a little "legal" color on the argument that the person posting didn't break the NDA, so all is OK.

Let's say Joe signs an NDA about GoD. Bill knows that if he posts publicly, he can be sued for breaking the NDA. So Joe says to Bill, hey Bill, I can't post about GoD, but you didn't sign an NDA, so you can post. How about I tell you what I know, and you post it? He does, and Bill posts.

If all of that happens, SoE has a potential case against Bill for tortious interference with contract. Also, SoE can sue "John Doe", an unnamed person who violated the NDA, and then subpoena Joe, and force him to say, under oath, who told him about GoD, and whether he and Joe had conspired to break the NDA. Once Bill is forced to cough up the name (and he can't refuse to answer, that would result in him being held in contempt of court), SoE could substitute Joe's name for John Doe's in the lawsuit, and go after poor Joe from there.

Will SoE do this? Probably not, for several reasons. First of all, the cost of enforcing the NDA would be greater than the value of nailing Joe, and perhaps Bill too, to the wall. Second, the publicity would be negative. If SoE really cared, they'd probaby send Joe a nasty e-mail from their lawyers, threatening all the terrible things that could happen to him if he doesn't keep his mouth shut. And finally, minor breaks in the NDA, particularly as the release of GoD approaches, are actually in SoE's interest. It raises curiosity and interest in GoD, gets word of mouth moving, and makes it more likely that SoE will sell more copies of GoD.

However, please don't say that everyone is in the clear, simply because the person who did the actual posting hasn't signed an NDA. It's just not true.

I worked in the computer game industry at one pont for many years. The NDA is exactly what it says. A company has a variety of reasons to ask people to sign NDA's. In exchange for signing people are seeing product before it is released.

Hypothetical example -
Think for a moment about WoW. Imagine it has a real neat feature that will differentiate it from other products. Bob signs the NDA, tells Bill about the feature, and Bill tells Bert who works for SoE. SoE says, hmm neat feature - let's put it in our next patch (btw as a designer this is one of the reasons why outside submission is avoided).

Six months before that feature hits the store shelves it appears in a competing product. Depending on the circumstances and impact of an NDA violation a company could:

1. Get back to the initial violator and sue his socks off.

2. Decide to not allow anyone in the the outside Beta test program to test in the future - get a whole new group of outside testers.

3. No longer do outside Beta testing.

4. Only test parts of the product and withhold those exciting new features.

Act like the NDA is meaningless, divulge information, provide a forum where such information is displayed publicly and you potentially affect a company's willingness to take a risk letting users see a product ahead of time and incorporating their feedback.

There are professional third party Beta testers - they'd be glad to play EQ2 while y'all do not.

Just wanted to clear up any confusion given my experiance.
