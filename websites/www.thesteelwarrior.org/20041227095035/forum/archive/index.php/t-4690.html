There is a dps advantage on the mob's backside. Position the critter with its back to the PETS. Non tanking melee should also move to the backside, if they don't you could let them know they will land blows more frequently from the backside. But position the mob for the pets, which means you will be facing the casters, also giving you the best ability to respond to caster aggro.

As for healing, ask for a pulse heal if the fight leaves you at 50 percent. Stress the initial unpredictability of combat damage on the pull, until Mob is slowed.

Edit:
As for slower positioning. It is thoughtful for slowers to come to melee range, HOWEVER, if they cast from a distance there is a reduced chance of aggro. Range to mob is a considerable factor for aggro determination. Also, if the Shaman is ranged you have time to Sit to bring the Mob back.

I could not find this post in the forums here. I am pasting the post from a save I had made some time ago.

The extract below is from the Steel Warrior board, posted by Brokkah Narrow.

There are sections that will apply to all classes.

1) Mob Positioning

What are the types of positioning required?

i) Pin/Cornering - Vindi style fight. Everyone on the mobs back, MT in the corner. You can disregard everything said below if we are cornering as positioning is fixed.

ii) Wall pin - Aaryonar style fight. Mob body is in the wall, head sticking outwards. Melee balance on either side to pin it in place. Positioning very important, melee must be constantly aware of mob drift.

iii) Wall Walk - Dagarn style fight. Everyone on the mobs back and sides. MT walks the mob up and down a wall, calling swithces. Positioning not too important here, although all melee directly on the mobs back means more switches, which means a reduction in dps. Attacking from the back left/right quarter allows you to still avoid the mobs defensive skills whilst helping to reduce the speed of push.

iv) Standard Walk - Eashen style fight. Everyone on the mobs back. Positioning quite important to avoid lateral drift. Melee must spread evenly across the mobs back, allowing the MT to walk in a straight line back and forth calling switches when needed. If you see the mob drifting laterally, attack off, reposition yourself to counter, carry on.

v) Circle Walk - BoT Fire Tower style fight. Everyone on the mobs back. MT will spin/walk in a circle, positioning fairly important to avoid drift into walls or caster area. Pets especially need to be managed carefully.

vi) Mob in place, non-rooted - Auliffe/Cursed style fight. Mob must be kept static. positioning is VITAL in these fights. Melee and pets must constantly reposition themselves to ensure the mob is not drifting. MT can reposition slightly but generally this tactic is used where space is at a premium and drift cannot be allowed to happen.

Why is mob positioning important?

i) To avoid harmful AE
ii) To allow line of sight damage dealers to do their job (Mage, Ranger)
iii) To allow rogues to Backstab
iv) To keep main tank/raid in range of healing classes and nukers.
v) To allow melee to maximise their dps.
vi) To prevent corpse being unreachable to loot
*vii) To avoid "cannot see target" messages. (see note 1)

What are the limits on a MT's ability to control positioning?

Your MT can control the direction the mob faces, thats all. He has no control over lateral push(strafe), speed of vertical push and to a lesser extent wheeling of the mob (ie where it seems to be pushed around in a circle). Also his level of aggro limits the amount of re-positioning he can do by summon/back up or straight moving.

What factors effect mob position?

i) Pets - Pet push is one of the biggest factors in positioning. In order from most push to least my experience shows that pets push in this order - Beastlord, Mage, Shaman, Necro, Enc Charmed pet. Others do not do enough damage to warrant mentioning. It is the pet caster's responsibility to ensure their pet is providing a balanced push on the mob. Be proactive about this, if you can see a mob drifting and your pet is contributing to that drift then back it off and move it to counter the push. Ideally before the fight starts, pets should be positioned evenly around the mob to minimize their push. The only exception to this is where we pin (or corner) a mob, in which case stick em all on its back. On a mob where we do not corner it NEVER EVER USE YOUR SWARM PETS. Seriously, the dps increase from it is not worth the insane push these pets can generate.

ii) Melee - Melee damage contributes to mob push. It does it in a couple of ways - straight melee and special attacks. Straight melee weapons push in this order, most to least push - Blunt, Slashing Piercing. Blunt weapons can push a lot. Piercing weapons hardly push at all. Special attacks such as kick, bash, slam and monk flying kick all push more than normal melee. This can be used to your advantage in 'counter-push' situations. It is melee's responsibility to balance themselves around the mob they are attacking. Do not all stand in a clump, arrange yourselves so push is spread as evenly as possible. This will help prevent wheeling and lateral drift. MAX MELEE RANGE. There is a reason this gets screamed every raid. The further away from the mob you are, the less effect your melee has on push. It also helps with aggro management but I will discuss that later on. Finally max melee range will allow you to avoid some AE effects that you would otherwise catch, and if(when) we get to the elemental planes there are mobs who AE RAMPAGE for quad 2-3k damage. You need to be at max melee range to avoid it, so now is a good time to start practising.

iii) Spells - Direct Damage and stun spells probably contribute the least to mob push due to virtually all bosses being immune to stun. However, spells with a knockback component can still move a mob. Again, try to not bunch up and all cast along the same axis, as this will just push the mob along faster.

What do I do when positioning starts to break down?

STOP ATTACKING
BACK PETS OFF

These 2 things should be done immediately it becomes apparent mob positioning has broken down. This will give your MT a chance to re-align himself. Now get back into position and start attacking again on the MTs call, arranged logically to push the mob in the desired direction using the suggestions raised above.

Listen to your MT. He is controlling the direction of the fight, if he needs melee to move then do it. If he needs pets moving then do it. If he asks you to stop attacking then for gods sake DO IT. We dont shout and scream for our own amusement, it is to ensure mob slaying goes as smoothly and efficiently as possibl

If in this situation you have aggro, try to move as close to the MT's position as you can.

Can I help 'counter-push'?

Be aware during fights. Experienced players will know roughly where a mob should be positioned during that fight. If you can see it start to drift then by all means move to counter it. On cursed for example, melee are constantly moving to balance the mob in an area roughly 5 foot square. The same tactic we use there will work on a larger scale if you think intelligently about the direction the mob is moving, and what you can do to counter this.

2) Aggro Management

What is aggro management, and why do i need it?

Aggro management is the process by which you ensure aggro remains on the main tank, and away from you. It is necessary in raid situations to allow the healing power to focus on 1 person, and keep the mob pointing in 1 direction. This allows the rest of the raid to do maximum dps with the minimum of risk.

Warriors generally tank boss mobs due to 1 discipline, defensive. This allows us to cut melee damage taken to as much as half, but has the side effect of reducing our own damage output by 35%. This makes us increasingly reliant on procs for aggro, and also the 'hit and hope' taunt button. Additionally some warriors will have the area taunt skill which has a side effect of being a 100% successful taunt button. Unfortunately it is on a 15 minute timer, so normally is not available more than once a fight.
(* The "incite" line of taunt has been added recently, which does help somewhat.)

These are the limits imposed on warriors, it is your job to work within the confines of them. If the warrior loses aggro once its established, its not his fault, its yours.

How do I manage my aggro?

All -

WAIT FOR ASSIST. The main tank waits to call assist for a reason. It is to give the debuffers time to debuff, heal chain to settle and to ensure he has sufficient aggro to keep the mob on him for as long as possible. I dont care if you have a method for reducing your aggro, there is absolutely NO excuse for attacking a mob before the MT calls assist. You arent helping him, and potentially you will screw his aggro generation up.

Do NOT sit on inc, or for the first few seconds of the fight. You are asking to get splattered.

Keep above frenzy HP levels. Nothing a warrior can do including AE taunt will override frenzy aggro due to low HP. It is your responsibility to get out of AE range, get a heal, or both.

On a tank switch - STOP ATTACK, CALL PETS OFF. Let the new tank regain aggro, he will call assist again when ready. Treat a tank switch as effectively the start of a new fight and you shouldnt go wrong.

Melee - (* see Note 2)

Cycle attacks. Your dps will massively overhaul the main tanks via pure melee. Just turning attack off for 1 round every 15 seconds or so will help him.

Put your aggro proc weapons away. Any weapon proccing slow, root, stun, knockback, rune or pure aggro should be bagged or at the very least off-handed.

MAX MELEE RANGE. A lot of mobs aggro is based at least partially on proximity. The further you are away from the mob, the less chance you have of getting unwanted aggro.

Use your aggro reducing skills. Monks and SKs should FD, it has a fixed aggro reducing component (go to monkly-business and check if you dont believe me). Rogues evade, rangers jolt if it can stick.

Hybrids, dont use aggro spells such as stun, snare, slow, ranger imbues with stun component. There is 1 situation this is acceptable and thats where you are the puller. In that case, go to town and transfer the aggro to warrior via AE taunt.

Casters -

Time your nukes/dots. Dont start chain casting your biggest DD from the word go. Start slow, and build it up towards the end. There is no way any Damage caster should be getting aggro from a warrior on a raid.

Use your aggro reducers. Boggle, Concussion, Spell casting Subtlety, the tash aggro reducing thing from PoI. All are gonna help.

(* my edits - Birdienumnum)
Note 1.
"cannot see target" -- Seems that this is dependent on whether the mobs feet are in your LOS (line of sight). In some cases this may be because of the terrain, in others because the mobs feet are in a wall. Levitation will help the casters LOS in most cases.

Note 2.
I would add a 3rd section here for Melee Management.
Good melee management will aid the crowd controllers, healers and other casters.
I would even go so far as to suggest that when in a raid situation, that someone be appointed as melee leader. This leader would NOT be a tank or pure melee class. A magician would be a good choice. The magician is far enough away to have a bigger picture of the fight. This person could call for tank switches, direction switches, and melee placement, as well as mob positioning. Good melee management will most probably save a wipe.

A couple of my preferences concerning non-raid exp groups, all other things being equal.
I prefer facing the casters, in part because I can see them but also because it can make targeting the mob slightly easier for the casters if I'm not in the way.
In some experience groups where puller is bringing mobs to a stable camp, especially in pick-up groups, the entire camp can drift as people engage pull after pull (basically, people rush the mob on each pull, and over time inch away from the initial camp). This is sloppy, and can be avoided if puller pulls to single spot and everyone knows where the spot is.
I like it when puller pulls to the pets. I can grab agro (if I'm not pulling) and postion the mob so the pets are at it's back faster than the casters can move their pets . Also, if pets are "guarding here" then everyone in the group knows exactly where the fight will take place (or start, at least). Obviously, the pets should be guarding the right spot, heh.
Basically, I figure that if I properly and efficiently position the mob, I add many times more damage than I do through my own melee. To do that positioning, I sometimes need my group to do certain things (or stop doing certain things). So, in stable camps early on I'll often tell folks that I want to engage the mob right here (standing where I want to be) and that I'll position the mob so it's facing this way. It really only takes a second to get everyone on the same page, and unless you go out of your way to be an asshole, few people will think it amiss that the main tank wants to decide where the fight will take place, and everyone will know how to position themselves and it will be much easier for your groupmates to adjust what they're doing to increase efficiency.
Don't be afraid to talk to people in pick-up groups.
It does seem to me that the role of a tank in mob positioning is really one of the things that distinguishes good tanks. It also seems to me that eventually everyone who is learning how to play a warrior starts thinking about these questions.
Perhaps there ought to be something in the library on this?

For rangers using archery: keep the mob out of walls.

Regarding pets: I will always reorient to present the mob's backside to pets, simply because (as a pet user myself) I know how damn hard it is to move pets where you want them. I rely on the pet casters to make sure their pets are all on the same side of the mob, however, so that it's <I>possible</I> for me to turn its back to them all.

Making sure the mob's backside is to the pets is relevant for other things than the backstab pet, though. Melee damage is dramatically improved by the pet not having to go against the mob's combat skills. Also, mobs that enrage can <I>chew</I> through a pet's HP, but if the pet is behind the mob, it's not an issue.

Regarding rogues: I don't reposition to turn its back to the rogue. Rogues can move as well as I can, and I have other responsibilities. I am <I>very</I> careful, however, to make sure that the mob's back is not to a wall or tucked into a corner; there's no percentage in making the rogue's job difficult or impossible. If there are pets present, I will reposition according to the pets, and trust the rogue to reorient accordingly.

I developed these skills over the course of several hundred LDoN normal missions (I lack the defensive AAs for LDoN hard). Those have also strongly shaped my attitudes toward the other topics under discussion: watching my HP, and pulling while not at full HP. Therefore, please view my comments on those two from that perspective (well-geared warrior still stuck doing LDoN normal missions, usually with near-elemental or elemental-level groupmates).

Watching my HP: in my experience, there are two distinct classes of healer: those with whom I have to watch my HP at all times, and those with whom I can completely ignore my HP unless things go <I>completely</I> to heck. It's not even really a gradation, in my experience; healers either have a sense of the timing, pace, and flow of the fight, in which case I can ignore my HP unless things get incredibly messy--or they don't have that sense of timing, pace, and flow, and I have to watch my HP at all times, even on simple double-pulls or what have you.

I'm comfortable watching my HP, and doing things like popping Defensive, turning off attack, going Furious, etc. But I generally choose not to group with such healers again--it's not worth the hassle, and it decreases my efficiency for other tasks (I regularly dual-box my warrior and my beastlord, often with primary slowing duty and Ferocity maintenance on the beastlord, as well as target selection on the warrior, so I can be fairly busy even in LDoN normal).

With a "competent" healer (that is, one with that sense of timing), I just don't bother watching my HP except in true catastrophes. It's just not useful. Yay for competent healers!

Pulling while not at full HP: usually, on LDoN normal, our enchanter, bard, or cleric pulls for us. Yay Pacify. But the stuff in normal hits for so little, and enchanter crowd control is so effective, that we almost never use CHeal, and my hit points at the start of a fight are mostly irrelevant. *shrug* If we were running LDoN hard missions, where the mana efficiency of CHeal can be critical, and where an unslowed mob can chew through my 7kish hit points in a surprisingly short period of time, I'd want a puller who waited until the healer had topped me up, and a healer who took the time to make that final CHeal land just as the mob turned and tried to run. But at the level of difficulty cross ability that I've been playing at, it's just not relevant.

Hope this helps,
