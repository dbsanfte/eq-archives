Hey Hanadan :)

I see the point about class neutral percentage increases, but I think it leaves something out.

I've been trying to make this point on PoN, with limited success so far, but class neutral percentage increases are not neutral in effect, because of declining marginal benefits.

The key point is that the warriors and knights are not compared against each other in a vacuum: mobs are involved. Since the point at which people get exp from mobs is fixed, there MUST be a point of diminishing returns for extra mitigation. That means the class with a lower initial base gets more out of a class neutral percentage increase, since they have further to travel down the declining marginal benefit curve.

We can argue about the magnitude of the effect of declining marginal benefit, but there's simply no question it exists, since the point at which people get exp from mobs is fixed.

For example, here's a thought experiment. Is the difference between a 100,000 hp tank and a 50,000 hp tank particularly relevant, if the environment is unchanged? Is it as significant as the difference between a 10,000 hp tank and a 5,000 hp tank? Not really, even though that's 50,000 hps potentially we're talking about. The reason is diminishing returns, or declining marginal benefit.

This is why hybrids are not losing out when they get the same percentage increase to both sides at the same time (despite whatever Thott says). That's true, even though the absolute amount of the attribute earned is smaller. In fact, over time they gain ground with those changes, since mobs are fixed. The only time the declining marginal benefit curve changes shape is when mobs are upgraded, typically at a new expansion. (Actually, it probably doesn't change shape, but people would be repositioned on the curve.)

That's why LDoN scares me. If what I'm saying is true and has an actual effect, specialist classes in general, and pure melees in particular, are in trouble. Not only will the normal progression of mudflation mean generalists benefit relatively more (given their greater average distance from the point of diminishing returns in each of their skill areas), but here's a new expansion with no particular promise of greater mob power. For example, when people first zoned into a PoP zone like PoV, the damage output by the mobs was a little shocking, and the smidge extra mitigation offered by warriors had value. Similiarly with raid targets. Now PoV exp mobs are trivial, and rangers, bards or shaman tank them just fine. Mudflation pushes people down the marginal benefit curve, and benefits generalists (hybrids) disproportionately.

There's simply no question the declining marginal benefit problem is real; the only issue is how strong is the effect.

Note: here's where raids vs. exp is a real problem. It's easy to argue about declining marginal benefits on exp mobs; on raid targets with that curve so far out it's a lot harder. That's why warriors have to be balanced by additional utility or aggro in exp groups, not by extra mitigation that could spill over to raids, where the marginal benefit curve might still be in a challenging place.

It actually does apply, in the following way.

1. (a/b) != ((a+100)/(b+100)), where A and B are not the same. We can all agree on that I hope. This is why multi-class, multi-attribute items are a problem.

2. (A*1.1) != ((.75A*1.1)+(.25B*1.1)), where A and B are the same IFF (if and only if) values above 1 for either A or B show diminishing returns. (Yes, I know this is kind of a stupid expression, but I'm just trying to illustrate how tanking and casting sides might be rendered.)

That is,

1. We all agree all/all itemization screws warriors.

2. My further point was that, given the fixed position of the mob exp level to players, class neutral percentage increases ALSO screw warriors, even though they look the same. They just do it more slowly. Hybrids should get progessively smaller percentage increases over time, for proportionate balance, assuming no changes in environment. Otherwise, specialist classes hit the point of diminishing returns much earlier; that is, the more rapidly diminishing returns of the specialist should be balanced by the progressively smaller percentage earned by the generalist. That doesn't happen when everyone is getting the same percentage increase at the same time, including both parts of the multi-class chars. This is how shared AA hurts specialist classes.

Mudflation and the fixed exp point are why balanced percentage gains by generalists kill specialist classes over time (in addition to flat-out bad itemization).

Edit: here's an effort to illustrate what I'm saying, since I don't think I'm writing it well.

all/all sucks and clearly disproportionately benefits classes that can use multiple attributes more than those that can't. so I'll leave those alone.

But say a warrior is 100% melee, while a paladin is 75% melee adn 25% casting. You'd think that by adding 10% to all attributes it all works out, right? After all,

100 x 1.1 = 110
(75 x 1.1) + (25 x 1.1) = 110

It's a wash, right? But now let's say the point of diminishing returns FOR ANY SINGLE SKILL (tanking or casting) was 101. Now the warrior just got most of his increase past a point of diminishing returns (and so did a specialist caster). And the further he gets past that point, the less value each additional percent has. Meanwhile, the hybrid got clear improvements well short of the diminishing return point on both sides. So even though the percentages and the cumulative amounts are the same, the effect isn't. His total growth path is much larger.
