Well, first let me say that I don't believe experience grinding has ever been really risky - knight aggro control hasn't changed that. Second, it's not risk free either.

Fighting mobs even in Tier 2, a non-uber paladin can get the smack down very quickly. I have all of the tanking AA's (well, minus planar durability which would add about another 200 hp) and can't really improve my gear much outside of LDoN. Trash mobs in PoV can still put the hurting on me to the extent that I need to be chain complete healed - not all of them for sure, but some.

Now let me qualify that some. My group rarely has a slower, so when I'm fighting mobs that are hitting for 500-600, it takes my group's being on their collective toes to keep me alive and win. I have to stun for more than just aggro - I need to slow the mob's damage as much as I can. Without the mob's having it's magic resistance debuffed, Akera gets resisted a lot (and it only works up to 61st level mobs anyway - I have one stun I can rely on to hit mobs that are, say, in the "back cave" of Valor).

In other words, for my group, experience grinding is far from risk free. It isn't like I expect to die a couple of times a night, but I do die in regular experience grinding - if the healer is waiting to start a CH at 40% and the mob gets a spike - especially if I get stunned - I'm dead (it's annoying to die stunned when I could have used Lay Hands, but it happens every couple of weeks - just the roll of the dice). The healer can start their heals at 50% instead, but then I'm a mana drain.

To put it another way - aggro lock only trivializes things when you have a slower. The warrior tanking advantage is important only when you don't have a slower (how many times have people said "my wizard can tank a slowed mob?").

Knights had snap aggro and practically aggro lock (I do lose aggro - it's not perfect) before PoP. Slow existed before PoP. However, the combination of slow, extremely hard hititng mobs and aggro lock is, I believe, what has really let warriors out in the cold. Nerfing our ability to generate aggro takes away something we've had for years because in SOME contexts (PoP and, to a lesser extent, LDoN) it makes us much more desirable for groups. The same goes for slow, for that matter.

The environment changed. Think of it is natural selection - slow and aggro control became much more vital because of how the game world changed. Warriors were not allowed to EVOLVE to keep up with the game world, so of course "natural selection" means that warriors will start losing spots in groups. I don't know how this isn't obvious from looking at the game world as a natural environment.

Paladins (and shadow knights to some extent) DID close more of the HP gap (both due to itemization and new spells) on top of already having abilities that became MORE desired as the world changed. It was a NATURAL progression for paladins to gain more as they levelled because all spell casting classes gained more.

However, where the real screw up is, as I said in the article, warriors got almost ZILCH as they levelled from 60. You didn't keep pace with your "competitors" in the environment. In the natural world, when that happens, you get extinction of the species that didn't keep up. That's what's happening now. Just this morning, my wife saw someone /ooc "LDoN group looking for paladin." Her response (to me, not in the game) was, "you guys are going to get nerfed."

In nature, there's no outside "force" that can make a species do better by giving it some new adaptive abilities - Norrath isn't analogous to the real world in that sense. SOE has and has had the power to have warriors keep up. One thing about the game that is practically undeniable is that there's been a caster bias since day one. Years ago paladins were considered MUCH more as melee (the old "75% warrior, 25% cleric" thing) - and we got the shaft because of it as well. Now our abilities are much more balanced between the caster and melee side (we're primarily melee, but we rely a LOT on our caster side to melee well) and so we see more benefits as they improve casters again and again.

I've really been trying to mentally put myself in the shoes of a warrior (which has been helped by those of this community that have stopped by PoN to post their ideas in a reasonable and calm manner). One of the thing that strikes me most when I do that is, "What do I have to look forward to as I level?"

When PoP came out, I didn't level due to how they restructured the blue con range. I valued my ability to go somewhere with just my wife (oh, and my 2-boxed druid) and get experience. I didn't want to be forced into needing a slower just to get experience. The months that I spent just grinding out AA's without levelling, I was sorely anticipating getting my new spells. I couldn't wait to level up and get Ward of Nife, Pious Might, Deny Undead, Aura of the Crusader, etc. I knew they'd make a big difference for me, but wasn't willing to sacrafice my flexibility for them.

Now, if I imagined myself as a warrior instead, what would I have been anticipating? A few more hitpoints, a bit better tanking performance and a slightly larger damage bonus? Well, I got those as a paladin too and, though I understand their value, they weren't exactly something I'd really look forward to. Further, I wouldn't be able to see their effects on my performance except by parsing anyway. How about a warrior in a group where a parchment/rune dropped? Assuming I'd be allowed to roll, I guess it represented some decent cash for a while, but that's it. A paladin missing spells would be itching to get back to PoK to turn them in.

How in the hell did they make an expansion where several classes got almost nothing from it but other classes got a bunch of stuff? LoY didn't show QUITE the same disparity, but it was there. I was already 60 when LoY came out, but I still did the spell quests to get Light of Life - it was "better" than Superior Heal simply because of the cast time, even though it healed less. My druid uses Pure Blood constantly in LDoN. So, again, what did pure melee classes "get" to balance out what casters got? It would make sense for them to get nothing ONLY if casters need to make up some ground because pure melees were so far ahead.

Now, I understand from casters that Luclin was SORT OF a dry spell for them because of the long fights and inability to provide consistent value over the fights (thus the need for all the mod rods, etc.). It was not party for melee, either, though, with the high AC values of mobs, the small DI that made mitigation almost worthless, etc. Luclin was a dry spell for everyone in that fashion. Maybe SOE thought they "owed" something to casters after Luclin? *shrug*

Even if warriors had been still in balance, it would have been a reasonable thing to do to at least give them some new "flavor" abilities so they were getting SOMETHING out of the expansion - just on a basis of "fun" (not that that necessarily fits into EQ ;)).

Anyway, an average paladin will tank noticably worse than a warrior IF they are using equipment of similar quality AND the paladin doesn't cast any spells. We can almost completely close that gap by our spell casting, at the cost of mana. Finally, that's the achievement of the design impetus behind the paladin class - we use spells to make us the equal of warriors. However, that assumes that spell casting comes at a cost - mana.

The problem is that for SOME cases, mana is no longer a cost. A paladin with VoQ, Spiritual Purity (or whatever), FT10, MC3 and even a bard is never going to run dry of mana in an experience group. They are going to be able to chain stun, heal and also nuke.

However, that's not the majority of paladins. I have FT4, which is an outlier for a casual paladin (Earring of the Solstice and Jaylia's Boots). A RICH casual player can do that or slightly better (e.g., Narandi's Crown). A rich casual player who is also a high level tradeskiller can do even better by getting the 8th shawl. However, few "casual" players are either rich or master tradeskillers (a few are, but the vast majority are not).

Even with KEI, which I have 90% of the time (it DOES get dispelled, or overwritten with a debuff, or just drops in the middle of an adventure because you thought you had 4 hour KEI and it was 2.5 hour KEI), I have to make a choice: chain stun to hold aggro and mitigate damage OR use my other spells. I can't cast Deny Undead or heals WITHOUT actual, honest to goodness downtime IF I'm using Force of Akilae and Force of Akera.

For high end paladins, it's a different story and I understand warrior complaints about it. I've had people on PoN tell me I'm dumb for not casting Deny Undead because they can hold aggro with stuns AND cast 1-2 Deny Undeads per mob. It ALWAYS turns out that that person has extremely high mana regeneration and it's very frustrating to have people (your own class and others) judge you by that standard.

However, those paladins worked for those abilities - it is what makes them uber and makes me unuber. The balance issue is that I don't see such a distrinction between an uber and unuber warrior. Sure, the uber warrior has higher AC and higher hitpoints. That's true for paladins too - I raid buff to around 7k, which is laughable to the really high end paladins.

Warriors just do NOT have the room to grow that knights do, and I tried to touch on that in my article. Where are the melee "focus effects" that would allow warriors to actually tank better?

Also, as I said, you could nerf paladins to bring them in line with warriors. Then we'd be in the same boat as you currently are - looking up from the bottom of the heap while casting classes get improvement.

Instead, it makes more sense that warriors also gain in a manner similar to how casters and hybrids gained. I thought that my idea of having runes & parchments result in new melee abilities for warriors was sort of an epiphany, though it's probably been said on these boards before.

I can tell you that the casual paladin is nowhere near the level of godhood that some people seem to believe. We are still very limited by our mana regeneration - if we're tanking, we don't get to use our other spells (if we do use them, it means the group gets to sit and wait while we regenerate out mana). My normal group actually has 3 paladins in it - which greatly limits our abilities, I should point out. When I'm tanking, the only spells I get to cast are stuns and root. The other two paladins are free to use their spells, so they help the cleric with healing, use deny undead against appropriate mobs, etc. A second paladin in the group is free to use those spells to the point that their mana regeneration allows, but the MA, if he's a casual paladin, is limited to a very great degree. (I don't mean to imply that paladins stack well - we don't really, though perhaps we do stack better than warriors - we'd be better served by having two DPS classes than the other two paladins, but we play together because we are friends, not because of the classes we play.)

For a casual player, a paladin is an effective class. We can do what we were designed to do. We have a small amount of utility in addition to being able to tank - no one would invite us for that utility if we weren't also tanking, however. I do believe we have it much better than warriors do right now, however. The solution should be to provide warriors with tools that make them current with the game, not to nerf paladins back by two or three expansions. Instead, SOE continues to make the same STUPID mistakes (e.g., the same armor for all the plate classes? What the hell?).

As a few people here have said, I think most of us at PoN are behind you and know full well that you are underpowered. There are some, I think, who are afraid to admit that for fear of being nerfed to oblivian (I hope the better approach is to point out where we are better and describe how warriors can be improved). Then, of course, there are those few who seem to have blinders on. Unfortunately, they are also the ones who go around posting to other message boards a lot. To be honest, I used to come here to read fairly frequently and posted ocasionally. It's become a bit inhospitable to be a paladin in these parts, though I can't blame you for your furstration. I'm trying to make the class balance board at PoN a better place by stomping on those few who are unwilling to discuss things calmly and reasonably (even if they completely disagree with me ;)).

I'll give you my ideas. Do not dismiss them because they are detrimental - do not dismiss them because you fear how far Sony would go with them. Dismiss them ONLY if they do not end with viable tanking power distributed more equally among the three classes than what we have now.

1. Low level hybrid spells should work on mobs like low level pure caster spells do. Although you CAN, as a pure caster, drop a level 9 spell on a high level mob, with rare exceptions it is inferior to using a spell gained at a level on par with the level of the mob.

3. Taunt should, for all classes, work on a sliding scale - at level 1, it gains the top of the hate list +1. at level 65, it should scale to top of the hate list +150. As you grow in power, your abilities should scale upward. Actual changes to the rate at which it works, I leave for the more math inclined of us.

Looking at the original balance items--

I am going to rate the Terrible, Bad, Poor, Average, Good, Great, Excellent.

1.) Smaller mana pool. A hybrid’s mana pool was based on their actual level minus 8. For example, a 9th level paladin had the mana pool of a first level cleric.

This is by no means unreasonable. Every RPG that I know of, uses this to balance out a 'hybrid' class. And what I mean by 'Hybrid' is a class that uses melee AND spells. The benefit of being able to use both should be that you are not as good as a pure caster or a pure melee. Since EQ uses a mana system you should NOT get as much mana as a pure caster. This is excellent balance.

Desist is not an upgrade to Cease, neither is any stun we get until the AA one. Holy Might is a bit of an oddball, and most Paladins don't seem to mind that it never gets upgrades so I won't discuss upgrading it here.

Level 9 – Cease (15 mana), 1.0 second interrupt stun, caps at 30
Level 15 – Desist (25 mana), 2.0 second stun, caps at 30
Level 30 – Stun (30 mana), 4.0 second stun, caps at 55
Level 30 – Cease II (30 mana), 1.0 second interrupt stun, caps at 55 *new spell
Level 49 – Holy Might (60 mana), 4.0 second stun with damage, caps at 55

Level 55 – Cease III (60 mana), 1.0 second interrupt stun, caps at 65 *new spell
Level 62 – Force of Akilae (65 mana) 4.0 second stun, caps at 65
Bunches of AA – Divine Stun, 1.0 second interrupt stun with small knockback, caps at 68
