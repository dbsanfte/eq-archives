Brabble
There are several reasons why theoretical magelos showing 8K non-raider gear have little relevance to whether there is a functional skill-up path for nonraiders to this level. I also question the proposition that one or two real magelos of similar level prove much either, other than there are a couple of people out of hundreds of thousands who have played this game who decided to spend much more time and effort to get worse gear than they could have had had they joined a raiding guild.

In fact, Battleblade's own posts listing potential target mobs for nonraiders contain better indications that most casual players might advance their characters in interesting ways (I myself have picked up an intriguing suggestion or two along these lines from BB and others--spent one day last weekend scouting out a zone that ended up looking doable for my friends, in fact), but these lists still present some functional difficulties for nonraiders that have little to do with player skill. Essentially, the reasons that you see a lot of these non-LDoN target mobs untouched by casuals have to do with some combination of the following:
1) you need a group or two to do to do it if you aren't already so well geared that the encounter is trivial, and it's a long crawl to the mob (for some casual guilds this can be a problem, because if this is a true pathway and not just a one-shot deal, then getting 6-12 people together repeatedly for 4-6 hours on the same nights will be difficult for them).
2) the mob's loot table is narrower than the classes required to camp the mob, and the target drop rate is rare to ultra rare. Thus most of the people needed for the fight are certain to get nothing out of it, and all of the people will get nothing most of the time--and that's if the mob is up. It's hard to hold casual groups together when the reward for success and the reward for failure in a fight are, all too often, exactly the same--just as it would be to hold a raiding guild together under the same circumstances.
3) If a group of friends rotates target mobs useful to each class in the group, but can only commit to long camps twice a week, then between failures, mobs that don't drop the "right" drop, other people camping/farming the mob, etc etc it can take months for all members of the group to upgrade any given slot. Expansions will come out that outdate your gear faster than you can update it, unless you are playing so long and at such long stretches that you could get gear that was 40% better by joining a raiding guild. This is exactly what is happening to many non-raiding players, even the unusually serious ones who post here. The frequency of the introduction of new expansions is increasing at the same time the frequency with which nonraiders can advance through each previous expansion is decreasing.

The reason that LDoN camps are one of the few places that casual players can get pick up groups (or fill out their groups) is not that casual players fear the risk of non-LDoN mobs. It's that everyone in an LDoN group can advance his character by fighting here. The basic risk and reward tradeoff for casuals is a lot more like the risk and reward tradeoffs faced by raiders than most raiders here seem to want to believe: the problem for casual players is not that they cannot accept more risk for more rewards, it's that the reward curve is much flatter for them than raiders.
Also, the LDoN rate of advancement is quite different for a raider hitting some hards to fill in aug slots as opposed to a nonraider attempting to do enough easy LDoNs to get from whatever the "average" newly minted lvl 65 player has to being outfitted with 8K hitpoints, anger III augs, visible armor upgrades, etc.

But whether or not I'm right, I don't think people quit eq because they think that they have exhausted all technical upgrades in their armor. It's not why raiders quit, and it's not why casual players quit.

Brabble
There are four strengths inherent in LDoNs for casual players:
1. Every class, no matter what your group make-up, has a chance of recieving a reward. This instantly makes it easier to get a group than many zones, where the best mob drop may be great for a warrior, but nothing for the cleric, or nice for the cleric, but nothing for dps classes, etc. For casuals, the problem--the real risk- with zones with a rare drop for a single class is not that the mob is dangerous or that you might wipe, the risk is that you organize your friends go out there, kill your target, nothing drops, experience sucks, and you've wasted everybody's game time--sometimes a big factor for casual folks who can't get together every night. Wasting one day is wasting half a week in real time. Never happens in LDoNs.
2. LDoN is easy to get to and has a reasonable time requirement for casuals. No three-hour struggle to get everyone to some far-off zone and fight your way into the target mob. No flagging, no killing much harder mobs to get to the easier mob that has the drop you want, no content locked behind raid mobs.
3. It's level restricted: you do level appropriate killing with no powerleveling and no one KS's you. Thus there is no fighting your way to some mob and then have some drunk Uber two-boxer kill your target with his left hand while laughing at you, watching reruns of Sex in the City in the hopes of seeing two girls kiss, listening to headbanger music, and godonly knows what he's doing with his right hand.
4. It really was designed at least in part with casual player's progression in mind, which after PoP is, in and of itself and regardless of the LDoN's drawbacks, very encouraging.

I used to enjoy the assassinate and rescue missions the most - and at the same time, even a group of guildies would be unlikely to go along, as they wanted their upgrades and needed points for it. I kinda thought that those two missions shoulda had a little more time and mebbe more points attached, but that wasn't the only thing what killed doing them, for us.

It was annoyances like rescuees refusing to pass empty rooms, or for that matter, that they were on a fixed path instead of /following a party member, and that they don't run. I mean, we *got* that it was akin the hedge walk and similar events, but it busted the vibe for us. (It's a rescue - follow me, be quick, and keep quiet!) Assassinates were a little anti-climactic - we could just as well do a collect mission, track the named, and get the points *and* a drop out of it. The margin of error was a little more slackened, and we would still find ourselves stretching our limits battling through to the named. (We actually got pretty good at juggling 4-6 critters at a time, eventually.) The frustrations weren't challenging, just annoying.

It would have been fitting, I think, to have the assassinated mob drop a decent loot item, or have the rescuee hand over a *token of gratitude*. We chatted one night that it could have been a signet of some sort (NO DROP, LORE), to be exchanged with the Adventure Merchant (or the Recruiter who gave the mission) for a NO DROP item of choice. This could've encouraged repeat attempts, heightened the odds of someone getting a useable item, and avoided the *no such class present* scenario.

At a successful and timely rescue, there could be sump'n like: *Thank you, brave adventurer, for my rescue! Would one of your party [accept this ring], as proof of your daring? I am certain that one of my peers will reward you handsomely for it.* The party would roll, then hail the NPC. Assassinate targets could have a *Gem of Freaky Statue Power*, same turn-in. No gem if it's slain in overtime.

A middle level of difficulty, once normals became easy, would have been a boon as well. The challenge was eventually narrowed down to keeping morons out of the group.
