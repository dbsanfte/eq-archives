What this all goes back to is the amount of agro you have.

Let me be as clear as I can when I state this - a warrior can NEVER have enough agro for my wizard to max DPS in a group setting. More generically, you can say -

A warrior can NEVER have enough agro for <insert class here> to <insert high agro job here> at max efficientcy in a group setting.

Read that carefully because it is true, even for top end Time+GoD geared warriors. The key here is "in a group setting." Group agro is a lot different than raid agro. Not only is agro a lot more likely to be lost in a group setting, but it is also exponentially more important that you don't lose agro.

If you are not getting the maximum amount of agro possible, then you are holding the rest of your group back. That means slows and debuffs go in slower. That means clerics have to wait longer to throw heals and will often get stuck having to toss in a couple of small fast heals so that they can buy back enough time to use larger more agro producing heals. That means your DPS classes are having to throttle their damage back, letting the mob live longer, leaving more opportinities for the mob to do damage and possibly kill someone.

The question is not CAN you hold agro with a 2her, the question is can you get as much agro with your 2her as you can with your 1hers.

In the vast majority of cases, the answers is no, you cannot get as much agro with your 2hers. For endgame warriors, this is less of an issue due to the gap in agro generation from high end gear. For the people that worry the most about riposte damage, they also are the ones who need to squeeze every bit of agro they can to make the group more efficient.

Trading 15% more mob DPS for 50% more agro is a small price to pay when you factor in the added benefits your groupmates will get from thr extra agro you have.

I've never played a wizard, but concerning the general rule that aggro generation on the part of the tank is often a bottleneck in single-group damage output, I have to agree with Wandor.
The better the tank's aggro, the faster the mob can be killed and the less danger non-tanks will go splat. From the point in the game when it becomes dangerous and mana inefficient for casters to get hit through the point it becomes dangerous for non-plate melee to get hit clear through to the end game, this is always true.
Something else that's true: if you are a good player and people like you, your groupmates will not announce in /g that they are throttling their damage output because your aggro blows. If they see you are doing what you can, and you simply don't have the best weapons/gear, they'll quietly adjust. So, just because you're holding aggro just fine doesn't mean better aggro would be wasted, or that you have "enough" aggro.
There are all sorts of situations where using a 2H weapon might make sense, but all other things being equal, the trade off is that it will cost you aggro and almost certainly force others in your group to back off on the aggro they are producing. Generally, that means they will be doing less harm to the mob.
A 2H weapon, then, is sort of like insurance. You trade some aggro for a decreased risk that riposte will kill you. Since the AC/hp/mitiation of each warrior is different, and since mobs vary quite a bit, the risk you face might be different from the risk another warrior faces. What you need to do is figure out whether the decreased risk is worth it for you.
If you are grouping often with the same people, it seems logical to me to ask the healer what he thinks: are ripostes causing spikes to the extent that they give him problems healing efficiently? If no one knows, then experiment and see what happens.
I'd say in many cases, some riposte issues can be solved by getting a few hundred more AC/hps and more AAs. Of course, that's like saying poverty is easily solved by getting rich.
But when Haass says he thinks 2H weapons are overrated, I'm fairly certain that what is behind his thinking is that you must deal with AC/hp/mitigation development in your character. If you do, the added safety value of using a 2H weapon decreases, particularly against non-named, non-boss mobs.
I don't necessarily agree that a warrior shouldn't be tanking in places where mobs are causing him problems. I'd say every once in a while you should tank in situations where it's very difficult for you/your group to win.
Finally, fighting in an experience group always involves negotiating where you are along a risk/benefit curve that can move around a good bit as you go from pull to pull. I'm always extremely leery of statements like "If the wizard isn't out of mana, the problem is you're not pulling fast enough."
The easiest skill in the entire game is to pull faster in an experience group. Too bad really fast pulling is almost never a solution to the problem of maximizing a group's kill rate. A wizard who isn't out of mana is *not* a problem for a group. But a wizard who can't nuke when and how much he would like because he has to wait for the tank's aggro to build and build *is* a problem for a group. And it's certainly not a problem that faster pulling will solve.
