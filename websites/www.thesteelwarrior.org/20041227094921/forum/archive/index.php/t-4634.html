I dual-box a 65 warrior and a 65 beastlord (not on raids, but for grinds; raids, I pick one or the other so I can focus better). One of my big frustrations back when I had only the warrior was that I had no way to buff newbies.

Yep, you heard me right. I'm a naturally generous person, and I actively enjoy helping people out with buffs. And somehow logging on my druid alt or my friend's chanter and buffing people just wasn't the same.

Now that I've got my beastlord up to 65, I can drop beastcrack on people, I can Spiritual Vigor, I can stat-buff better than a shaman for everything but agility and HP...now, I'm happy.

Beg for buffs? Please do! Unless I'm late for a guild raid, you can bet I'll take the time to hit you up with some haste, some stats, some HP, some regen...why not? It doesn't cost me anything but time, and it builds goodwill.

That being said:

1) I don't always see every tell. If I'm in tell-hell, or briefly AFK, I don't want to be considered "rude" because I didn't scroll back far enough to see your tell.

2) Whether or not I respond to tells depends entirely on how stupid the person is. If I get a tell from "Eatmyshorts Donkeybonker" saying "D00d, u got buff 4 me?", I'll quite happily ignore the hell out of him. If I get a tell from "Lanariel Treedancer" saying "Pardon me, mighty mistress of beasts, but can you spare an enhancement for my body or mind?", I'll not only reply, I'll go out of my way to help. I think of it as applying much-needed selection pressures to the roleplaying environment in EQ.

And I've been known to ask for buffs myself. But because I'm a practical little ogre, I don't do it by standing in PoK and shouting "Anyone casting KEI for plat?!" I do a /who, looking for people of the appropriate class and level. And then I send them a /tell, one at a time, saying "Pardon me, noble high elf. Can you spare the time to gift me with KEI, in return for a generous donation?"

Is this rude of me? Judging by the reactions of those I ask, not in the slightest. Most people I've met are happy to help; some of them even reject donations (as I do, when buffing someone below about level 30). As with anything else in life, it's a matter of courtesy.

So. Do I think it's rude to ask for buffs? Not if one does it politely. Do I think it's rude to ignore a buff request? No, although it's certainly more gracious to explicitly deny it, and optimal to deny it with a brief explanation.

*shrug* If I didn't love playing my warrior so much, I'd be playing one of the true buffing classes, like an enchanter or a cleric. As it is, I give what buffs I can with my beastlord, and ask for buffs when I need them without fear of being rude.

On the question of slow percentages: while you are of course quite correct that a 75% slow is significantly more than 10% better than a 65% slow, I'm not convinced this has as much practical value as one might think. Specifically, remember that XP mobs tend to be balanced so that a group without a slower can (in theory) still take them down. Therefore, pretty much any slow (including the gimp 50% beastlord slow) is enough to make the mob stop being a threat.

My beastlord, in specific, does not yet have her 65% slow. I've got the 40k it tends to cost. I just have better things to spend it on. When we're doing LDoN adventures, Graa handles the slowing even though we have an enchanter standing by with extra mana. Why? Because she can take the initial aggro hits better than an enchanter can, and we just don't need more than 50% slowing.

Now, if we were doing Hard adventures, yah, I'd need the 65% slow. But for normal adventures at level 65, a 50% slow is more than enough to have our cleric up and meleeing even while our bard is pulling at full speed.

And given that, the difference between a 65% slow and a 75% slow just isn't that relevant to the way we play.
