I don't know that the taunt button works *exactly* as described by "top of hate list +1" but I do think that description is close enough to be functionally accurate.

Therefore I do not mash taunt when I can tell that the mob is already on me.

I don't know what level your warrior is, but by and by you'll find that there are several ways of gaining agro. Some, however, are not really under your control at the moment you lose agro. soe agro factors are:

1. Regular melee attacks. Yes, it's a good idea to remember to turn on autoattack, lol. Other than that, it's not much under your control. Using dual wield will give you more agro than using a 2-hand weapon, generally speaking.

2. Procs. Try to max your dex and use the best weapon you can, obviously, but for the most part you just have to cross your fingers and hope you get procs when you need them. If you've just made this warrior, it's likely that procs won't be much available to you for a while, and you don't have to worry about it until then.

3. Disarm. Tests have shown that this really does not add to your hate in the midst of a fight, so I don't use it for agro-building purposes. Sometimes I use it for fun, and because I can get pretty "twitchy" when I tank and I run out of buttons to push but feel a powerful need to DO SOMETHING NOW GODDAMNIT. Some mobs actually hit harder/faster when you disarm them, so you have to know the mob. I've also used disarm when I'm in a pick-up group with a speed/ninja-looter and I want the mob's weapon. No, I am not joking. If you walk up to a green mob and hit disarm, it will attack you, so there's some sort of initial hate value to /disarm, but no real practical value in a fight, I don't think.

3. Range attack. Agro on range attack seems oddly out of proportion to either the damage that I do or the frequency of attack. In other words, under certain circumstances, I can use a bow to tag a mob being pulled to camp and the mob will head for me. This can work surprisingly well, though I know of no tests that have analyzed why. Also, this can be done at greater range than using the provoke line, and basically is a free ride to the top of the hate list when it works. Can be very useful in outdoor zones when you are MT but not the puller. (If you are the puller, then clearly a bow is your friend too.)

3. The provoke line. If I've tagged a mob with an arrow as the mob is coming to camp, the next thing I do (immediately when mob is in range) is use the provoke line, which depending on your level will give you a more or less significant head start on hate. The provoke line is also quite useful when I am pulling certain mobs in indoor zones, partly because it can be used at range and partly again because it gives you a hate head start, and that hate builds, so the sooner you get to it the more hate builds and the sooner the button will refresh. If I don't have agro, and taunt and provoke are both up, I hit taunt first, then immediately hit provoke. I don't see any reason not to use the provoke line right away in a fight. Hit it as soon as it refreshes for as long as the mob is fighting and you have END available.

3. The taunt button. Appears to me to function as "top of the hate list +1." If I'm on the top of the hate list, I don't hit the taunt button. I want the button up if I lose the mob, and the longest seconds in the game for me as a tank involve waiting for taunt to refresh while the cleric/other caster is getting the stuffing beat out of him. If I lose the mob to another melee, I hit taunt and, if I'm lucky enough to have the provoke line refresh, I hit that. Taunt first because if it works, I jump to the top of the list, then provoke, because the hate it generates is additive. If I lose agro to a caster, it's taunt then shield and provoke if available. If slam is up, I use that--basically, I'm throwing everything I've got at the mob as fast as I can. There are exceptions to this, however. The first exception you'll notice has to do with enchanters. If they draw agro, very often they'll want a chance to re-mez the mob. If they successfully mez the mob and you hit the mob, you'll breakt he mez but probably not have agro. What you will have is one pissed off enchanter in your group, heh.

4. /shield. I don't normally use shield to protect other melee, with a few exceptions (for example, if I'm not MT I might shield MT if I have reason to believe that an incoming heal will be a little too late, but those situations are, well, situational). I will shield casters who draw agro. Normally, I taunt first, partly because to shield someone you have to be very close to them. I taunt, then if mob turns to me, I don't shield; if taunt fails, I immediately shield. I believe that shield has some +hate factor, but I don't know of any tests that have definitively shown that. In my admittedly unscientific observation, the mobs turn to me immediately after I /shield far more frequently than chance or my own melee damage would account for. I can't actually recall when you get the ability to /shield--level 30 sticks in my head, but it's been a while--when you get it, make hotkeys for it for the appropriate caster(s) in your group. There are various threads on /shield on this site with suggestions on hotkey set-ups for /shield.

5. Other stuff: A failed "beg" will also draw initial agro, but I never beg mobs because like disarm, it doesn't seem to have much practical value in the midst of a fight. Some warriors used to advocate useing beg on mezzed mobs, but for some time taunt has given you a message that tells you whether you have successfully taunted the mob. I just break mezz with taunts--taunt until you get the "you have taunted..." message.
Proximity to the mob is also helpful--it sometimes pays to be the closest player to the mob. Rooted mobs will always attack the closest player, so if you want a rooted mob to hit you, you need to be standing closer to it than other players are to it.
Sitting down can also increase your agro, but you'll have to play with that to see when it makes sense to do it.

does this eliminate any agro you have generated through incite, procs?

Lets say you have 1000 hate from a mob (via incite, procs, melee). A caster hits the mob with a spell and is put at 1300. You successfully taunt, which puts you at 1301. (It may not be exactly 1, but it's simpler to refer to it as 1 instead of saying "a little bit" each time). If you then proc Anger3, you would be at 1901. This is why it is much more effective to use incite after you successfully taunt, and not before, otherwise it's wasted if not at the beginning of a fight.

Regarding Disarm:

Varaho did some tests that proved that disarm does nothing to the aggro list, other than just put you on it. I then did some more tests and confirmed his findings. It doesn't increase aggro at all, just puts you on the hate list.

Regarding Beg:

Three things can happen after you hit beg. You can succeed, fail, or get a critical failure. A critical failure is the only one that gives aggro, and it is also very rare. Consider also that as you use it more, your skill goes up and critical failures get even more rare. Add in the fact that you have to turn attack off to use beg, it's not worth even touching. Some also believe that some mobs are "immune" to beg, where they just give a fail message, and there is no roll. Those mobs are basically all non humanoid / intelligent creatures. Haven't seen any proof of this, but wouldn't suprise me.

Actually, I believe all attacks are indeed synchronized. Have you ever noticed how you can hit attack, and nothing happens for several seconds? Why is that? The processor is waiting for the beginning of the next tic. All combat is divided into 6 second tics. Consequently, a complete round of combat must occur before agro is determined, i.e. it doesn't matter who hits first because the code factors everyone hitting in the same tic. The order of spam on you screen is immaterial. Once all damage is calculated, agro is determined for that tic of combat.
