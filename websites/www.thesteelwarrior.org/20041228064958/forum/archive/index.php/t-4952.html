Ah, the magic of spreadsheets. I did some back-of-the-napkin math, assuming a few different DIs.

First I assume
damage = DB+[RND*DI]

Now, we don't really know what the heck is happening with the part of the equation that's in square brackets. Traditionally, it returned evenly distributed numbers from 1 to 20. Now I'm assuming they are jacking with the numbers that come out of it somehow.

Here are 3 possibilities.. RND returning...
1) from 1 to 19.
2) from 1+1 to 19 (ie, 1 1 2 3 4 ... etc)
3) from 0 to 19

What I found, which is obvious with a little thought, is that the higher DI is, the more benefit we get from any manipulation of RND. So a mob the a DB of 5000 and a DI of 5 will be a tough mob no matter if we're defensive or not, or have any new mitigation. Conversely, a mob with DB of 100 and DI 200 would hit wildly and those hits would be very mitigatable.

I found that methods 2 and 3 above yield virtually identical results, only differing I believe by DI/20.


DB DI Avg 1-19 1+1-19 0-19
100 5 152 98% 97% 97%
1000 5 1052 100% 100% 100%
1000 100 2050 98% 95% 95%
1000 200 3100 97% 94% 94%


DB is the damage base, DI is the interval. Avg is the average damage assuming pure DB+RND(20)*DI. The next three columns show the percentage of the average damage using the specified method.

What we see is that
on line 1...a wimpy mob, the three methods yield 2%, 3%, and 3% mitigation.
on line 2... A consistently hard mob yields us no mitigation. DI simply doesn't matter.
on line 3...A reasonable mob produces 2% to 5% mitigation
on line 4...A bastardly mob produces from 3% to 6% mitigation.

What it amounts to is that the higher DI with respect to DB, the more mitigation we get. And that I don't see us getting anywhere close to 5% mitigation in any reasonable scenario using the 1-19 method.

I did a little more doinking and have determined that, with the 1-19 method, 5% mitigation CAN NOT happen. Looks like the resulting formula has a limit at 95.238%. So the best we can possibly get is 4.762%

Of course, all of this is in supposition that the base formula is still valid.
