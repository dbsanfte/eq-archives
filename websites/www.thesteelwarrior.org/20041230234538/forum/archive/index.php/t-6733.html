Just about everything above is correct.
My thoughts on a couple issues:
1. YOu have five main avenues of managing agro: the speed/ratio of your weapons, the proc of your weapons, the incite (provoke) line, taunt, and your brain. Early in your career, procs are the least important of these, because A) most weapons that have good procs you can't use yet and B) your procs are in part based on your DEX, so you might only proc once a minute. Sometimes the proc will happen early in the fight, but really before a minute goes by on old-world mobs, either the mob is dead or you and your cleric are dead. Later in the game, with top-end weapons and the right AAs, procs become progressively more important.
1. A. You might look at gearing your warrior for four phases: pre-mid 40s, 40s-55ish, 55 on, and Endgame. You mention having enough pp to outfit a warrior, but you should seriously check the prices on ornate molds ("55 on") before you say that. I'd be inclined to avoid buying really expensive twink weapons for your warrior pre-40s, for two reasons. One, you won't use the weapons long, and two, nothing, and I mean nothing, helps you figure out how to hold agro like having trouble holding agro. There are weapons around that are so good that your pre-40 warrior would need to do little other than get in melee range and hit autoattack. If you don't have those weapons, you have to work on all the other things that help hold agro.
1. B. Get and use a bow. Pull with it when you're a puller, tag incoming mobs when you aren't. You'll be surprised how helpful it is to tag the incoming mob with an arrow for getting initial aggro. Use your incite/provoke. If both taunt and provoke are up at the same time and you're trying to get the mob off someone else, hit taunt then provoke/incite--in that order, but almost at the same time.
1. C. Practice all your weapon skills. Yes, there are some 2-hand weapons you can use to hold aggro at level 55-on, some of them are cheap, and some of them will fall into your hands without warning.

5. Kick is good because every once in a while you'll have the option of being turned into some bear or wolf or somesuch. This will increase your stats, but (even if you're a large race) remove your ability to slam (what Haass was talking about). Kick will stun mobs after you reach 55, too. That said, for large races slam has a higher skill cap and will generally be more useful. I pretty much mash the slam key, but pay attention to caster mobs and try to time your slam to interrupt them. If you are fighting a mob that runs at low health, time your slam to that it's up when the mob turns to run. Slam stuns the mob for a round or two, so stopping the running mob for a bit is good.

Some general things:
1. Learn how to use /shield.
2. I recently pl'd a shaman from level 10 through level 21 in about five hours in PC and then M Seru. Then I spent two days methodically casting trivial spells in an attempt to plug the enormous skill holes that had developed in my toon. It's very tempting to pl, but if you really want to learn how to play a warrior and melee is new to you, I'd avoid too much pl'ing. Nowadays leveling early on is so easy that your toon will advance faster than your own ability to play an unfamiliar class will.
3. As MT you decide certain things: where will the mob be fought? What order will the mobs be fought? What direction will the mob be facing? You should know the answers to these questions, and have reasons for those answers.
4. Warriors have a deep hp pool and excellent mitigation, and these things are useful to a group primarily because they save healers mana and they reduce the risk others in the group face. A good tank always seeks to reduce risk to the rest of the group and answers the questions in #3 above in such a way that the group fights most efficiently. Your ability to do damage yourself is trivial compared to the amount of extra damage a group can do fighting behind a good tank rather than an ignorant tank.
5. Communicate with your group. You're a shaman, so you know how difficult it is to time all the buffs you throw out there. Update your casters on your buff situation, and let them decide how to manage their mana. Figure out who calls "run" in tough situations (probably either you or the main healer). You'd be surprised how many needless deaths occur because people don't follow simple orders, or don't know who is supposed to make a decision. When to run is just one example, but: pick someone, and if "run" is called, everybody by god run. Some casters like to be the last to zone, but in my groups, I'm the last to zone (assuming I'm still alive). If raid leader says stand still, stand still. If raid/group leader says move up, move up. Etc. If casters are LoM in a group, don't be afraid to call a med break--always vote with your cleric.
6. Know things: which mobs are casters? Which are higher levels? What is the re-spawn rate? What is their aggro radius? Where are your group members and what is their condition (that is, if your cleric's hitpoints start to fall, you should know immediately why, and if you need to pull a mob off him, you should know where he is so you can run right to him)? Where are the adds? What are the pathing characteristics of the mobs you are facing?
7. Some nice things to have: By the time you reach level 50, I'd be carrying: stat bonus food and drink, Everfrost gate potion (even ifyou are an evil race: Everfrost gate drops you in a spot fairly close to the PoK book and there are never ever any guards between you and the book, and Everfrost gate potions are cheaper than gate-to-bind-point potions), some ability to SoW myself, some extra gear to bump up resists. I also carried stinging wort potions, which saved me on a late heal a couple of times, and I carried a couple of peridots on me to give to casters if they needed them.
8. Before lvl 55, in normal experience groups, the number of times your healer has died before you have died should be fewer than the number of fingers on one hand. The number of times your healer has died and you lived should approach zero. You should be extremely embarrassed if a skilled caster dies in the course of doing his job and you live. (Stupid casters can actually do things that make it impossible to save them, but when a good caster dies and you lived, you almost certainly made an error.)
9. It's all really simple: Mobs do the dying, you do the killing. Don't get them mixed up, and you'll be fine.

Add in situational awareness to your bag of tricks. Process what is going on around you in game in terms of the group, respawn, other groups etc.

In addition, as you play, pay attention to what your group mates are doing in terms of position, casting, etc. The more you know about your own class and others and how it all fits in together the more you can adapt. Remember, while each class has "class defining abilities" there are also lesser known abilities that are very handy and can be used to make a successful and fun group.

Lastly, learn how to judge the group's abilities. This means communication within the group to see how folks are doing on mana, position etc. It may mean slowing down the pulls or even moving to a different area if someone can't keep up. There is a difference between challenging yourself and setting yourself up for a train wreck.
