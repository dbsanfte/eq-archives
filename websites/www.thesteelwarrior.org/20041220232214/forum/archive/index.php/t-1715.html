Things to learn for warrior:

1) how to help the puller split mobs
2) how aggro works and when to use your taunt button
3) mob positioning
4) mob kill order (cleric mobs, nuker mobs, melee mobs) as well as mez order (if you don't know which to attack next, ask the enchanter they appreciate it)

By the time you get near the 40s, you pretty much know how to pull, but now, the monks and bards are getting alot better at it than you. So, learn to help them. Let the monk know you have a ranged attack. He'll teach you how to help him split them.

You need to know how aggro works more than any other class. You need to generate as much as you can for yourself and know how to fix the aggro gen of other members. Learn what the shaman and chanter slow and tash spells look like when they hit the mob. It doesn't always say 'slows down'. When you are looking for those things, you are more prepared if you lose aggro then.

Mob positioning is pretty easy to do and is one of those things that gets me 'great tanking' compliments the most often. If I pull, I bring mob in and turn it around. I'm looking at the rest of the party with the mob between us. If another is pulling, I usually stand looking at the rest of the group so that I can grab aggro when the puller and mob run by me....putting us in the same general position of me on one side, party on other. Learning to circle strafe will help if you have a rogue in party. As the mob gets pushed around, just always keep the mobs back to the rogue.

Mob order can be a little tricky, but it's probably something you do without thinking by now anyway. Keep in mind that the party is going to look to you for their kill target. So pick which needs to die first and let them know it. I usually kill any healer/slower mobs first, then nukers, then all others, but situations vary depending on the mobs.

Hopefully, this is all of some use to you.

Peeling: I would gather this means using your bow or whatnot to pull a mob off of a puller, such as a monk or SK. Am I correct?

Also known as 'tagging'. When a monk or sk FD's most of the mobs on them will walk away, sometimes 1 or 2 will stick around a little while longer. That's when you want to tag them.

Procs: I've found that weapons with procs that include a stun portion (not just damage) really help to draw agro. I don't have any anger or enraging blow procs yet, but stuns work well too.

It's not about the damage that you do, but how you take it. If the cleric is CH'ing you many times during a normal fight, you might want to check your gear. In BoT I can usually go 2-3 fights before I need a complete heal (unless a named shows up :P). Items with regen help as well. If you regen for over 60 per tick with buffs, that will save your healer some mana too as you will heal up while waiting for the next pull.

Know when to use 2h over 1h dual wield. If a mob hits for a lot more than you are used to and you find you are getting riposted a lot, change to 2hs or 2hb. You will get off less attacks, which means fewer chances of being riposted (I don't think mobs get this ability until around level 35 anyway), but the damage bonus on the 2h weapon makes up for it usually. Dual wield is good to use if you have the hp/ac to take the additional blows or if you are just a DPS warrior on a raid, etc. Always be ready to switch your weapons around depending on the situation you are in.

That's about all I can think of right now, the best way to learn is to just play and find ways to make stuff easier for yourself. The best way to learn is by making mistakes.

Lots of good advice here. The stuff about mob positioning and working with enchanters is very good.
In some areas with lots of mobs that can be single pulled (Overthere, dreadlands, for example) I wouldn't necessarily assume other classes can pull better than you can. Get yourself a 125 or 150 range bow, make yourself some 150 range arrows, pull away.
Whether or not you pull, immediately start figuring out the pathing characteristics, the spawn rates, agro range, "toughness," casting abilities and so forth of the mobs you are fighting. Also, pay atention to how your group is functioning, and who is doing what. If you are in a pick-up group, people come and go and that sometimes radically changes how fast you can kill mobs. If you know what everyone's been doing, you can anticipate how changes in mobs and changes in group make-up will influence upcoming fights.

I also always liked to fight facing my group. Tell your group you will do this. It helps not only rogues, but to some extent pets do max damage. Also, it allows you to react faster if a mob heads for your casters (especially an add or someone else's train comes up from behind the group).
Do make a shield hotkey and use it. You might have to explain how it works, because so few warriors use it in the 30s and 40s.
Pay careful atention to your casters' mana, expecially your healer's mana. Ask periodically. It will give you a pretty good reading about whether the group can stand faster pulling or not.
Don't run outside your caster's range when he starts casting on you. Especially if you are pulling, the impulse is to run off, but it's very irritating to your healer if you do that.

You will make mistakes. Try not to make the same one over and over, heh.

Keep track of your buffs, and mention when one goes down. In theory, your caster (say, shaman) is timing them, but practice sometimes that's very hard for them to do.

Don't be a loot hog. Call for casters to loot if the melee have been grabbing all the loot. But don't allow corpses to pile up either, as they can cause targeting problems.

People have addressed a lot of topics, but let me focus on pulling, because bad pulls are what wipe most groups, not crap tanking.

Pull higher con mobs first. This reduces the chance of getting an add.

Know when to bow pull and when to prox pull (aggro from close proximity to the mob). Bow pulling can cause the mob to use default pathing, which can sometimes be very bad for you. Prox pulling, or very close bow pulling will cause the mob to go straight for you rather than using the default pathing.

Mobs that have their backs turned to you have a lower agro radius, use this to your advantage when trying to pull singles.

If you have an enchanter or cleric helping with pacify, establish a pattern of always pulling the left mob (or right one, it doesn't matter which, it just needs to be consistent), so the pacifier knows which mob to pacify.

If you are pulling mobs that respawn, try to break the camp and then establish a pattern of pulling the mobs in the same order. This will save you from having to rebreak parts of the camp constantly. It always takes more time to break a camp than it does to single pull repops, so once you have a pattern of keeping the spawn down, you can usually weave a few more mobs from outside the camp into the spawn cycle.

If you pull caster mobs, don't pull to camp and let them nuke you from a distance. Duck around a wall and they will come for you, as they need line of sight to start casting. If there are no walls, move past your camp such that the mob will stop in the middle of your camp to start nuking you.

If you have multiple pulls, do not let them all bunch up, this makes your chanter's job very difficult. Rather, step into the mobs as soon as the first mob gets to the place where you want to fight. There is a bit of skill involved here, but it causes a very nice effect: the first mob will be behind you, all the adds will be in front of you. Then turn around to fight the mob you pulled. As a result, you will be facing the camp fighting the mob you pulled, with adds beating on your back. Yes, you will take more damage because your back is exposed, but it becomes so freaking obvious to the chanter which mobs he needs to mezz.

Someone mentioned this above, but once a mob is mezzed, pull the mob you are fighting away from the area (move and then sit down if you don't have strong agro)

If you pull multiples and your healer is going to have a hard time, or your chanter is slow locking mobs down, train your group. Yep, train your group. Run over to someone who is not the healer/chanter/slower and let them get prox agro from the adds. Think of the rest of your parties hitpoints as your hitpoints, until the mobs are under control. The idea is not to kill your group mates, but rather to lessen the burst dps you are taking at the start of the fight. (Now the better rangers and shamans out there will snare or slow one of the adds to help ghetto mezz, so use this technique only if your party doesn't really click well)
