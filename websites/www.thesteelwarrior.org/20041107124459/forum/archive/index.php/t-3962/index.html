Allow me to educate you on "Knights" and "warriors".

Knight:

This was the name applied to mounted warriors of aristocracy in western Europe from the ninth century onward. The paladins of Charlemagne were among the first knights of European ages.

Knights were born under a noble roof and granted knighthood, thus allowing them to command troops and throw them head on into battle to do their bidding. On average, one out of sixty knights was valorous, on average only one out of ninety would show this, and on average only one out of two hundred put themselves in harms' way when it could be avoided.

For a few reasons this occured:

1) The knight wasn't the superior in fighting in every situation. You had specialty orders of knights who were well trained in a specific style of fighting, and then you had the average knight. He was nothing more than a noble who was given a sword and the right to fight/command troops. On the battlefield the knight, if under command of another noble, would be in a division of horsemen, would charge the enemy and generally leave the battlefield. They and their training were useless in anything but their initial charge with a handful of exceptions. While yes it can be argueable that knights were trained to use swords, it more got in the way to have big, bulky horses that can't manuver wasting the lives of knights then it did to have them plough through and wait for other orders. Again there were specialty knights who charged on foot, or it could be argued that they were called around for another pass, but this was not the norm, nor did it have desireable consequences more than 95% of the time.

2) A Knight was the intellectual superior to the common warrior. There was no question about this. The average warrior's intellect was not on par (on average) with his knight or his lord. The lord got greater education, had access to more books and writings, and spent more time reading them than a warrior would choose to. The warrior would generally spend more time tending to his family or training in the ways of war. The warrior, when it came to battling on the battlefield, was the superior in using his weapons and utilizing himself, while the knight was the superior in navigating him for optimum effectiveness.


3) Given the choice between death or having a nameless anonymous soldier dying in your stead, the knight would choose 95% of the time for the anonymous soldier to die. That's what he was raised to believe, that his life was far more important.


Allow me to educate you in a few knightly orders.

Knights Templar:

These guys started out as paupers, part of the Poor Fellow-Soldiers of Christ and the Temple of Solomon...religious orders formed during the Crusades into the Holy Land. Being inspired by the idea, a large portion of nobles joined in with them, bringing along their craftsmen and troops etc. etc. However there was more to this order then "freeing the Holy Land"...in fact much more.

They began setting up nearby churches and religious buildings as banks for themselves and other nobles, funded by those who had come under the promise of glory and valor. After the Crusaders were pushed from the Holy Land in 1291 they set up a major financial business as bankers, drawing the envy from both the secular rulers and the clergy.

In essence, they were financially holding a large portion of Europe by the balls. (Funny this doesn't sound like an order of valiant soldiers and warrios to me).

Now bare in mind, whlie the Knights Templar were in the Holy Lands they were some of the most ruthless and foul persons there. Their names were struck with awe by those whom had heard the bard tales and malevolence and fear by those who had witnessed first hand what they had done.

Several historians agree that they brought about a huge reform in the ways Knights and their counterparts were looked at by nobility and royalty, causing a tighter leash to be placed on knighthood on the whole.

Some have even compared them to Hitler's S.S., as a kind of bully force that would go around and not only try to quell Islam, but slay any who defied christianity in the harshest senses.

They would ride onto the battlefield and fight Muslims...along side their warrior counterparts (men-at-arms, sergeant at arms, pikemen, archers and the like) and would get slaughtered in the process.

In 1307 the monarchs of France, England and Spain began attacking and shutting down Templar Headquarters, arresting them and charging them with heresy, witchcraft, and immorality. Though the Pope had originally brought forth most of the ideas for, funded, and championed the Knights Templar, in 1314 he had the leadership of the Knights burned at the stake for herecy and witchcraft and the order disbanded.

Knights Hospitalar


These knights originally helped serve as hospitalmen to the order of Saint John in Jerusalem...an order formed by crusaders in the eleventh century for clergy attached to a hospital that tended to sick and needy pilgrims in the holy land.

Now after several defeats at the hands of the Muslims in Jerusalem, the Clergy reformed the Knights Hospitalar to take a more aggressive military role in crusade matters, moving from their healing duties to defensive duties.

Being sent to Jerusalem in 1187...the city fell and so the order transferred next to the city of Acre, a fortified coastal city. There it resumed its duties of caring for the sick, while in turn patrolling the lands for infidels and killing them on sight.

In 1291, Acre fell and the hospitalars moved to Cyprus. A few decades later, in 1310, it moved to Rhodes, which it took conquered and ruled as an independent state.

In 1522...it fell to the Turks of the Ottomon Empire.

After being given the island of Malta by Emperor Charles V in 1530, thy ruled that and finally with the help of the Holy roman Empire held off further Turkish Assaults. When left to their own devices in 1798...they fell to General Napoleon Bonaparte. Now they exist as a military order in the island of Malta.
