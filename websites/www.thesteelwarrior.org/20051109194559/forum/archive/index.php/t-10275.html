I finally went with a motherboard with 4 memory slots in it and got 2 500 MB sticks. 1Gig can pretty much handle any game out there right now, and if you buy something that needs more memory, the other two slots are there waiting so you don't have to throw out the memory you already have (assuming you can still buy the same kind).

PCI Express makes zero difference in game performance right now. From a gameplay standpoint, it is a waste of money. However, I think there are 2 reasons to consider it.

1) It is most likely here to stay, and 2 years from now you might have a hard time upgrading your video card if you don't have a PCI Express motherboard.

2) GeForce 6600 (see the video card question)

If you decide to get a GF6600 it might be worth getting a PCIexpress Motherboard just cause the difference in the mobo prices will be less than the AGP video card difference hehe.

1. On performance, they are both about the same, just get the fastest you can. From years of building different systems for home and businesses, the only difference I've seen consistantly between the two is reliability. With Intel, that extra money that you are paying is for the extra reliability that Intel provides in thier products. Keep in mind that this is an opinion, but a very experienced one that I feel strongly about. In fact, I'm writing this on my daily/gaming machine which is an AMD system, processor and chipset. I've had failed processors over the years and all of them AMD. 4 of them in the past 3 years. I haven't had a Intel processor fail on me yet. Fortunately, AMDs customer service is top notch, and I'm usually with a replacement proc with a day or two. It could be the luck of the draw with me, but regardless it does make it very hard to put Intel and AMD on the same ground by reliability standards.

If money is absolutely no issue, go Intel. If it isn't, go AMD. If reliability is very important to you, go Intel. If you don't mind waiting a few days after a failed processor, then save a nice chunk of change and go with AMD.

That said, I will continue using AMD products in my home systems because the savings is very significant.

2. 1 GB is the sweet spot for games right now. if you were running EQ2 I might say that adding another GB would be a good idea, but since you're just running WoW then it's not really an issue. Grab a stick of name brand 1 GB DDR RAM. (I prefer Crusial *sp?*, and have for a long time.) You don't need anything fancy. If you ever need more, then just go grab another stick, and pop it in. I say 1 GB sticks because its easier to replace and troubleshoot when something goes wrong, and it makes adding more memory easy because there is no way that memory slots are an issue unless you only have 1 slot on your motherboard. (Keep in mind that some memory technologies will not be optimal unless you have a stick on each memory channel, like those Nvidia chipsets, I believe.) Make sure that the motherboard supports 1 GB sticks, and that your brand is speciicly suppported to insure that no issues insue.

I wouldn't worry about DDR2 yet, you'd just be paying a premium for new technology and the problems that they bring. I highly doubt you would notice a difference in preformance. That's strickly an opinion though.

3. PCIX is the way to go here. The technology is simply superior, and is the next logical step. AGP cards won't be supported forever. more than likely, you will upgrade your video card before the rest fo the machine is outdated, so having a modern hardware interface is probably going to be a good thing. I don't think that you are going to be able to get a really good card on AGP 2-3 years from now, much like PCI cards now.

4. RAID is the rave now. It's really simple though. Do you do a lot of very large file manipulations like video capturing and editing? Otherewise, I wouldn't bother with it. I infact have a raid 0 SCSI array on this system. I haven't noticed any major difference while playing games. the only time, I've ever notice a difference was when I had this box setup as a PVR. Except for phenomenaly short load times (blink and you miss it) on EQ, EQ2 and WoW that's the only difference. Average framerate has stayed the same on EQ and WoW, however while traveling I did notice slightly less loading glitches on EQ2, although I haven't seen any significant average FPS differences. If you want to waste money, go for it, but I couldn't recommend it unless you are doing something else with your computer than regular windows, internet, email, and games with the system. Again, this is highly opinionated, I have no solid measurements

5. Get the fastest card you can afford. I have a ATI 9800 and no complaints. Of course it can't do everything, but I can run my games a decent clip. But really, your video card is the heart of your gaming system, collect cans, sit on street and peddle kittens, I don't know, but get a fast card, even if it hurts. Fast drives, CPUs and memory and fancy technology won't make up for a budget card. When you buy the card, make it a good one. Don't forget lots of onboard memory, 128 MB is a very bare minimum.

Hope this helps, but keep in mind everyone has different experiences with computer parts, so its hard to keep opinions out of recommendations. Keep in mind that your Diablo 2 is probably being run at max already, I've yet to come across an iso view game that wasn't lacking in engine performance, though I can't even begin to guess why.

#1 - Intel or AMD processor? I've built 3 AMD boxes in the past 5 years and would like to give Intel a shot at my cash and compare it to the AthyXP. Are AMD chips that far ahead of Intel? There are so many different chipset nowdays, and I can hardly tell the difference between a companies own products, eventho I'm fairly hardware literate. What would you suggest for a processor in the $300 range?

Definitely not the time to go with Intel. You can get AMD chips for much cheaper, and net similar performance. I must admit, Intel is better at pure number crunching and productive applications, but AMD owns the gaming field. As suggested above, the AMD 64 line is great. Be sure to go with socket 939, as they are phasing out the high end 754 stuff in favor of turning 754 into a budget performance line.

#2 - Is 1gb of DDR enough for anything in the next 2yrs or should I spring for 2gb and not worry about memory ever again? What about DDR2? Is it that big an improvement over the current DDR memory?

DDR2 is really an intel only thing, and the technology is new. It hasn't matured enough to warrant using for any purpose, and its MUCH more expensive than DDR RAM, for next to no performance gains. You can't even use DDR2 with AMD chips anyways, so definitely stick with DDR. 1GB is more than enough for all games out today, but 2GB will eventually be the norm, probably in the next year or two when the next batch of big-name new engine games start coming out. As reccomended above, get a motherboard that has at least 3 memory slots, and just go with 2 512MB sticks of ram for now.

#3 - AGP 8x or PCI Express 16x? The mobos with PCI Ex seem to cost quite abit more than those with AGP 8x. Do you think PCI Ex is here to stay for awhile or should I just stick with the budget AGP?

At this time, I'd have to reccomend PCI-Express. AGP is still going to be around for awhile, but PCI-E exclusive cards are already coming out, and a year or two down the road, you'll have a hard time keeping up (performance wise) with recent tech as PCI-E WILL become the norm.

#4 - I have my heart set on two/four Western Digital Raptor SATA drives in RAID0 config in the new box. Any horror stories? Are they as good as ppl say they are?

I'd have to say that, for home use, RAID isn't worth it, no matter which kind of array you're going for. Invest in a nice big hard drive, and use DVD RWs or another hard drive to back up your personal files only.

#5 - On the topic of Video Cards, which ones should I absolutely avoid. I've heard Geforce 5200s are cheap but you get what you pay for. Any recommendations for a card in the $150-250 range?

Avoid the entire Geforce FX line. People who bought them will try to justify their purchase, but when it comes down to it, in price:performance ratio, they're not worth it period. For the $200-250 price range, there are two cards that really can't be beat right now: The Nvidia Geforce 6600GT, and the ATI Radeon 9800PRO. The 6600GT is better, but you can get a 9800PRO for a little less.

If you want assistance in picking out specific parts, I'd be more than willing to assist you. I'd only need to know what your total budget will be, and what parts you can save from your current computer (If you can re-use your current monitor and operating system, it'll significantly cut costs on a new machine)
