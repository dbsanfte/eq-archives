Way too many factors involved.

If you ask a specific instance, the probability could be solved Im sure.

For instance, right now at 2k AC, most PoP mobs hit me for min about 40-45% of the time. The remaining 55-60% of hits are pretty evenly spread over the remaining 19 Damage Intervals.

The odds of any single hit on me being for max damage is 60/19 or 3% of all hits are for max damage. Considering that there is also a 50% hit rate (50% of swings are misses/dodges/ripos/parrys) that means the actual chance per swing of a max hit landing is around 1.5%

Chance of being 1 rounded is 1.5%^4 or 0.015^4 X chance at the mob making dual weild and double attack checks.

Lets guess and say most quadding bosses, quad 50% of rounds. That means the chances of a round of 4 max hits is 0.0000025% or 1 in 39 million rounds.

Thats good stuff, but unfortunately not the whole picture, because often a quad of DI = 18-20 will kill you just the same as a quad of DI = 20. Hitting for near max is enough to kill you. Also there is the chance of getting a riposte at the same time, and also the chance of Flurry, chance of catching 2 rounds of melee between CHs.

Its all a game of chance, and lowering the size of hits via AC, or lowering the rate of landed hits via evasive, both are very effective at reducing the chance at bad rounds to a level where they will likely never occur.

Usually when you do this kind of study you want to estimate the errors. As an example it would be nice to know; if I were to repeat the 1815 AC test with 813 hits, can I expect the average hit to be 311.7 again? As you probably already know, the answer to this question is no. What I would like to be able to say is; with 1815 AC the average hit for this particular mob is 312 +- some number, with some degree of confidence.

Since you didn't post the standard deviation for your samples I can't give you exact confidence intervals for the real average hits but I can still make a rough estimation.

The error is porportional to the standard deviation and inversly proportional to the square root of number of observations. So we have:
REAL AVG HIT = MEASURED AVG HIT +/- k*STD_DEV/sqrt(n)

where k is a constant from the students distribution with some degree of confidence (k=2 corresponds to ~95% confidence) and n is the number of observations.

We already have the number of observations and the measured avg hit so we only need to make a rough estimation of the standard deviation. I don't have any logs from sol ro but I found some 1200 hits from a soldier of fire in my logs. The standard deviation for that set was 194 which I will round to 200. Now assuming the sentry in sol ro have the same Max and Min hit (733, 173) as a soldier of fire in PoF we can use 200 as an aproximation of the standard deviation to get an idea of the statistical uncertainty in your test.

For the 1815 AC test:
REAL AVG DMG = 312 +/- 2*(200)/sqrt(813) = 312 +/- 14

and this I can say with 95% confidence if I neglect the fact that the standard deviation might be somewhat off.

Note: a good estimation of the standard deviation is (Max-Min)/2.. this will always be an overestimation of the error and in our case it would lead us to the conclusion: real avg dmg = 312 +- 20 with more than 95% confidence.

Note II: As you can see the only way to lower the error is to increase the number of observations since the error is inversly porportional to the sqrt of number of observations. With 3,000 observations our error would decrease to +-7 and with 10,000 observations the error would be down to +-4.
