When you're in software QA, you are constantly crushed between two competing (and often conflicting) urges:

1) ship it with all bugs identified and/or corrected, or
2) ship it on time

Most complex software (and EQ falls in the category) has so many permutations possible in its code that testing each of them in a reasonable time period is literally impossible. QA managers usually draw up a matrix of all those possible scenarios and then take a look at the delivery schedule. In the years I've been involved in software QA, I have never had enough time to test every scenario. So, what you do is create something called an orthogonal array. This tool gives you a way to deploy your test resources in the most efficient manner possible; its intent is to touch as many of the options as possible. The pay off is that you reduce your risk.

But you're always at risk. And something always gets through. Competitors don't factor in, because they're subject to the same imperatives.
