Brabble
"This is my first war and I am trying to make sure he has all the right stuff once I get to a higher level."

Okay, if you're serious about this, don't upgrade your weapons. Don't do all your leveling with the druid. Group now. Group with people you know who you know know how to play their classes, then group in pick up groups. Learn to read what your group is doing and how they are doing it. In pick up groups, learn as fast as you can who can play and who cannot and make any adjustments you can. Watch in particular what happens when personel changes in a group. Certain changes will cut or increase healing, runners, adds, crowd control, dps, buffs.
Know the buffs others can cast on you, what they will do for you, whether they require reagents. If someone fails to cast a buff on you that you know is useful, ask politely, in a tell, for the buff as time and mana allow.
Let your groupmates know when critical buffs are about to fade.
Time the mob spawns everywhere you fight. this should be automatic and you should be able to do it in your head. Know the characteristics of the mobs in the zone: their relative ATK, hps, MR, pathing, aggro radius, potential to run.
Start positioning mobs now, and never stop doing it. Position with respect to pets, to group members, to the pathing of other potential wandering mobs; stay within range of your casters. There is always an optimal position to tank a mob in. At your level, it hardly matters whether you position the mob optimally, but do it anyway. Practice when it doesn't matter, not when it does. Do this when it doesn't matter, do it when it does, do it early, often, always, and twice on Tuesdays.
Know and use your abilities and capacities. Range weapons, /shield, slam/bash, bind wound, sit, taunt, proximity aggro, incite line--use 'em all, use 'em in the right order at the right time. Is your group on a med break and your shaman is canning? Are you standing there at full hitpoints doing nothing, rather than using your BW skill to make the shaman's canning 1/3rd more efficient? Is the mob you're fighting a caster? is your bash/slam up? Do you see a casting message?
Know the abilities of your groupmates' classes, and do what you can to help them do their jobs, particularly enchanters who are attempting to mez mobs.
Judge the feel of the power of your group. How fast can they kill mobs? how much rest do your casters need? Ask mana. If healer/crowd control is low, you call the break. If a caster needs a med break, always second the motion, especially for healers/crowd control. Med breaks are shorter than corpse runs. If group is not out of mana, pull faster, but ramp that up gradually: a bad puller believes that faster pulling is always better, but a good puller pulls at the maximum rate the group can kill, with very little time under or over that optimal risk/return curve.
Know the levels and classes of everyone in your group and where they are set up. Watch the health bars of your groupmates. You shouldn't have to look for your cleric when his hp bar starts to drop--you should already know where the cleric was set up. Know where the mobs are. If more than one mob comes to camp, you should have a darn good reason for tanking them in a certain order. There is always an optimal order. Tanking in the optimal order may not matter at the level of the mobs you're fighting now, but there will nevertheless be an optimal order and that's the order you should tank 'em in always. Practice when it doesn't matter not when it does.
Be cheerful, calm, and don't be afraid to talk strategy with groupmates. Don't run out of arrows, water or food when you're fighting. Don't whine when you die: it means that the mob was hitting you, which was a good thing.
In some ways tanking as a warrior is made up of a whole lot of little things, none of which matter by themselves, but all of which matter in the aggregate.
