Although it's been posted repeatedly in one form or another, I put together what I believe is a clear statement of the Warrior/Knight class balance issue and why many players believe changes are justified and required. I'm not trying to get Knights nerfed or to spend their days /lfg. Rather, I'm suggesting Warriors need some improvement in order to restore parity.

While the player community may have opinions about class balance issues and roles, SoE produces and maintains EverQuest. They are the ultimate authority.

SoE's website contains Class Descriptions (http://eqlive.station.sony.com/library/classes.jsp). It is reasonable to expect that this information is accurate and identifies important class characteristics.

--"their heavy armor"

The Warrior Class Description states in part, "When combined with their heavy armor and excellent defensive skills, warriors are unmatched in their ability to survive the most brutal battles."

Warriors should be able to wear "their heavy armor" as distinquished from the other plate wearing melees which one is led to believe must wear something lighter.

While this might be true for class specific armor, pre-planar melees (the majority on most servers) tend to wear the best armor and accesories available to them. These are almost always WAR, PAL, SHD, BRD/All for example, Great Shadow Platemail is one of the better breastplates in the game and wearable by all. This not only provides Knights the same AC and HP gains enjoyed by Warriors, but adds Mana to their mana pool. In other words, Knights get more benefit from this armor than Warriors.

PoP Warrior class armor should have more AC and HP's than SoL WAR, PAL, SHD, BRD/All armor.

--"excellent defensive skills"

Warriors have substantially the same defensive skills as Knights with some minor (10%) advantages in Dodge, Parry, and Reposte and they have the Defensive discipline.

--"unmatched in their ability to survive the most brutal battles"

A Warrior may have a raw 800 HP advantange over a simularly equipped Knight. But since Paladins always have at a minimum their self-buffs, the actual Warrior HP advantage over a simularly equipped Paladin is 450 HP [800 HP - Aura of the Crusader (350 HP Paladin self buff)] at best.

In order to have even a very modest HP advantage, Warriors must purchase Virtue and Brells before grouping (and not die) or only join groups containing a Cleric and a Paladin. However, groups often assume that any buffs they can't give a Warrior, he won't have. Since a Paladin can buff himself and the group they are without question prefered in any group lacking a Cleric over a Warrior.

In almost all experiance groups a Warrior HP advantage does not exit. A more detailed post discussing this issue may be found at
What Warrior HP Advantage? (http://www.thesteelwarrior.org/forum/showthread.php?s=&threadid=3485)

Because of the widespread use of WAR, PAL, SHD, BRD/All armor and accesories, marginally inferior but still excellent defensive skill, and a mana rich environment that does not confer any great advantage on a Warrior's 400-800 additional HP's, Knights are nearly matching Warrior survivability in the raid environment and for all practical purposes match it in the experiance group environment.

--"the focus of all their enemies' attacks"

The Warrior Class Description continues, "Warriors also learn to taunt their opponents, making themselves the focus of all their enemies' attacks, allowing the less resilient members of their group to remain safe from harm."

Although the role of MT appears only in the Warrior Class Description it is performed by the other tank classes as well. Since it is explicitly cited as a Warrior role, Warriors expect to be better at it than other classes that have additional abilities.

There are several charcteristics needed to MT: survivability and the ability to obtain, hold, and if necessary re-gain aggro.

There is no evidence whatsoever in the Class Descriptions that Knights "should" out aggro or out Taunt Warriors. The only mention of Taunt and the ability to make oneself the "....focus of all their enemies' attacks...." is in the Warrior Class Description.

Skill caps and AA abilities seem to reflect the view that Warriors were in fact to have an advantage in this area:

Taunt Skill Cap.......Warrior 200, Knight 180
Archery Skill Cap.....Warrior 240, Knight 75
AE Taunt...............Warrior yes, Knight no

Warriors do slightly more melee DPS than the other tank classes which helps them to hold aggro. All of these classes can weild weapons which "proc" aggro increasing effects.

Ignoring the effect of spells for a moment; itemization, skills, and abilities are intended to give Warriors greater survivability and taunt.

In actual play, however, we discover Warriors often weild a BoC and another aggro weapon which not only reduces DPS, but causes them to take more reposte damage which completely negates any mitigation and HP advantage.

We discover that despite the Warrior Class Description, Warriors generally do not enjoy greater survivability, DPS, or as we shall learn - competative ability to make themselves the "....focus of all their enemies' attacks...."

--The Problem

Knights can use easily obtained/low level spells to augment or even totally replace the mechanisms provided in the game to obtain and hold aggro.

Not having to obtain and use aggro weapons allows Knights to use a wide variety of weapons. Since 2H weapons produce more DPS than a 1H past level 53, and the DLY is usually greater thus minimizing reposte damage, they are generally used. However, since Knights do have higher quality 1Hdrs available they can elect to use a shield in order to increase their survivability.

Because 2H aggro weapons are scarce, Warriors often Dual Wield. The most common aggro setup for Warriors is a BoC and another weapon. The Warrior mitigation and HP advantage does not exist if they must Dual Weild when MTing and they face mobs that reposte (a not uncommon situation).

Since Knights use spells to obtain aggro the only random factor is their fizzle rate (typically quite low). While Knights may use the Taunt Button in order to insure they stay at or near the top of the Hate list, casting a spell or two usually gives them aggro and allows them to build Hate. Spells also provide Knights with what players call "snap aggro on demand" and "ranged aggro"; capabilities not possesed by Warriors which allow groups to fight much more effectively and safely.

Warriors on the other hand rely on damage output (low), random weapon procs and the Taunt Button (whose success is also governed by the Random Number Generator). They take longer to acquire aggro, have greater difficulty retaining it, and find re-gaining it if the mob aggros on a group member at any distance from them quite challenging.

-- Conclusion

Knights essentially match Warriors "....in their ability to survive the most brutal battles."

Knights are better able than Warriors to make "....themselves the focus of all their enemies' attacks, allowing the less resilient members of their group to remain safe from harm."

Since Knights also have numerous spells useful in the group setting, they have become the prefered MT's in experiance groups.

There is little reason for Warriors to exist as a distinct class if they are only needed to MT difficult raid mobs while in the majority of the content Knight's are equipped to fill the Warrior Class Description better than Warriors themselves.

Okay, fair enough. How hate works 101, as best we understand it. When a spell is cast against a mob, we'll use a stun as an example, a set amount of "hate" is generated. For our purposes we'll say 300 "hate". This is soley for the purposes of example, we're really not sure how much "hate" a stun generates. Now, Shaman slow, for the sake of discussion we'll say generates 250 "hate".

If a Paladin stuns a mob on incoming, he's generated 300 hate from the get go. If a Shaman slows right afterward, because his spell only generates 250 hate, the mob disregards the Shaman for the time being and beats on the Paladin. As the Paladin melees his damage adds additional hate as does any additional stuns he casts. And since the Paladin's spells have a set amount of hate associated with them, he can fairly well predict how many he needs to stay at the top of the "hate" list. Typically this is 3 or so stuns, give or take, depending on how aggressive the casters you're grouped with are or how well equipped the melee are.

Now, lets consider a Warrior in a similar situation. The mob is brought back to camp, the Warrior has nothing he can do that will establish a significant amount of hate from the get go. So he starts at 0 hate, or very close to it. He taunts the mob, now if the taunt is successful (which it is about 60% of the time from our best tests) the mob will turn and beat on him. If it fails, it will continue to beat on the puller. If at this point the Shaman casts slow on the mob, he generates that 250 hate we discussed earlier. If the Warrior's taunt succeeded, he is just a little bit higher on the hate list than the puller. If it failed, he's below the puller on the hate list. Either way, the likelihood is good that the Shaman is going to get aggro from his spell. The only times this would not be true would be if a) The Warrior has been beating on the mob for a pretty significant amount of time, or b) if the Warrior had a procing weapon that happened to proc prior to the Shaman casting slow. If neither of these are true, and very often they aren't, then the Shaman gets aggro and the Warrior attempts to taunt the mob off the Shaman. Again we're back to that 60% success figure. And keep in mind that for some reason when taunt fails, it seems to fail in strings, so you'll get 3, 4, or even more taunts failed in a row. And when this happens you're waiting another 6 seconds between taunt attempts to try again. I think you can see how this would mean the Shaman is spending a fair amount of time tanking if that happens. And while it doesn't happen all the time, it's not really that uncommon. You might have it occur 2 or 3 times in an evening, more or less.

Once the Warrior has successfully taunted off the Shaman, he is now pretty high on the hate list, since a successful taunt moves you to the top of the hate list plus just a little, we usually consider it +1. So for our example the Warrior would be at 250+1 hate, plus whatever additional hate his melee was producing and adding in procs, if any. So at this point he has a fairly good lead on the hate list, unless his group really decides to let loose. The thing is, if his group does decide to let loose, there isn't much the Warrior can do about it. He can't generate more hate at will other than the amount randomly generated by his melee damage and his procs. So one fight the Warrior could have very rock solid aggro, because he got a good run of procs. While on the next fight his aggro could be much weaker because he got no procs at all.

This can be frustrating to the casters, because one fight they can let loose with almost as much as they want. The next, they have to hold back considerably or they get a beat down. It makes them kind of pissy.

Yet with a Paladin, this isn't the case. If his group decides to unload more, he just casts another stun or two and viola, he's climbed up the hate list faster and keeps aggro.

So when you are grouped with a Warrior and cast a stun early in the battle, the Warrior taunts off you. This gives him effectively your 300 hate +1, which is a nice lead on the hate list. And unless you're chain casting stuns or taunting yourself, he's liable to stay ahead of you and everyone else in the group from that point on, barring the group really dumping damage on the mob. This works well enough, if the group is willing to take both a Warrior and a Paladin. Many times they're not willing. They'll take the Paladin because his survivability is not a "lot" worse than the Warrior's and the predictability of his aggro generation is "much" higher. And they'll fill the second tank spot with a high DPS producer, to increase the damage dealt and therefore, theoretically, the amount of experience gained in a set amount of time.

And there is the core of the issue.
