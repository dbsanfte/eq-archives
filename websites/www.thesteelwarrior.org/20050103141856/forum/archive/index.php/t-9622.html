Yep!, Moons follow the same rules as any other massive bodies in space. Physics doesn't distinguish between a planet, sun, or moon, They all follow the same laws. The only reason they are called moons is because they are in stable orbit around another planet. If they orbited the sun, their primary orbit was centered near the sun then they would be called planets. Pluto is smaller than many of the larger planets moons.

Jupiter's moon Io is almost at its Roche limit, that is what causes it to be so volcanically active. If you were to set off, say, a 10 million megaton nuke (Do it on the 4Th of July) directly in front of Io as it orbits Jupiter, you could possibly slow it enough so that its orbit crossed the Roche limit and the tidal forces from Jupiter will then tear Io apart. This would not, however, just result in a nice little bunch of asteroids orbiting Jupiter in a clump. Due to collisions between the debris and the slightly different orbits of the big pieces, there would be a lot of debris ejected into the rest of the solar system (very bad for us on Earth) and a lot would also fall into Jupiter. There would also probably be a large ring formed after a few hundred years from what did remain in orbit around Jupiter.
