This list is from a letter given to us today after the EQ team met to discuss what they'd heard in our individual meetings. Keep in mind this isn't a final list or even close to it. They got a ton of information today, and this is more of a "to do" list for the time being while they finish going through it all.

Comments in Red are mine.

Dear Guild and Community Representatives,

Thank you very much for a great Summit! By all accounts it was a huge success. I can speak for the EverQuest Development Team and tell you that we're all grateful for the input you provided. In fact, in trying to generate this list to giove you this evening, we realized there was so much to go over that we wouldn't be able to get a complete list together in time. We're committed to not only listening to your feedback, but working with you to make EverQuest more enjoyable for years to come.

So, we decided to list the Top Ten themes we would address starting Monday, as well as the most common themes that we'll come back with more information on by mid-week next week.

A quick discussion resulted in a list of items that we will immediately address:


Add scripting to shut off empty instances of Plane of Time
This is the first step towards them fully instancing time
Raid Messages of the day
Improving how we handle beta and the testing process
John Smedley appologized for how this was handled in GoD. BetaBuff didn't sound like it would be around much longer.
Increase the number of debuffs allowed on MOBs
Increase buffs on players through AAs
Yay.
Note field on raid window
Improved consent & drag for raid, group, and guild
This includes /consent for Raid, Group, and Guild. Other things mentioned were the ability to drag multiple corpses.
Improve corpse drag radius
Make charms not no-drop
Guild and individual rewards for guild beta testing
Work on larger To Do list...I know this one is a little random


Future items we will immediately begin looking at and discussing are as follows:


Plane of Time instancing and other issues
Back flagging solutions to help with high-end guild atrophy
This is a great one. Ideas thrown around were guild flags, extra flags, 85/15 rule everywhere, etc.
Improved raid and guild functionality
Ideas brought up were Secondary/Tertiary assists, /assist raid being the same range as /assist, drag and drop groupmaking in the raid window.
Auto loot all for your corpse
Improvements to raid XP
Review the epic 2.0 quests
They are thinking of making these a progressive type quest (think coldain ring)
No more alien NPCs ;-) (focusing more on familiar Norrathian themes)
Solo LDON-Style instances or similar content
Issues with item augmentations
I believe this includes focii
Possible enhancement to guild wars


Of course these aren't all of items. You brought us a CD, 2 books, 20 printed sheets, and a manifesto. We'll need to go through all of those and our notes before we can really get a good tally of all the wonderful ideas and questions that were presented.

THank you again for your feedback and participation. WE've had a great weekend and a wonderful display of things to come.

Sincerely,
Robert Pfister
Executive Producer
EverQuest

Compared with the wide-ranging discussion in the other thread, I have to admit that this set of issues they are addressing is disappointingly narrow. Is Sony rearranging the deck-chairs? Is "a wider corpse summon" really in the top 50 of the major issues that needs to be addressed?

There is an elephant in the room (WoW) that is coming and coming fast. And no one is talking about it. Already several major (top tier) guilds across the servers have died in anticipation of WoW- people are selling accounts, dumping items to bazaar and leaving. Why bother playing another 3 months when you will be leaving in 3 months time? Why sell your account and items in 3 months time when plat prices will be much lower? Yet, I see nothing about how EQ and WoW will fit together- what sorts of players each will attract?

What I would like to see is a list of services EQ intends to provide to each of its customer types- a "business model" like you'd see in any other type of business:

-raid guilds will see more of xxx events; and
-non-raid guilds will see more of yyy events.

Sony needs to sit down and carefully think what it is providing its customers. For example, let's take my guild:

I'm the raid leader of a casual guild. We raid (4 groups) once a week 9pm - 1am EST on Friday nights, and then run 1-3 group events maybe another 2 nights a week. I tend to think I represent a reasonably large proportion of the EQ playerbase (20% maybe). My guild members are almost all 65 with around 100 AA and some a lot more. We are all mature players. Most have been playing EQ since 1999.

What events exist for a guild like mine? It can't take long to travel to or to CR from, as time is very restricted. Anything requiring keying or flagging is out. Everyone is well-equipped from PoP, so loot from a large amount of the zones is undesirable. We can't choose our time to raid, and we don't have a large number of trackers, so valued timed spawns are out.

The answer of course is that there is very little now for my guild. GoD provides a few new zones that are not locked, but equipment drops are very low even in Ferubi. LoY raids are not worthwhile. LDoN raids are too hard. PoP-flags lock us out of the decent zones. Luclin and Velious are either trash or timed spawns we will never find up. We are left with the open PoP zones and just getting lucky with bosses in other expansions.

If WoW provides a better match for my guild's requirements, we will all be leaving and taking our 80-100 accounts with us. What is Sony doing for its customer base?

This fact is especially sad given that my guild has been playing this game for 5 years, and that the tools to match my guild's needs ALREADY EXIST! Instancing is a perfect tool for MMORPGs. A full set of LDoN raids of all difficulty types would keep casual/non-raider guilds happy for a long time. No new content needs to be added- Sony simply has to flesh out existing content. I can easily see instancing being the model for all future RPGs and 3-7 day random spawns as dying with EQ.

My plane landed in San Diego around 2:00 pst Friday, and I was greeted at the baggage claim by a guy named Wink Winkleman. He took me out to the bus which Thomas Taylor (aka Vannoth aka Cyphos(sp)) was there. He's the guy that puts together all the fan faires and such - real cool guy and our host for the event along with Brenlo.

The bus takes me to the hotel and I just chill for a bit talking to some of the SOE and other players that were there already. At 5:00 we left for PetCo park to see the Padre's vs the Brewers.


On Saturday the buses left the hotel at around 8:00 for SOE headquarters. When we got there we all got name tags and sat and listened to a little talk by Smed and a few designers about some general issues and what we were trying to accomplish by being there. He said this was an open thing, if we wanted to see something we could see it - he just asked that we not take pictures of certain things on the tour. Woody took a pic of smed holding his wallet too :D

Next was a tour of the offices. We got to see everything...My group went with Tom and we saw the GM pit first. Yes, GM's do exist, I saw them. On a sunday morning at 8:30 am they had probably 9 ppl in the pit and the queues were all empty. That's a great thing to see. Their new CS Assist tool is great.

After that we basically just toured the buildings. The server room was pretty impressive. Smed showed us the "eq server of old" which is just monstrous...then he showed us some new servers they are going to be switching to soon which were no bigger than my tv. They're really fast too.

Once we got done exploring the offices (it took a little while...we really did look at everything) then we broke into our round-table discussion groups . The theme of this meeting was "Problem Fixing". We had Brenlo, Smed, Shaun Lord and a couple others in there with us. The meeting went pretty well...surprisingly. Smed was writing down a ton of notes, and looked surprised by some of the things we mentioned. He was really interested in it all though.

After that, everyone went to get lunch. They had hamburgers and stuff but I went to check out EQ2 and omens instead of a burger :P

Eq2 looks fantastic graphics-wise. It was running on an alienware system with a p4 3.0, 2gb ram and one of the newer nvidia cards. In the options, you can either pick and chose from what looked like hundreds of different things. You can customize it to look as good (or bad) as you want it to. If you aren't quite that savvy though, theres also a box with ~6 settings that covered the whole spectrum pretty much. When switched to the highest settings, it would crawl. The system played it fairly well on "Balanced" though, which still looked amazing.

After that I headed down to check out the oow models and epic weapons that they had complete. The lead graphics designer was messing with some of the models and I got to take a look at them. I also got to see all the epic weapons that they had "finished" (that term is used loosely because only about half had models at that point [rest had concept art i got pics of] and none of the partical effects were implemented yet). Someone put a hell of a lot of time into these. Most of them have etchings that glow in the dark, and a TON of bumpmapping and detail involved in them. They really do look epic. I'd try and describe them, but i'm sure one of the other ppl will have some pics on their sites soon enough.

Next was a Customer Service meeting. Tom told me their CS reps actually asked whether they should have security in the meetings...i liked that. It was a good talk though, theres a few changes coming to customer service very soon, which I listed in the first post.

Next came round two of the developer discussions, this time the theme was "Moving Foreward". This time it was a little less us telling SoE what we thought off the cuff, and more of them asking us specific questions, and getting our feedback on it.

After that we headed back to the hotels, and to the Rock Bottom Brewery San Diego.

There was a ton of information during this event, and it's no surprise that they needed a little more time to go through it all. Everyone was really positive though and things went well I would say. There wasn't much (if any) aggression towards the staff. It was more about helping out the future of the game, than blaming and ridiculing what had been done in the past.

Several thoughts.

First, concerning this summit, I agree with Kailyn's assessment in post #77 of this thread. From a game design point of view, high-level guilds and players are a point of pressure--content has to be developed that both gives them more to do and is attractive enough that serious players will want to do it. It has to take long enough to complete that the design people have a chance to come up with the next thing. And, it has to be done in such a way that it doesn't completely bork the game economy or mechanics. It seems natural to me that this sort of summit would start with those players. How, really, could SOE start anywhere else?

Second, top players and casual players are in this together. Unless SOE makes the game fun for a variety of players, then some groups of players will simply stop playing. If that happens, declining revenue will make improvements for the remaining players economically difficult to justify by a publicly traded company. Thus, it seems logical to me that SOE be interested in dealing with casual players' concerns, and from what I can tell at the very least the feedback at the summit and from various class sites must have impressed this upon SOE (and this site was not alone among class sites where casual-player issues came up. I suspect that the spontaneous eruption of these concerns from many different sources would be as attention-getting as any single arguments about specific issues.)

Third, casual players and top players are in this together. Yep, I said it twice. Arguments in this thread about how long it would take a casual player like me to get great gear in LDoNs if I were in a large guild and geared to tank hard LDoNs and never had to rely on pick-up groups and never had to spend 30 minutes waiting to fill out a group and could put in the same amount of time doing adventures as serious players put in raiding --- lol, well, these arguments are simply not applicable to my situation. Likewise, for me personally a low drop rate on some GoD uberpointything01 is, to put it mildly, of no consequence at all. But I can certainly see why it would be a serious matter for someone who repeatedly and at great risk kills the mob that drops the uberpointything01 yet never sees the drop. Particularly if the drop rates for items needed by various classes is not kept in line, entire guilds could stall out trying to get one class the gear it needs for the guild to take on the next level of content, which, uh, would suck, to put it mildly.

Fourth, I understand that Ixess has some reason to be angry with SOE, but saying that Raaj sounds like he's been paid off is so blatantly offensive that if it weren't so utterly absurd I would have had to pull out the profanity stick and thumped you with it, Ixess. A civil person would apologize.

Finally, it seems to me that on several issues there is widespread agreement here:
1. Some game mechanics elements need a lot of work, particularly the collision system. Other issues such as the failure rate of taunt against higher-level mobs could become much more critical if the level cap is raised to 70. A serious overhaul of the way in which game mechanic changes and expansions are tested would be extremely helpful. Many of these issues are well-known, long standing, and should be examined sooner rather than later. I doubt any of this is news to SOE, but still.

2. Some kind of tuning of LDoN augments and point system. In particular, many people here strongly that some means of removing-and-saving or trading in augments would greatly reduce player frustration.

3. There are a number of progression issues, such as flagging/back flagging, that are frustrating for a lot of players. Expansions that have a trial that is more difficult than the content it unlocks are frustrating for many players. I suspect that backflagging-like issues are a pain for guild leadership too--either your guild works hard to get players flagged only to see those players jump to a more uber guild, or the Uber guild finds itself continually re-doing old content just to keep a good supply of vital classes around. Or your small guild simply can't do such a big raid and getting an alliance worked out with another small guild and getting everyone's casual schedule set up becomes a nighmare.

4. Very very few quests work in this game. I'm not just talking about bugged quests; a quest that works 1) is possible to do by a character who can use the reward given if the quest is completed, 2) Offers a reward that is in-line with the time it takes to complete the quest, and 3) is fun and cool to do and not simply some mind-numbing green camp for rare mob that rarely drops the thingy you need or a difficult camp that involves the questing player convincing large numbers of friends to spend hours camping or fighting mobs for a trivial reward.

5. Instanced content should seriously be examined as a way to expand some aspects of the game. Obviously, poorly designed instanced content won't really help.

6. Most folks agree that there has always been, is now, and should be a great deal of difference between the power of top characters and those who do not have the top gear. The wider the gap (and this gap would get wider with a level 70 cap), the more helpful it would be to have content with graduated difficulty. Graduated difficulty here is related to #5 above.

Okay, I was going to add that tradeskills have been seriously shortchanged over time, and that some resources need to be put into providing tradeskillers with useful things to make. But, heh, not sure everyone agrees with me there.

I agree with the previous comments. EQ transformed too much into a raiding/grinding game. It got away from fantasy, group adventure, questing, and fun. The focus of the game became the big raid, with hundreds of hours grinding exp just to become more effective at more big raids. Part of the problem with the endless balance discussions is that the game got boiled down to the few repetitive tasks involved in raiding.

Keys, flags -- these things should never have replaced QUESTS as the focus of the game. That's one reason why people STILL like to farm epics, even though at this point they are mostly junk. It's just one of the few sort of interesting things left. One or two keys or flags for dark and mysterious places like VP or Sebilis back in the day, that's one thing. But the endless and miserable progression that is (somewhat) Luclin and (especially) PoP was a terrible, terrible development for the game. It divided the player base and set up tiers of haves and have nots, not on the basis of individual skill, but on the ability to log many consecutive hours of play in large raiding guilds.

I was a member of two guilds in my 5 years of EQ. The first was a sort of casual guild, that gradually got more and more casual, as it became clear that we weren't racing to end content. But early on, we were among the early guilds clearing Hate and Fear. As the game/progression requirements ratcheted up, we lost ground, and PoP finally simply broke the guild. Where my "casual" guild had formerly had the ability to field at least a few groups competitive with most of the more serious guilds on the server, we lost that. It became very apparent the guild simply could never make it through PoP, and those more interested in progression simply left. A few excellent groups could break Hate and Fear for a guild, but they can't kill MB (without being geared/AA'd beyond that level). Once that cycle starts, it's very hard for a guild to get off the treadmiill. The decision had to be made to endlessly farm epics for twinks and do LDoN, or hook up with a raiding guild and try to progress. My friends had mostly all already quit, and I was very friendly with a raiding guild, so I joined them.

For a season, all was well. I always had played a lot, even as a "casual" player (which just meant I didn't raid all that much; I was on all the time), and I enjoyed the raiding. We broke into elementals some 9 months ago or whatever, and seemed poised to enter Time. Then I guess some kind of guild fatigue struck. Many people quit/left at the same time, including me. I never even intended to quit -- I got busy with rl stuff, and drifted away. But now I can't even really seriously contemplate going back. For what? To grind away again in Earth rings? Herd 40 people together, make sure we get enough clerics, and spend 2-3 hours tediously farming an endless succession of mobs for 2 drops? Backflag people by doing the same raids over and over and over and over again? Crank out another 200 AAs farming exp mobs in dull outdoor zones, where each AA gets me an increment of extra power that's barely discernible?

I've sort of thought of the change in EQ as analogous to the difference between driving cross-country and flying. Driving is sort of slower, more personal, and surprising and little interesting things can pop up if you're open to that. Flying is hours of boredom punctuated by moments of terror (or real pleasure). You have to pay the price to fly -- the ticket, standing around in airports, crowding into airplanes, etc -- and you're working on someone else's schedule. Big payoff though -- it can be amazing (if you like it), and lets you go places you couldn't get to before. EQ became totally oriented around flying, and the moment of contact with a big mob, for which hours and hours of preparation in farming, exping, etc, were necessary. It was a shift from a more general process of fun, to a more crystallized few moments of excitement (inc Vox!) which must be paid for by endless (and tedious) preparation. I think both are necessary for a really rich game experience, but somewhere EQs driving experience got short shrift.

Sorry for rambling. In short:

-progression is too rigidly defined, and too contingent on a certain style of game play (mass group raiding)

-not enough FUN stuff, especially questing

-dramatic division of the player base into haves and have nots, which LDoN, while a good effort and a good thing, is not sufficient to address
