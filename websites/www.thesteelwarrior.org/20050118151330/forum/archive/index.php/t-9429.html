I'm sure folks will correct me if I'm wrong, but if i want the strongest possible aggro in the first round of a fight, the sequence will go like this:

Case 1 (I'm pulling)
1. tag mob with arrow
2. Wait for mob in the location I want to tank it. (If long pull/good line of sight, might have time to tag incoming mob with arrow a second time)
3. Get a round of melee in on mob.
4. Use incite line.

Strictly speaking, I'm not sure #3 is necessary; however, it looks a lot to me like you don't get the full hate of the incite line if you use it to do the initial pulling (before either tagging with arrow or meleeing). At level 55 you have to be tanking a very tough mob indeed for slow to have to land before the mob ever hits you. There are a number of classes that have better pulling tools than warriors do, but in normal LDoNs I prefer to do as much of the pulling as possible. And, in fact, much of a normal LDoN can usually be single or double pulled; a couple of times each adventure you'll come to a room with 4-5 dark blue mobs that will all come unless you have pac/split pulling class.

Case 2 (someone else is pulling)
1. Assist puller and tag incoming mob with arrow (assuming you have good line of sight on mob)
2. If #1 does not get you to the top of the aggro list, taunt mob; if arrow does get mob on you, do not taunt.
3. engage with melee
4. use incite line.

Needless to say, all of these things should happen very quickly; there shouldn't be a pause between melee engagement and the incite.
The drawback to this is sequence is that if slow lands before you can engage mob, mob is apt to jump the shaman (because you haven't used incite yet). However, if you see the mob peeling off toward the caster, you can always incite at that point as it's an instant effect.
If you have a low aggro puller (say, a bard) then it is easier to get the mob on you when it gets to camp, obviously. However, if you have a high-aggro puller (say, an SK) when you do take aggro you'll be much farther ahead of the casters. I've been in some very efficient groups with SKs, because they can generate big aggro, and can take a hit until i piggyback on their aggro. If you don't get into an aggro pissing contest with the SK, either of you can tank, you can ride his aggro, the group has offtanking, steamroll tanking, and named tanking all covered nicely.
Obviously, it helps if the puller actually brings the mob to you, rather than to some random location in or near the camp. It also helps if your shaman stands right next to you during the initial slow. If slower wants to sit down after you secure aggro, she should back up a ways before sitting.

Other aggro considerations:
1. Do not use taunt if you already have aggro. The "+ some small amount" of additional aggro you get is trivial compared to the potential advantage of having taunt refreshed when you need it.
2. If taunt and the incite line are both up and a mob turns from you, the proper sequence for regaining aggro is to taunt then incite. A successful taunt will regain aggro and the incite hate will sit on top of that. If taunt is unsuccessful, you still get the incite hate, at least.
3. Disarm will put you on the hate list, but nothing more than that as far as I know. Forget it and begging.
4. Sitting can help, but you have to be in trouble before it's a good idea.
5. /shield <your shaman> will add to your aggro, I think, although I have no parses to prove it. If I were you and duo'd a bunch, I'd have it hotkeyed.
6. You should always be the closest player to the mob when you are tanking the mob. Proximity aggro is free.
7. The cases where you are losing aggro when another character's proc fires is probably just an effect of the random number generator. Aggro will often bounce to the first person who gets off a stun proc. If a cleric starts to melee with a CHoS or something, I figure that cleric is prepared to tank through multiple taunt failures on my part. If not, they'll generally decide to quit meleeing at some point during their run from bind point back to the group.
8. You mention LDoN dd/lifetap procs. My baby 54 warrior just got the heated blade aug from N. Good stuff here from Brabble. The only change for me is if I'm pulling, I don't wait for a melee round. I fire incite as soon as I can. I often tag the mob, and stand spamming incite until it hits, and THEN run back to the group. This lets the "DoT" portion of the incite add up more while the mob is running to me.

What brabble suggests for when working with another puller is pretty much the best scenario for aggro efficiency. However, oftentimes your puller is being lame and doesn't run straight through you, so you aren't easily in taunt range. Or you're fighting stuff that will wax your puller in 1 round if taunt were to fail. In that case if my arrow tags didn't get aggro I go ahead and fire incite, and hope it peels the mob off before it gets to camp. It's not ideal, but it prevents anyone else from getting hit, and I still have taunt and /shield up if the slower gets aggro.

Read and reread his "other considerations". They're spot on. Don't spam taunt, use it only when somebody else has taken the top spot on the aggro list and you need it back. I'll add a few of my own, too.

1. CHoS + SotB is a good combo. I used them at your level and did well. Try and get people to do LGuk ldons and save for at least a single ykesha aug for your mainhand. I wouldn't buy anything else with your ldon points until you have one, although heated blade wouldn't hurt anything I guess. Ykesha is good aggro, adds dps, and is cheap. You can get Anger2 if you really want but most of us don't think it's as good.

2. Make sure your dex is buffed as high as possible in a group. Take chanter haste, that werewolf form thingy or NDT if it's available, make sure you're wearing hos+dex from the shaman all the time. If you're less than 255 dex, your procrate is getting gimped.

Beyond that, if you're still not holding aggro pretty consistently, people just need to be a little more careful, or they're significantly overgeared.
