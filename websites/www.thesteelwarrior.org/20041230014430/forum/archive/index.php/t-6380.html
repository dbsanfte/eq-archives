Mmmm. There's actually some game-theoretic basis for that (fixing major exploits but not major bugs). Remember that we're dealing with a persistent world here, and that their primary goal (from an evolutionary point of view) has to be player retention, not player enjoyment.

If they permit a bug to persist, they will have many annoyed players. But very few of them will be annoyed enough to quit the game, especially if the bug only affects a small percentage of dedicated players (such as "all raiders", or "most posters on Steel Warriors"). And the vast majority of players will never even really notice, or care that much if they do. And they (the vast majority of players) certainly won't care that some raiding players are being hugely inconvenienced.

Now suppose that there is an exploit that lets everyone whose character name starts with "Haas" gain XP at 100 times the normal rate. As long as this is not general knowledge, it hurts no one (not even SOE), and nobody cares. But as soon as it hits the message boards, it hurts the game enjoyment of *every single player that hears about it*. Envy and jealousy are incredibly powerful forces. "There's a banker in Befallen giving unlimited plat, and you didn't know about it in time." will get rid of more players in one day than twenty days of "And then on my high-end raid, I got ported to the zone and died 20 times in a row to a bug."

SOE is simply making those choices that ensure that they will continue to exist as a business entity. And since my continued enjoyment of EverQuest depends on them making those choices in precisely that way, I have to support them in theory, even when the effect of their choices is personally aversive to me.
