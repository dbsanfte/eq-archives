The Biography

Biography. The word made us shudder when we were little kids. When that paper with the dreaded sentence came down the aisle to us: "Write a biography of so-and-so." Biographies meant long hours of research and writing reverently dedicated words to some person with a strange name who we didn’t care about.

The dictionary defines a biography as the written account of another person’s life. Considering that you are now writing a biography for your character, it won’t be nearly as difficult. (Note: If you haven’t yet created a character with at least some general quirks in their personality, stop right now. Characters born of biographies tend to be boring, and that’s not what you want as a genuine role-player.) You have almost complete control over all of your character’s past deeds.

Why bother writing a biography, you ask? Take a look at how important your own past is to you. Now look at your character. You want him to be as close to a living, breathing being (such as yourself) as possible - and that includes a history. Having this recorded allows other people to refer back to the past of your character, and base their character’s actions on that.

So how to get started? First, make a list of the unmistakable habits of your character - the "how" of your character. This can include the way he reacts to certain things, an emotional tendency, what kind of people he is attracted to/repulsed by, or a special name or physical feature. Leave room next to each "quirk" for the next step.

Once you’ve written down all the quirks, take time and think about each one. Why does the character have this trait? What could cause the character to have such a certain reaction? This is the most difficult part of the biography process. Some traits can be natural, such as the loose tongue of a dwarf. For the most part, though, it’s up to you to create a past that not only explains each quirk, but makes sense.

Think about your character. Would he have been a prince or a farmer? A rebel or a loyal guard of Antonius Bayle? Establish your character’s social status and work from there. Who were his parents? How was he raised? How was he treated? Finally, what events caused a change in his life to result in who he is as a person?

Having explained all your character’s personality, you can finally start writing his biography.

Back to the dreaded biography assignment of our past. Generally, all our biographies went something like this (fill in the blanks):

"_____was born in _____ on ______. He went to school at _____, and graduated on _____. He married _____ on _____ and had _____ children. He did _____ which helped us _____ He died on _____ in _____."

You can imagine, of course, how reading thirty-odd of these biographies would have put a teacher to sleep. This type of biography is fine when about someone we will never meet. But when it comes to a role-played character that is technically "alive," this kind of biography is essentially weak.

One of the major rules of biographies (and I will repeat this) is DO NOT REVEAL EVERYTHING. "But it’s a biography," some whine. "It’s supposed to tell their life story." There are two problems that can occur when revealing too much of your character :

1. No one wants to hear your character’s complete life story. (Those of you with children can relate.)
2. There are those who, after reading such a biography, will make one of two horrible assumptions: either their character will instinctively (and mysteriously) know your character’s entire life, or they will know their weaknesses. Both are spiteful acts in the role-playing world (and violators always seem to escape prosecution.)

Remember that a biography’s purpose is to give other players a general feel for your character, so that in case they meet him, they know what to expect of the encounter. It is not to directly inform other characters of said information, nor is it to bore the reader to death.

That aside, it’s time to explore your creativity. I’ve compiled a list of five common styles of biographies, along with a summary of each. Pick one of these, or create your own style. The point now is to be a creative informer!

THE SUMMARY: The "life-story" biography, except that it generally tells only certain aspects of the character’s life, and it doesn’t go into much detail if any at all.
THE LIFE-CHANGING EVENT: This is the most common style used. It is a story of the most life-altering experience of the character. During the story, a bit of past and a bit of personality shine through.
THE FLASHBACK: Similar to the life-changing experience, the flashback is a story set in the present, with events linking to memories of the past; tends to reveal the life-changing experience.
ROOTS: A story of the character’s childhood only; best used for characters whose childhood had the greatest life-impact, even if the life-changing event wasn’t during those years.
MONOLOGUE: Told from the character’s point of view. It can be serious, but tends to be comical, and generally reveals a great deal of personality.

Final tips:

-Make sure that the circumstances of the character link together sensibly.
-Don’t reveal everything to the reader.
-Try not to exaggerate circumstances, unless it’s your character talking.
-Don’t make a mold character - a character who has the same past as 100 other characters. Be creative.
-Experiment, and don’t settle for second best on your biography
