Zombie 		posted 06-11-2001 05:32 PM         
I did a lot of research a little toying around with a packet sniffer and discovered that you wouldn't really need all too much bandwidth or processing power to run a decent... say... 100 user server.

First of all, lets look at bandwidth. I played with a packet sniffer on one of the live Everquest servers, and found out a number of things. First, you send/receive about 5 to 10 packets a second to and from the Everquest server. The packets average out to be about 70 bytes per packet, so thats 350 to 700 bytes a second. Now, that's just for one user, so lets pretend you are hosting 100 users. That's 35,000 to 70,000 bytes a second, or 35k to 70k per second. That's about average for a cable modem/dsl user. So bandwidth isn't really a problem until you start pushing 200 users. Then you're going to start getting lag.

Now, onto processing power. RAM isn't too big of an issue because these packets aren't going to stay in memory for very long, because 90% of the packets sent from the server (i assume) update the locations of the NPCs. Most of the stuff that stays in memory is client side. But, what we have to look at is disk latency and processor speed. Graphics tend to be more processing intensive, data tends to be more disk intensive. Most of the packets sent from the server, from as much as I can tell, update the locations on the NPCs if they decide to move or what not. The rest of the packets come from spells, broadcasts, shouts, etc. etc. So most of the processing comes from keeping tabs on where the NPCs and the PCs are and sending that out ot all the players. But, that's more of a bandwidth issue, because your client computer is keeping track of all the locations of all the NPCs just as much as the server computer is, just the server is sending out the information to more people. The shouts and says and /oocs, etc. are not processor intensive at all, considering most of them are just little 50 byte packets of chat.

However, one thing you might want to look into is the disk latency. There's going to be a lot of data processing in terms of items and peoples characters, but that's not really a big issue either, because most hard drives have more than enough power to do the kind of work we're looking for. Unless you have one of those 1500 rpm hard drives that came out back in '94, I think you'd be set.

So what we're looking at is anywhere from a 500 mghz - 1 ghz processor to process all the information, and a DSL or Cable line for bandwidth, and you could support anywhere from 50 to 100 users. With a cable, it would probably be less during peak times because of shared bandwidth, but during non-peak times, you could quite possibly get more than a DSL user would get. Anyway, you could most definitely push 250 to 500 users with a T1 and a 1.3 ghz processor. And when we think about it, each zoneserver at Verant holds about 6 to 7 zones. The crowded zoneservers probably have somewhere around 250 to 500 people during peak times. (Of course the servers hosting Toxxulia Forest and Grobb probably aren't getting much action but that's besides the point). And Verants processors are no more revolutionary than the ones in our home computers, they just have the T1s to back them up with, and about 20 computers for each world server.

So, if Verant did decide to release the files, the hosting of the servers wouldn't really be a problem. Realistically, there's no way someone hosting a server off of a home computer is going to get more than 100 people on his server at the same time. The only servers that are going to have more than that are going to be super-dedicated servers that run off of peoples T1 lines from work, college, etc.
