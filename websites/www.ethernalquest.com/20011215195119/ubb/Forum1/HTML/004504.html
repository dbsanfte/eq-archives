Hmm, I didn't bother reading the rest of the thread, because I'd rather just cut to the chase. Here's my solution:

P must know S's sum because he knows his own product can only be the result of multiplying two prime numbers. Let's say the product he knows is 6, which is only produced by 1*6 (not valid because 1 is disallowed) and 2*3. So clearly x=2 and y=3, and S's sum is 2+3=5.

When S hears that P knows his sum, he knows that the only way P could know that would be by factoring down to primes x and y as stated above. He takes his sum and checks for which two prime numbers add up to it. In the case of 5, he can add 1+4 (not primes and not valid anyway because 1 is disallowed) or primes 2+3. Obviously with only one valid pair of primes, he now knows x=2 and y=3 and that P's product is 2*3=6.

Okay, I got it, I was wrong about them taunting each other, that really was part of the problem. My bad. Here's the proper solution:

Professor P says S cannot know the sum because he knows that, for each X*Y that multiply to produce his product, adding X+Y sums to a number that can be summed up in several different ways. Therefore he assumes that, just as there are too many ways to produce his product for him to know which is the correct one, there are also too many ways for professor S to figure out his sum.

S thinks the same thing, but vice-versa. His sum can be split up into several valid X+Y's that, when multiplied instead, always produce X*Y with multiple ways to factor.

However, when P understands that S has a number which splits into several valid X+Y's that produce multiple factorings from X*Y, he uses that knowledge as well as the knowledge of his own product to deduce X and Y.

S can then do something similar based on the knowledge that P had enough information to figure his sum out.

I'll show you how I got X and Y. Note that I'm not showing you all the trial and error that got me past the fail cases to the one that works...

P knows a product of 24. This can be arrived at by 3*8 or 4*6 or 2*12. For each X,Y, X+Y = a number which can be split up in several different valid ways (ie. 2+12 = 14 also = 3+11,4+10,5+9,6+8,7+7), so S cannot know his X,Y for sure, and therefore cannot know P's product either. He says so.

S knows a sum of 11. This can be arrived at by 2+9, 3+8, 4+7 and 5+6. For each X,Y, X*Y = a number that can be factored into three or more prime numbers (ie. 4*7 = 28 = 2*2*7, so X,Y could be 4,7 or 2,14), making it impossible for P to know his X,Y for sure and therefore impossible to know S's sum. He also says so.

Now, P figures out that only a few X,Y pairs will cause S to say such a thing. He looks for X,Y values in his set of possibilities that would sum up to make S say that. He runs through each of his own potential X,Y values, adding them up and then checking the sum to see if every possible split gives an X,Y pair that multiplies to produce a number which can be factored into 3 or more prime numbers. He finds that only 3,8 satisfies this (4,6 fails because 4+6=10 factors to 2*5 only, and 2,12 fails because 2+12=14 factors to 2*7 only). He now knows that X=3 and Y=8 (or vice versa of course) and that S's sum must be 11. He says so.

S deduces how P figured his number out and understands that one of his own X,Y pairs multiply to produce a number that would allow P to figure it out. He checks his own potential X,Y pairs to see which ones would multiply to produce a number which would allow him to figure this out, and only 3,8 passes (2,9 fails because 2*9=18 can split to 5+13, and P would know 5*13=65 wasn't in S's potential set because 65 only factors to 5*13, likewise for 4*7=28 split to 5,23, and again with 5*6 split to 7,23). S now knows X=3 and Y=8 and therefore knows P's product is 24. He says so.

End of story.
