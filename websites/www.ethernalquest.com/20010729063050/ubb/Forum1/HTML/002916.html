first pick out

motherboard, chip and RAM

find a combination you like, and pick these first. not all mobos work with all types of ram and all processor types and speeds, so take that into consideration.

next, get

case and power supply.

make sure the power supply is adequate for what the motherboard says it needs (extra doest hurt). You will have flaky intermittent bad things if you dont have enough power though.

next get peripheral cards

graphics, sound, network.

then get storage

hard drive, floppy disk drive, cd(r|rw), dvd, etc

then get input devices

keyboard, mouse, joystick

then get output devices

speakers, printer, monitor


of course, omit things your machine doesnt need (1 printer on the network suffices, only gaming machines have a use for a joystick, etc)

then put it all together and install the OS(es) of your choice.

I saw only 1 person mention fans. Don't skimp on cpu fans. A cheap $10 knockoff is a fast way to cpu failure. Get a peltier(?) fan . It's only $35 right now. Your local computer shows are great places to get good prices. Belkin cables are also a good value for your money.

Zombie 		posted 06-22-2001 07:22 AM            
Do you intend to build this dream system? Don't write off an Athlon system... save a ton of cash for performance that can meet/beat a 1.7GHz P4 in many situations. Unlock the CPU and do a little overclocking. 1GHz 266FSB Athlon can do around 1.2 easy with some decent cooling. Just make sure you've got good control when clipping the HSF to the CPU so you don't crush it. Might be worth the $35 to get a Duron to practice on. Or a shim if you've already got a keychain.
I'm setting up a system around that core right now (mirroring my drive). Cost me about $300 for an Epox 8KTA3, 1GHz-C Athlon, 256MB of Kingmax "PC150" SDR, and a Thermoengine. $550 for an intel mobo and the p4 chip? PLUS the cost of much more expensive RAM to get the performance it needs.

Here's a little advice that so many folks I know learned the hard way. Don't get the cheapest damn case on pricewatch. So many of these generic cases are flimsy optimized to be constructed as cheaply as possible (read: sharp edges, no features to help in construction and maintenance). It's a lot more fun to tinker with your computer if you don't have to undo 12 screws in the back, slide the entire U shell back the entire length of the case, and slice your wrists trying to screw in your drives. Drive rails, detachable drive cages, extra fan spaces do wonders.

in response to the guy that said the geforce MX was a good buy. yes, it's cheap, but it is a crippled card. MX stands for a 64 bit memory pipe instead of the standard 128 bit. if you are going to build a dream system the order of quality to look for in video cards is MX(bad), far as i know there's no abbreviation for standard memory, and DDR(best)

if it's a dream system you want get a video card built on DDR memory. it has the best throughput of all the models.
