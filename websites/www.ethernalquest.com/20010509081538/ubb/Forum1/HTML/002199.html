Work is just work. It's not something to take seriously. Many people have a parochial and small-minded view which posits that we must be "faithful" to our employer or that the employer owns us or we owe them.

It's like when companies look for a replacement for person X when person X doesn't know they're being fired. Or even when Person X is being fired and there is no replacement being sought. Is there some loyalty issue the company considers? Heck no -- it's money. If they don't like you, tough... if they want to save a few bucks, tough.

It's the same in reverse, however and that's what people fail to realize. All the job is -- is money. You show up at work, do X hours of actual work (like 32.5 at most places -- 40 hours minus 45 mins per lunch). They owe you pay (plus sick-time, plus vacation plus overtime (depending))...

You are their tool. They use you and pay you N dollars so they can earn N+ dollars.

Business is like a nightclub. If you get hooked up with someone less than ideal, move along. Businesses do it all the time.

Company loyalty never existed. There is no place that you are going to go which will assure you with any reasonable certainty that will never let you go. It's not just America; Japanese companies are letting a lot of people go nowadays, and Japan prided themselves on being lifetime employers.

It's a new world. Stuff moves fast. We can bury our head in the sand and just pray that we don't see the future steamrolling over us... or we can forget about fixing it, and just exploit it. Is that ethical? Of course -- like they always told us, when life gives you lemons, make lemonade. They didn't say, when life gives you something sour, make it better.. they said sell it off, exploit it... make everything you can from it.

So, to answer the original question, no -- PC Anywhere seems not to work. But playing from work -- provided there is no firewall or ports being blocked, should work.

Also, the ability or permission to play games from work does tend to increase productivity. EQ MIGHT be different, because it's not a quick game... Pinball, FreeCell, Solitaire, Duke Nukem 3D or Doom would be better choices.

Yes, playing at work will use your bosses "assets"... but he is already going to claim them as deductions and depreciate them anyway. The assets are paid for already. It's like buying a 500 car parking-lot for a company with 10 employees and then having a hissy-fit when other people park there. Nothing is being lost when the other people park there.

EQ doesn't eat up much bandwidth. If you've got a T1 to your office, you can handle a bunch of people and still be able to get web-pages, email, printer-output and official office stuff done without noticeable delay. If you have a DSL or Cable (or worse, a dial-up) then you're going to be straining it -- 2 EQ gamers would eat up all of the "extra" bandwidth on DSL... after that, other data transfers would be noticeably impacted).

Other games eat less bandwidth (Doom, Quake, DN3D...), so they'd be better suited for intra-nets and local networks.

Whether it harms the company or not, your boss can fire you for any reason they want. That's ok. We can always leave whenever we want. If our boss implements a new High-Heels On Tuesday policy, or starts wearing contact-lenses or cheats on his wife, then you can leave and there is nothing they can do. So the case is really quite balanced.

That's why you can't take it seriously. It's just work.

Here's a nice method I use to get away with pretty much anything I want through my company's corporate firewall. This method requires an SSH account to be set up on a machine outside your company's firewall. I simply open an SSH connection, and create a port forwarding rule, which forwards any traffic from a port on my local machine, over the SSH connection to the remote machine, and from there to any hostname and port I specify. From the firewall's point of view, all I'm doing is opening an SSH connection to the SSH server. The packets are encrypted, and the firewall is privvy to nothing I do over that connection. If your firewall doesn't have port 22 open, then you might have to run the SSH server on a port that is open. If you're stuck behind an HTTP proxy, then you're screwed.
