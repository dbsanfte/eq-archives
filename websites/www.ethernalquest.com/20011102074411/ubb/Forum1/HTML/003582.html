I tend to look at things logically, so it's difficult for me to imagine time as a plane that you can travel upon. Personally, I view time as a series of events. For the time being, I'll disregard that.

If it were somehow possible to travel through time, I don't believe that killing your own grandfather would cause any effect on _your_ particular time line, therefore, you would still be in tact.

To further explain, imagine that there are infinte time lines. Let's say that the time line that we currently exist in is time line A. Now, as we travel back through time to kill our grandfather, we end up in time line B. Here, I'll make the assumption that all time travelers are effectively attached to their original time line. Therefore, by killing our grandfathers in time line B, we have no effect on time line A - so we still exist since we aren't "owned" by the current time line. Of course, any travel through time would cause a branch in the time line, so you would never be able to eradicate yourself. For example, after killing your grandfater in time line B, if you want to travel to the future to see the effects, you would end up in time line C.

As I said though, I don't believe in this stuff, so it's all irrelevant.

mmm... temporal silliness...

Froggy and his multiple time-lines hit upon the generally accepted answer.

Time is just another dimension to existence, like length, height or width.
The problem for us is that we lack the direct sensory input for "time", we rely on approximations such as a clock.

Anyone who has any interest in this sort of thing should read Kurt Vonnegut's "Slaughterhouse-Five", the Tralfamadorian view of time is quite interesting.

This being said, there really are no paradoxes in the sense of the original post, due to time travel to the past being impossible, at least for humans in their current evolution. However, there is time travel into the future (time dilation) typically caused by immense gravitational distortions (E.G. a quantum singularity), even our own planet causes some minute change in the flow of time through its own mass.

Back to the multiple time lines...

There was a short story in Analog a while back about a guy who invented a time machine and went back to kill Hitler at his most evil point, but without having actually committed much evil, when he arrived, he was told to get in line. Due to the multiple time lines, everyone who killed Hitler only killed him for their own personal timeline. Ends up the guy who invented the machine opens up souvenir stands in time/space at every major event in history.

This idea that external relatity is dependent on the observer for its existence doesnt hold water.

The difference is subtle but absolute, mere alteration is NOT the same as creation.
