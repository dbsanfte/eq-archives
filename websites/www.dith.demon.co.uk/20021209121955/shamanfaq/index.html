  Character Creation and Development

What is the best race for a shaman to be? This is mostly a matter of personal taste. Each race has pros and cons and the "best" race depends on individual play style.  

Barbarian

Pros: Good starting wisdom. Good faction with all "light" cities. Small experience penalty (5%).

Cons: Poor night vision but this is quickly overcome by spells. "Cookie cutter" race due to commonality.

Iksar Pros: Natural Regen helps reduce downtime for all levels and helps when cannibalizing after level 24. Has 1 AC bonus capping at 40 AC at level 60 [How much is it at level 65 now?]. Start with 75 Forage (does not raise) and 100 Swimming (does raise). High starting wisdom. Iksar Plane of Hate armor is usually considered superior to the other races' rune etched armor.

Cons: Highest exp penalty shared with Troll (20%). Faction is poor with all cities other than Cabilis and requires considerable work to be non-KoS*. Unable to wear Plate armor including the fabled JBB. Due to being a medium race, Iksar do not have the Slam ability that Barbarians, Trolls and Ogres do.

Ogre Pros: Highest natural stamina and cannot be stunned from the front. Generally accepted to be the strongest race at Level 60+ when the regeneration of Iksar and Trolls do not matter as much. Cons: Faction work will be needed if you wish to go to most old-world cities. High exp penalty (15%). Largest race in EQ and has difficulty getting into many areas. Shrink at 19 alleviates this and is now usable outdoors.

Trolls

Pros: As with Iksar, Natural Regeneration will help with downtime. Worshipers of Innoruuk can get a quest necklace that casts Clinging Darkness (snare + damage over time).

Cons: Highest experience penalty shared with Iksar (20%). Same faction and size issues as Ogre.

Vah Shir Pros: Sneak and safe fall at level 1. Faction is comparable to Barbarian. No experience penalty reported (0%)

Cons: Food consumption double normal races. Starting city is on Luclin [Less of a con after PoP]. Due to their medium size, Vah Shir do not have the Slam ability that Barbarians, Trolls and Ogres do.

 

The faction penalty of being an evil race does not apply the same in Velious or Luclin. Velious factions are much easier to raise than Antonica and Kunark, and Luclin factions are mostly the same for all races. Everyone is treated the same in Shadowhaven, but Seru and Katta don't like evil races. All the large races (barbarians, trolls, ogres) can use Bear Form to reduce size. This works indoors and outdoors as well. For example, a Barbarian won't fit into the Elf-made doors in Kelethin and Firiona Vie, but a Barbarian in bear form will. With the release of Planes of Power (PoP) the importance of your faction within the old world diminishes even further and unless you really want to work on some old quests your faction will not matter much.

 

Where should I put my starting stats? Conventional wisdom states that you should put as many of your starting points into Wisdom, with any remainder going into one of several other areas. The Shaman's Crucible is anything but conventional, however. If you are brand new to the game and still learning everything, you can safely put them into Wisdom and Stamina and won't hurt yourself. The net result of the stats within the game is marginal and with work you can max out any stat you really care for. A slightly more aggressive mix would be to put as many as you like into Stamina and the remainder you can put anywhere, I suggest Wisdom. The reason for this suggestion is our mana regeneration is tied to our hit points and your Stamina determines how many hit points you have.  

What is the best way to spend AA points? There really isn't a "best" way to spend AA points. It really depends on how you want to develop your character. Common advice is to wait until level 60 before raising AA abilities. The most common path is:

3 Run Speed 3 Regeneration 3 Mental Clarity 1 Cannibalize This will get you the cannibalize ability, which converts 1924 hit points for about 1024 mana. It is also the beginning for getting to the class title quickly, since most prerequisites are met for all class abilities. Other common paths are to put points into stats that weren't raised when the character was created, making up deficiency in wisdom, stamina or agility. There really isn't a right way to go, this depends on personal preference and playstyle. Shamans who do a lot of high-level raids tend to get Mass Group Buff and Quick Buff. Shamans who solo and melee a lot like the Rabid Bear. Shamans who do a lot of single group encounters without clerics for healing might go with the heal related abilities. The following is suggested before getting other Archetype and Class AA abilities: 3 Quick Buff 3 Spellcasting Mastery 3 Spellcasting Reinforcement Quick Buff reduces the casting time for all buffs and buff-like spells by 50%. This includes Focus of Spirit, Celerity, Torpor and so on. Spellcasting Mastery reportedly reduces mana costs by 15% and it reduces fizzle rate. That adds up to a lot of mana saved over time. Spellcasting reinforcement adds 50% duration to any buff or buff-like spell, including Torpor, (Primal) Avatar, hastes, regeneration. It doesn't seem to affect group spells, at least on anyone other than the caster. This may have been fixed, though.

Skills, Statistics and Abilities

What good is wisdom over 200? Originally, there was not much use in getting wisdom over 200. At level 60, each point was worth 1 mana. Now, however, each point is worth 6 mana. This is a decent improvement, but it isn't as vital as getting more hit points, resists and AC might be (see below). Are any stat buffs worth casting? Which ones? "Worth" is subjective. If you feel that the improvements are worthwhile, then by all means cast the buffs you like. Here's what the stats "do":

Strength: Affects carrying capacity directly. Improves damage in some capacity. Strength does affect blacksmithing skill raises and successes to some degree. The effects of strength have been tested over and over by members of the Shamans' Crucible, quite extensively. The general consensus is there is no consensus. The benefits of strength buffs for the mana spent are still highly debatable. Dexterity: Affects chance to process (proc) weapon effects. Also affects chance to gain weapon skill raises and possibly some tradeskills. Agility: If agility is under 75, AC takes a huge hit. Otherwise, at high levels 5 agi == 1 ac. The benefit is debatable. Stamina: Very worthwhile to cast this after the mid-levels (mid 20's and 30's). At low levels the shaman's stamina buff doesn't yield many hit points per mana spent. However, this is a great buff to use at higher levels. Many shamans who raid high end encounters find that they have maxed their stamina anyway. At level 60, 1 point of stamina == 3 hit points for priest classes. [Anyone know what it is for Warriors?] Charisma: Useful for getting lower prices, up to 105 charisma. Rumored to affect the resist rate on debuffs like the walking sleep and malise lines but this is not 100% confirmed. Charisma has some effect on charm duration as well. It is also rumored to affect the Enchanter mesmerize spell line. For more information about stats and tradeskills, read this. The combination str/dex/hp buff Focus of Spirit is a great way to not only buff hit points well, but also to alleviate requests for str/dex. This is a level 60 spell, but fortunately there's lower level version, called Harnessing of Spirit, which shaman get at level 49. In general, HP, STA, AC, Regeneration and Haste buffs should be cast in that order. After those, other stat buffs can be cast. Cleric AC buffs are superior to shaman and enchanter haste is superior as well. Druids have group regeneration, which is usually preferable, though shamans can have group regrowth at level 56. The order is situational, but recommended so longer duration buffs are cast first. Also, if the classes mentioned are present, their buffs are generally preferred. How do I get started with Alchemy? Go to your trainer and spend as many training points as you'd like at level 25. :) Seriously, first you need to train in the skill so you have the basics. Then it is recommended that you put as many points into it as you can to max it with the trainer. The max is based on level, and like any tradeskill alchemy can't be trainer higher than 20. Then begin making potions. You'll need a medicine bag and the appropriate herbs. The least expensive way to raise alchemy is to work on potions that have a trivial level slightly above your current level. This will take a lot of time, but it will cost less in the long run. You can either sell your potions back to the vendor or try to sell them to players. However, most low-skill potions are not in demand. Some potions are high enough in demand that you can make some money with alchemy, though not much. Spirit of Wolf, Shrink, Damage Shield, Haste and the Gate potions are the best sellers. Make a bearskin pouch so you can create charged potions that take less inventory space. Charged potions can be either 5 or 10 charges. Keep in mind that Alchemy is capped based on level, unlike other tradeskills. Instead of the 250 maximum, alchemy is capped at 180 for level 60. This thread discusses the success rate formula for tradeskills. The formula is: ( 1.15 * Skill ) - ( Trivial - 70 ) Also check this thread for an Alchemy FAQ. How do I make a bearskin pouch? Combine a thick grizzly bear skin and a silk cord in a sewing kit, this trivials at Tailoring 68. Silk cord is made by combining three silk threads in a sewing kit. Silk threads are made by combining two spiderling silks in a sewing kit. Why is alchemy so expensive? Most tradeskills in EverQuest are expensive. Alchemy is expensive because it allows classes that don't have certain abilities (spirit of wolf and damage shield, for example) to have quick and easy access to those abilities. Unfortunately, that means it is a tedious, difficult and expensive skill for a shaman to raise. Some shamans have spent upwards of 40,000 plat getting their skill to max (180). Why is it important to keep my Defense skill maxed? How do I catch up? Keeping your Defense skill maxed is very important to a shaman. You can earn 5 points of defense skill per level. The skill for shaman is capped at 200, so at level 40 you will be done earning defense points. Defense skill points give us free, innate AC. As we know, good AC keeps us from getting hit, or losing lots of hitpoints when we are hit. To raise Defense, get attacked by blue or higher con monsters. The lowest blue are usually best due to lower damage. Also, pets are suitable for raising defense. Simply have another player with a pet (mage, necro, beastlord, shaman) duel and let the pet attack. Heal as required. If possible, have a damage shield up. Damage shields increase the frequency of the defense skill raise. I group all the time and I never get hit, what can I do to raise defense? Well, there are lots of ways to raise defense. You can try the melee soloing method described in this FAQ and raise it as you get exp. You can root the mob and stand close to take hits while you are getting exp in a group. Others also suggest asking a necromancer or enchanter to let you duel their pet in an arena and using that method to gain skill points. The pet must be blue and this method requires 2 people to be standing in the arena rather than out getting exp, but is the safest. What weapon skills should I keep up? All of them! Our epic is a piercing weapon, so I advise keeping at least piercing at a functional level. I would recommend keeping at least piercing and 2 hand blunt skills up as you level. Piercing so you can still melee and use a shield and 2 hand blunt so you can still get in some good hits.

Playstyle techniques

What is "slow" and why should I do it? The "slow" or "walking sleep" series of spells raises the delay of the target's attacks, slowing down the rate in which it deals damage. Shamans actually have the best attack slows available, up to 75% at level 60 for Turgur's Insects. These spells are vital for reducing damage from certain mobs. Slows are what makes a shaman so powerful in a group, though many people may not realize that. Slowing mobs will allow a single shaman, group or several groups to kill something they may not be able to kill. It is the backbone of all major shaman solo kills. A shaman can melee toe-to-toe some mobs with a slow that would otherwise rip them apart very quickly. Please note, however, the lower level slows until Tagar's are fairly low powered. They can be used, but the effect won't be noticed much. Okay, I know why I should slow, but everything seems to resist, these spells use a lot of mana! There isn't much you can do to reduce the resist rates. The malise line of spells can help reduce the resist rate of a mob, but the malise spells are on the same resist table as the slows. Only Malo at level 60 is unresistable. Grouping with an enchanter might help, their Tash series reduces magic resistance easily. Other than that, recasting the slow until it sticks is about all you can do. Also, there is a disease based slow at level 54, Plague of Insects. It is only 25%, but it is better than nothing on those mobs that are 100% MR. Keep in mind, however, that resisted slows generate a large amount of aggro, even more than when the slow actually lands. Also keep in mind that some mobs can't be slowed at all being 100% immune to magic or to slow itself. Read this thread for a discussion on which mobs can't be slowed.

What is "cannidancing" and how do I do it? Basically, it is "catching the meditation tick." Every six seconds is one tick. Each tick is when mana and hit points regenerate, among other things. Cannidancing is a method of cannibalizing so one can also meditate. The method is as follows:

Sit down. Watch mana bar carefully to see when it updates. Since the HP and Mana bars update at the same time, watch the HP bar or HP display in the inventory screen if the mana bar is hard to read. Stand, cast cannibalize. Wait for cannibalize to finish casting, then sit down immediately. Repeat the cycle. It may seem silly, but mana regeneration is actually much faster while "cannidancing" than when meditating or just casting cannibalize. At higher levels when fighting mobs that summon, sitting down can be dangerous. Sitting generates some amount of aggro for certain mobs, and mobs do max damage to sitting targets. So if a shaman is cannidancing near a summoned mob, they might get summoned when they sit down and hit for max damage. Most shamans stop cannidancing entirely when they receive Cannibalize IV. The recast delay is too long to effectively catch the "med tick." Here is a link to Arkand's original guide to cannidancing. Some shamans set up a hotkey for cannidancing to make it easier to do, especially when playing a shaman with the left hand (i.e. powerleveling or raiding). The following is a sample hotkey. Adjust the pause time depending on your connection. The recast for cannibalize (13) is 1.3 seconds; one tick is 6 seconds. /sit off /cast 5 /pause 24 /sit I want to solo with my shaman instead of grouping. How can I solo effectively? That depends greatly on your playstyle. There are basically two methods of soloing with a shaman. Rotting. Meleeing. "Rotting" is a method using the shaman's awesome arsenal of Damage over Time (DoT) and the Root spell. Generally shamans that equip themselves in wisdom gear (see below) use this method. It is fairly effective and efficient, but also quite slow. A typical fight is very simple. First, cast a root spell on the target. Next, load up as many disease and poison DoTs as possible, starting with the highest-level spells first. Then cannidance/meditate while the creature dies slowly (and painfully!). Reapply root as necessary. Flash of light can be used as well for a "poor man's fear" to buy time to root. Meleeing is a method using the shaman's "slow" spell line, pet and weapon. This method is also quite simple, though more dangerous than rotting. First, summon a pet and buff with regeneration and haste. Buff yourself with AC, HP, Stamina, haste, regeneration, as desired. When you are ready to fight, cast the best "slow" spell available (turgur's, togor's, etc insects) at the target. If there is time, cast DoTs on the target, such as poison. When the mob is in melee range, attack with the pet. Keep aggro on yourself so your pet doesn't die. If you have a right click damage item such as the Spear of Fate or Jaundiced Bone Bracer, use it as appropriate. Heal yourself as necessary and cannibalize for more mana. Many shamans that use this method go solely for AC and HP equipment, so they live longer in these types of fights. Both methods are effective for soloing, either for items or experience. Whichever is used depends on personal preference, though lately most shamans tend to mix the two, mostly using the meleeing method. For more information on soloing, check this thread. Equipment and Gear Fungi Tunic, shaman or monk item? Don't be so greedy to think that the Fungus Covered Scaled Tunic is a shaman-only item. It is actually incredibly useful and powerful for anyone that can wear it. It is most beneficial for Monks and Shamans, though. Monks use it as one of the highest AC chest items they can get without a raid-sized group and to regenerate while feigned. Shamans use it to assist with cannibalizing; the HP regeneration helps mana recovery immensely because the shaman doesn't have to wait as long for their hit points to regen as well. Wow, the Fungi Tunic sounds like a really great item! I seen it sells for a lot of plat though. What level do I have to be to camp it? Where does it drop? The Fungi Tunic drops from the Myconid Spore King in Sebilis. It is his rare drop. I would suggest a shaman be level 55 before trying to camp the King (with a strong group). The Myconid Spore King himself is hard to slow, consider using Plague of Insects. I am a Vah Shir shaman; I can't wear the Fungi Tunic. What can I do? Have no fear, in the Fungus Grove zone; by doing the Caller Quests you can get an identical item called the Fungus Patch Vest. It is wearable by Vah Shir, and shaman of all other races as well. It is only wearable by shaman level 51 and over, however. Hmm, I am lower level. I can't afford the Fungi tunic or the Fungus Patch Vest. What other items have regeneration effects? Another nice regen tunic is the Ceremonial Iksar Chestplate. It does not have the same rate of regeneration as the Fungi Tunic/Patch Vest (15hp/tick vs. 5 hp/tick) but it is wearable by all shaman at all levels and is sold for less than 10k on most all servers. It can also be camped by a single group in Trakanon's Teeth in the high 40's/ low 50's. There are other regeneration items out there, but not many. Items such as the Fungus Covered Great Staff are of more limited usefulness/availability. Which stats are most important for choosing equipment? (Wis, AC, HP, etc) This is a heated topic that varies from shaman to shaman. Each "type" of equipment is important to some degree, and everyone has their personal preference. Generally speaking, a good mix works well. Here's some information for each stat to help you decide how to equip your shaman. Wisdom: A shaman's mana is based on their wisdom. The higher the better for a larger mana pool. However, total mana is not always the most important thing for a class that can convert their hit points into mana. 200 wisdom is the soft limit; points after 200 don't change the mana pool as much as before 200. Here is the formula for mana per wisdom: ( wis below 200 * level / 5 ) + ( wis above 200 * level / 10 ) So the amount of mana per point of wisdom scales a lot while leveling. at level 10, 200 wisdom is: 400 mana at level 10 wisdom below 200 is worth: 2 mana per 1 wis at level 10 wisdom above 200 is worth: 1 mana per 1 wis at level 60, 200 wisdom is: 2400 at level 60 wisdom below 200 is worth: 12 mana per 1 wis at level 60 wisdom above 200 is worth: 6 mana per 1 wis Each point of wisdom reduces your fizzle rate by 1%, you can never have a fizzle rate higher than 95% or lower than 5%. Stamina: Hit points are influenced by the amount of stamina a character has. The higher the stamina, the more hit points that character will have. Shamans have stamina buffs to help raise this until the stat is capped. At level 60, a shaman can buff stamina by 50, so 205 is a soft limit. Hit Points: Hit points are one of the most important stats to a shaman. The more hit points a shaman has, the more they can cannibalize. The more they can cannibalize, the more mana they can regenerate quickly. Also, when a shaman has the cannibalize AA ability; having a high amount of hit points is a tremendous help, so the shaman doesn't kill themselves instantly. AC: Armor Class (AC) determines how often a character will get hit in melee and how much damage they will take per hit. AC is important for shamans because it affects our pool of hit points and therefore mana (via cannibalize). The less damage taken, the more hit points we have for cannibalizing. Resists: Resists are mostly important at high levels or on PvP servers. High-level mobs have AE damage based on one of the resist types and having high resists helps alleviate damage and negative effects. Recent changes to NPC casters made them more intelligent about which spells they cast, which can be very hazardous to those with low resists. PvP server players have high resists to avoid other player spell casters. Equipment choices are based on personal preference over which of the above is most important to the individual. At the high end, it doesn't really matter. Shamans doing epic raids with their guilds are going to get equipment that has high stats for wisdom, hp, ac and resists. At lower levels, the choices can be a bit more difficult. The rule of thumb is to watch how you play. If you find yourself running out of hit points before mana, then more HP and AC items might help instead of all the wisdom items. If you find yourself running out of mana before hit points, then more wisdom and mana gear might be in order. In the high end game, effects on items become just as an important consideration as the stats on those items. Flowing thought or +15 wisdom? Fungal Regeneration or +40 hp? Again, this all depends on playstyle and personal preference. The thing to keep in mind that for a spell casting class, mana regeneration is of highest importance. Whether mana regeneration is achieved with a larger mana pool, a faster regeneration rate (flowing thought), more hit points and cannibalization, or regeneration of those hit points, it depends on the individual. Okay, so what equipment should I use at level ##? That is best left up to you. Follow the above guidelines and you should be fine. I want to use some AC gear, but I've heard about AC "caps", what are those? Though nothing have been proven, general theory states that ac provides the most noticeable changes when it breaks a 100's marks (900, 1000, 1100). Example, you would notice a far greater difference going from 890 ac to 900 ac than you would going from 1010 to 1020. Some people believe there is also a noticeable change at each 50's mark. A generally accepted theory is that after 1250 ac, we see relatively little improvement for each point of ac we increase. I heard the Jaundiced Bone Bracer (JBB) is a powerful item, how do I get one? The Jaundiced Bone Bracer is a part of the shaman Kunark plate set of armor. All Jaundiced Bone armor drops in Karnor’s Castle and Sebilis. The Jaundiced Bone Bracer itself is AC 14 with the effect Winters Roar. That is a right clickable direct damage spell. It has an 8 second cast time and does 263dmg. It is not useable until a shaman is level 45. It drops in Sebilis in the "Crypt" area. This is past a locked door that only a rogue can open, with picklocks. Specifically it drops off Arch Duke Iatol, it seems to be his rare drop. I would suggest a shaman be level 54 or so before attempting to do the crypt, with a solid group of that level. Though you could get in there sooner with higher level groupmates. You can also find the JBB for sale rarely on most servers. Prices seem to range from 10,000-25,000 platinum. Why doesn't Verant make a Jaundiced Bone Bracer for Iksar shamans? Iksar are not allowed to wear plate armor due to having a higher AC than any other race. Jaundiced Bone armor is the shaman's "plate" from Kunark, so the Bracer is of course included. The Iksar make up for the deficiency by having a high base AC, as well as having superior Planes armor from Plane of Hate.

Class Issues How should shamans be balanced? This is a very broad question. There are probably more answers than there are shamans. Why don't these two spells stack? Several spells don't stack for various reasons. Most common is the two spells share the same effect. Initially, torpor couldn't be cast on someone that was slowed, because of the slow component of torpor. The resist buff spells (endure cold, etc) used to overwrite the malise spell line. There's many more examples, but many of these common issues have been fixed.

Spells What's the deal with Torpor and Malo spell drops? Both of these spells are incredibly powerful. Malo, being an unresistable debuff, makes it easier to land other spells, such as slow, on very difficult mobs. Torpor gives the shaman a very potent heal, despite the ill effects - snare and attack slow. These two spells can be the difference that allows an encounter to be beaten. Malo and Torpor are both rarely dropped by a number of level 60+ mobs in Kunark. These mobs are emperor (Sebilis), Tolapumj (Seb), Protector (Seb), Trakanon (Seb), Gorenaire (DL), Sev (EJ), Talendor (SF), Prince (Chardok), Queen (Chardok), Overking (Chardok), guardian wurms (SF), and the dragons in Veeshan's Peak. Sontalak in Western Wastes can drop them as well, but they are very rare. Where do I get my high level spells? I can't find them anywhere. Depends on the spell. Most of them are dropped by various mobs. Some are available for sale from vendors; Firiona Vie and Overthere. A very few are available by turning in four more commonly dropped spells to an NPC in Firiona Vie or Overthere. Check this link for more information.
