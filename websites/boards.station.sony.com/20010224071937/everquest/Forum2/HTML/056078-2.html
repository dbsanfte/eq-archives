I have to agree with this thread.

One solution (and its too late probably) is to cap damage at a certain level. Like, say at level 35 a rogue shouldnt be able to do over X damage per attack or tick, or not over Y when backstabbing as fast as possible. So, using internal formulas, if the character's damage meets or exceeds the value, it caps. You could even cap hit points and other attributes by level, and you could have a buffed and an unbuffed cap. That way, if you could twink your rogue or whatever enough, a lot of items would be wasted.

But its too late to do this on EQ.

On a mud I used to code on and run, damage might be capped to your level. A level 50 warrior could not do more than 50 points of damage in a hit, and that value was factored in with his highest attack rate in mind, assuming he hit every time. THEN, a certain percentage of that max damage was guaranteed, because of the warrior's skill with weapons. He would have (for example) an innate +25 damage. So, even with an off-the rack sword with no magic on it, and with minimal or no magic items, he might do 30 points of damage. So, a warrior with off the shelf equipment was a force to be feared, and the variety in weapons in the game allowed a warrior to easily make up the difference in the damage he could do between the 30 base and 50 max in any way possible. So most warriors would float at 40-50 damage. Even buffs from other classes couldnt let him exceed 50, and the game was balanced vs the number 50 as far as monsters are concerned. That way, it encouraged variety in weapon choice. Clerics were capped at 35 on melee damage for the intervals they were hitting at, and they got 20 of it for free, at level 50, say. And when spell effects of clerics were considered, they had magic effect caps also, and these were hand tailored to work with their melee caps. Etc etc.

It wasnt easy, but you *could* have many combinations of groups, even an mostly-melee group could do well.

But that has to be done at the design level up front.

The only solution I see for EQ has to be done by players. a) voice your complaints in a rational manner and b) dont play over level 50.

Yes, many of up paid money for the expansions. Maybe that is our loss, though Kunark can be enjoyed from 1-50. But, I have found much pleasure in playing at the lower levels, and all the fun of getting groups of practically ANY combination of classes works in the lower levels, especially pre-35. Anytime I find myself frustrated by lack of balance or mudflation, I retreat to a low level character with a group of friends and work up again and have fun with it.

So twink all you like, or dont, but enjoy the lower levels. If you cant resist twinking yourself, have everyone start their seconds (or thirds or 4ths or 16th) on another server where nobody knows anyone else.

Or try team pvp for a change from level 1 if you are up for a real thrill.

Either way, you CAN get that fun and friendship going someway or another. That doesnt mean you shouldnt insist that Verant fix the problem, but it does mean you can have fun and avoid being mad.

Works for me.

So what happens? The average player's level increases... some get bored and start others... but then over time, they get higher as well. I'd be willing to bet the average player's highest level character is well over 40 now, including the casual gamers. I'm considered casual by "uber" guilds, and I have 3 characters over 40, and 2 more in their late 30s. Verant is forced to add more content for higher level players and don't add any mid game content, simply because there are so many more high level players. As time goes on, it only gets worse.

Wanted to add a big comment here.

Are you familiar with the parable of the three blind men and the elephant? It's an excellent life lesson. Essentially three blind men are presented with an elephant and asked to describe it. They all were examining the same elephant. One man found the elephant's trunk, and said "elephants are like snakes!". The next man found the elephant's leg and said "no no no, elephants are like trees!". The third one found the tusk and said "no no elephants are like spears!"

My point is that I don't want us to latch on to specifics and miss the bigger picture. Perhaps the easiest way to label the problem without the "mobflation" label are these two simple facts with very far reaching consequences:

1) Ever since Kunark, Verant has made it a point to further "diversify" the classes (ie, make the specialists better at their specialties)

and

2) Then they design encounters based on whether or not the specialists can pull it off, and whether or not everybody else can simply find something to do.

The easiest way to describe this is "mobflation", but specifically it's a double-standard in the mob/dungeon design process, over which we as players have really very little input on.
