I don't think there are major problems with just being oneself, thou needs not speak thusly.

Roleplaying means keeping out of game chatter and game mechanics chatter to a minimum. It means following the naming rules.

Also, I think you need to stay in character in terms of some things that you do. By taking a particular role you are making a bargain to act within that role. Druids and rangers do not spend their time hunting peaceful bears--there are limits, that won't stop them from hunting lions in karana if they go aggro on you.

Good characters do not group with evil ones, generally speaking. When I have grouped with an evil character (I'm a 1/2elf druid btw), I have generally expressed my displeasure through a variety of jokes and minor taunts and have done so only when in a large group and for a purpose. I have turned down evil races for groupings--which has been painful because the trolls and ogres are almost all roleplayed to the hilt (why else would you play one). As a half-elf I do not group with dark elves--period. In fact, I taunt them at ever opportunity and I've had some great interactions with them.

There are also limits imposed by the game system--if we have to refer to game elements, we have to do so and it seems pointless to me to try to fake them, particularly in the heat of battle. I'm sure in the "real world" of Norrath, people wouldn't scream "TRAIN TO ZONE!" but given the need to warn people, I don't think it's poor taste to do so. I'll also say OOM if I see someone running off to pull something, because the guy's probably going to have something coming before I can think and compose "hark, my magical energies are fading, I must rest and seek the power of the earth to replenish myself." OTOH if we're about to leave a city, I might put it that way. I do try to keep most of my out of character speech to tells or group tho (obviously TRAIN! is an exception). And I do extensively chat in the OOC channel.

I tend to roleplay the most in downtime, while I'm in cities, and when I meet people for the first time.

It's about getting into the environment of the game. Frankly, I've played videogames for over 20 years at this point. EQ isn't a very good one. If you want to bash and powerlevel, Diablo 2 is far superior. If you want to frag, UT or Q3 has way better gameplay, not to mention the hamburger flying thru the air. The point of EQ is to get into the world, into your character, and into an alternative state of mind--I'm not saying one can't play it another way, but I will say there are better games if you're looking for something else.

Raychel, human druid, is much quieter, tends to keep her opinions to herself, but is very principled and will leave a group almost immediately if they propose doing something she disapproves of (like killing NPCs).

The spelling error in the title reminded me on how we used to call pen and paper RPGs that had too many die rolls and table "roll-playing games" instead of "role-playing games".

And EQ most definitely is one of these roll-playing games in which the game mechanics don't reward you for playing a role. Dwarven paladins are in groups with dark elf necromancers and troll shadowknight. Good aligned races kill NPC dwarves in Butcherblock for experience. Druids fear-kite animals. Doing something "evil" with your evil race character will get you banned as fast as with a good race character.

The only roleplaying thing in the game mechanics are factions, and those are often not that important. You don't get more or less experience points from killing a NPC of an opposing group. So, if you want to roleplay, go ahead, but don't expect any reward from the game or much support from the other players in the game.
