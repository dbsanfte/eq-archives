Having some experience working on an ORPG, which could've grown to MMORPG status but got stuck at MORG, had the producing company not bailed out in the last phase, I can't help but agree with the most of original post.

Considering the producing company -did- bail out, we were left with relatively high debts, and an unfunded and unfinished product. Instead of giving up (as so many companies have in the past), we looked for alternatives, and found a place to host the game. Sadly enough, most of the team decided to resign from the project, and to work for -money- instead of the -possibility- of monetary compensation for time. Which is only natural.

We managed to finish the game and release it, and run it without too much problems. That was, until we had to create bugfixes and patches. Lacking programmers, there was only one option left to us. It's the same option we're still using after two and a half years, with moderate success. The entire team; GMs, DEV, Support, you name it... All dedicated volunteers.

The money which is generated with the game (subscription fee of approximately $10/month), almost all goes into bandwidth and periodic maintenance. Anything that's left is saved for emergencies.

So, games can be run for a relatively low cost. The people involved are enormously motivated and passionate, often pouring more time into game related issues than into their jobs. You'll find extremely talented DBA's, sysadmins, engineers, artists, (network-)programmers and testers who are willing to help out, and who continue to make such a game a success. All because they care for the game.

However, because there's no "leftover" money, it's increasingly hard to compete with the "eye candy" games, where there's funding in abundance, where one nifty 3D engine after another is created, and where the most fantastic game concepts are thought up.

People seem to forget that a lot of money also goes into upgrading a game, or into creating a new game.. and it's that aspect that's lacking with companies which manage to release and host a game despite funding, and with companies which fail to release a game alltogether. Not because they don't want to, but simply because the funds run out for whatever reason.

I personally think that the price to play MMORPG’s should be at least $15 per month and maybe even $20. However, there is a catch. I think that in order to secure this industry there has to be a way to ensure that people can play multiple MMORPG’s and not pay for each one. That would be too much money to expect people to pay.

I think the solution to many of the problems in this specific market would be to have some third party company step up and start a universal charge system for MMORPG’s. Example: I enjoy playing Everquest but would like to log into AC, or UO just to check things out every once in a while. Instead of playing for all three services I would log into my MMORPG account and just play whatever one I felt in the mood for. At the end of the month I would only see one charge on my account regardless of what game I was playing. Lets say my monthly payments to this service was $22 per month. The service would take around $2 for itself and then split the remaining amount based on the percent played to the MMORPG’s. So if I played EQ 70% of the time and AC 30% of the time EQ would get $14 and UO would get $6.

A service of this nature would help ensure that the online game industry remained competitive because customers would be willing to play multiple games. If customers are willing to by and play more games then the developers can get more investors. They would not have to be as concerned about one game monopolizing the entire industry because players would be able to flip back and forth and play what they are in the mood for. Another nice thing is that the company that provided the service would have very little overhead and has a great possibility for profit.
