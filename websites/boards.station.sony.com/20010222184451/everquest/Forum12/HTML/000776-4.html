>>1) Let Bard Songs/group spells affect our pets...

>affect pets. It would restrict the power of

So, a group that's four melees, a bard, and a cleric has an acceptable balance of bard power when he sings, say, haste, but a group that's three melees, one mage plus pet, and a cleric is unbalanced? There's still only four toons benefiting from the spell. Four close-combat types are hasted and doing increased damage. (And, as we know, the pet does less damage than the high-level melee damagers anyway; the four-melee scenario gets more of a boost than does the second.) The mage is cowering in the background, whatever his buffs. There's no additional power added here because the mage is "buffed" with a buff that is orthogonal to his role.

>Pets are balanced internally with each class that gets them.

So mage + pet = ranger (say), but it's balanced to allow rangers to be boosted by group spells, but not (mage + pet)? That just makes no sense. If the classes are balanced without the buff, then allowing only one of two classes to be buffed would have to be unbalanced.

I can see people thinking "oh, no, a druid could be in a group with five mages, and he'd be buffing ten people at once! That's way too powerful!". But that argument neglects the fact that the buffs in question help only melees (which is to say just the pets) or casters (which is to say just the mage). A mage-n-pet counts as one character, not two.

Group buff power doesn't really scale as the number of figures, but rather only as the number of figures that _effectively use the buff_. For instance, group damage shields are generally not all that effective, because you've only got one or two tanks that will be getting hit. They don't rate a mana cost for all six people, but at best about the two that you'd put individual shields on. Similarly, group regen, or HP buffs, really only help the melee classes. The int casters get little benefit from the HP anyway. For the group buffs that spring to mind, only half the mage-pet duo will be gaining any real benefit, and thus group-buffing a mage-pet doesn't add twice the power to the buff, but only the usual linear increase.

CotH:

>There is a reason that Call of the Hero doesn’t
>summon people across zone lines already. As the
>spell exists right now it allows people to
>bypass content that they were never intended to
>bypass, making some encounters far easier than
>intended.

In what way? You can't Call anyone to a place the mage hasn't already reached, "encountering" all the "content" on the way there. And you know the mage isn't getting there by himself; he's got a whole group or two fighting him down there.

CotH is used for exactly two things as far as I know.

First, it's occasionally used for "decoy" pulling, where a sacrificial tank takes a lot of critters out of the way, and then a second puller pulls the real target. Hopefully you snatch the decoy back before he dies. CotH is basically just a substitute here for several other pulling methods that accomplish exactly the same thing (including the original sacrifice decoy where the puller _intends_ to die, counting on a easy xp res later. We are talking about level 55 here, after all, and serious raids, not pickup groups). CotH adds no power that wasn't already present. It just allows mages to partake of one aspect of usefulness to a group.

Second, CotH is used as a pure utility spell. Rather than having everyone sit around at a dungeon entrance, bored to tears, while the last party members shows up, the first five can fight their way in, and Call the latecomer afterward. Similarly, if a group member has to leave (for RL reasons), you can pick up another waiting person in the zone and add them to the group without much pointless tromping back and forth. All CotH does here is save people a little time. If they fought their way past the yard trash to the X Room, they can fight their way out and back again. CotH here is just a downtime reducer, not a power increase or content bypasser.

So what does Verant fear so much about this spell? I really don't see it.
