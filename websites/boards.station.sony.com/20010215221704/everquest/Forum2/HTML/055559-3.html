Since you like analogies and examples:

The law is steal and have your hand cut off. If I have morals, or better yet, ethics, I may decide that it is wrong for me to steal, regardless of the punishment. So I won't steal. A moralistic person, having morals, will not steal either, regardless of the punishment. A practical person, will not steal, morals or not, becuase he doesn't want his hand cut off.

Obeserving a man stealing. A practical person will not report it, not wanting to get involved.

A moral or ethical person may, or may not be moved to report it, depending on his apprehension of the situation.. is he stealing food for his family? or is he stealing money to enrich himself? is he a professional, or just motivated by desperation? was he violent, and does he pose a grave danger to the neighborhood? One is obliged to think.

A moralistic person would report him regardless. Theft is theft is theft, and that's the law and he knew the law he should have expected the consequences. Without laws everyone would steal. (incorrect, but frequently posited) If he didn't want to risk the consequences he should not have stolen.

All of these positions have an internal logic. Two don't require any thought. And those two are thus the two most common positions held.

Of course opinions posited have the presumtion of validity on the part of the person presenting them. That is not moralism.
