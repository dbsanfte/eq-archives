I have table-top role played for over ten years. I’ve Live-Action role-played for just about as long, long before there were finalized “rules” available for purchase. I’ve mudded and pbm’d as well. This doesn’t mean much in the grand scheme of EQ though, since it’s my observation that EverQuest is actually an ADVENTURE GAME set in a fantasy setting. Aye, true, the ability to role-play is there for the taking, and I do believe more of Norrath’s citizenry should take advantage of it. But I do not believe the issue is about role-playing verses… whatever else there is to oppose it (powergaming perhaps?).

I do believe that it is about community, and about consequence. In today’s society, consequences exist for those who would take actions against the “societal norm.” While it is an unfortunate truth that many of society’s most brilliant and innovative are somewhat ostracized by the rest of the crowd, that minority suffers because of an age-old ingrained survival mechanism: Different will often get you killed. Blend in, be part of the herd, don’t rock the boat, and you will be able to continue quietly with your day-to-day life. Here is how it relates to the rudeness so prevalent in EQ, and on other server-based games; and here’s how it relates to role-playing versus power-gaming in general.

By nature, most role-players are a flamboyant lot, a group that is inherently “different” in that they stand out. Combine this with a group of persons who are exploitive of a system that has little in the way of consequences, and you get a pretty nasty standoff. What happens is this: in general society, poor behavior and antisocial activities are punished. Online, role-playing types are free to role-play without the fetters of a physical body that can't actually cast magic, or shoot a bow, or a thousand other things. They free themselves up. However,online, where the consequences for such actions are greatly lessened (hence the role-players can be "different"), you also see more rudeness and general self-centered behavior emerge. Why bother playing nice, when there is no “punishment” for getting ahead any which way you can? After all, by setting up a “game” situation where the object IS to “win,” getting ahead by any means necessary is almost par for the course. Add to this the “anonymity” of the internet where the careful rules of society do not apply (the internet has it’s own rules, based more on general consensus of the vocal minority) and what happens is more rudeness by more people who can basically get away with it because there is no way to “punish” them. Toss in role players, who inherently play by very structured rules, speech, and mannerisms, and you end up with a pretty rough situation all around. One group sees the EverQuest as an elaborately beautiful set in which maintaining a persona can be easily attained via the computer avatar—and the other group sees it as a place where consequences for actions are on a completely different strata. (There is a median group, a group that I call “casual gamers,” but typically no one honestly has a severe problem with them.)

What needs to happen then, is that the silent majority needs to join the vocal minority. If you are a person who enjoys role-playing or powergaming, then by all means, do so—and get others to do it with you. If you feel that politeness and propriety are important to you, then maintain that level of behavior for yourself to encourage it in others. Don’t just up and leave because you meet the vocal and rude (or just plain different) majority.

The other thing that needs to happen is a system of actions and consequences needs to form, via the PLAYERS. No matter how many “play nice policies” Verant tosses out, such things are nearly useless until the majority of PLAYERS make antisocial and exploitive actions “punishable” by a set of consequences.

Aye, this is just one person’s opinion and a few random observations. Rather than curse one another out, or toss up my hands and leave at the antics of the role-players or the tactics of the powergamers, I intend to stick it out. I believe that by maintaining a presence on Povar server people who want to make a change can actually do so. The trick of course, is knowing what you want and persistently trying to achieve it.
