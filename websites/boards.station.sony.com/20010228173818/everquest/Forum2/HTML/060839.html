The Cleric’s Testament

Father Absor, Reverend Brad and The Holy Mother Verant, we – your humblest servants of Norrath - do submit a petition of our woes that you might look upon us with favor when considering the Blessed “Vision” of EverQuest. It is not our intent to vilify, nor chastise, but merely to put forth in a rational and logical manner grievances that we feel must be addressed. Please forgive this humble servant if my words offend or inspire wrath.

To begin, let me speak briefly of the history of the Cleric as commonly accepted by a majority of the Role-Playing Community. In summary, it can be said that while Clerics come in all shapes and sizes, there are only two “schools” of Clerics: Warrior-Priests and Scholar-Doctors.

Scholar-Doctors are found in Temples across fantasy lands poring over musty tomes and religious parchments while debating the philosophy of life and spirituality – and performing the occasional healing act or medical remedy for those in need. Theirs is a sheltered life, cloistered away from the troubles of daily life in the lands. Their job is important, for every society needs someone to ponder the “Wherefore” and “How” of life, but their job does not place them in harm’s way.

Warrior-Priests feel the urge to roam the land spreading the word of their Deity through actions, not study. Commonly seen smiting heathen foes in battle and healing those in need. These Warrior-Priests believe that their good deeds and actions will act as a shining beacon to the less holy in times of need and darkness. Warrior-Priests are the “footsoldiers” of the Clerical communities - hardy souls willing to give their own lives to benefit others while spreading word of their Deity.

The warrior-priest idea is the traditional RPG Cleric. This is what it means to be relied upon by the whole church as a messenger / representative to the world. One MUST be able to defend others, sacrifice oneself and act as a stalwart defense against evil. A Cleric cannot spend his/her life in prayer and meditation, let alone sit behind a rock during combat - and gain respect among potential converts! A Cleric MUST be in the thick of battle!

Our troubles in Norrath stem from what appears to be an unclear idea of what it truly means to be a Cleric. If a Cleric wants to be a Scholar-Doctor, let them, but don’t expect them to pay $10 month for the privilege of sitting in a castle doing nothing while the rest of Norrath’s vibrant community revolves around them in heroic battles and daring raids.

Too often the wandering Warrior-Priests of Norrath find themselves acting like “mobile” Scholar-Doctors - sidelined during major conflicts to meditate for divine power, instead of acting as a Warrior-Priest, fighting amongst our peers to smite the foes with modest blows of our maces, Holy Words of Power and the ubiquitous healing.

Recent petitions to our Holy Mother Verant, Reverend Brad and Father Absor seem to have fallen on deaf – or at least misunderstanding ears. We do not seek to lessen the gifts you have blessed to our brethren the Shaman and Druid – but to enhance our viability under the commonly accepted auspices of what it is to be a “Cleric Warrior-Priest”: Holy, Dependable and Bane of the Undead.
Holiness:

Regardless of what “real-life” beliefs one may have – being “Holy” is an integral part of becoming one with the idea of what it means to be a Cleric. EQ lacks any sort of direction or implementation in what this means other than the changes your Deity selection makes among various factions. Even so, this is invisible to the player as it is contained only in the coding of the software. We need something in-game that represents this intimately close tie with our Deity. After all, no other class (possibly Shaman or Druid on lesser extent) has such intimate contact with their Deity! How can this manifest itself in-game? There are many possible ways and we have no assumption of imposing our thinking upon your world, but may we humbly suggest that you explore such options as:

a) A spell/ability that enhances mana regeneration to reflect this closeness to the source of our power and return us to the thick of battle faster where a true Warrior-Priest belongs.

b) The ability for Clerics (solo, not group) to teleport to the site of their most intimate contact with their chosen Deity - or perhaps to any temple across the land.

c) D.O.T. shields against the Undead. (A skeleton punches YOU for 24 damage! A Skeleton is burned by the Aura of Holiness for 22 damage!)

Dependable Party Member:

In every piece of fantasy literature, fictional history (Robin Hood etc.) and D&D gaming session, Clerics were portrayed as in integral part of the adventuring team – but more importantly, as a stalwart second to the prime warriors in combat. Should the primary warriors weaken, the Cleric was capable of stepping into melee and through dealing & receiving fairly impressive amounts of damage, act as a secondary fighter. Certainly not on par with the warriors, but not as far behind as pure-caster classes either.

In Norrath, we find that once we have achieved even modest levels of experience (say level 15+) our melee ability becomes worthless. Barely even useful for distracting attention from group members and keeping our arms strong enough to hold the voluminous prayerbooks we so often must crack open in the middle of battle. The plate armor we can wear to defend ourselves is a worthless bonus when we spend entire battles on the sidelines, reading our prayerbooks.

We humbly request that you consider the following as potential remedies for what many Clerics feel is a harsh penalty and not in line with overwhelmingly common perceptions of what a traveling Warrior-Priest must do in order to brave the hardships of adventure and spread the word of our Deities:

e) Increasing our offensive skill cap to the equivalent of a Warrior / Paladin 10 levels our junior. Thus my 32 levels of experience would allow me to fight as well as a 22nd level Paladin in melee – minus the special combat skill such as kick, bash, riposte, etc. We do not ask to become primary melee fighters - that is not our job. We must, however, stand ready to defend the party in a modest fashion should the primary warriors fall and our divine power be exhausted. Dealing a paltry maximum 18 damage at more than three times the delay of a Paladin with my Cold Iron Morning Star at level 32 is an embarrassment to the Warrior-Priest idea.

f) Increase our defensive skills to reflect the potent defensive power of our solid armor that the Deities have allowed us to wear. Else we may as well enter battle dressed like our Scholar-Doctor brethren in nothing but Clerical robes and Sacramental Cloth to free our bodies of burdens in favor of greater speed and carrying ability.

Bane of the Undead:

When called upon to perform an exorcism, be in the heat of battle, or amongst fellow Clerics at a Temple, we should be the unequaled masters of ending the horror that is a soul trapped in an undead body. What Necromancers do, we must undo. An innate ability of every Clerical stereotype is the ability to focus our direct link with our Deity into a tangible force that repels the undead. When holding forth our holy symbol and standing firm in our belief in the power of our Deity, none but the strongest of Undead should be able to withstand our faith!
In Norrath, we often find that our holy spells dedicated to the banishment of the undead are overshadowed by the mundane magic of Mages or Wizards! Holy spells should do markedly more damage than the mundane magic of Mages and Wizards against the undead – and doubly so since we must expend divine mana as opposed to this being an “innate” ability! (See also DOT shield above.)

/ooc

As a class, we may be “Balanced” according to the grand “Vision” of EQ, but if so, we are Balanced to be Boring. We are not warrior-priests, but instead Field Medics. I’m not one to complain without reason (aka “whine”) – which is why I have attached a comparison chart that shows class by class analysis of “high value” in-game abilities. This is not a “power comparison” merely a note of what “choices” a class has to use their power. These abilities are those which make classes desirable to a group and/or make them able to solo effectively. In no particular order, the abilities are:
Enhanced Melee abilities (Bash/Kick/Parry/Riposte/Slam etc.), Melee Bonus (Critical Hits/Crippling Blows/Backstab), Spell Casting (General ability to cast varying spells), Movement Enhancement (Speed/Levitate), Teleportation (Group), Healing, Invisibility/Camo (Allows easier travel, Corpse recoveries, Dungeon infiltration/escapes), Summon Pet, Damage Over Time (D.O.T. allows Kiting – or Solo kills above expected levels), Buffs, Class Unique Skills (Self-heal through drain, Potions, Charm, Poison, Lay Hands, Track etc.), Other (Extreme damage spells or melee, Disciplines etc.)

Adding the total of all abilities for each class (at levels below 50 as I am not familiar with post-50 spells – nor ever will be with my Cleric unless something changes to make it more interesting) we see that (to no surprise) Ranger, Shaman and Druid top the list with 9, 9 and 8 abilities (to choose from) each, respectively.

Who resides at the bottom? Warriors & Cleric scored only 4 each. The two “anti-Cleric” classes of Necromancer & Shadowknight ranked high with 7 each and all other classes came in at 6 abilities each. With Warriors and Clerics at the bottom with the least amount of useful/desirable abilities, I think I have a legitimate complaint to bring to your attention. Why? Simple – I do not have enough CHOICE of effective abilities to pick from with my on-line time. If I am lucky to find a group in need of a sub-39 (XP Res) Cleric, I become the designated healer, which amounts to casting heals and sitting to med. Literally, Buff, Heal, Med. When I try to enter combat to add my paltry 18 damage once in a while, I am admonished to “Sit and med for more heals”. If I am unlucky enough NOT to find a group, I have NO choices whatsoever. I cannot solo, not even against Undead – as my “Warrior-Priest” abilities amount to nothing.

At least Warriors, our brothers in the “Low Versatility” realm, have the active and action-packed job of pulling and killing enemies. Something that makes their on-line time exciting and proactive. Clerics are entirely too passive under your “Vision”. Don’t get me wrong, I enjoy healing friends and strangers alike, but I gain no EXCITEMENT from this action. Nor do I have hope of excitement, since I can’t simply ungroup to solo. Logic and common ideals would imply Clerics could at least solo Undead of “White” cons and even the occasional “Yellow” when fully buffed and with a decent weapon and armor combination.

Due to a lack of pre-EQ information regarding your “Vision” about Clerics, many of us entered the life of a Cleric expecting something much closer to the commonly accepted idea of a Warrior-Priest as described above. Knowing what I now know after 15 months of EQ, I would never have chosen life as a Cleric to begin! (FYI - I always play a Cleric as my primary character when Roleplaying.) I have no desire to start over in a more appropriate “life” that more closely matches what I expected from my years of experience in the Fantasy gaming realms - as it would take me a similar amount of time to reach an equivalent level.

I respect the right Verant has to design a class as they see fit, I am however, disappointed that of all EQ classes, only Clerics fall terribly short of what is commonly accepted among RPG fans and in particular, Dungeons & Dragons, of which EQ is blatantly (blatant as in good, not bad) designed after.
Please consider our plight and I implore you to research into our grievance. While Clerics are not disappearing from accounts, they are disappearing from actual playing time. More and more Clerics are relegated to “on-call” status, or simply left on an account unplayed, except when needed. Unless you can count the ACTIVE players and keep a tally, I fear Verant has lost sight of this phenomena.

1) Level 30 rogue is buffed by an ungrouped level 60 cleric. Let a spectre or similar mob beat him down to 1-2 bubbles of health. Obviously this is ungrouped. Cleric promptly then kills the spectre (level 33-37 mob) and you start the cycle.

2) Cleric single pulls another spectre. Rogue engages, and cleric CH's the rogue. Now all hate is on cleric. Rogue kills spectre at his liesure (at level 30, the level 37 specs can take a while). Cleric is 23-27 levels higher, so the spectre doesn't hit much.

3) Spectre finally aggros rogue (sometimes he doesn't even aggro). Let the spec beat the rogue down to 1-2 bubbles, then banish the spectre. Rogue gets the kill.

4) Go back to step 2 and repeat.
