Greetings all,

I decided to post a better description of the datarate.txt file.

The Datarate.txt file can range in numbers from 1 to 10. This is basically the size of the packets you will receive. The larger the number, the larger the packet size. It normally defaults to 5.0. For strong fast connections many players increase the number to increase zoning times, and speed in the game. If you feel you have a good connection, you may try 7.0 first to see how you like it. Some people go all the way up to 9.0. With a high number, if you ever have packet loss due to connectivity problems while playing you will lose more packets. With a large amount of packet loss you might see a lot of ghosting. This is where things will appear to be in a spot that you see on a screen but are actually somewhere else according to the game. If you feel you have a weak connection, you may try 3.0 first to see if it helps. This reduces ghosting but can increase zone times.

The best way to troubleshoot connection problems is by running a trace route. Goto start, programs, ms dos prompt, or type in command.com on start run. In this command line prompt type in tracert <domain name> the domain name will change from server to server. Below is the domain names for the servers your tracing to.

In the trace route you want to see everything under 300 milliseconds. The lower the number the better the connection. You do not want to see any *'s. This usually means the packet has just timed out.

You can try different connection numbers to your ISP, if you have a dial up modem. Sometimes this takes you a different route and avoids problem areas. Usually routing problems are temporary and you just have to wait it out.

Ok, I guess I should have expected that response. Modifying the packet size will help some, but with the amount of information being sent during boss level encounters, it has a fairly small effect.

Let me lay it out. A computer can only receive information over a network, convert, and process it in a certain amount of time. When you overload that, it gets queued up on a client side buffer. The buffer is meant to handle spikes in data traffic and will usually flush out rather quickly. When you sustain a transfer rate beyond the capacities of the receiving computer to convert and process the data, you will eventually fill up and overflow the buffer. When that happens, the client will cut the connection. That's just the way it works. Obviously, the better the connection and the bigger the buffer, the longer that will take to happen, but you will eventually drop anyone if you sustain the transfer rates you currently have during raid level encounters.

The reason modem users are the ones who usually drop is because modems can't handle nearly the amount of information that cable/dsl/t1 users can. Once the modem users drop, the information flood lessens, the buffers of the remaining people flush, and all is happy in the world again. If you were to have a raid of all perfect connection DSL users, you'd still lose people to the buffer overflow until the information load lessened to the point where the people left remaining could handle it.

This is why you have server side rate limiting. What this does is watch the amount of traffic being sent to each individual client. When it reaches a certain point, it throttles back and drops non-essential information until the amount of data falls back under the set limit. This stops you from flooding a client and kicking it off-line.

Let me throw out some suggestions on how to reduce the amount of data being sent to the client and hopefully eliminate this problem.

- Move the client filters to server side filters. If someone doesn't want to see damage shield info, for example, theres no reason to send them that data at all.

- Set up a client side command that toggles a server side flag. The flag would tell the server to stop sending all non-essential information to the client. It would likely result in tracking being inoperable until the flag is shut off, but that would be a small price to pay for being able to stay connected during a boss level encounter.

- Audit the information being sent to the clients and trim out anything unnecessary, like how much cash a creature on the other side of the zone is carrying. This would also take care of your ShowEQ problems to a certain extent.

- Enable/change the rate limiting on the servers.

I really recommend passing this thread on to a network engineer. It's fairly basic networking information and they should be able to immediately see where the problems lie and hopefully correct them. As Everquest ages, its moving towards more and more boss level encounters and this problem is affecting more and more people.

Consider that we are allowed to play the game in 640x480, with low level of detail and low particle effects because some of us don't have real high end systems. The "experience" may not be as pretty as someone with a big, bad P3-2Ghz system, but at least we can play. I'm sure those of us in that sitation are glad to have that option.

Others of us are cursed by living in an area that has NO high-speed internet connectivity. In my area, for example because the phone lines are about 50 years old, I can not connect any faster than 28.8k. DSL is also not an option, as I live about 19,000 feet from the central office, and AT&T (our only cable provider) "has no plans to roll out broadband access in your area at this time."

However, I have no options in EQ to truly degrade my connection performance to make the game playable. Using the filters, in their current implementation, does not help - the data is still being sent to me, but my client is choosing not to show it. Therefore, I still get bumped all the time, regardless of my filter settings. Realistically, I'm going to have to cancel my subscription because I am now level 57, and most, if not all, of my fun comes from participating in large-scale raids with my guild. Because my link gets flooded, I can't contribute after say, 15 seconds or so of the fight beginning, so really, I can't participate at all, and this is very frustrating.

(Believe me, I've optimized my crappy old 28.8k connection as far as it'll go. It's not my fault 28.8k connections top out at ~3.5k bytes/sec!)

The only solution that'll help me is if EQ's servers could be told not to send certain types of data to me in the first place. Here's what some folks are suggesting as far as filtering data being sent to the client, along with a my pro/con description.

Damage Shields
Visual Impact: None.
Data Impact: Significant.
Notes: This information does nothing for the enjoyment level of the game, and in fact, makes it difficult to see vital communications between party and raid members. Most people filtered this as soon as it was available, and have never looked back. Some folks, spellcasters especially, do want to know when a creature has a damage shield active, so they know when to dispel it. However, in most large encounters, casting annul magic a few times is standard procedure, so actually seeing this information is unnecessary.

Others Misses
Visual Impact: Marginal.
Data Impact: Significant.
Notes: I'm pretty sure that if another PC misses, it does not show them swinging their weapon; if this is true, then visual impact becomes "None". However, this more than anything probably causes more links to become flooded in large encounters. Most people, by level 5 or so, have turned this filter on as to facilitate seeing communications.

Bard Songs
Visual Impact: Depends on Particle Setting, which is usually set to OFF for large raids.
Data Impact: Significant.
Notes: Even with particle density set to off, most bard effects still show visually to some degree. However, in large-scale raid situations, this information is deeply extraneous. Granted, the bard song information will still need to be sent to each individual client to show the songs affecting *them*, but it is not absolutely necessary to show bard affects for all other members of a raid. I would predict that most people would turn this off for large encounters, then back on for smaller group experiences.

Others Hits
Visual Impact: Significant.
Data Impact: Significant.
Notes: I'm sure this is what triggers the client to animate the various meleers being rendered. While this is pretty, and can be considered part of what makes EQ so engrossing (look at the big ogre swing his sword!), we should be able to turn this on and off when it will adversely affect our connection (again, big encounters).

Auctions/OOC/Shouts
Visual Impact: None.
Data Impact: Significant, in some areas.
Notes: On our server, I get bumped just walking through high-trade areas. East Commons has 100+ players in it at any given time, all spamming auctions as fast as they can. Imagine my joy when I can not even pass through there without getting bumped, which is a double whammy, because depending on trade activity, I can sometimes not even log in!

Something to consider also, is that by allowing your servers to filter this data before it gets sent to the client, you are decreasing Verant, Inc.'s bandwidth requirements. This could, in theory, reduce lag for *all* customers, and might even significantly reduce your monthly network traffic and equipment bills! I say this because WE the CUSTOMERS really do care about YOU!
