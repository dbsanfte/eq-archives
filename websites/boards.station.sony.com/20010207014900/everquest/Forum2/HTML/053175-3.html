Great post!

--"Part of the problem is that this game inherently requires zero skill. Items and levels win. "--

To me this is the crux of the problem. In a game where a characters ability is more defined by his gear than his skills is going to suffer Mudflation big time.

Level focus is not normally too much of a problem in most games like this, but in EQ it is starkly amplified. In games such as Meridian59 the power to level curve was logarithmic. ie. the more levels you gained the less difference in power. e.g. The power difference between a lvl 25 and 30 is more than the power difference between a lvl 50 and lvl 60. This means the 10 hours a night guy is more powerful than the 2 hours a night guy, but not in a manner that warps the game making it unplayable for the 2 hour a nigh guy.

EQ is the total opposite of this. Power to level curve is exponential. Your 10 hour a nighters make the game almost unplayable to anyone with less than 4 hours a night to throw away.

Kheldan,

Respectfully I disagree with you about the economics of UO. Don't get me wrong, there were *LOTS* of problems with UO. The first few months in UO there were more bugs than EQ has had in its entire existence. But I'm talking about economics and mudflation here.

Economies rely on buying cheaper products over and over again and larger products infrequently. UO did well with this. Reagents, armor, and weaponry were disposable (since dieing was not that big of a deal) and land/houses were extremely valuable. People could use trade skills to support constant demand for armors, potions and etc. That's why I would never mind personally mining for hours. I knew there was a market for ingots. Is there a market for items produced by trade skills in EQ? The only way demand is going to raise is to make the cost lower and the utility greater. Otherwise, trade skills are just a feature nobody will use. Yes it was silly to buy black pearl off player vendors at 20gp each. But if it’s there and you have the money, that’s the trade off. I think the availability of reagents at npc shops was just about right. There is one potion, which has a market in EQ and that’s sow. Compare that to the utility of healing potions and exploding potions in UO. If you played the game for a long time, you’d realize how central these things were to combat.

When you have such a small supply of goods and so few players, who can afford the prices created by such a small supply, you can’t have a dynamic market. Combine that with a monopoly control and NO DROP, you have the stale and boring economics of everquest.
