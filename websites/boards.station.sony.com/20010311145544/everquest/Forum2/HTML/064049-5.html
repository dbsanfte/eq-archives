I died constantly, and couldnt even kill low blue things regularly. While we were seaching for a cleric to come into the zone to rez him since there were none there, we got trained. I believe I also saw a couple of posts saying 51 would be ok, but the vast majority of the shaman posting were asking for Superior Heal at 44 or 49. Since we got Greater Heal at 29, going to 53 without any increase in healing abilities a very long time to go without.

Someone at Verant decided to ignore the majority of us that were talking from experience playing the game, and simply moved Superior Healing from 53 to 51. I call that a stinking placebo.

There are now 2 corpses laying in Karnors Castle that would not be there had they done what we asked them to do. But they refuse to listen to us. They refuse to believe that we want this to be the best game that it could be. They ignore us.

And then they wonder why we get frustrated and upset with them.

>"I'd like to offer a couple suggestions and then provide a small case-study to illustrate why the implementation of such suggestions (or ones similar) are needed.

I'm not in a position to argue company revenue with anyone.

>"I suggest that one member of your current staff be assigned as advocate to each class. Such a person should have as their mission in life to understand the wants and needs of that segment of customer."<

So you are suggesting that we add another duty to each member of the dev team that they read all the class sites and become experts in the play of a certain class (which, it seems, would also require that they play that class up to 60th level on a live server)? They already do that. Every one of us plays the game. Members of the dev team play as many hours as anyone. >"Each class should have an interactive page on the EQ website through which to see a regularly updated status on their issues."<

I would argue that there aren't so many 'issues' as you seem to think. To keep up the kind of interaction you seem to want would be a full-time job.

>"You might say that you already have such advocates as part of your tuning team. Great! Tell us who they are. Have them engage us. His (or her) only desire or job is to push through narrow objectives. We can't do that. We have to look at the entire game. Narrowing your focus to just one class is counterproductive.

>"These 14 folks would, of course, also have to maintain the objectivity one would expect of a Verant employee as opposed to a purist player. The "good-of-the-game" would temper the natural desire to advance the position of their advocacy group beyond what is reasonable."<

Ah, sheesh, maybe I should read one paragraph ahead before responding…

Here's where you'll have to trust me. An advocate that is objective and looks for the good of the game, would be unable to please the players that truly feel that they need an advocate. If a player points out a problem and is logical about the problem, he doesn't need an advocate. That problem is a problem, regardless of the class of the player.

I've spent a lot of time lately getting to know the desires of the players that identify themselves with a particular class. I've written up those desires, purposefully excluding the ones that did not take into account the good of the game. And yet, as is obvious by your post, I am not considered an 'advocate' for any of them. Why is that? I have done as you have described. I have listened to the players, responded to them (over a longer period of time than I would have if I was only dealing with one class), and listed their concerns. I have been an 'advocate' as you described.

And yet, you clearly do not see me as such.

*shrug*

Perhaps some do. We communicate them as we get them, for the most part. Sure, we keep some things secret because we truly believe that discovery is part of the game. The only thing that your advocate group could do better than one person could do (and I know, I'm that one person), is react more quickly to their… constituents. But the dev team is busy. Their work has to be done. If we added another duty to them, that of advocacy, then you might get faster responses, but they would be responses like, "well, I spent my morning responding to your e-mails and discussing your concerns with you on the message board, so I didn't have time to code in the changes you asked for…"

I don't see that as a better way to do things.

We do that now. There is no timeline about your class that you do not already know about. I've made a list, and we're looking at it. That's the timeline. They do.

Again, I do that. It's just not happening as fast as you'd like.

So once we have taken a look at the list that I have compiled and decided which ones we are going to act on and which we are not, then I will come back to you with a revised list. But we don't charge twice the price… I suppose if we did, then we could double the size of the EQLive team and hire people to be advocates. But that does not mean that our current system isn't good. But we can't have someone go to every customer's house and get their opinions first hand, either…

It's all about reasonable expectations. We meet those whenever we can. But we can't meet the unreasonable ones, even in the short term because we couldn't keep meeting them, and that would be a disappointment to our customers too.
