Kaljorge (did I get that right? heh) raised an interesting point.

I would ask this question though - are the goals of "infinite" longevity (ie there's always something challenging to do at any level) and the goals of accessibility (ie you can grab your friends whoever they are, and play with a good risk/reward ratio as long as you are at least moderately balanced) mutually exclusive goals?

I would say they are exclusive only if it means an accessible world to the moderately balanced group becomes trivial to the "perfect" group.

You can address this several ways

1 - design seperate zones for "normal" groups and "perfect" groups. Literally segregation.

2 - alter class balance until the risk/reward of the moderately balanced group is nearly identical to the "perfect" group. This is virtually the opposite of diversifying the classes (Verant's Kunark 50+ battlecry) but at least what it loses in class diversity it makes up with in grouping options.

3 - Design zones that place a very high value on versatility and actually penalize or heavily marginalize singleminded classes. From a design standpoint, I imagine this would be difficult to work, to put it mildly.

It's my belief that the VI is inordinately fascinated by the progress of guilds like FoH, and tends to design around such guilds, something like a scientist with his labryinthes and his favorite maze rat.

In fact, the trend I see is this. Design for the extremes so nothing slips through the cracks and let everything else fall where it may. Now this is good engineering if you're designing a 747 to land safely in Dulles airport at night, with one engine out, 80mph crosswinds, and a thunderstorm. That's fine for designing an airplane or network security, or preventing people from "hacking" an MMORPG, but as *game design* it's very shallow thinking, especially in a game meant to appeal to legions of fans.

Since MMORPG's are really in their infancy, I suppose we have to go through the rounds of games that are too easy, too hard, soulless, powergaming playgrounds, etc etc before designers come to understand their market.
