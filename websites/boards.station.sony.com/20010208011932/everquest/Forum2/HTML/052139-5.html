Alan,
If you had done this “talk” a couple months ago, we would have given you a different response than what we are saying now. We were content with our favorite class. It was powerful, challenging, unique, and above all, special. We had our problems and frustrations, like everybody else, but that was part of the fun this class offered. When we overcame those obstacles, we found out those problems taught us how to play the class better. Even that stupid, uncontrollable pet taught us a few things about crowd control and grouping. If you haven’t played an Enchanter before, then you’ve missed out on the incredible rush that crowd control gives…and if you have, you know exactly what I’m talking about. And when you end up in one of those “Holy %^&#!” fights, when you come out on top and a group mate says, “Enchanters rock!”, they aren’t cheering your character…they’re cheering you. Gordon Winn once described Enchanters, after the pet nerf, as a “quiet” group, and we were a quiet group, we were too busy having fun to complain. Of all the classes in EQ, Enchanters had it good, until…

It took a while after Velious was launched. We wandered around in Iceclad, Eastern Wastes, and Great Divide, and Kael Drakkel. Oh sure, there were plenty of Giants running around, which we can’t mez, but we knew that coming in. It wasn’t until we started filtering into the higher level dungeons that we found out…Enchanters got slapped down, and slapped down hard. We found out that not only were our most powerful spells useless here, but all of our offensive spells were useless. If we go to Velious, it’s to do one job, and one job only, buff everybody else with haste and Clarity.

Velious was the proverbial straw that broke the camel’s back. Enchanters are now insecure about their position in Norath, and perhaps a little paranoid. In Velious, crowd control was treated as an exploit, much like kitting, and programmed against. Now we sit wondering if the nerf bat is going to get pulled out, and our favorite class is going to be beaten silly with it. I’m not saying that you’re planning on nerfing crowd control. I don’t know what your company’s plans are. But now, when I’m in Sol B, or over in Kunark, every time I “hear” someone yell “Enchanter’s rock!” I’ll turn my head to the south, and say to myself, “They did it once. They can do it again.”

Useless and inefficient spells
The Enchanter class probably has the largest collection of spells, but that also means they have the largest collection of useless spells. Some spells were good ideas that never really worked. Others are situational spells, where a situation where they would be useful, was never found. One spell has a problem with the cost of casting it, versus the benefit it gives. And there are a couple spells which are “broken as intended.”
Lull Series
The Lull series (Lull [1], Soothe [8], Calm [20], Pacify [39], and Wake of Tranquility [51]) is supposed to reduce the reaction radius of any mob it’s cast on, and allow nearby mobs to be pulled without agroing the “lulled” mob. All of the spells are single target, except Wake, which is an AoE. I say supposed to because these spells have a high resist rate, as high or maybe higher as an Enchanter’s nuke (before applying Tash). Since Tashing a mob before Lulling isn’t an option (Tash is an agro spell) and a resisted Lull agroes the mob, usually pulling all the nearby mobs as well, the spell is utterly useless. Compare this with the Druid’s spell, Harmony [5], which is an AoE and is totally irresistible (there is no resistance check), but has the restriction of outdoor only.

It would be nice if these spells could be made irresistible (or “nearly” irresistible), or reduce the resists on the spell and eliminate the risk of agroing the mob. Otherwise, please consider removing the line of spells from the game. After all, what is the point of having these spells in the game (and what IS the point of adding another in the RoK expansion) if we aren’t supposed to use them?
Strength Debuffs
Enchanters have to lines of strength debuffing spells, the Weaken series (Weaken [1], Ebbing Strength [12], Feckless Might [20], Insipid Weakness [34], and Weakness [44]), which reduces strength only, and the Enfeeblement series (Enfeeblement [4], Disempower [16], Listless Power [29], Incapacitate [44], and Cripple [53]), which reduces Strength, AC, Agility, and sometimes Stamina and Dexterity.

The spells are situational, however we have never found a situation where they would be useful. By the time we get the opportunity to use these on “normal” mobs (old world and Kunark), the mob is already half dead, and when we were finally faced with mobs that had more fight in them (Velious) the spells were resisted as much as our other spells.

I don’t have any ideas about what to do with these spells, but I thought I’d mention them.
Whirl and Dyn’s
These spells (Whirl till you hurl [12] and Dyn’s Dizzying Draught [29]) were supposed to be 12 second, single target stuns, which were supposed to spin the target like a top (Cast this spell on a player and you’ll see the animation.) Dyn’s also has debuffing qualities to it, as well. The problem is these spells are instantly broken by any other stun, be it spell or slam/bash.

How about changing them from a stun to a root, plus debuff. Give them the duration of a similar level root spell, and the same limitations. And please, put in the animation.
Curse of the Simple Mind
This is a level 29 spell, which reduces the intelligence and wisdom of the target by 15 to 20 points. The only effect, this spell has, is to remove one spell’s worth of mana from the target, IF the target is at full mana already. If the target has already cast one spell, then the debuff is useless.

How about, instead of an Int/Wis debuff, something that temporarily impairs the target’s ability to cast spells. In exchange, the spell would be resisted every tick, like charms.

A second option would be to make this spell strengthen the effects of charms (read make them a little more reliable). In this case, the spell would have a short duration, like 15 to 30 seconds. Both options would have a long (2 minute) recast time.
Rune IV
Rune IV [44] has a 71% increase in damage absorption over Rune III [34] (164 points), but at an increase of 1,234% in reagent costs (10p for Peridots vs. 7g for Jaspers.) Compare that with the 95% increase (112 points) Rune III has over Rune II [24], for a 50% increase in reagent cost (7g for Jaspers vs. 5g for Bloodstones,) and the 115% increase (63 points) Rune II has over Rune I [16], for a 150% increase in reagent cost (5g Bloodstones vs. 2g Cats Eye Agates.)

And before you ask the same question you asked the Clerics. The answer is no, we do not get reimbursed for this expense. These are out of pocket expenses, which everybody considers the cost of doing business.
Boon of Garou
I won’t bore you with the stuff everybody else is complaining about, but I will ask you this question. Why, in the name of all that is holy, does this illusion proc a life tap? Talk about blurring the line of class distinction.
Velious
We are not having fun! If you’re going to put insane levels of magic resistance on mobs, please give us some way to chip down that resistance. Enchanters and Clerics are the only two classes that have no offensive spells that aren’t based on magic. Put Enchanters in a zone full of magic immune mobs, and I’ll show you and Enchanter whose only job is to buff all the other players with Clarity and haste all night. Oh boy! Oh joy!

These aren’t balance related and this is more a wish list of new toys, but this is as good a time as any to bring them up. Perhaps you can set them aside for later consideration.
Illusions
Unless you’re playing an agnostic Dark Elf, illusions really don’t play that big of a role in an Enchanter career. Maybe this is a sad commentary on these spells, maybe not. Anyway, here are some ideas that would be nice to see.

The higher level the vial of mana means the more powerful enchantment would be put on it, but the high level of skill would be needed for a chance at success.

The idea is to push Enchanters away from the other trade skills, by making them invest time and money, and maybe even skill points, into a separate skill. It would also introduce an element of risk into the enchantment “process”.

An enchantment skill could also be used by Enchanters to make reagents for more powerful spells (like a super MR debuffer). Imagine if an Enchanter, in order to cast one of their offensive spells, had to have a ready supply of made reagents, and while those reagents were NOT stackable, but the ingredients to make them were. An Enchanter would be spending part of their down time preparing for the next fight. Also, imagine the Enchanter sitting right next to the Necromancer in the newbie area auctioning for stacks of frogs whiskers and chicken lips. If you want to be the master of reducing down time, you have to pay for it with a little down time of your own.
