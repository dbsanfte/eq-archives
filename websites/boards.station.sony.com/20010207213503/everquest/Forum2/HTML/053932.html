Copyright infringement is actually pretty straight forward.

Firstly, anyone who takes a copy of a screenshot from something inside EQ or from a manual [art, text, etc.] now has a copy of something that has the potential to be covered by copyright law.

The next question is "is the material already in the public domain?". In simple terms this means has Verant already waived its protection under copyright.

Lets look at the clause in the EULA:

*******

You may not copy (except to make one necessary back-up copy), distribute, rent, lease, loan, modify or create derivative works, adapt, translate, perform, display, sublicense or transfer the Software. You may not copy any of the written materials accompanying the CD-ROM. You may not reverse engineer, disassemble or decompile the Software except to the extent that this restriction is expressly prohibited by applicable law. The Software may contain license management software that restricts your use of the Software.

******

WOW!! pretty sloppy, but even so it may be good enough. This clause clearly states that the writtent material with the CD is prpotected under copyright. In the case of the CD it refers to the software. It does not explicitly refer to to the written and graphic material contained therein. Nor does it say whether you are allowed to print a manual if you so desire. It is definately unclear in this area and Verant should proably change the clause.

Before we dig a little deeper please be aware that there is a difference between breach of contract and copyright protection. Breach of contract can only arise between two individuals that have signed a contract, copyright protection is granted to the owner of the work and can be enforced against third parties that have not signed a contract. [sorry to state the obvious if everyone knows this ]

So we have two cases. Things that Verant can do to individuals that have signed the contract and things they can do in general to protect their assets.

If you have signed a contract with Verant [the ELUA] and you a in breach of contract then Verant can take legal action. Doesn't mean it will get them much money but they certainly could stop you doing what you are doing -- "cease and desist"

Lets look at the copyright side of things. Certainly Verant has protected its software, the physical CD and the printed material. However, it has not expressively protected the content of the software, or, more importantly, limited and specified its use. The content is the names of characters, story line, graphics, information on how the game works etc.

Given this we just have standard copyright protection and it basically has a conept called "reasonable use".

For instance, printing the manual would be considered reasonable use, selling copies of the manual to others would not be reasonable use. Writing a new manual that contain significant portions of the old manual and selling it would not be reasonable use. Writing a completely new guide with new text and selling it ...... *hmm* it might not be protected, its a gray area, and I will explain why.

If Verant aggressively went after anyone printing, showing or using their material on any web site I think they could make the point that their items are still protected by copyright. However, they have not, in fact they work closely with many sites and certainly don't have a problem with guild web sites. They also clearly have a feature for taking screenshots and seem to have no problem with people using the feature. They seem to have no problem with people displaying these shots on their web sites. Sorry Verant, I think you have let this into the public domain -- very sloppy. You should have at least forced people to always include a Sony copyright statement on any graphic etc.

This is a shame, if Verant had better protected the content they could have shut down Ebay on copyright infringement alone [the text and graphics used to describe the sale of items would have been an infringement.

In most cases reasonable use means "limited use" and not used to generate profit [more complex than this, but this is the simple view]. Since many of the bigs sites are clearly making money from explaining and using Sony material and Sony has taken no action I think they have let this material into the public domain. For instance, I could give you 20 screenshots of the game and get you to pay me $20 for the time it took me to assemble them and this would be pretty legal [a little gray, but in the end legal].

So, an eBayer is not doing anything illegal on Ebay offering in game items for "sale". Even the showing of copyrighted materal is OK because Verant seems to have allowed them to go into the public domain [slap Verant on the wrists for not being a little more careful ]. The only thing left is breach of contract. So if the person making the EBay sell is a person currently using EQ with a signed contract they may be in breach of contract. Since the ELUA does not expressively forbid selling ones time while playing eBay it is unclear whether it is truly a breach of contract or not. However, when the person in the game actulally makes the exchange it may get a little clearly [not a lot, but a little clearer].

In the state of California Verant would be on shakey ground having a contract that expressively says that third parties cannot gain personal income/profit true selling time of expertise that in anyway is derived from their knowledge, use or exprerience in using EQ without the express permission of Sony...... blah blah blah.

So, can you protect virtual items? Yes, but Sony has not done a good job in this case. Read the copyright stipulation son a music CD and you will see a well written piece of literature. The ELUA should be rewritten to expressively protect content and to clearly state what is reasonable use and what is not. At the moment it is a mess and the mess is of Verant's own making. It may be too late to fix this in EQ, but someone should get the legal side of this worked out before the next gen games arrive.

Stoutfoot:

I tend to disagree that what you say is the end-all-be-all of the Ebay argument.

You say that Verant owns the servers, owns the data, owns the virtual item. I agree with this.

Where the difference comes in is that when you get an item in EQ you basically are earning the right to use that item. You don't own it. So what is happening is someone else is selling the rights to wield an item in-game. The other person still doesn't actually own the item. The just bought the right to use it.

To use an analogy: I buy a membership to Bally's Fitness center. I don't actually own anything in the gym. I can't walk out with their dumbells. When I leave the gym I have to leave everything that was there in the gym. I have only purchased the right to use their equipment at their site. Bally's allows you to sell your membership to someone else. That person hasn't purchased Bally's equipment. They have only purchased the right to use that equipment. Now, if Bally's was extremely crowded and was no longer issuing memberships someone might be tempted to purchase a membership off of someone else at an inflated price.

The question is does Bally's legally have the right to not allow you to "sub-let" your membership? Does a landlord have the legal right to prevent you from sub-letting your apartment. Does my bank have the right to prevent me from selling my leased vehicle to someone else as long as the payments to them are made?

What's being sold is not ownership but the right to use an item. That is what needs to be argued against. It's such a simple argument to say that no one can own an item in EQ since it's true and everyone knows it. What you need to argue is the real issue of selling the lease of those virtual items.

Under copyright law, you can sell a legitimately obtained copy of the work and profit from that sale without violating the copyright in the work. It's called the "first sale doctrine."
