For whom it may concern:

I just want to state that I concider myself to be a technicaly literate person -- a "computer whiz" if you will. And even I am stumped to my very core as to why my system just locks-up when playing everquest.

For awhile, I thought it was the SoundBlaster Live! AWE chipset that was mucking things up...getting the PCI bus master controller confused. However, tonight I confirmed this is not the case.

I own both a Turtle Beach Montego II Quadzilla sound card, and the newer SoundBlaster Live! X-Gamer card.

I first installed the SB card (see my system components below), with all the DirectX updates, driver updates, Windows2000 patch updates, etc, BEFORE I installed everquest. I can verify that EQ did not install or touch any system component files.

My system would lock up at random times. Sometimes it would lock up after only two minutes of playing, sometime I could game for an entire 6-hour session without locking up once. So, I completely uninstalled the SB components and drivers, installed the Quadzilla sound card (following the special Aureal Vortex II Windows2000 setup procedures) and played everquest. I'm actualy experiencing more lockups with the Quadzilla than I did with the SoundBlaster.

I don't know if I can totaly blame the Everquest software yet. DirectInput might be to blame (maybe.) I've noticed that every single time I locked up, I was doing some sophisticated mouse+keyboard movements (like a circle straff, or hitting multiple keys with mouse moving around, etc.) Never once have I loved up without actively doing something on the keyboard and mouse.

Granted, it *might* be some way EQ is interacting with the DirectInput interfaces, but until Verant engineers let us know just exactly which interfaces their speaking to and how they're doing it, it will be difficult to exclude the EQ software as the cause of all this.

In all of this, I have to admit that I have had this lock up problem while playing Quake III arena. Quake III arena does use DirectSound and DirectInput (but not Direct3D.)

So the moral of the story is...it's not SoundBlaster specific. All these problems are either:

DirectInput (Microsoft's problem)
DirectSound (Microsoft's problem)
EverQuest (Verant's problem)

I highly doubt any device driver is to blame. I know a little bit about how WDM drivers are created, and I can tell you that Microsoft has such strict control on exactly how a driver can work and interact with windows, that it's difficult to write faulty drivers. (They either completely work, or never work, no in-between.)

This is a home-build system, and is NOT overclocked. I don't know what else to add, other than I am totaly completely stumped as to why my computer completely locks up, forcing me to do a hard reset.

KropDog,

A few things can cause crashing or freezing while zoning. This problem is often caused by too many programs running in the background. Please try the following before playing EverQuest:
In Windows press <CTRL><ALT><DLT> Close all programs except Explorer, Systray, and Rnaapp (if listed) by left clicking each of them and choosing End Task. If attempting to close a program in this way causes a system reboot/crash, then you'll have to close the program manually by finding its icon in the system tray and closing it there or removing it from the startup menu. Be careful here; as programs removed from the start menu will not start up automatically when restarting the system.
Lets go over some of the other things that might cause problems.
You might also have an IRQ conflict. Right click on my computer> go to properties> go to device manager> click on computer and view resources for the IRQ’s. If your video card shares resources it might inhibit its performance. You might need to take other conflicting cards out and put the cards in other matching slots in your motherboard to free up the IRQ for the videocard. Check your IRQ setting number to see if you have any numbers that match. Feel free to ignore PCI steering. Perhaps your Sound card and Videocard are sharing resources. If they are you would need to pull one of the cards out and place it in another compatible slot.

Sometimes disabling the sound card solves the problem, this suggest an IRQ conflict or a sound card driver problem.

In some cases it can be a problem connecting to a particular zone box on that server. This would suggest a port number being blocked by a firewall or another type of network security program. For trouble shooting purposes, remove any of those programs from your computer and try to zone again? There is also a chance your ISP has a firewall. A different ISP can sometimes help in these situations.
Your video and sound card drivers might need to be updated. Find out which video card and sound card you have.
Here are two ways to determine what sound and video cards, and drivers you have.
Click on start, and go up to run. In the Run window, type DXDIAG than hit ok. This will launch the DirectX diagnostics tool. Click on the tab that says Display to see what video card you are using as well as the latest driver you are using. Click on the tab that says Sound to see what sound card you are using as well as the latest driver for the sound card.
(For people with two display tabs, one is a 2d card and one is a 3d card. You will most likely want to update both.)
Another way to check for your cards is to right click on My Computer at your desktop. Scroll down to properties, click on properties and go up to device manager. In the device manager, if you click on Sound, video, and game controllers, you can determine what audio device you are using, and if you click on Display adapter you can see what video device you are using. If you click on either device and then click on the driver tab you can determine which version you are using.
(An accelerator card will sometimes be located in the Sound, Video, and Game Controllers section.)
After you find out what video card or sound card you have, please visit our main Web site (http://www.everquest.com), visit the support section and examine the sound and video FAQ’s. This will usually have locations for the latest drivers for your cards from the manufacturers’ web sites. Please download the latest driver for your card unless it has been recommended to download an older set of drivers. Sometimes, an older set of drivers is more compatible with your system and you will have to shuffle around until you find out the correct driver.
When you download the driver it will ask you to either save to a disk or open file from its current location. You should check "save this file to a disk", and download to your Documents section. Open up your Documents section, and find the drivers you downloaded. Open up the download, which should be in zipped form.
Normally, you would download the driver or drivers to your desktop in zip form. Your computer should have an unzip program already installed. If you do not have an unzipping program please download the trial version from www.winzip.com . You should unzip the download into your Windows temp folder. Make sure you know the exact location of the folder. It should be extracted into the windows directory as: C:\WINDOWS\TEMP\DRIVER NAME AND VERSION NUMBER.
Please read the Readme.txt that is in the download that you have done. Follow the directions carefully, as they are almost always the best directions to correctly install the drivers for your card. It is highly advisable that your print these instructions out, if at all possible, before the installation.
Sometimes old drivers still present in your system will cause problems. Usually these drivers will not display themselves in regular Windows so you have to go to Safe Mode to remove them.
If you have Windows 95, reboot your computer. You can enter safe mode by hitting F8 when the message “Starting Windows95” appears.
With Windows 98 Reboot and hold down the left control (ctrl) key after you reboot your computer and you should, after a short period of time, see a menu which has an option for Safe Mode. Please choose the Safe Mode option.
Once you get into Safe Mode right click on My Computer, left click on Properties, go to Device Manager, and left click the plus button next to Display Adapters. If there are any listings for cards you no longer have or multiple entries for your current video card then remove them. You will want to do the same thing under sound, video and game controller, for sound card entries.
Here is another problem that could cause you to crash to desktop or freeze while zoning. You might not have enough memory. I would recommend that you have at least 600 MB of free space on your hard drive and that Windows is allocating your memory.
Right clock on my computer. Scroll down to properties. Click on Performance. Click on Virtual Memory. Check the Let windows allocate your memory. This will normally maximize the amount of memory available.

There are also various virus programs that can be downloaded. Some can affect your connection. I recommend that you download a free version of a virus checker from www.avp.com. Do a virus check. If you have a hybris.worm virus type it will effect your wsock32.dll. This will inhibit your connection to EverQuest.

Your hard drive might have a power saving feature. Normally EverQuest uses RAM for you to play. When you zone, EverQuest and your computer access your hard drive. If the hard drive does not respond quickly enough, you may get dropped. If you can turn off the power saving feature, it might also increase your zoning time and prevent you from crashing or freezing.

Good Luck
