You make good points, Meleager, but I differ on my interpretation of some of the actual effects of them. First off, I'm not sure how much of the exlclusivity of "uber-content" is due to big chunks of time and how much is due to just having spent tons of time in the game generally. If I (a decidedly non-uber gamer) spent 2-3 years playing Everquest, never more than 4 hours at a time, could I reach most of the content? If you knew 30 people well in-game (of the right classes) and had agreed on a time, could you pull off a raid in 4 hours? And, of course, who you know in the game isn't related at all to the size of the chunks of time you play and is much more related to how much time in general you play and in how much energy you invest in it.

As for the attitudes of the "average" person playing the game... I'm in two minds. Your assessment may be, in fact, relatively correct. But it's also alien to most other types of games. There are very few non-computer games in which I would expect to reach the highest level of skill without fundamental, life-altering changes. I could possibly be a decent golf player if I played a round once a week. But I won't be as good as a professional, unless I decide to drop my life and pursue golf. This seems fairly close to Everquest. The difference, however, is in expectation. I don't EXPECT to become a super golfer without sacrificing other parts of my life. I don't EXPECT to get the rewards a professional gets or expect professional golfers to play on my local metro course and nothing more difficult. Why do "average" Everquest players expect to be able to compete with the (insane IMO) people that spend 8 hours per day playing? I don't know, but I'd hazard a guess that they are conditioned that way from single player computer games. In those types of games, you could pretty much expect to achieve everything everyone else did, because it is completely noncompetetive and is geared so that everyone can experience the entire game. The multi-player model is totally different in its expectations and assumptions, and I think people haven't really adapted to it yet.

As for group size... I just plain disagree with you here. In fact, I think the problem with the big raid-style groups at the end of the game is that they weren't something you were practicing all game. IMO, requiring larger groups of people at least requires that you work reasonably well with other people and aren't someone totally anti-social. I'd like to see a lot more things for much, much lower levels to do in 30 person groups. I think that if people got used to multi-group playing from the beginning of the game, that many fewer people would have trouble getting into raids at the upper end. I have absolutely no trouble with a requirement for 30 or 40 or 50 or whatever the hardware can handle for killing the biggest and baddest monsters at any given level.
