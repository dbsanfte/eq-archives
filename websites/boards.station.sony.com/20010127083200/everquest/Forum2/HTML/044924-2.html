(I didn't read every single post previous to this, so some of this may have been repeated)

Ill go back to the main post and respond to a few problems, and not just the downtime subject which the majority of the posts seem to be geared toward.

My solution would be to add more adventuring and excitement to the trips. I do not believe it still can be done in EQ, just because of the way the game works, because the game is geared toward wasting as much time as possible. So here it is.

You used the example of Frodo travelling to Mount Doom. Well let me ask you this. How many times during his trip did he end up fighting monsters? How many times did he end up exploring? Without exacts, the answer would be plenty. Now how many times on your way from Freeport to Qeynos, (not counting teleports) did you fight monsters and how many times did you end up exploring? You either a) didn't fight at all, because the monsters you came across were green and didn't attack you, and didn't explore because the maps are designed for easy access from point a to b, or b) you did fight something but you died because it was entirely to strong to take out by yourself and you didn't explore because the maps have nothing to explore but plains and little hills. Now neither of these ways are fun. There are 2 ways I can think of to improve this.

First, there needs to be more monsters to fight, and monsters that don't completely destroy you if you do happen to encounter them. Id say double or even triple the spawn rate of monsters, make them twice as easy, but cut the exp they give in half. This way you level the same, but fight more. And when your running around, the increased monster spawn will increase your chance of fighting, and when/if you do fight, you have pretty good chances to win even if your alone. But like I said earlier, this isn't possible in the current eq, because of the soloing taboo and for you to solo a monster, it would have to be low low green (if your high level) which offers no exp or any good loot whatsoever, so it is avoided because it just wastes time. Now if the monsters were to actually give exp (but not enough to effectively level off of) and have the rare, super rare, uber rare chance of dropping half way decent items, I believe it would feel more exciting. Of course there should be some tough monsters that should be avoided such as hill giants and the such, but don't penalize us with all the other mobs (because if wasting time is the penalty, then fighting monsters that give no exp or loot is wasting time, therefore its a penalty).

But this goes against the "if its green you get no exp" rule, which I think should be done away with. You COULD level to lvl 60 off of lvl 1 rats, but it would take you years, so no one would camp rats to 60. But if every time you came back to town, you killed some rats or wolves or bears on your trip, through the course of a month or 2 (real time) you might actually gain a bar or 2 of exp. Now this is pretty minimal, but yet, it isn't a complete waste of time. No one would level off of stuff that gives 1 bar every 2 months, but through the traveling process of a year you might actually gain something instead of just wasting the time running. As for loot, I think normal mobs should have a super rare chance of dropping good items. This way you want to fight everything and anything hoping it will drop some really nice item, instead of avoiding all the spawns and looking for 1 specific monster.

Second, and as far as exploring, this I believe is due to poor map design. There needs to be more obstacles on the maps. 90% of the outdoor (non dungeon) maps are just squares with hills. The zonelines are on the edge of the squares, so to get from point a to b you just follow the zoneline wall and your there with 0 fights. Doing this over and over again gets very boring. The maps need to be designed differently. More like Rathe Mountains or beholders maze. Maps that force you to wander INTO the map instead of following the zoneline around. Another idea are maps that require you to go through another zone or dungeon to get to another part of the same map. For example, maybe 1 map has a HUGE cliff. Unclimbable by any other means but entering the cave at the foot of it. This takes you into a dungeon where you fight your way up to the top of the cliff, where theres another zoneline that takes you out the top of the cliff of the zone you were just in, where you can continue on your way. Or maybe you don't even need a zoneline, make it like the goblin maze in the lake of ill omen, but REQUIRE that you HAVE to pass through the cave to continue to another part of the map. Another example would be a map where there is a giant river running through the middle of the map, and only 1 bridge, located in the middle or somewhat centered on the map. If you fall into the river, the only way out is to swim to the end of the river at the edge of the map, which would be a zoneline to another zone. Like what was done in warsilk woods, but to an extreme. And when you fall into the river, the river is full of piranhas and other nasty fish that attack you. Or if some maps must be designed in the square fashion, make sure to load up the zoneline with monsters, so if you do want to follow the wall, you'll be in for some fighting. What about using paths like in the commons and places like kithicor woods, but make the pathing of monsters mostly away from the path. That way the safest, shortest path actually is through the map and not running along the edge. But of course have some monsters roaming through the path for the adventuring purpose, just not as many as following the walls would be.

This is what I believe one of the core problems with eq is. The game is designed to waste as much time as it possibly can. You said that downtime is used as a penalty, but the reward system is more based off of reducing the penalty instead of gaining anything. To put it simply, the game wastes your time and the goal of the game is to avoid wasting as much time as you can. But you will always be forced to waste some time; therefore you are always being penalized no matter what you do. I think of games like diablo2, where you relentlessly fight hordes of mobs that cover the whole screen as you tear through them, hacking and slashing, casting and exploding monsters, and when its done you say, "wow! That was intense!" Then you come to EQ, and you fight 1 monster, for about 5 min, sit for 2 min, pull 1 more monster, and repeat. Oops, pulled 3 monsters, were dead! That is dull. Now imagine that you could actually pull 3 monsters, and fight 3 monsters without mezing or rooting them, and still win. Or maybe be able to pull 5+ monsters and fight them all at the same time and win. That would be more intense and exciting. I think the key here is "dead time." There needs to be something to do during these times instead of just waiting.

Lastly, one of the last posts I just read mentioned about having the zones overlap a little bit, and this is a great idea not only for normal servers, but also for pvp servers. Iv been playing this game a long time, and I still sometimes end up rezoning because I hit the wrong move key or was knocked back into the zone by a mob. This would solve many problems. First you would never zone right into a train if one was on the other side, this would stop people from ping ponging or zone dancing on pvp servers to avoid death, and you wouldn't be knocked back into the zone by a mob or another player (like the infamous qeynos hills/karanas problem).

Brad, here is something about EverQuest that would lead people to believe that downtime is about money.

When you start the game, your downtime is almost nil. This makes sense. You start out small and everything is proportional to that.

Level 10. Here you can start to feel that downtime in a tangible way, e.g., man I have been sitting here for *two* minutes. It is a contrast to how you were playing, but it is negligible. Why? Because the uptime is just about the same as it always has been. You are well matched with your opponents and are in great balance with the environment.

As the levels get higher- so does that downtime commitment. AND you get weaker versus the environment. When you are weaker versus the environment- risk is much higher and therefore downtime is more likely to be encountered.

At this point, if a player can realize what is happening, he or she has two choices. Quit. Or keep playing. By the time a player is seasoned enough to realize just how weak he or she is versus the environment, understands how the hell he or she landed in such a trap, and perchance decides that the reward is no longer worth the effort, he or she is probably level 35 or greater.

It is at this point when this player will say, "I can't just throw away all the time I have invested into this." And they play a little more in hopes that it can get better; that their investment can still be worthwhile on a day to day basis. But as many high level players can swear, this just isn't so. Players get weaker- the bang is gone, and the game just gets HARD. However, they'd be damned if they would just toss away 90 days of play time.

That is why so many people come to these boards so frustrated that they are viscious and ready to attack anyone that says G'day.

I love my character- I love this game. But if I knew what it would turn into when I first heard about it, I sure as hell would have never played it. I got stuck in that cycle described above. A lot of people did. Those are the people that post here the most. The people that accidentally got caught with their pants down and started playing a revolutionary game. Why is it revolutionary? This game actually preys on hope to keep people playing.

Downtime is just one of the frustrating things we put up with as we either:

A) Hope it gets better one day down the line.
B) Refuse to throw away what we have done so far until a viable alternate product is produced.

That is why you hear complaining about everything under the Norrathian sun. We won't just toss away our played time, so we sit here and are so intimate with every little detail of the in game experience that we get sick and tired of this or that. It truely is a paradox of the mind.

I can walk away from all this and feel foolish for having wasted so much of my time. OR I can foolishly stick around and hope it gets better. 8 out of 10 will stick around- nobody wants to feel as though they lose something. That's just human nature.

/em agrees with the last poster:

Exploring new lands in a game that you pay for monthly should not be fearful in the extreme sense. Why not keep the exp penalty, but return the equipment to the player? Why should 1 death potentially cost the player their entire equipment base?

I have played AC, and concur that the vitae penalties work much better, along with losing the most valuable item on you. You have to go back and loot that item off your corpse.

But with EQ, the exp penalty is much greater.

Not only do you potentially lose hours/days/weeks worth of work, but you can also lose equipment that you've earned/bought/traded for permanently.

I agree that death should be penalized, but should there be a risk of permanently penalizing the player in terms of equipment loss? I've always thought that thinking to be a little extremist.

I know many players that are able to devote hours to one raid, accounting for the possibility of CR. I, on the other hand, do not have that time. It's not because I don't have time to go exploring and dungeon delving. It's because I don't have the time to pad into the equation to account for possible CR for myself and everyone else.

While clerics are a necessity for high-level raids, they must also contend with these issues.

In conclusion, the post-50 game has generally been disappointing for me for these very reasons.

Design issues surrounding the death penalty, that have haunted EQ since release, have manifested themselves into "Time vs. Survival" beasts.

I apologize if this is a representation of my slanted opinion.

To me, death should penalize the gamer, but should not pose the risk of permanently wiping away that player's entire equipment list.
