Greetings, all!

Actually, I have seen several specials (History Channel, A&E, etc.) dealing spcifically with armor, and one excellent segment was devoted to the various sorts of chain and plate armor.

I was surprised to learn that not only is it possible to swim in chain or plate, but also to do handstands and cartwheels. The myth of medieval knights being knocked onto their backs and being unable to get up is just that--a myth. As the narrator said, what would be the point in wearing "protection" that would kill you deader than a sword thrust just for falling down? This does not allow for different-use armors, though, as it is my understanding (not expert) that heavy cavalry of some periods (knights who fought mounted and ONLY mounted) had EXTREMELY heavy armor, but also that it only covered from the waiste up--that their legs and...how shall we say, privvy members...were protected by special saddles built for the purpose.

As a side note, I have seen (so far unproven) assertions that at least one medieval (12th century) knightly order included the necessity of smimming for half a mile fully armed and armored (though presumably without the horse) before the rank was conferred.

You can swim in chainmail because putting so many variables into an mmorpg, would have taken a lot of time while not really adding much to play.

Things like that exist in pen and paper games because play is greatly accelerated. "We travel to the next city"

"Okay, you arrive at the next city uneventfully", says the GM after rolling some dice.

Due to that, the GM also has time to roll for encumbrance in water and to make checks against stamina or whatever to see if you remove your chainmail before drowning, etc.

An mmorpg might have stuff like this someday, but they seem to be going in the other direction and removing such micro-managing, such as the need to eat. If you think about it, having to remove chainmail before going across a stream or something would only seem nifty the first time. After that, it would be irritating having to swap out armor then swap it back in or nearly drowning because you forgot.

I believe that elven chainmail, or magic armors, in AD and D don't actually encumber you (even as far as swimming goes.)

Removing armor would be in a game where you also had rust checks for armor when it was raining, checks to see if armor broke, checks to see if you fumble while fighting causing bad things to happen, etc. All that kind of gets in the way of fun.
