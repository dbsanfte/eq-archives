Brad: "Also, EverQuest is a VERY item-centric game, and significantly increasing the chance of item-loss would turn out to be a much harsher penalty than what currently exists."

I posted this elsewhere, but it seems like it will get seen more here!

Brad has stated that downtime is an important game-balancing penalty portion of the game. You want to try to avoid it. Whereas I agree with the philosophy, I also think that EQ, as currently designed, has been built such that downtime is an integral part of the gameplay and hence, is unavoidable.

The most common suggested answer to reduce downtime in EQ presently is simply to get in a group. While I agree that doing so does give the illusion of having less downtime, I think it is also necessary to point out that the total gross downtime when compared to the net gain of experience is really not that substantial. In other words, while it is true that you spend less downtime between encounters because you have depleted your resources less in the previous one, you also gain substantially less experience. Truth be told, downtime isn't really reduced, rather it is just spread out over many, many more encounters.

Now then, this isn't intended to be a rant. Rather, I want to look at some of the things that are causing downtime and suggest some design tweaks that I think would genuinely work towards reducing downtime in the game.

First off, we have the time spellcasters must spend scribing scrolls into their spellbook. I can see no good reason that this downtime needs to exist whatsoever. Why force players to sit and look at a glorified egg-timer for a minute or so per new spell? It serves zero gameplay purpose that I can ascertain. I think that it should be set at a fixed 5 seconds or so per scribed spell. Players would still need to memorize spells normally.

Next, we have the issue of binding for melee classes. Why not allow melee classes to be bound as freely as are the casters? Melee classes would still need to find a willing caster to bind them but they could be bound anywhere it is legal for a caster to currently be bound.

Now I recognize that corpse recovery is considered a necessary penalty for death. But, as the game currently stands, melee classes are often penalized MORE for their deaths than are casters simply because of their inability to be bound as freely as are the casters.

What about excessive travel time? Why not introduce something like "touchstones" or fixed points to which players could immediately transport themselves. Perhaps each player could have one and only one touchstone point active at any one time (much like a bind point). Since players would need to physically touch one of these items, they would necessarily needed to have traveled there previously. This would greatly help things such as long and boring boat rides between continents.

These are fairly minor things. For the big issues we have Health and Mana recovery. I think that most of the players would agree that, in general, too much time is spent sitting down simply waiting for these resources to replenish. I know of numerous players (and I am one myself) who often brings a book to read during EQ playing sessions just in case I get into a situation where I'm doing nothing but sitting around waiting for one of these two resources to recover.

I suggest that EQ seriously consider implementing Health and Mana potions into the game. I realize that doing so could drastically change the way the game is played and so I think they should be introduced only under very carefully controlled conditions, which are as follows:

The consuming of a Health or Mana potion by a player exactly follows the pattern currently in existence for casting spells. That is, there is a little bar that says, "drinking potion" instead of "casting" when one is consumed. Once the drinking time is complete, the effect fires off identically as though it were a spell. If the player is interrupted during the drinking time, the drink and its effects is aborted (and the potion is even possibly lost for good with a message something like "You've spilled your potion!"). This would prevent them from too radically changing the complexion of combat. You wouldn't get warriors who could reasonably drink a potion during a heated combat. Remember, the idea here is to reduce the time spent sitting around doing nothing, not necessarily changing the way the game is played. Heck, drinking times for potions could be double the current casting times for equivalent spells and they would still be highly valuable.

Which is the next condition. Health and Mana potions would (and given the current EQ economy) be quite expensive. Perhaps costing 3s. per point of desired restoration. A meager 50 hp restoring potion would thus run around 150 s. or 1.5p. A bigger 300 point heal would cost 9p.

The high cost would have two important effects. First, it would help to prevent abuse. Players who want to use them would know that they are darn expensive and thus probably only used occasionally when they feel that their current downtime isn't worth the wait for ordinary recovery. Second, it would provide a much needed drain on the money supply in EQ. As we all know, the older a server gets, the less and less money becomes valued. Players with a lot of cash would now have something to actually spend it on rather than hoarding it up for that one rare item or, worse, flooding the newbie zones with insanely rich twinks.

Note that this would make soloing for some classes *that cannot currently do so* a reasonable proposition but only at a high monetary cost. Personally, I think that would be ok. It would open up the game to another way of playing. Grouping would probably still be preferred just because that 12p heal potion is essentially free if you have cleric in your party.

And, if Verant is bold enough to consider implementing potions of this sort, it could also provide a whole new opportunity for a trade skill. Potions could be made by players, again at a reasonably high expenditure of resources, and then sell them to others for a profit.

That's it. I would love to hear other suggestions on this post!

Greetings.

I have been reading this topic and my interest was immediately peaked so I thought reply with the hopes that some of my thoughts and suggestions could benefit both VI and the players.

Let me first start by quoting an excerpt from one of Brad's initial comments.

---------

The fundamental advancement mechanism in MMORPGs is Time Invested; therefore, Time Lost needs to be the fundamental death penalty if the principle of risk vs. reward is to be preserved.

The time lost can be in the form of experience loss, an experience debt (a cool idea, btw), items lost (providing it takes time to acquire items), corpse retrievals, etc.

As a corollary, being efficient in terms of time spent is the quickest way to advance (assuming advancing quickly is your goal, of course). Downtime, which is likewise often debated and criticized, is also a penalty based on the Time Invested/Time Lost principle. Minimizing or even eliminating downtime using intelligent tactics in a well-balanced group is an efficient time investment. Failing to do so incurs a Time Lost penalty, though probably not as severe as dying.

-Brad

------

Brad's logic here is very sound. There can be very little doubt, as least in today's MMORPGs, that the fundamental advancement mechanism is time invested. MMORPGs were never meant to be based on dexterity, or reflex, or an easy straight line to acheiving your goals. It follows the old axiom that "some things are worth fighting for."

Being effecient in terms of time spent is therefore the quickest way to advance, as Brad has already mentioned. The design intent behind EQ is that the most effecient use of a players time (in terms of advancement) is to use intelligent tatics in a well balanced group.

If you notice, however, most players in the EQ community find that soloing is the quickest way to advance. This is contrary to the design intent of EQ. Soloing is also the activity which incurs the greatest amount of downtime. A well balanced group has much less down time, but players find that advancement is considerably slower. This, I believe, is the heart of the "downtime" problem.

This can really only lead us to two possible conclusions (or perhaps a combination of the following conclusions).

1. EQ does not function as it was intented (in terms of how a player advances quickly).

2. Players in the EQ community are using group tactics which yield slow advancement.

I personally believe that both of these conclusions contribute to the downtime problem. I also believe that each of these conclusions feed off one another (ie: EQ not being designed as intented encourages ineffecient tactics, and ineffecient tactics encourge the players to request changes from VI which would bring EQ further away from its design intent.) However, I also believe that something can be done about both of these conclusions to help eliminate the downtime problem.

Lets first look at conclusion 2 - Players not using effecient tactics in a group. This is a very difficult topic to discuss, since group tactics vary so much, depending on who you talk to and the situation in question. However, the common theme that keeps the players from acheiving quick advancement in a group, I believe, is the hesitancy to take risks and to try new or different tactics. In general, players in the EQ community will find a tactic that works and then stick with it, and partially close their minds to other possibilities. This follows the old axiom "if it ain't broke, don't fix it."

In this case, however, I think risk and change are something that we as players should be experiencing almost everytime we play EQ. So, what I suggest to any player reading this... don't be afraid.. try something new... think of something new.. suggest something that has more risk, but if done right, will increase your groups effeciency.

Here is a little tid-bit of information that may encourage all of you. It is possible, at L58, for a group to advance at a rate of one YELLOW (not blue) bubble per hour. No, I am not joking. I am completely serious. For any of you who wish to find out more about this, check out the news entry for Jan.2/2000 at www.graffe.com. To achieve such effecient advancement does involve taking more risks than we are accustomed to. However, these risks are virtually elimated by using the right tactics and group complement.

Second, lets look at conclusion number 1 - EQ does not function as it was intented (in terms of how a player advances quickly).

Advancing quickly in a group requires two very important things to happens.

1. You must be part of a relatively balanced group with people you can trust.

2. Your group must use tactics which yield fast advancement.

One of the problems with finding a good group is that the people you come to know and trust will not always be there. Players come and go... other things come up... people move... get a new job... get busy at work... etc. A lot of the time your long lost buddies come back but it often happens that your level difference is now too great to be able to realistically group together.

Another reality of EQ is that the higher in level your character gets, the more dangerous your encounters will be. This has the affect of wanting to group with players your know and trust, making it more difficult for groups to embark on risky encounters or new tactics if you have to group with players you don't. However, as I mentioned earlier, players come and go and its inevitable that players that you know and trust will no longer be able to group with you, forcing you to try and find others whom you can get to know well and trust. This becomes increasingly difficult as you level.

Its a circular problem. The higher in level your character gets, the greater the importance to have a good group which you can trust, but it also becomes more difficult to build that network of friends to build that group.

So what can VI do to help a player build that network of friends to build that good group? VI has already done a number of things to aid the players in this: guilds, emotes, various text communications modes. However, I believe something could be done by VI to help pool players together, of similar level, similar playing times and similar playing styles.

What I suggest is a player database on each of the servers. The database would only be accessable from the character selection screen. Each character a player has would have a profile which the player can edit. The profile would contain things that the server already knows (like level, class, race). Anything else the player could enter him or herself. Things like playing times, tactics you wish to try, style of play, classes needed for your group. There could then be a find button which would query the database to find players that match your profile (not exactly of course, but within reason). You could leave a message to another player to request that you meet at a certain time and place in game to actually play together and try out your mutual ideas.

So that is what I think. Of course I am just giving a very brief and rough outline of what the desired feature should do. It has to be thought through more thoroughly before anything could be done. But that is what I would like the VI team to do... think about a new feature such as this. Perhaps do a poll of all the players to see that they think. Or various polls to determine how this feature should really function.

That is what the real penalty in games like this is. There is no need for added punishment like experience loss or needing 15 minutes to med back to full.

The challenge and therefor the punishment of the game lies in accomplishing or failing to reach goals (slaying dragons, breaking in a hard room, completing a quest, etc, etc, etc).
The failing to accomplish a goal is what makes guilds/groups/players go back the next night and try again, and again and again until they win after which they move on to conquer the next challenge.

All you have to do (and have done sofar) is provide more and harder challenges.
