I'd have to take issue with defining the casual gamer in the same fashion that you have in your post. I find Mr. Wrinn's statement unrealistic on several levels.

a) It is certainly possible to play hours and hours every day and simply fritter the time away. It is not possible to play a few hours each week and make meaningful progress. There are too many time sinks in the way. It takes too long to travel to hunting areas. Even if you end a session in the place you plan to hunt in next session there is no guarantee that it will be possible for you to do so upon your next login (the area may be camped, overcrowded, etc). If you are not playing one of the annointed solo classes then you will have to find a group, which takes more time (and invokes any number of possible difficulties; I'm sure we all have our horror stories about pickup groups). One corpse recovery will end your night, and no, such a player can't get someone to resurrect him because he's a casual player who doesn't have a guild backing him up. Spawn rates and drop rates are too low for anyone who does not commit large blocks of time to the game to have a reasonable chance of acquiring desired or, in the case of fighters, needed items.

b) The 'casual gamer' is *not* supported, because the game consistently caters to the higher levels. Apropos: recent statements from Mr. Wrinn on the issue of required levels for Velious that 'lower levels are transitional', and of the recent Halloween events (particularly the unleashing of Faydedar in Lake of Ill Omen) that 'lower level PCs are the victims that need to be saved'. Certainly one could *define* a casual player as someone who is satisfied just hanging out and shooting the breeze with online acquaintances, but that's not a satisfactory definition. The simple fact is that the vast majority of players expect to get something more than a chat room with graphics.

This is really about how you define a casual player.

I am a casual player. Because I don't play that much, I don't expect much. If you play only a few hours a week you really can't expect to obtain high levels. This is true in any game. And you wouldn't expect to be paid as well at work if you only showed up fifteen hours a week, nor would you expect to be a highly ranked tennis player if you only played every ten available tournaments.

Gordon is right. Your play-style isn't determined by your play time. If you are a casual gamer you don't play a lot because playing EverQuest is a low priority thing in your life. That's a casual gamer.

Someone that plays very little because of time restrictions in their life (like working 60 or 70 hour weeks) may or may not be a casual player, depending on their desire, not their time played.

A time restricted non-casual gamer will most certainly be frustrated with EverQuest because they can’t get the stuff that they want. A casual gamer knows that they aren't going to get what they want because they just don't play much. A casual gamer has no problem letting a year real time pass, while only having 15 days played on their character, before gaining a certain level or a certain item because they are playing casually. A time restricted non-casual player will be frustrated at the lack of progress. But their frustration is not really with EverQuest. Their frustration is with their limited time to play.

Yes, EverQuest rewards players for the amount of time they play. That's how an advancement system works. The same is true of any game with such a system. Quake (to use the most common comparison) does not have an advancement system, so you are not rewarded by the game for time played (though you will likely improve in skill as time goes on). But in, for example, D&D your character advances over time. The same thing goes for all persistent games.

Let me address Venture's points:

>"a) It is certainly possible to play hours and hours every day and simply fritter the time away. It is not possible to play a few hours each week and make meaningful progress. There are too many time sinks in the way. It takes too long to travel to hunting areas. Even if you end a session in the place you plan to hunt in next session there is no guarantee that it will be possible for you to do so upon your next login (the area may be camped, overcrowded, etc). Spawn rates and drop rates are too low for anyone who does not commit large blocks of time to the game to have a reasonable chance of acquiring desired or, in the case of fighters, needed items."

In those few hours each week you make just as much progress as anyone else does in those few hours. If it took you X amount of time to run to a hunting spot, it will take just as long for someone else to run to that spot. If it's camped when you get there, it would be camped for the other guy too. So you both spend another X minutes running elsewhere. Just because one player needs one week real time to do that running due to limited play time and the other might do it in one night does not mean that we are punishing the player with limited play time. I accept that getting into a group is added time for each log in. But corpse recovery takes the same time for every one. As a casual player, I had a guild backing me. I never lacked for assistance when I needed it because we were organized casual players, choosing to play at about the same time every week.

>"b) The 'casual gamer' is *not* supported, because the game consistently caters to the higher levels. Apropos: recent statements from Mr. Wrinn on the issue of required levels for Velious that 'lower levels are transitional', and of the recent Halloween events (particularly the unleashing of Faydedar in Lake of Ill Omen) that 'lower level PCs are the victims that need to be saved'. The low to mid-level player is thus left rather justifiably feeling that he is being left out, and that he is regarded as a second-class citizen (even a 'victim'...as a GM of nearly 25 years experience I can tell you that a good GM never casts the PCs as victims, it's just not fair to them)."

As a GM for only 20 years, perhaps my opinion isn't as valid as yours in this. But being a victim is FUN! I know you won't believe this, but my group gets a kick out of roleplaying all sorts of situations. Sure, being trounced isn't something that we enjoy all the time, but that doesn't mean that it's not an interesting role. And you are also ignoring the many lower level events that happened on Halloween. Faydedar was not the only creature abroad that night.

As for Velious, as EverQuest continues, the average character level is going up. We provided a lot of content for low level characters to start out because that's where the average player was. As they play longer they gain levels. Velious provides content for an aging population (not that we're getting old…). In an overall game sense, the lower levels are transitional. That does not make them worthless or ignored.

But none of this is related to 'casual' gamers. It does relate to time starved non-casual gamers. I would tell them that they should be glad that this content will be there when they arrive at that level.

>"c) Progression past the mid-levels requires taking part in organized raids on tough areas of the game; this in turn means the player must be able to schedule his real-life commitments around game concerns like spawn times of rare mobs."

True, at least in part. You can progress past the mid levels without ever going on a 'raid'. Rare mobs are not the main source for level advancement in EverQuest. But if you want that one item that is super powerful or whatever, yes, you will have to join a 'raid' type group. I argue that a casual gamer won't care about that one item, or will understand that it is beyond easy reach. A time starved non-casual gamer will have to make time for it.

Yes, players with less time will be limited in their advancement per week. But if you look at total time played (without looking at the real-life time expended to gain that time played) I think that you will see that the casual gamer advances nearly as fast as anyone else (though casual gamers are also less likely to be concentrating on advancement). And a casual gamer has fun doing it. It's the time starved gamer that has trouble, and that's because of their desire to have more play time more than it is due to EverQuest itself.

I find Mr. VanCouvering's reply to be even more unsatisfying on even more levels than Mr. Wrinn's.

Quotes delineated by '-----'

-----
This is really about how you define a casual player.
-----

In the trivial sense that one can always attempt to define oneself into victory, perhaps. But as one of my instructors used to say, that just makes your argument logically unassailable and totally uninteresting.

To the original poster, and I believe to the majority of EQ players, a 'casual player' is one who plays in short sessions as opposed to prolonged ones.

-----
If you play only a few hours a week you really can't expect to obtain high levels. This is true in any game.
-----

It is not. It is particular in current CRPGs to EverQuest. UO, AC and D2 all allow much more reasonable advancement to the casual player.

-----
A time restricted non-casual gamer will most certainly be frustrated with EverQuest because they can’t get the stuff that they want.
-----

A time-restricted player *is* a casual player. It doesn't really do any good to harp on this point, as it will just cause the necessary semantic change in the original objection to return the argument to this very point.

-----
In those few hours each week you make just as much progress as anyone else does in those few hours. If it took you X amount of time to run to a hunting spot, it will take just as long for someone else to run to that spot. If it's camped when you get there, it would be camped for the other guy too. So you both spend another X minutes running elsewhere. Just because one player needs one week real time to do that running due to limited play time and the other might do it in one night does not mean that we are punishing the player with limited play time
-----

Mr. VanCouvering's argument pretty much hinges on this point, and it doesn't hold water.

Consider three hypothetical EQ players: Tom, Dick and Mary.

Mary is a hard-working young professional woman. Between her career and her active social life she can only find time to play EQ for maybe 2 hours (120 minutes) at a time, once or twice a week.

Dick is a typical GenX slacker, living in his parent's basement and working at Starbucks (while pursuing his master's in nuclear basketweaving). He can easily arrange to play 6 hours (360 minutes) a day several times a week.

Tom's still in school, and often cuts class or ignores assignments to squeeze in 4 hour (240 minutes) EQ sessions. He's looking forward to being Dick in a few years.

So let's say it takes 30 minutes to get to a hunting spot and get underway (a conservative estimate; one ship ride throws that number in the trash). Well, it's the same 30 minutes for everyone, right?

Uhm, no.

For Tom, that's 30/240 = 12.5% of his gaming time.

For Dick, that's 30/360 = 8% of his gaming time (roughly).

For Mary, that's 30/120 = 25% of her gaming time.

Mary's getting tied up in non-game tedium twice as much as Tom and three times as much as Dick. Since that overhead is likely incurred for each session, even if Mary plays three times as many sessions as Dick she's still actually PLAYING less than him. Likewise a corpse run will impact her playing time much more heavily than it will impact either Tom or Dick's. (This is just like that low-delay-weapon issue that's giving the designers a hard time.)

In a similar vein, a 2-hour block is simply not enough time to camp anything that could be considered a rare spawn. You're never going to see it.

-----
As a casual player, I had a guild backing me. I never lacked for assistance when I needed it because we were organized casual players, choosing to play at about the same time every week.
-----

Casual players aren't organized. Casual players don't schedule their real lives around EQ playing times.

-----
But being a victim is FUN! I know you won't believe this, but my group gets a kick out of roleplaying all sorts of situations.
-----

Mr. VanCouvering is correct; I don't believe him. I don't believe anyone actually creates RPG characters, a task which requires a fair amount of work, only so they can serve the role of extras in a Godzilla movie. And you are also ignoring the many lower level events that happened on Halloween. Faydedar was not the only creature abroad that night.

'Lower level' must be taken to mean the usual L35 required to survive a GM event.

-----
In an overall game sense, the lower levels are transitional. That does not make them worthless or ignored.
-----

But that is the impression the players get, which leads to increasing dissatisfaction as players find that more and more content is being added for character levels they can't reach because they don't have the ability to commit large amounts of time to the game.

-----

But they will never get there...that's the point.

True, at least in part. You can progress past the mid levels without ever going on a 'raid'. Rare mobs are not the main source for level advancement in EverQuest. But if you want that one item that is super powerful or whatever, yes, you will have to join a 'raid' type group.
-----

If you are playing a fighter class, you *need* that 'super powerful' item, because the game has been balanced with the assumption that you will have it.

Case in point: my character is a Ranger, L41. My equipment is Ivy Etched or better in all armor locations, which means I basically have L30 gear. My next set of armor is found on the Plane of Fear; the one after that is in high-level Kunark dungeons. If I am to continue playing past the next few levels I *must* improve my armor, and that means major-league camps. These are the kinds of camps where you have to show up for multi-hour sessions several times just to earn the right to roll should the item drop. The same applies to my weapons, which must be upgraded if my performance is to meet the requirements for my level, and I will have to get a haste item from somewhere. The alternative is to stop playing the character, because a fighter class PC without equipment commesurate to his level is useless to his group. (Just as an aside, yes, I know about Tree Weaves and Sleeves of the Ancients and whatnot; they're no easier to get.)

The situation is less grim for caster classes, as most gear is little more than a convenience to them, but they do have to contend with spells that require rare Research components, and since they are competing for group space like everyone else they have to keep up with the Joneses if they want to continue to get group invites.

-----
But if you look at total time played (without looking at the real-life time expended to gain that time played) I think that you will see that the casual gamer advances nearly as fast as anyone else (though casual gamers are also less likely to be concentrating on advancement).

As shown above this statement is simply incorrect; casual players can't hope to compete with players who can commit to large blocks of time even if they match them overall hour-for-hour. The game's time sinks prevent it.
