Monk VS Rogue

Thus far in the Monk discussions, the main comparisons have been Monks to Warriors and Monks to Rogues. The propensity so far has been to mainly compare Monks to Warriors, from what I have observed, and to point out that a Warrior’s damage output is only ever so slightly less than what a Monk can do, yet Monks are at a gargantuan defensive disadvantage compared to Warriors. All good points, and true that Monks should out-damage Warriors by a large margin due to the defensive penalties paid by Monks and due to the “vision” of Monks as the premier toe-to-toe damage dealers.

However, I think really that Monks are far more comparable to Rogues. Let’s have a look:

Main Defensive Skills:

Monk – Rogue –

Dodge Dodge
Block Parry
Riposte Riposte
Disarm Disarm
Instill Doubt Instill Doubt
Feign Death Evade

This list is virtually identical, with the notable exception of Feign Death and Evade. Now I admit that I have never played a Rogue so you Rogues correct me if I’m wrong here, but I would venture to say that these two skills are somewhat comparable. The difference being that Feign is better in solo situations, and Evade being better in group situations. I do perceive Feign to be the more useful skill, giving Monks a bit of an advantage there. However, I would also point out that Rogues don’t suffer from the weight limitations that Monks do, which I see largely as the Monk’s penalty for receiving the FD skill.

Other Useful Skills:

Monk – Rogue –

Bandaging Bandaging
Safe Fall Safe Fall
Sneak Sneak
Mend Hide
Pick Locks
Pick Pockets
Sense Traps
Disarm Traps
Poison

Rogues get more skills here, but Monk’s Mend is a great skill. However, Mend is really mainly applicable if soloing, when grouped you probably have a healer and it’s a mute point. Also note here that the Rogue’s Sneak/Hide combo provides awesome stealth to the Rogues, this helps make up even more ground on the Feign Death skill; and remember that Rogues don’t pay that weight limitation penalty.

All in all, I’d say that Monks and Rogues are pretty darn balanced and equal to this point. They are also have largely the same disadvantages (Poor armor, low hitpoints, etc.) Now to the problem area…

Main Offensive Skills:

Monk – Rogue –

Dual Wield Dual Wield
Double Attack Double Attack
Special Attacks Backstab

Again, very comparable with the exception of the special attacks. Rogues’ Backstab does huge amounts of damage whereas Monks’ special attacks have marginal damage output. Here is where the classes largely diverge. How is it that Monks, the class which is supposed to be the premier offensive melee class, put out so much less damage than Rogues?

Most people have proposed placing Monk damage output somewhere between that of a Warrior and that of a Rogue, I disagree. Monks and Rogues are highly comparable classes with the exception of damage. Their damage output should be relatively equal.

Now I can just see the little wheels turning in VI’s collective mind right now… “Hmmm we should just nerf Rogues.” NOT! Both Rogues and Monks pay gargantuan defensive penalties compared to the “base class”, the Warrior, for the benefit of being highly offensive classes. The solution to balancing Monks is to up their offensive output to be equal that of the most comparable class, the Rogues.

Regards,
Zipadee

There are a lot of useful posts here, and some not so useful, but most of them seem to focus on two things. The first is increased damage output, and the second is some method of staying in the combat longer (More HPs, better AC, etc.)

There should be no doubt that monks were intended to do the most toe-to-toe melee damage of any class. In the first release of EQ, monks dual wield, and I believe even their double attack, capped much higher than any other class, which gave us more strikes per combat cycle, thus more damage. Our dual wield skill is also provided starting at level one, and advances to a maximum of (Level+1) *7, which again increases the number of strikes that we get, even when compared at lower levels. With the release of Kunark, other classes were given improvements to these abilities, but monks were not, because our dual wield was already capped at 252. Warriors grew closer in damage output to monks, but according to tests done by Abashi, monks were still better at causing damage, even with the weapons that Kunark introduced. With the advent of Velious, weapons have become more powerful, and the results of Abashi’s previous tests no longer apply.

Why should Monks do more damage than warriors? Because we are strapped with plenty of restrictions in order to be able to have that ability. We have a fourteen-pound weight limit that not only dictates our choice of equipment, but also lessens the amount of loot that we can carry. We require more time to level up, because we are making more banking runs, and having to save longer to buy better armor and weapons. Finally, we wear lighter armor, giving up between 33% to 75% of the AC that comparable warrior AC items provide.

What I propose should be done to restore the damage is to give monks an innate modifier to their damage, by fist or weapon. As a suggestion, add one point to our BASE damage at level fifty-one, and an additional point for every two levels thereafter. At fifty-ninth level, a monk would have a total of five points of additional base damage, so a weapon like the Cane of the Tranquil would be a 16/25 base weapon instead of an 11/25.

This change would be neither over powering nor out of character for what one would expect for a monk. Additional damage would make up for the lost advantage in the number of strikes a monk enjoyed before the release of Kunark. A hard cap on skills due to a programming oversight shouldn’t cause a class’s growth and progression to stagnate. In addition, a monk (Martial artist) should be more efficient with a weapon than the hack-and-slash plate wearer.

As for increases to our damage absorbing ability, I think the biggest/most logical improvement would be to increase the skill caps for riposte/dodge/block to 252. Monks aren’t supposed to allow someone to beat them into hamburger, they are supposed to be quick enough to evade the majority of harm directed at them.
