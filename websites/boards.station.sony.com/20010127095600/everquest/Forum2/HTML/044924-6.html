Let me preface this by saying that my family and I have been playing EQ for almost two years now. My son, two daughters, and myself all play on a regular basis. We have two accounts with characters ranging from level 54 down. All on the Tallon Zek server. Never before has any game held our attentions as long as this one, and I want to commend Verant for creating a truly historical game. (and yes, there is a wife. But if we let her play it cuts into our time and all she does is take screenshots of the sunsets anyway!)
I admit I don't read the boards very often and I may have missed a very obvious point.
But, in regards to downtime being used as a penalty:
Recently there seems to be a very interesting conversation going on, on this subject. This has prompted me to make my first post. Now comes the dumb question.
Why does my body have to stay where it is, when I die, with all my equipment on it?
This creates the infamous corpse recovery. Which, in my opinion, creates a major amount of downtime in the game. And does not make much logical sense. Granted we play in a world where no one ever really dies, which is magical in itself. Not that this should seem anything but normal to the citizens of Norrath. So perhaps we can ponder this truly naive question.

Why can't my body spawn at its bind point with all my equipment still on? This idea seems so incredibly simple that I realize it must be basically flawed. One of the major objections would be the lack of a corpse available for resurrection. In that case, I propose, leave a naked corpse where I died that will decay in three hours. Since this is the length of time I have available for a resurrection anyway. If something must be left on its so it does not vanish. Perhaps a stylish toe tag. :-)
I realize the risk of losing your equipment was an intended factor. But let's be honest. 99.9 percent of the time that won't happen. I've never felt that this was a risk. The only time that this actually becomes a factor, is on a plane raid. In which a player, thru no-fault of his own, can not back to his corpse. These are the only times i have seen this happen.

This simple idea would eliminate a major amount to downtime. And, eliminate a lot of problems.

Let's look at it this way. Getting prepared and grouped to go adventuring is like paying your dues for the reward you hope to gain at the end of your journey. There's plenty of downtime just in that. Although the anticipation and socializing involved makes that pleasant. As to Risk vs. Reward. I still have the possibility of dying and losing experience and gaining no reward. That's plenty of risk. I then have the option of taking the penalty of experience loss, or seeking the aid of a cleric to gain back some of my loss.
On a corpse recovery. I have the possibility of dying and losing experience on the way to my body. That is a very unbalanced risk vs. reward. And has happened to me too many times. :-(

Being that I play on the Tallon Zek server, this would also address a lot of the issues we have in PVP. Again. dieng on a corpse recovery in PVP, I don't believe, was an intended penalty. But it happens. And the issue of major raids, where the corpses of the vanquished enemy, create a state of paranoia in anticipation of their return.
Imagine the satisfaction of invading an area and defeating an enemy, and watching their bodys vanish after looting their cash, or after three minutes, whichever comes first. Never again what I hear the shout of "CR" and listen to a poor soul suffer the humiliation and beratement sure to follow.

Loot and Scoot would vanish..! thank the Gods!

So I ask, why is the corpse recovery necessary? EQ has afforded me and my family thousands of hours of enjoyment. None of which, were on corpse recovery! This is downtime I can do without.

Thanks for listening...

I imagine this thread is way too unwieldy for posts bureid this deep to have an impact, howerever, I do have a general theory on the usages of downtime.

1. Downtime, as it is currently implemented in the game, is limited by the downtime that is imposed upon the melee type characters. With no meditate ability for health and the additional difficulties of not being able to bind, melee characters are the measure by which a group can progress. This imbalance becomes most evident when you observe small group or solo dynamics. A magician and necro, for example are going to be much more efficient that a monk and a warrior, even though both classes have a limited healing ability and are capable of dealing an immense amount of damage. This problem is further magnified if the monk or warrior dies and is forced to accept a long downtime for a naked corpse recovery and accept the xp loss from a rez not being available.

To reduce this imbalance, which favors caster classes so heavily, I suggest that a system be looked at that would allow a melee bound character to summon his corpse. This summon would result in an xp restoring rez to be impossible. However, the double penalty, for time loss and xp loss, is currently much more damaging to a non-caster. However allowing a bound player to summon his corpse and items cheaply, will not upset class balance for anyone except possibly at the highest levels of the game. I could understand if there are certain zones where a body cannot be summoned. (planes VP etc) But I do not feel that a human warrior, bound in the Arena, hunting in Cazic Thule, should have to pay a 30 minute or more longer downtime penalty, that the rest of his caster group, who are bound just outside the zone.

As it is currently implemented, the game of EQ is balanced as far as gameplay and player power. It is far from balanced though in player convience and enjoyment. Bards are one of the most underplayed classes. They are, argueably, one of the best classes in the game. The reason they are played so rarely is based upon out of game reasons far more than in game. To be a good bard requires more work and manual dexterity, upon the part of the player in real life, than any other class. Additionaly, the bard requires more xp to gain the same level of rewards as a non hybird. So, in effect, the game balance of EQ says that to be a bard you must kill more to earn the same levels, you must work harder sitting behind the keyboard, you must accept that you have low hps and will die alot but cant bind anywhere near where you will die and you must accept this system because...your group could be too powerful if we didnt restrict you. A large amount of the downtime penalties are imposed upon a small percantage of the classes. Lets make this fair for all involved.

of all the aspects of Downtime in EQ the one that is most damaging and most discouraging to players is the one that keeps them from playing at all. That is the Downtime from assembling a group and getting to a destination. At higher levels it usually takes in excess of an HOUR to get into a group and get the group assembled for combat at a dungeon. Efficiency only goes so far here since you are dealing with 5 other people at least one of which may be remote or slow, or delayed etc. The group MUST wait for all members to be present before venturing into the dungeon. If anyone leaves from the group then the odds are very high that the rest of the group members must also leave at the same time. The group usually must evac out and try to find another member to replace the lost one and reassemble at the dungeon to enter it again from the start. This situation exacerbates the up front grouping Downtime because of those players that have only 2-3 hours at stretch to play. By the time they are in a group and ready to enter the dungeon they may only have an hour and a half to 2 hours left to play. Other group members are VERY reluctant to take group members who only have a limited time to play and then force the group to evac and find a replacement and start all over again.
If you only have say 3 hours to play and you cannot get in a group and into a dungeon within an hour you may not be able to get a group at all that playtime and are essentially forced to camp out having achieved nothing but frustration. I cannot tell you how often this has happened to me. This does not make me enjoy the game it only contributes to a growing frustration at not being able to play a game I enjoy.
This situation also greatly restricts which dungeons I am able to adventure in because to get a group in a timely manner I must camp at a highly populated dungeon so my chances of getting a group are reasonable within the time limits. =================
On Solving the problem of Downtime:

Here is the problem, the downtime is forced into the
game to moderate the advancement of players and balance
the magic and melee against the environment. Without
any downtime the players become god-like quake players
that have continuous combat without any reason for
any other players or classes.

So usually Mana and Health and perhaps Stamina are used to
pace the game a certain way and balance things out.

Here is my solution, considering an EQ-like model as a baseline.
(UO2 seems to have magic as a time based recharge/spell system,
with some spells taking a week to recharge) Others have talked
about how to do it. Basically taking all that combat downtime and
combining it into a single pool of downtime later or before
normal combat.

Have another pool of "energy" that the player can decide
to use up. Call it their daily endurance, rather than their
instantaneous endurance. This daily endurance regenerates when
logged out or in taverns.

So the scenario in EQ is that a person casts all their mana
or uses up all their stamina in combat and they have to
sit down and wait for it to regen in the middle of the
dungeon. Their instantaneous mana and stamina have been
expended. In the new scenario, that same player would be
able to decide to either sit and wait for the mana to
regen, or use some of their daily endurance energy to refill
themselves immediately. Now they would have to be careful
because that energy is limited and the smart player would
use that only when they have to for emergencies. Also the
mage could convert it to mana, the warrior to stamina or
health, the hybrids could convert it to both. The player
decides how they want to spend that daily energy.

This allows many possibilities, some casual players may
only have an hour to play. So they set their energy regen
to automatic and play for one hour with excellent
mana/health/stamina regen. Then they fall back to normal.
So they log or go to a tavern or inn to regen back that daily
energy. The players that want to stay online longer would
carefully use up that daily energy pool for emergencies or
here and there when they weren't in a group and if they
wanted to regen they would go to a city tavern and check
the bulletin boards or auction/employment/mission/quest boards
while regenerating their daily endurance.

So you have effectively transfered the downtime control to
the players hands. They can decide if they want to spend their
daily allowance at immediate constant combat, or for emergencies
or not at all. They control their own downtime.
You have given casual players something for their play style.
And you have given hard core players the ability to have
bursts of mana/stamina or health when they need to for hard fights.
Once they use up their daily endurance, then they go back to the
normal downtime rates. This may help get those players off your
busy server and free up bandwidth when they log off to regenerate.
Instead of staying online overnight for long periods of regenerating.
The downtime regeneration occurs offline just like a person's
downtime. When they sleep, their character sleeps. Now you tell
me, well what stops them from Pleveling one char logging off
and Pleveling another char, well that daily endurance could work
by account instead of by character. Or not, depending on the
balancing.

Now the rates of exchange, and speed of regeneration would have
to be balanced, but the model could still work. For example it would
take alot of daily endurance energy to convert that pool to health.
It wouldn't cost much to convert to stamina, and it would cost a
fair bit to transfer it to mana.

Binds could be more readily available, but the bind-o-rama that the power-levelers here want is NOT an answer. The big issue seems to be not being able to bind when you hit a new region (not a new zone, but you're on a different landmass etc.)

Karana is too large. Every single whine I see about the amount of time it takes to walk somewhere is about Karana. And they're right. It's large, it's dangerous and it's dangerous in the same ways from zone to zone.

Death is terribly fearful. People are afraid to die and this affects their actions. This is IMHO a VGT (very good thing). I'm tired of games where death is meaningless (AC) or frustratingly inevitable (UO during it's psychopath-PK days). I've found dying in the wrong place to be a monstrous irritation--I've been even more irritated that it has almost always been my fault for doing something stupid.

The issue of those of us who are adults with jobs and 4-6 hours of playtime a week vs. people with 4-12 hours of playtime a day is a VERY real one. I think it would be an excellent idea to put a realtime limited server up for casual gamers. This would eliminate some of the ubercamping and the economic distortions caused by uber-gamers vs. normal people. Just set up a server marked -20 and at 19 hours of gametime within the last 7 days, it lets you know you have an hour and when you hit 20, you log. You can add to the number of irritations that we casual gamers have, that it's harder to make long term friends because we meet a young character, help them, and the next time we see them they are 14 levels higher because they don't have other obligations.

What really makes me fearful about a lot of the suggestions to nerf things for low-time gamers is that they would be a HUGE invite for k0olios to use the shortened time to become ultimate power-levelers. It takes too long for Spawn X. If you make the time 1/2 as long, will it mean you wait half as long or the a do0D camps it twice?

Frankly, I wouldn't mind an adults only server. I find it disturbing when I feel I have to limit my in game comments because I don't know who I'm gaming with. I'm seldom obscene or even off color and generally roleplay, but a mildly off color comment is a slightly different thing when I discover the ranger who's g-stringed butt I just commented on with a double entendre after staring at it for 5 minutes on auto-follow turns out to be a 13 year old girl. If you created an adults only server, this would eliminate this problem as forbidden fruit being what it is, it would concentrate non-adults on the "adults only" server :-)

The problems with requiring mixed parties is one of the blessings of EQ. If you want to solo, I think maybe you gotta go buy an AC account--it's easy there. This probably accounts for my one good friend after a year of AC vs. my dozen or so good friends after a week in EQ. It can be irritating and require time to assemble a party, but it is a sacrifice that adds an INCREDIBLE amount to the game.

While we're on AC, a great many of the changes people here want are available in AC--instant gates, painless death, unlimited solo ability. And frankly, they have negative affects on roleplaying and the amount of socialization people have. It's a tradeoff and IMHO I very much like the EQ equation.

On socialization and downtime in cities. There is absolutely nothing to do in cities other than sell and train and hail looking for quests. There's no focal point. SELL SOME CLOTHES! Not armor, not class based, but simply clothing meant for going out on the town. In AC, there were already a large variety of colored clothes, but they just added dresses to AC rather than men's only styles and it has added an incredible amount to roleplaying within towns. Getting interesting clothing creates an atmosphere for non-combat time which promotes socializing and a constructive use of city downtime. Imagine a group of sharply dressed folks sitting in the local tavern looking to put together a party vs "OOC: Druid 14 LFG" And it gives you something to do before all the members of your dream party arrive. You would be simply amazed at what putting pretty clothes in front of people does.

And those most angry of the complainers: I hate to say it, but it's your playstyle. You are obsessed with leveling and items and getting ahead. Don't you just enjoy the game? I don't worry about how I'm getting ahead or anything else. I group with people because I enjoy their company. The game mechanics promote this and I welcome it.

Like the general comment to PKers in UO, "aren't you looking for Quake?" I have to say, for the "I wanna solo and get my items and XP" types, aren't you looking for Diablo II?

By the way, I don't buy the "customer is always right" arguments. These things have implications which in some cases it's obvious have not been fully thought out by people.

And accusing VI of having a "vision" thing similar to Origin destroying UO with it's PK vision is simply insane. I don't care how much downtime you have, it's simply not as bad nor is it as obviously without merit.

I think a big problem with EQ down time is that the risk vs. reward isn't balanced due to the following factor:
Risk is constant.

Reward is random.

If I go on a Planar Raid, I am not going for experience. Raids are a bad source of experience, and a good source of loot. In fact, going to the planes almost means CERTAIN death, but there's a small chance of getting any of the nice equipment that drops in that plane, and less chance of getting a decent amount of exp.

Look around the game and you'll find that there are often many more risks than rewards, and the penalty of a risk (amount of Exp lost for dying) is generally greater, and more constant, than the rewards (amount of exp for killing something, phat lewt dropping off a mob)

The problem that people complain about isn't that there is downtime, I believe. It's the fact that that down time is constant and you know it will happen, and it's the same every time. But you can go out and seek a reward, spend several hours pursuing it, have all the requisite components of a group on hand, be able to gain the reward in every facet except for random chance, and that random chance is what keeps you from getting a reward.
