This post addresses the balancing issue of damage amount, mana efficiency, and damage over time. It is a simple set of examples of what Grimstaff and others refer to as increasing the wizard's damage potential. Its final section deals with grouping.

To illustrate what I mean, I would like to compare the stats for the level 49 Ice Comet and level 51 Draught of Fire spells. As a level 52 wizard, I use DoF as my primary spell because it exemplifies the concepts of fast damage and mana efficiency.

The Draught of Fire stats are:
-----------------------------------------
Decrease Hit Points (HP) from 643 to 688
Range to Target: 200 feet
Mana Required: 215
Casting Time: 3.0 seconds
Spell Recovery: 2.25 seconds
-----------------------------------------

The Ice Comet stats are:
-----------------------------------------
Decrease Hit Points from 1110 to 1120
Range to Target: 200 feet
Mana Required: 400
Casting Time: 6.30 seconds
Spell Recovery: 2.50 seconds
-----------------------------------------

Fast Damage
-----------
You can see from the stats that a wizard can cast five DoF spells in about the same amount of time that it takes to cast three IC spells. That adds up to 3460 DoF damage to 3360 IC damage over about 26 seconds.

But what is far more important, is that a wizard can cast DoF more often ... once every 5.25 seconds for DoF versus once every 8.80 seconds for IC.

The fast reaction time of the DoF spell makes it a preferred spell in a group because it can hit a mob more often which is what wizards are supposed to do.

The Ice Comet spell loses here because of its long cast and recovery times.

The conclusion is that wizards need more spells with short cast and recovery times; not more spells with long cast and recovery times.

Mana Efficiency
---------------
This one is simple also. The DoF spell ratio for damage versus mana (688/215 = 3.2) is much better than the Ice Comet ratio (1120/400 = 2.8).

The DoF spell ratio in its initial form (643/215 = 2.99...) is still slightly better than the IC ratio in its final form.

The Ice Comet spell loses again because it uses 400 mana to cast for 1120 damage where the DoF spell uses 430 mana for 1296 damage (2x casts).

The conclusion is that wizards need more mana efficient spells as we achieve levels.

Damage Over Time
----------------
This section is for the Uber-Mobs in Velious where wizards run out of mana long before the Uber-Mob is dead. If you compare the DoF spell cast time to the IC spell cast time, you can see that a wizard can sit for 3.75 seconds between DoF casts.

This changes the damage amount in favor of the IC spell because the DoF spell does 2064 damage over 26 seconds where the IC spell does 3360 damage.

You would think that the IC spell has an advantage here; however, the wizard has lost 1200 mana casting the IC spell compared to a loss of 645 mana casting the DoF spell. And the wizard gets to sit for 11.25 seconds per three cast cycle recovering mana.

If you carry this analogy out far enough, you can see that the IC spell will eventually lose because the wizard has no time to recover mana; he will run out of mana; therefore, he must sit and meditate.

The DoF spell, however, uses about half the mana and gives the wizard time to sit and meditate between casts.

The conclusion is that wizards need highly mana efficient spells with very low cast and recovery times to be useful against any Uber-Mob.

Summary
-------
Short cast and recovery times are good for wizards; long cast and recovery times are bad.

Mana efficiency is good for wizards because the more mana efficient a spell is, the more damage it does per cast; hence, the more damage it will do over time.

Short cast and recovery times provide more time for wizards to sit and meditate between casts. High mana efficiency provides lower mana usage which reduces the time a wizard must sit and meditate. This combination allows us to fight the Uber-Mobs. Anything else is bad.

Increasing mana efficiency per spell makes changing our mana recovery rate a moot point. There has to be a balance between the pool size, its usage, and its recovery rate.

Group Friendly
--------------
Before I start this section, I should like to say that I agree with Grimstaff and Absor ... a wizard's primary jobs are fast damage and evacs (or portals).

If the wizard class is properly balanced with all the wizard bugs removed from the resistant mobs, the wizard can be the "heavy artillery" of any group once again.

That should be enough to make our class "group friendly". If it isn't, then wizards can make their own groups ... two tanks, one cleric, one enchanter and two wizards, or three wizards depending on the mobs and location.

The most efficient group I have ever been in for gaining experience had no meleers and no clerics in it. It consists of an enchanter, a mage, and me ... the wizard. The enchanter pulled, I snared, the mage pet rooted and we all blasted.

We started in The Overthere; then the Dreadlands (we needed tanks for the Dread Widow); we are in Burning Woods now; last will be SkyFire and the Kunark dungeons.

And then on to Velious ... with more wizards, I hope. *smile*

Balance Issue:
Wizards on Raids.

Absor, I know you want to skim, but this post is something I believe important, and so I will add as much background as I can.

I am a 52 Channeler, and have been on raids from Dalnir to Fear to Kael. I am frequently placed in “caster” groups. This is because of the way wizard hp totals work, and only a secondary healer is required to keep wizards alive. Either the shaman can heal me up, or not. Either way, CH would take too long so clerics are better left with warriors.

Tanks chanters and clerics are in their own groups (frequently with rogues). They’re “tank” or “melee” groups. The split of these two types of groups is extraordinarily commonplace, and so I have a feeling that every caster class knows what I am talking about.

Typically there are 3 tank groups and 2 caster groups on big raids, the number can vary quite a bit, but typically not by more than +/- 2 groups.

Whenever we kill things on raids, the caster group never gets xp. What? Never? No. Never. I would like to state right off the bat that it is NOT because we are outdamaged. Frequently we’re killing things with 10k hp. Two wizards casting ice comets can pump out 4.4k dmg themselves in 16 seconds. Being in a group with all casters means that the mage is tossing out dmg, the necro is dotting, and the shaman is dotting, not to mention pets. In total a caster group easily does 60% of the damage to a given monster.

There is often more than one caster group in a raid, which puts the melee groups totals even lower since a given monster is not alive for more than 16 seconds.

However, when the mob falls, the xp does NOT go to the group that has done the most damage… something is very very wrong. It goes to one of the melee groups, the caster damage is wholly ignored when divvying up xp for slayings on raids involving multiple groups.

Another example:

I was in a pseudo-caster group with 2 wizards, druid, shaman, ranger and shadow knight. We were fighting a spite golem (totally immune to magic, unless lures are used—even then 50% resists). The wizards were at the time 51, and so we relied totally on the shadow knight and ranger to do damage. The SK tossed out a HT, did some melee and the ranger went to it, and lo and behold, we got xp! From Wizard Balance board 9 (page 5) posted Feb.10 (reformatted here):
---------------------------

PROBLEM: Wizards in many encounters are less desirable than other classes due to their reliance on a finite pool of mana, low relative damage, downtime and lack of DoTs, pets or group buffs.

IDEA: Increase effectiveness of some Wizard spells according to how many other people are in the Wizard's group.

DETAILS: Instead of giving wizards these other abilities, allow the wizard to “Draw Power” from the other members of his/her party. This ability would scale similar to the group experience bonus, and would allow a wizard to contribute more in larger groups.

Things that could be scaled based on number of people in the wizard’s group:

1. Decreased mana cost for certain spells, based on group size.
2. Decreased resistance for certain spells, based on group size.
3. Decreased casting time for certain spells, based on group size.
4. Increased maximum damage for certain spells, based on group size.
5. Increased mana regeneration rate, based on group size.

If any or all of these were implemented, a wizard would become a more valuable member of a group, while not increasing solo capability in any way.

Verant has once stated that they were interested in ideas about wizards “chain casting” spells between one more casters to encourage grouping. This idea is similar.

---------------------------

This scaleability could even be further customized both for number of people in a group as well as the kinds of classes (for instance, more fighter-types could improve damage ability; more caster types could lower resistance, etc.). This could also be scaled against the relative size of the opponent. (Bigger opponents = bigger benefit).

There were a number of similar posts throughout the Wizard discussion along these lines. Many good ideas have been put forth, designed to make the wizard a desired companion, particularly on high-level raids.

OK you people won't go to a distinct web page to read some more on the topic, and it doesn't matter if there's a bit more logorrheia on the message board, so here's my answer for each item (sorry for referring to some of my web pages, but these references are LINKS in my original text; by the way I don't think that the item of "group friendly" should be added):

ITEM: Increased mana regeneration.
Obviously this is meant to help play in full group, NOT to help Wizards kill tough mobs in solo. I doubt that the problem is severe (I just added my analysis of the topic in my page on exciting Wizard fighting, point 3), but it could be solved by means of an automatical mana regeneration bonus in group, working the same way than the experience bonus. And/or there could be a mana regeneration bonus in dungeons (not necessarily the same for each dungeon; the experience bonus per dungeon can be a guideline). Typically, a Wizard will solo only outdoors, not in a dungeon, because there's not enough room to kite, and Root bombing is unreliable (actually, Druids, who are supposed to be "masters of the outdoors", solo better than Wizards in dungeons, thanks to Root dotting, which is more reliable than Root bombing).

ITEM: Spell efficiency.
Except for the comparison with Magicians, this item is essentially the same as the previous one. Personally I don't mind if my DD spells are only about 10% more potent than the Magician DD spells, as long as they are quicker, and I have personal defense spells which Magicians don't have, and exclusive and superior AE damage to compensate for the absence of a pet. The quality of our AE damage is a problem, but that's what my site is all about, so I won't try to summarize it in a few words here. I have another idea for the famous "massive monster" problem, which was felt in Kunark already, but it might require too much programming: the Wizard DD spells could "resonate" with higher-than-average life energy, and produce increased effects when the mob's "hit point / level" ratio is above certain values. However, I guess that the NAMED RARE SPAWNS in Velious were made with high hit points specially to avoid people killing them solo, so, again, the reality of the problem is uncertain. Note that the outdoor solo capabilities of Wizards were made substantially higher, when Root and Snare became stackable (the mana regeneration, even taking into account the mana spent to maintain the mob Rooted and Snared, tends to be higher than the mob hit point regeneration), and I feel I can take on mobs bigger than before.

ITEM: Percentage Damage Spells.
Why not. There was such a spell in old Diablo, it was called "Bone spirit" (it took away 1/3 of a mob's hit points). Forcibly, the hate-building would be a fixed amount, regardless of how many hit points were removed (spells in general create a fixed amount of aggro, regardless of the results); but here, to obtain maximum results, there is a temptation to use the spell very early in the fight, creating an interesting dilemma regarding the timing of the cast... still, I believe that such spells would open the door to endless game balance issues (they could be too powerful on Dragons and Gods, etc).

ITEM: Level Cap on spells.
Ooo yes, this is a very old injustice. Don't remove the cap entirely, but make sure that Wizards are reasonably able to affect deep reds which fighters of same level are reasonably able to hit.

ITEM: Al'Kabor line of spells.
Mmm... I didn't catch the pun, but never mind ! Beware that we already have an AE spell with lasting stun (Thunderbold, level 54). Besides, an all-or-nothing spell with split-second stun interrupting spellcasting has very little value if the stats are bad (the level 39 Al'Kabor spell already functions that way, and it isn't better than the others). Adding a few more targets may be an acceptable solution post-50, but not pre-50 because the spells are far too weak. An AE debuff would be great; see my page on Al'Kabor.

ITEM: Flaming Sword of Xuzl.
Maybe I'm misreading, but I think that making Mordenkainen's Sword (oops, the Flaming Sword of Xuzl) "immune" isn't the point. The Sword must never generate any aggro toward itself, so it can't be killed by targeted damage, and it can't act as a defensive lure (so Wizards don't tread on the pet classes' grounds). Of course, it must generate a bit of aggro toward the Wizard. Now there's the case of non-targeted damage such as riposte and AE damage. It could be unfair to make the Sword "immune" to this, whereas regular pets are not. The solution lies in hit points. As long as the Sword can't act as a defensive lure, it doesn't matter if it has a lot of hit points or only a few; just decide how much riposte and AE damage the Sword should be able to withstand, and give it hit points and resistances accordingly. By the way, were the graphics corrected ? The Sword has to be flaming.

ITEM: Harvest.
I CAN HELP YOU with the spell gem bug. This is not specific to Harvest, I had the problem in the past with Tishan's Clash. There is a vicious link between certain hotkeys (when the hotkey is used to activate an item such as Staff of Temperate Flux, Solist's Icy Wand or Journeyman's Boots) and certain spell gems, and the outcome is that the spell gem is untimely refreshed on the client side when the item's effect fires off. The spell gems and hotkeys can be switched and moved until there no problem remains (I did that); for instance, the deficient spell gems can be used for "instant" recast spells such as DD, so that the untimely refreshment is irrlevant; but it would be better if the bug was analyzed and fixed.
As for Harvest not being effective post-50, all I can say is it's still somewhat good at level 48 (if only the full meditation time wasn't longer and longer...) If Harvest starts giving less than the standard three-quarters-of-a-bubble beyond a certain level, it's disappointing. I don't mind if you allow us to cast the spell more often, but NOT if the price to pay is a smaller mana gain: being able to summon that much mana in one shot is a precious asset, and I don't want to lose it.

ITEM: Rain Spells.
YES ! take the pets out of the equation. YES ! Reduce the resist rates a bit. YES ! Add a wave (but it depends on what you call a "wave", I suspect you mean "hits": I usually say that Rains generate 4 "hits" in 3 "waves", because the concept of "wave" holds the idea of one or more things occurring at a given moment, with a repetitive pattern; two "waves" can't logically occur at the same time). The pre-50 Rains definitely need more hits (1 or 2 more), or larger hits. For the game's sake, I warn you: do not add hits for the post-50 Rains; they are already very good, and fixing the pet bug and the resist rates will be quite enough for them.

ITEM: Atol's Spectral Shackles.
The fizzle obviously needs a fix, or I might continue to use Bonds of Force instead when I'm 51.

ITEM: Snare/Enstill.
About Bonds of Force: sorry, you're 100% wrong, it ISN'T odd. You must remember that this is a late spell, added some months after the game was released. Most of the late additions to the Wizard repertoire (Bonds of Force, Harvest, Invisibility to Undead) have had a special twist in it, allowing Wizards to do something which they were totally unable to do before, and which looks like a specialty belonging to other classes; but with a special cost, limitation or drawback. Consider that the AE feature of Bonds of Force is primarily an INTENTIONAL DRAWBACK, because, in solo, it gives only one chance to stick (if failed, the Wizard will be in melee, and runs the risk of Snaring him/herself with the second attempt, and must switch to Root, Yonder or Tishan's Clash to regain distance), and IT SHOULD BE SO. The fact that Root and Snare can now stack has moderated the drawback already (we can Root, and then make additional Snare attempts). If you change Bonds of Force to single-target, you will be giving Wizards the message: "let your group down, and go out kiting mobs in solo like Druids do". The opportunity to Snare a few mobs at once sometimes compensates for the drawback and for the high mana cost. Changing the spell to single-target wouldn't "make more sense": Druids don't get AE Snare, and Wizards don't get single-target Snare, nothing wrong with that. If you want to reduce quad-kiting, the solution lies in nerfing the Column/Pillar spells with shorter range, as you once suggested, NOT in changing Bonds of Force, which is a honest and distinctive spell as it is.
About Enstill (and Paralyzing Earth): yes, these spells have a problem, they never repay their double mana cost and longer cast time. Giving a fixed duration (45 seconds ?) would be fair - still, I would probably continue to use Immobilize even for parking mobs away from a fight, because Immobilize (or Root) is cheaper and more versatile, and I wouldn't want to memorize both. But I might use a fixed-duration Enstill as an opening spell to try and prevent double pull. Besides, these spells are not Wizard-only issues (I'm sure that Paladins, who can't even Gate, would travel much more safely across dangerous zones if they had an Enstill with fixed duration).

ITEM: Invisibility to Undead.
Why not ? Are Wizards supposed to be POWERLESS against undead ? We can affect them with DD, like any other mob. Consider that undead have some sense for perceiving their environment (creatures and objects), and we know that this sense is different from normal vision; but, whatever this sense consists in (infravision, ultravision, radar, electro-magnetic scan, or mystical divination), some form of energy is needed to convey the perception. Wizards happen to be masters of energies (not "elements", that's for Magicians): heat, cold, electricity, "force" (which can be understood either as gravity and magnetism, or as "pure" magical energy), AND light (sight spells, invisibility). It is reasonable to assume that Wizards have identified the type of energy used by undead to "see", and have found a way to fiddle with it. The spell is fair because it comes late (level 39), is self-only, and has a high mana cost (100). Necromancers get a self-only invisibility versus living, and Wizards get a self-only invisibility versus undead; nothing wrong with that.
By the way, Wizards being masters of energies including light, they could be given some more light-based spells such as Flash of light. Almost everybody can Fear or Intimidate in this game, why not us ? But I must admit that we're powerful enough without it.

ITEM: Teleport - West Karana.
It's funny that such a super-old claim is coming up again; the first time I complained about this was summer 1999. If you don't want to rebuild the zone, I have an alternate solution: make sure that Abscond takes the party next to the Qeynos Hills zone, and make Abscond a level 39 spell (same as West Karana Portal). Reduce the cost of Abscond a bit too, so that a level 39 Wizard can afford it. I don't remember if Abscond is dropped, or on sale at vendors, but make sure that the intermediate-level Wizards can get it. Abscond isn't a powerful spell, you can do all this. I know that Abscond is primarily meant to be an escape spell and not a travelling help, but there can be exceptions; Siren's Grotto is already an exception.

ITEM: Teleport - Cazic Thule Gate.
This is not a big deal, if only because there are ways to arrive invisible at destination (I made a page on Cazic Gate), and, when you're higher level, you don't fear the lizards. Some teleport locations ARE unsafe, it adds some spice. West Commons is marginally dangerous for Good Wizards, because of the occasional Kizdean Gix or Dragoon. North Karana is marginally dangerous for Evil Wizards, because of the occasional Qeynos guard. Cobalt Scar is substantially dangerous, but invisibility helps. The one and only dysfunctional portal is Wakening Lands, because of the "zone-in aggro bug" (when there's lag, your personal faction adjustments are taken into account some time later than when your character's body appears in the zone, so, Wuoshi freezes you even though you have worked your faction up, and he even sees invisible; I don't use this portal except intra-zone, or when I'm in a big hurry - I died unduly once and it's enough). The Wizard portals might be a bit more dangerous than the Druid counterparts overall, but this is probably because the Combine empire was over-confident and chose the locations less wisely than the Druids did. We are bright, and we can cope with the challenge.

ITEM: Fade, shadowstep and yonder.
If I'm correct, these spells allowed to go uphill when the game was first released, but this caused a few Wizards to end up perched at great heights in some places, and they were whining and calling GMs, so the spells were MADE unable to go uphill. Once you know the limitation, it can be dealt with; it doesn't limit the spells' potential that much. But it can be changed back to allowing uphill travel, I don't mind.

ITEM: Evacs.
Good idea. I also want to be able to make linkdead party members invisible, to help them survive if we're obliged to leave before they can log back on.

ITEM: Evacs and Abscond.
I don't know Abscond, and I didn't know that intra-zone Evac was meant to clear aggro.

ITEM: Abscond.
I don't know Abscond. It should be made lower-level (see "Teleport - West Karana" above).

ITEM: Concussion spell scroll.
I can think of a very good reason why the spell is only level 39, and still impossible to obtain without the help of 50+ players. Unless used preventively (I do it a lot), Concussion serves to break free from melee, so, spell interruption is a critical factor. You know that, when using a spell 10 levels below ours, we get a big bonus for regaining concentration. So, I believe that the spell CAN be 39 and still BE MEANT FOR 49+ Wizards; the level difference is nothing more and nothing less than a boon for casting it more easily in melee. Besides, sub-50 Wizards can still count on Root or Immobilize to break free; it is beyond 50 that Summoning makes it much wiser to pass unnoticed instead of counting on Root. Now, if a Wizard is able to get the scroll early, good for him or her.

ITEM: Melee-proof NPCs.
Looks like a false good idea to me. The poor fighters are down-trodden enough already.

ITEM: Diamond Rod.
I agree (although the Rod still saves a spell slot and some mana). If you remove the component cost, give me one of these and you can keep the Epic Quest weapon ! I think that the component was introduced because of the Manastone abuse. I believe that the Manastone was nerfed (doesn't drop anymore, and is shut off on the Planes and in the extensions) because there existed loopholes (the most simple abuse being Clerics eating up all their hit points to gain mana, and rinsing and repeating with Complete Heal, gaining hefty mana in the process). Well, a Diamond rod with no component would allow the same abuse, because the Manastone eats up the shield before it begins to eat up hit points. The Staff of the Four will allow the abuse too (besides allowing this abuse, it is one of the lousiest Epic Quest weapons of all, I mean, the effect is good and comfortable, but not Epic at all, we deserved something offensive). The point is that the Diamondskin spell itself munches a peridot. Only post-50 buffs should munch peridots; level 44 spells should use less expensive stones, 3 or 4 pp each, grand maximum.

ITEM: Imbue Fire Opal Scroll.
As far as I know, this spell serves for trade skills, and I have no opinion.

ITEM: Eye of Zomm.
I agree: Eyes are wonderful especially to explore pits, because they don't take falling damage, but as soon as they fall in water the fun is over. For the game's sake, you should make the Eye immune to player attacks, just as regular pets are (I won't tell you why, my Necro friend will be upset).

ITEM: Damage spell variety.
This is exxagerated. We never have to fall back more than one spell rank, and some ranks have two DD spells of different energy types (one heat or cold, and one "magic"). The only period in a Wizard's career when there's some serious trouble is levels 20-23, because the new DD (Force Shock) is all-or-nothing and unreliable, while the previous spell (Flame Shock) is much smaller, and at the same time the melee capabilities of Wizards, which used to be an appreciated complementary source of damage, become ridiculous versus mob toughness. But this is not really a matter of choice between different energy types, and is probably an intentional trial.

ITEM: Critical Hits with Spells.
More damage, I would say. From former discussions, the main point which I remember is that the extra damage mustn't generate extra aggro (a fully resisted spell does the same aggro as a spell doing full damage, so the amount of damage should be left out of the equation altogether).

ITEM: Target too low level bug.
Seems annoying.

ITEM: Quad Kiting.
Yes, it is hard and time-consuming. At least with the level 34 Circle of Force; I tried a few times and gave up. The most annoying thing with quad-kiting is that it makes other classes bitter and jealous, and with good reason; it's not that way I hope to shine in this game. You have suggested somewhere that the range (not radius) of Columns/Pillars could be reduced so that the spells can be profitably used only in regular group play; that's an excellent idea.

ITEM: Suppression spells.
Why not a Silence spell like in AD&D, preventing all spellcasting ? (Ha ha, joke). At any rate, I wouldn't want a mob to cast an anti-Gate spell on me.
I've got a related question: on giants/dragons/gods, stun spells and Mesmerize have a duration of zero. BUT... do they still interrupt spellcasting ? Giant spellcasters are rare, and there are usually a dozen players hitting them at the same time, so, it seems that no one was ever able to tell for sure if the zero-duration stun/mezz interrupted spells or not. Now that many Velious Giants are able to gate, I'd like to know if we're all wasting our time trying to stun them to prevent gating when they're low on life.

ITEM: Blink Spell.
I would never use such a spell, it would be much more disruptive than useful (it will make our group lose fights, and make us lose our body when travelling). But you may make the cast time of Shadowstep/Yonder even shorter if you want, almost instantaneous; this wouldn't hurt. Besides, two successive casts are often needed to gain enough distance to be safe, so, the recast time is an issue too.

ITEM WHICH IS NOT MENTIONED IN THE LIST:
A reasonable upgrade to the Solist's Icy Wand. I know that you don't want to do the same mistake than with Jaundiced Bone Bracers and Elder Spiritist Vambraces (although you SHOULD nerf them, or at least end the drop, if you regret having put them in the game). But there's room between a weak item and an overpowered item. For instance, the Solist's Icy Wand is overpowered in the hands of a level 13-19 Wizard (you should be more careful with the minimum level required to activate such items), but it's a fair item from level 20 to 38, getting quickly obsolete afterwards. Moderately high level Wizards need an item with a "damage/cast time" ratio about 4-6 times lower than the "damage / (cast + recast) time" ratio of their current DD. This is not good enough to replace our spells when quick damage is needed, but allows us to inflict a better base load of damage (better than casting and medding) when there's no hurry... If the item's ratio is 10+ times lower than our DD's ratio, the item is plain useless, because it's better, on the long term, to just cast real spells and sit inbetween.
