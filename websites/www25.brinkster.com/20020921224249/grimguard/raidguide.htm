One goal of this guild is to get our members to higher levels to go places that only large, organized groups are able to go. To this end, another goal is to prepare our guild members for such raids with lower level "training" raids. We will be having various raids planned for everyone in the guild to participate in, as well as other group/party events, such as:

  • Faction Raids (where party members can raise desired factions for quests or removing KOS status)
  • Dueling Tournaments (with the goal of learning the strengths and weaknesses of other classes)
  • PvP Practice Raids (taking groups into the Arena against higher level members to learn raid tactics)
  • Training Events (where trade skills training and language teaching, amongst other things, will occur)
  • And much, much more...

But here I want to discuss with my fellow Guildies some basic guidelines and suggestions for Party Raids. A Party Raid is usually a combination of two or more groups with one common goal - to kill as much as they can and get as much loot as possible. With this in mind, there are many things to consider and remember when participating in such an event.

1. Do not be afraid to die.
This is a very important rule, believe it or not. Raids are not typically designed for gaining experience, but rather for the fun, the exploration, and the loot. An unfortunate "side effect" of raiding is character deaths, sometimes more than once. It' s a fact of raiding that is unavoidable, but with practice and coordination, can be kept to a minimum. So do not get mad or frustrated if you die, because it will happen to everyone at least once. But most importantly - DO NOT PANIC.

2. Try not to focus on your own hit points.
This basically follows #1. Not only is it a distraction from whatever your task may be, but it can also prove deadly to everyone. If you are running low on health, it is very easy to panic, run off, or shout frantically for help. This however will only serve to create more confusion and distraction, and could end killing many more people, with the potential to wipe out the entire raiding party. Therefore, DO NOT run or panic. If you're hitpoints are getting low, don't start yelling for a heal - it is your group healer's responsibility to heal you and they are watching all hitpoints. If you do end up dying, wait until you are sent a tell from the Raid Cleric(s) - do not send /tells yourself as this can create more confusion and distraction if the group is still fighting. We will have a rez-capable cleric at every guild raid so that we can get our members back into action as soon as possible, as well as to minimize the experience loss from death. This is important because every member of a party raid will have an assigned task, whether it is healing, buffing, nuking, snaring, melee, etc. We need to recover as soon as we can from mishaps to maintain our efficiency and effectiveness. Remember that no matter how small you may think your role is, it isn't. Everyone in a raid party is equally important to the overall success.

3. Main healers should focus only on their group's hit point bars.
As I said before, we want more than anything else to maintain our focus and think clearly and quickly. The primary healers should not be concerned with the combat around them, but rather the status of the hit point bars of their group mates (every group will have an assigned primary healer). Should the primary healer come under attack, they should announce so in group say (/gsay). Melees and pets should attempt to taunt the attacker off of the healer, even if it means abandoning their current target (unless that target is on the run and near death). I cannot stress enough the importance of keeping your group's primary healer alive. I have seen many good groups overcome seeming insurmountable odds because they were able to keep their healers alive, and I have seen many strong groups die to inferior enemies because their healer was eliminated. To minimize distraction, the main healers are the only ones that should be asking for assistance in /gsay if under attack. (In the planes, it is of the utmost importance that clerics survive. It costs 250pp to port one group into the planes, and if things go bad, clerics are told to camp out so that they can rez people back into the zone and thereby minimize the expense of ports, as well as the pain in returning to the camp point.)

4. Listen to your Raid Director and Group Leaders.
The Raid Director will generally have had a lot of experience in large groups and raid events, or at the least should know the zone quite well, so you should feel much safer under their direction. They will use /shout for directions, instructions, or combat commands (i.e., if there is a certain target all groups should focus on due to strength, abilities, etc.). Group Leaders are to use only /ooc. Excess communication is unnecessary and to be avoided so nothing is missed. No other raider should use /shout or /ooc. If you are lost or in need of something, tell your group, and the Group Leader will use /ooc if necessary. If for any reason a raider has to go /afk in RL, tell your group. The raid doesn't necessarily have to be halted unless you are the primary healer, mezzer, or assist. If the group leader feels that his party cannot function properly, announce the /afk in /ooc so that the Raid Director can act accordingly.

5. Turn off all chat modes except /shout, /ooc and /gsay.
I can again not stress enough the importance of maintaining focus and minimizing distraction. The Raid Director and Group Leaders will use /shout and /ooc to communicate to everyone as necessary. During the raid it is recommended that everyone minimize non-related chat so that they don't miss something, such as a sudden drop in a group member's hit points, or an urgent command issued by a director or group leader. I also recommend using /afk with a message describing your status, such as "/afk Participating in a raid right now, please try and reach me later, thanks". This is generally sufficient for most people to understand that you are preoccupied and unavailable for discussion. But also understand, there will be some down periods when there is nothing wrong with joking around or recovering your wits from the previous close-encounter with death. Just be ready to get serious quickly if the action picks up again, or if the Directors instruct the party to move ahead, or brief everyone on what is going to occur next. Also make sure you turn off "combat spam", such as "Other Hits", "Other Misses", etc. If you don't know how to do this, ask a group mate for assistance. Battle spam can also be very distracting and combined with lag, could cause you to miss an important order or directive. There will be no reason to participate in /guild chat while on a raid, as this also detracts from focus and adds too much extra text flashing by.

6. Make an /assist macro if you haven't already.
This little macro will prove to be extremely useful, whether in groups or in raids, particularly for melees. All you need to do is take a blank social box, name it "Assist", and on the first line type "/assist name". "Name" will be replaced by whoever the main assist of the raid is. Whenever a mob(s) is pulled, click on this Assist macro so that you know what they are currently fighting. The Main Assist should also have an incoming message in /shout to announce what his current target is, or if he changes targets, for example "/shout I am attacking %T". Use your Assist macro at all times before casting or attacking - it really sucks to attack, nuke or DoT an NPC that has taken no damage yet, because you thought it was the one the tanks were attacking, and then find yourself being jumped by a really ticked off mob and have no assistance. The /assist macro is mandatory for all raiders. And remember to click on it regularly, just in case your "main assist" (the assigned main attacker of the group) had to change targets for whatever reason, such as to pull a mob off the group's healer.

Note: In case you don't know, the "%T" is automatically replaced by whatever you have in your target window at the time you say it. Try this - target an NPC or PC, hit enter to bring up your chat bar, type "Hello %T", and then hit enter again. %T will be replaced by whatever was in your target window at the time. For example, if you targeted Grimguard and typed "Hello %T", your chat screen would show "You say, 'Hello Grimguard'". If you didn't have anything targeted at the time, it would instead show "You say, 'Hello Target'".

7. Stick together, as a group foremost, but also as a party.
We want to keep everyone from dying if we can, and one way most people get killed is they wander off by themselves or get lost. The group leader of each group is responsible for making sure that everyone in his/her group is following. Do not just bolt off because you know where the party is going, and then discover so-and-so isn't there. When the party moves, keep a head count, so to speak, and regularly check to see that everyone in your group is with you. Raid parties should always move in small steps, not giant leaps and bounds. New and unfamiliar people easily get left behind this way, whether it's due to lag, or making a wrong turn, going back to find everyone gone. One way to do this is to use the 5-second run-and-stop method. You should never move constantly for more than 5 straight seconds without stopping briefly to allow everyone to catch up. Real life always intervenes, so if a group leader notices someone is still sitting where the group just left, he should announce it in /shout so that the Directors know to halt their progress until the person returns and catches up. In such an instance it is recommended the entire party back up to where they were in the event a wandering mob happens into the area and attacks the lone individual. At risk of sounding serious, keep in mind that a raid is not a sightseeing tour. If you stop to smell the roses, you may find yourself lost and alone, and shortly thereafter dead. Again, maintain your focus and stay with the party. So as you can see, it is very important to always stick together. Also, if you must go /afk, announce it to your group, don't just disappear, for when you return you may find yourself in an undesirable situation.

8. If at all possible, print a map of the Raid Plan of Attack and get familiar with it.
This will help to avoid getting lost and/or stranded, as well as helps the overall raid occur more smoothly. If everyone has a general idea of the layout, even if they have never been there before, they have a better chance of not getting lost. Every guild raid will have a Raid Plan of Attack posted on the website. It will usually be using a map offered by EQ Atlas (www.eqatlas.com) with alterations made according to where the raiding party will be going and what it will be attacking, as well as the path(s) it will take. Having this available beforehand will hopefully help to prevent losing anyone as well as to allow you to see what types of things the party will be encountering and what to expect. Every PoA will also have a description of mobs, loot, expected problems or Code Reds (mobs that should receive the focus of all groups should it be encountered), as well as bind points and a travel guide to get to the raid location.

9. Know your task and avoid deviating from it.
If you are the main healer, you should only be healing. If you are the nuker, you should only be nuking. If you are a melee/backup healer (i.e, Paladin), you should be meleeing most of the time and only healing if the primary healer is low on mana. I've seen many times in groups where a cleric or druid starts to melee because he/she is out of mana. A lot of times, someone ends up dying. Instead, that cleric should be medding for more heals, in case they are needed. Also, healers wasting mana on nukes is downright stupid - no matter how much mana you may think you have, something almost always comes up and proved you didn't have enough to waste. Always stick to your task unless a Director issues an overriding order, which generally won't occur unless it is an emergency. All tasks will be assigned previous to the raid depending on the number and class of people that sign up, but changes may need to be made for no-shows.

Please keep in mind that you should show up if you sign up if at all possible, because the raid will be planned with everyone that signs up for it. As I mentioned before, everyone is important to the success of the raid, and no-shows could seriously hamper or even altogether cancel a raid. Also, try your best to be online at least 1 hour before the scheduled raid time, to allow time for everyone to get transportation to the raid site if necessary, as well as to be debriefed on any changes or plans.

10. All raids will have assigned looters.
Generally speaking, Group Leaders are the only ones allowed to loot. In higher level dungeons it is easy to see who is supposed to loot, as the group that gets the experience is the only ones that can at that moment. Do not loot if you are not a Group Leader or an assigned looter. All coin loot and proceeds from "junk" loot will go to the Guild Bank, so Group Leaders are recommended to either have no cash on them when they go to the raid, or at least note how much they had when they got there so they know how much the raid party earned. If the Group Leader is a monk, they should assign someone else in the party to loot to avoid encumbrance.

All items of worth will be awarded after the raid concludes, or when an acceptable break in the action occurs. One exception to this is whenever NO DROP loot is encountered. If a Group Leader finds NO DROP loot on a corpse, announce it in /ooc. The Raid Director will permit all those who can and will use it to roll for it. We will use the "/random 1 200" command when rolling for loot drops (please do not use "/random 200" as this rolls between 0 and 200, not 1 and 200; we prefer that everyone uses the exact same rolling technique). Our guild raids award loot based on who can use the item - i.e., if something drops that is only useable by warriors, only the warriors will be permitted to roll for it. If something drops that is not useable by any class present (i.e., there are no warriors), then everyone in the party will be allowed to roll for the item. Certain special loot rules may be designed for certain raid sites; check the PoA for details.

Also, we understand that some raid locations have drops geared more towards a certain class than others (such as monks in Dalnir), but do not let this discourage non-monks from attending. You will want those same monks to help you get your stuff from another location later on that you could not possibly get on your own or with your own class. The Collective of Dark Honour is about helping one another, and we hope to accomplish that in our raids.

There you have it. The 10 "Rules of Raiding". Learn it, Know it, Love it, Live it! I know we have what it takes to be the best. This guide will help us work together better, leading to more successful Raiding. I see a Guild that can cast down the Gods and wrestle the dragons to the ground. This guide will hep us attain that. Now its time to prove that we can. - All Hail the Collective!

Grimguard Dirtnappin
