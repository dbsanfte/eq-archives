Re: Shadowbane Balance Suggestions

Well, damn, I just found this whole thread. Here's my thoughts:

PVP Goal #1 - PvP Should be Entertaining

In order for PvP to be truly entertaining for BOTH (the key here is BOTH) parties, whether they be single players or whole guilds, it depends on the following:

1) There has to be some measure of skill involved.

You can't expect combat to be really entertaining and fun if it's extremely mindless. Look at EQ. It sucks PVPwise. Why? Because you have very few options. You can memorize very few spells. Each class has one predetermined way of fighting. If you are a wizard, root and nuke. If you X, do Y. The end.

Instead of that, we need: If he does X, I can do A, B, or C. For the other person, he will need to react to A, B, or C with D, E, or F. This is like chess. For each move, there is more than one possible response.

Now, I know some of this is already in place in SB. But, the key is, there needs to be working countermeasures that can be used quickly and efficiently, even if you are not the one that initiated combat. Which leads into:

2) It needs to have a slower pace, not faster.

Damage cannot come in huge bursts if you want there to be skill involved. If I can nuke someone for 200 points of damage and they have 250 hps and I get the first shot off, I will probably win. However, if I can nuke them for 20, and they have 250 hps, this allows them to conduct some sort of defensive maneuver, throw up a damage shield, something to reflect a spell, something to absorb a spell, something. The key is they can act and do something. They don't need to be reeling from the initial blow.

Again, look at EQ. A wizard can nuke someone for like 2000 points of damage at higher levels. That is debilitating in an ungodly way. What can you do if you get hit for full damage besides run or sit there and cry?

Since we're talking real time combat here, the amount of damage needs to take into effect that people need to think for a moment, need to click here or there to get a spell or attack off. If damage is insanely high, it will partially defeat PVP being fun.

The problem is, possibly, like in EQ... high level mobs have a billion hit points. That same wizard needs to be able to nuke for 2000 damage, because that is only 5% of the monsters total life. That same sort of ratio needs to be kept in PVP.

PVP Goal #2 - I want Lots of PVP! What is the Delay?!

1) Why a time penalty is necessary.

A time penalty is necessary to prevent people from hopping right back into a fight. If you die and there is a big war going on, you shouldn't spawn ready to go jump right back into said war. Also, there should be repercussions to death. No permanent ones, but a temporary thing like this is probably necessary so that people learn to respect death, and want to live.

Hell, if the effect was more debilitating, it might even make it more of a rush to PVP. Perhaps if you could challenge someone to a duel in a specific area, where no one else could interfere... and consenting to this duel would flip on a special flag. This flag would make, say, an hour long death effect stay on your character and broadcast a message to the world. Or to a special message board. Or something. Then we get a mini 'trophy' and some adrenaline rush. Of course, they would need to be ways to prevent others from joining in, but still, I digress...

2) What should the penalty be?

This is the tough question. The penalty needs to be severe enough that people do not want to die. But, it also needs to not discourage people from playing or create a feeling of 'this really sucks.'

a) Items - I feel the item penalty to should be next to nothing. This is the kind of thing which promotes bad, bad, bad things. It can discourage people from carrying items (naked mage syndrome when pvp'ing) or it can lead to random ganking (ie, I sneak up on you invisible and kill you after you fight a monster and are down to 3 hps, take all your stuff and move on). Some of that may be fine from time to time, but it can easily become an epidemic which makes the game less of a pvp world.

b) Experience - Well, this all depends on how easy the experience is to get in the final version. If it remains similar to gaining experience now, sure, I have no problem to an experience penalty. If it is like EQ, where a death can equate to hours of real life time, then no. Some form of penalty wouldn't be horrible, as long as, again, it is not discouraging. If you die a couple times in level 55 in Everquest and can't get a resurrection, you'll be wanting to quit.

c) Time - Some duration of time, 5-10 minutes may not be a bad thing. As long as you can't be killed during the period also. You need to be safe until you are able to fight at full strength again. This can also lead to a period of time where people can sit around and talk, something which is necessary to the community aspect. If nothing else, it will serve as a deterrant to dying. People need to want to live. People need to get that rush, that fear when they see their health bar dropping down towards empty.

3) How does this relate to PVP Goal #3?

When people die, it provides a period of down time, at least as far as combat goes. During this 'time out' period, if there was some medium where people could communicate, some kind of chat medium for all deceased, deathshrouded players, or some such, that would allow more opportunity for talking to other combatants who have met your similar fate. Things like this are what create rivalries, as much as player X ganking player Y.

PVP Goal #3 - Strong Vibrant Community

Interactions cannot just be combat related. People need to be able to talk to each other. They need to be able to talk trash, to learn other's personalities, to interact in some way where they don't have to be worried about being raped by at any moments notice. There will be ample places for the combat to happen, but there also need to be places for non-combat interactions to happen.

In Neverwinter Nights (turn based) you could chat during the combat. This is ideal. However, it is also implausible in a real time game. In NWN, there were also inns where players had to go to rest safely, where people gathered and talked. This sort of thing is essential to creating a more immersive PVP world. If I don't know or care about the other guy, know how he acts, know what angers him, then I am missing out on a key factor of the world. With a strong community, it can help alleviate the random ganking (player killing) and promote a more intense form of combat. In NWN, players created tournaments, ladders, and other ways to test each other's skills on a more or less level playing field. With a strong community, this can happen. With a weak community, you have UO: Enter dungeon, Corp Por anything that moves, loot, leave. Woo.

PVP Goal #4 - Group A vs Group B OR Powergamers vs Normal Humans

The Powergamers will always have tons of stuff and advance quickly and be able to replace items quickly. As a result of this, it is often the case that Group A forces a tougher code on the land. By that, I mean that the powers that be try to make it harder for them to do what they do. This, in turn, crushes and penalizes Group B, the normal people, more than anything. The Powergamers will adapt and still roll. The normal humans will, instead, be punished.

Let's relate this to another real world game: golf. You have the Tiger Woods (Group A) and the Jeff Maggert (Group B). Tiger hits the ball 300 yards. Jeff hits it 250 yards. Tiger shoots an ungodly low score. Jeff shoots a decent score.

The Powers that be decide to make it tougher for Tiger. They lengthen every hole on every course by 75 yards. Who does this penalize? The longer hitting Tiger? Or the shorter hitting Jeff? Tiger can still crush the ball and have easy shots. Jeff, however, gets left even farther behind because he was shorter to BEGIN with.

So, the key is to focus on the normal humans. They are the bread and butter of the game. They pay the bills. They outnumber the powergamers. The miniority group, Group A, cannot set the standards, because it's just not fair to everyone else.

1) Loot - Item loot sucks, pretty much for everyone, except the person who is doing the killing. It promotes a greed based opportunistic combat system, which I would argue is incredibly bad for combat as a whole.

2) Death Penalties - Everyone should have death penalties, but I'm not certain a level 10 person should have the same penalty as a level 50 person. The lower level people will have it harder, know less, and get killed more often. You definitely don't want to discourage someone who is new to the game, as if you lose them early on, you will never get them back. The level 50 person will have learned to respect life and avoid death, so they will know if they die, bad things will happen. Some kind of gradual progression in death effects would seem appropriate, to a degree, if possible.

ITEMS, ITEMS, ITEMS!

Let's examine loot/item rules on a bunch of games and then I'll expound upon their PVP successes and failures.

1) Neverwinter Nights

No looting. Whatever items you had, you kept, always. The end result? People PVPed. They created tournaments. They created ladders to test PVP skill. There were no worries about constantly outfitting your character. No worries about having to spend hours replacing the items that someone looted off of you when you got a lag spike and died.

No Loot. Great PVP. There was nothing else to really do EXCEPT to PVP.

2) Ultima Online

Full looting. However, the key to ultima was, all items sucked (except a few unbalanced weapons). All items were easily replaced in about 2 minutes. When we used to raid, you could outfit someone else in platemail and a bow in the snap of your fingers. The end result? People PKed. They were out and about, trying to kill people. Sure, you had to worry about losing all of your loot, but your loot wasn't anything special anyways. Still, the game was flawed in the PVP department, so the combat wasn't spectacular and barely, if at all, turned into anything more than just raiding and killing unexpecting people. Every now and then a couple raiding groups would meet up and it would be entertaining.

Full Loot. Item Economy = Incredibly easy to get. OK PVP, but mainly faulted because of the system and lack of desire by majority of the player base.

3) EverQuest

Items mean everything in EQ. Just look at playerauctions.com. Items are so important to your character and to your feeling of advancement that they sell for hundreds of dollars. This is bad. This creates an economy where all you want to do is get items and then get some more items after that. If you can never be happy with your items, you'll never move on to the PVP world.

In an economy driven by items that make your character powerful, it is difficult to have fair PVP also. If Player X has God Item A, then he will always beat Player Y. This is bad.

Even on the EQ PVP servers, items play a huge part in everything. Rallos Zek (one item loot rules), I am certain, has a lot of opportunistic kills, to try and rape someone of an item. On the flip side, I am sure no one wants to be out and about with their godly items, either. Too much chance. You die and lose a godly item, you are set back hours or days or weeks. Sullon Zek (no looting, except coins) did better and had a lot of PVP. This was a step in the right direction, as people no longer had to worry abou that set back factor.

Unfortunatley, EQ PVP has/had so many unfixable flaws, that it's more or less irrelevenat anyways. But the RZ and SZ server still show an example of how items affect PVP.

Finally! The End! Oh Wait! "PVP WILL ALWAYS HAPPEN, SO SAYS WARDEN!"

This is untrue. Ganking may always happen. But PVP will NOT always happen. Sure, every game has people who will kill someone else given the opportunity. But, that's not what we're looking for. We want a skill based, community oriented, world of combat. And unless certain factors like minimal penalties and item looting problems are addressed, this world will not come to fruition.
