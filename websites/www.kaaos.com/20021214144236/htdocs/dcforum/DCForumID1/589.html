12. "RE: Question"
"Languages for programmers should be learned within two weeks after you apply yourself."

True, assuming you know all the basic concepts behind those languages. If you know C++, then you should know JAVA, Basic, Cobol, and every other equivalent language.

Knowing DirectX is a bit different as it's not on the same level as those other languages. Much of directX is easy and similar to other languages; however, some of it is very different from the concepts you'll learn in College. How much 3D math have you learned (this would probably be a class in linear algebra, comes after 2nd semester calculus)? Knowing stuff like that is absolutely essential to create a 3D engine. Physics is another essential for creating a realistic environment. No, DirectX is not that different from other languages, but it's different enough to give some problems to a computer scientist.

Other Points:
- Yes, a good physics/graphics system is essential for making a good game. But why would you ignore DirectX or OpenGL? Either of those can be low level enough that it would be stupid to ignore them. Unless you plan on writing drivers for every video card and sound card on the market. DirectX and OpenGL are godsends to game makers, not using them would be a very bad idea. I don't think you have a good understanding of what these things do, or else you wouldn't be saying not to use them. Even in games created through directX, you probably won't have a single line of directX on the server side.

- C++ is sufficient for good AI if you know how to work AI properly. I honestly don't know enough about lisp to bother using it.

- I don't plan on sharing my game with any publishing company. When you go to a publishing company, they take the vast majority of the profits leaving you with almost nothing for your efforts. That means that art will be a problem. I do have some methods to get past that problem, but at the very end I can always just pay an art studio to do whatever I wasn't able to get done. That will cost money tho.

- A design document is absolutely mandatory. No, you will never need one for any of the programs you create in college and you probably won't even need an outline. In college, a design document is something you do because some professor makes you do it. College programs rarely go over a few hundred lines of code. I can do a few hundred lines of code in my head, it sounds like you can too. Real programs take hundreds of thousands of lines of code. Depending on your programming style and all, you can probably cut it down to tens of thousands of lines of code. Without a direct goal in each line of code, you end up going back thousands of time and rewriting code that you've already written. Something that should also be noted is that there's plenty of coding monkeys out there and very few real thinkers. A good design document tells people exactly what you want, they don't have to think, just create.

- If in a year I'm ready to start coding the actual game, I'll look you up. Coding is not my strong point, designing is (I hope, hehe).
