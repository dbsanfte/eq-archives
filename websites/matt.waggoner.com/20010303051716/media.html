Parte the Firste.
The Silver Screen.

My favorite films of all time are the Star Wars movies. (Oh, that's original.) I don't rank the four, because there's really no reason to; they all complement each other. It wouldn't have been a great series if there'd only been one great film in it. (Okay, you pinheads, I like The Empire Strikes Back the most. Sheesh.)

(Keep in mind that the following "favorite films" are the ones I enjoy the most, not the ones I think are the greatest in terms of cinematic achievement.)

My favorite films of 2000 (note that I saw a measly 47 movies in 2000, compared to my 1999 record of 80): Chicken Run; Almost Famous; X-Men; and Crouching Tiger, Hidden Dragon. CTHD got an Oscar nom for Best Picture; I sure as hell hope it wins.

My favorite film of 1999, was South Park: Bigger, Longer, and Uncut. Trey Parker and Matt Stone are the cooles people who ever lived. (Phil Collins, while a decent artist and a nice guy, can go to hell.) Now, South Park is the movie I had the most fun watching, but if you want my opinion on the BEST movie (and I can tell you're dying to find out), that would be American Beauty, winner of five Academy Awards (Cinematography, Original Screenplay, Actor, Director, Picture). (Some other excellent films in 1999: Being John Malkovich, Toy Story 2, Three Kings, Dogma, Election, Run Lola Run, Fight Club, and, yes, Galaxy Quest.)

My favorite film of 1998, in other words, the one I enjoyed the most, was Shakespeare in Love, a wonderful period piece about Shakespeare himself. (And, lo and behold, it won a bunch of Oscars.) My runner-up was Zero Effect, written and directed by my fellow Windward High School alumnus Jake Kasdan, son of noted Hollywood screenwriter and director Lawrence Kasdan. The best way to describe it is comedy noir, I guess. I did like Saving Private Ryan quite a lot, but I didn't enjoy it as much as I did Zero Effect. Also, Clay Pigeons was a lot of fun, as was Rush Hour, effectively reviving the buddy-action-comedy genre. (Cough, hack, gasp.)

My favorite film of 1997 was L.A. Confidential. It's probably my favorite film of the entire 90's. It's a 50's-era L.A. crime drama, starring Kevin Spacey, Guy Pearce, and Russell Crowe, among others. A classic of modern American cinema. It's the best noir film since Chinatown. It's the movie Mulholland Falls wanted to be, and was nominated for 9 Academy Awards. It won two: Best Adapted Screenplay (Brian Helgeland and Curtis Hanson) and Best Supporting Actress (Kim Basinger). Unfortunately, Titanic won best picture. What a crying shame.

My favorite film of 1996 was Primal Fear, starring Richard Gere and Edward Norton. It had the best ending of any film all year. All right, all right, any film that I saw all year. And mind you, I'm the kind of person who deliberately does NOT try to figure out the ending to a movie, and therefore I tend to enjoy movies more than people who boast, "Oh yeah, I figured it out in the first ten minutes." (You know who you are :)

My favorite film of 1995 was The Usual Suspects. If you haven't seen it, do so. The ending of The Usual Suspects is truly classic. It was nominated for two Oscars and won both of them. Kevin Spacey won Best Supporting Actor as cripple "Verbal" Kint, and Christopher McQuarrie won for Best Original Screenplay.

My favorite film of 1994 was Shallow Grave, starring future Obi-Wan Kenobi Ewan MacGregor. A very suspenseful, very well-made Scottish thriller. Worth seeing at any price.

Before 1994, I was too young to have seen enough movies (not to mention remember them all and understand them all) to have any further favorites. I know you're absolutely broken up that the list ends here. (Sob, wail, moan.)

Parte the Seconde.
Books and other delusions.

I also love to read. Naturally, I have all of the Star Wars novels, although they aren't exactly great literature. So here's my favorite books and plays, in approximate order, Star Wars excluded. (Among the Star Wars books, the Thrawn Trilogy books - Heir to the Empire, Dark Force Rising, and The Last Command - are my favorites. However, Aaron Allston's Wraith Squadron series of novels are not only immensely entertaining, but hysterically funny -- I've never laughed so much reading a novel, let alone three.)

My favorite single book is Snow Crash by Neal Stephenson. It's kind of a less depressing, surrealist extension of the things William Gibson wrote about in Neuromancer and his other cyberpunk novels. It's also a lot more fun to read. Also, if you get a chance, read The Diamond Age, also by Neal Stephenson. It's not quite as good, but it's very VERY creative and very well-written. I also just finished Stephenson's Cryptonomicon, which is not quite as consistently enjoyable as Snow Crash was the first time I read it, but it is nonetheless a great read.

Another strong series I can recommend (even though I've only read the first of them) is the Dark Tower series by Stephen King. Everyone who's never read him will probably assume that all his books are horror. Well, they aren't. The first book in this series, called The Gunslinger, is much closer to fantasy ("reality-fantasy," I suppose) than anything else. And King is such a facile writer that you get lost in the work almost immediately. I highly recommend it. Aw, hell, read everything he ever wrote.

Another good one is a book called Gödel, Escher, Bach, which (when it's all boiled down) is about number theory, but it intertwines the subjects of math, music, art, computer science, genetics, strange loops, and neurobiology, among others. It's intensely interesting (it won the Pulitzer Prize for its author, Douglas R. Hofstadter, in 1979). I recommend it for anyone with a mind for that sort of thing.

and the Wheel of Time
 My favorite series of books is The Wheel of Time, consisting of:
  1. The Eye of the World
  2. The Great Hunt
  3. The Dragon Reborn
  4. The Shadow Rising
  5. The Fires of Heaven
  6. Lord of Chaos
  7. A Crown of Swords
  8. The Path of Daggers

They add up to about 6,200 pages of small print reading. They rival Tolkien for depth, breadth, and sheer enjoyment. I recommend them to anyone who enjoys fantasy in any capacity. (Among them, my favorite is the first book.) Go here for more Wheel of Time miscellany.

Speaking of Tolkien, director Peter Jackson is currently filming the Lord of the Rings trilogy as live-action films. The first one (The Fellowship of the Ring) is set for release around Christmas 2001, followed by The Two Towers at Christmas 2002 and The Return of the King at Christmas 2003. Should be interesting at least. Hey, if you haven't read The Hobbit and Lord of the Rings to begin with, you're freaking insane.

Here are the rest of my favorites, in no particular order.

  • Catch-22, by Joseph Heller. If you haven't read this book, READ IT NOW! It's one of the funniest, most bizarre things I've ever read. It's also, apparently, the only good book Heller ever wrote.
  • "Arcadia," by Tom Stoppard. This guy is a friggin' genius. He also wrote "Rosencrantz and Guildenstern are Dead" and "Hapgood," among others.
  • Einstein's Universe, by Nigel Calder. The only book I've ever seen that's able to explain Einstein's theories of special and general relativity in a way that even a braindead chimp could understand.
  • "The Glass Menagerie," by Tennesee Williams. Heartbreaking.
  • Hyperion, by Dan Simmons. This is easily one of the best hard SF novels I've ever read, and one of the most emotionally involving, too. Don't miss the sequel/conclusion, The Fall of Hyperion. It's really one big long story, actually.
  • Neuromancer, by William Gibson. Enough said.
  • The Prince, by Niccolo Machiavelli. You'll never look at politicians the same way again.
  • The Demolished Man and The Stars My Destination, by Alfred Bester. Two classics of science fiction literature; if you've never read these, then you're missing a lot.
  • Anything by Tom Clancy, specifically The Cardinal of the Kremlin, The Hunt for Red October, and The Sum of All Fears. All his stuff is good, but those three especially.
  • And, of course, the Principia Discordia, by Malaclypse the Elder. If you don't know what it is, you probably aren't cleared for it. Speaking of which, you should also read everything Robert Anton Wilson ever wrote, including the Illuminatus! trilogy, the Schrodinger's Cat trilogy, and, well, everything else. Foucault's Pendulum by Umberto Eco, too. Same vein.

Parte the Fourthe.
Musicians and such.

Hey, what favorites list wouldn't be complete without my favorite musicians? (By the way, congratulations on getting this far. You must really be interested in me to read all this... or you're stalking me. Either way, let's do lunch!) My number one favorite artist of all time is Joe Satriani, a guy you've never heard of who can do things with guitars you swore were impossible (and are illegal in several Eastern European countries). Most critics rank him, as a guitar player, just below Hendrix and Clapton. Personally, I like him better than either of them, but hey, there's no accounting for taste. So what kind of music does he play? Rock and roll! It varies from straight-ahead guitar rock to very odd experimental pieces that nonetheless still manage to carry a good melody. His softer tracks are some of the most emotional music I've ever heard. For a general introduction to Satriani, the best album to get is The Extremist (1992), which contains a good sampling of harder rock as well as slower pieces.

My favorite single piece of classical music is Mozart's "Overture to the Marriage of Figaro," which is played prominently at the beginning of Trading Places, which is (even way down here at the bottom of the page) one of my favorite movies.
