An Introduction

One of the most interesting activities to participate in within the realms is the process of shared fiction. Every day new stories are posted on message boards, and many of these stories are shared efforts. One story line may have two, three, or even a dozen different authors, all contributing their own particular style and characters. The plot is usually not planned in advance. Instead, each author posts whenever he or she sees fit, and makes whatever plot advances or twists seem appropriate at the time. The end result is a living, breathing story and no one can really predict the outcome.

The process itself is quite simple. An author starts a story and leaves it incomplete.  Another member then adds to it, advancing the plot. This process continues until the story is complete. Sometimes, there never is a true completion to the story! Subplots can live on for months, or even years.

Most authors use their character as the center of their own particular portions of the story, though this is not mandatory. Many authors make use of NPC's (characters that do not have any player counterpart) to advance the plot. Some examples have included a dwarven farmer, a goblin shaman, and a drow captain. Some NPC's have even become major players in a story line, sometimes more active than the author's own character.

Of course, any attempt at shared fiction which involves several authors has its pitfalls.  Since the stories are not planned out in advance, often one author's contribution will drastically alter the plans of another. Sometimes an author will have to discard several pages that were written offline after reading the latest additions. While this is all part of the fun of shared fiction, a certain amount of discretion is advised when writing additions. To this end, the following guidelines have been devised to help new authors enjoy this facet of online roleplay. Please keep in mind they are only guidelines, not hard and fast rules. Still, any author should consider carefully before discarding them.

Never Kill Another's PC Without Consent

This is the most sacred guideline of shared fiction. Most players are very fond of their characters, and it simply is not acceptable to kill them off in a story without their permission. Keep in mind this permission is not likely to be given, unless the player plans to retire that particular character permanently. This is the one rule that should never be broken for any reason.

Never Inflict Serious Harm on Another's PC Without Consent

Most players who involve their characters in a story line wish to keep that character active within the story. Hence, any maiming, dismemberment, feebing, etc. is usually an unwelcome turn of events if it happens without their permission. However, if you take certain steps, this can still be an acceptable plot twist. For example, should you decide that a certain character should get "feebed" in the story (hit with a Feeblemind spell), you might mention in passing that he or she was near a temple at the time of the atrocity. Therefor, a quick trip to the priest could cure the character handily, allowing that individual to continue in the story unimpaired. Note that cuts, bruises and other wounds are quite acceptable to hand out, even rather nasty ones.

Portray Other PC's Respectfully.

Don't make another players' character, guild, etc. look foolish or inept in your story telling. This, of course, is not relevant if the other player is intentionally PLAYING a foolish or inept character. In that case, have a ball.

Respect Other Authors' NPC's.

NPC's, while usually not as important as players' characters, are still to be treated carefully by those authors who did not introduce them into the story line. When an author introduces an NPC, it's usually for a good reason that may not become clear for some time. Any major changes to that NPC (like death) could drastically alter the course of the story, possibly ruining that author's plans. Some extra leeway may be taken in this area, however, as ruining an author's plan is not nescessarily a bad thing, and most authors are fully aware that once an NPC is introduced he or she becomes fair game for other authors. In other words, you are free to make alterations to another author's NPC's, but do so with discretion.

Remember Your PC's Limitations and Mortality.

While we all like to play the hero, remember your character is a mortal being with limitations. Don't try to solve every problem yourself. Your character's strengths are all the more dramatic after his or her weaknesses have been examined as well. If your character tends to solve every situation single handed, this means other characters will be stuck as second stringers most of the time. There's plenty of glory to go around, so let others share in it. Also, failures can be just as interesting to read about as victories.

Don't Resolve Complex Situations Too Quickly.

When writing about an event that is large in scope, such as a major battle, let it evolve slowly. Shared fiction is a slow process, and should be treated as such. Allow every author a chance to get involved, and leave them openings to take advantage of. A single battle could take dozens of posts to resolve if there are many characters involved in it. If you end it too abrubptly, some characters may not get to see any action at all.

Maintain the Plausibility of the Story at All Times.

This is more of a general writing guideline than a guideline about shared fiction, but it doesn't hurt to mention it. When a situation is finally resolved, resolve it in a believable fashion. Don't rely on divine intervention, magical items that had not been mentioned previously, or the like. Everything should be clearly plausable within the laws of the AD&D universe. If you can't find a believable way out of a tough situation, take it like a dwarf. The other authors won't allow anything too terrible happen to your character.

A Final Word

Once again, all of the above (except for #1) are guidelines. There may indeed be times when it is appropriate to ignore one or more of them, but do so carefully.   Also, DON'T HESITATE TO E-MAIL OTHER AUTHORS with questions, possible plot twists, etc. etc. Some storylines work best with some some rough planning in advance, however vague.

Most importantly, have fun and don't be afraid to get involved! Most authors like nothing more than having new writers jump into their storylines and muddy the waters a bit. If you're feeling a bit uncertain, E-mail them and ask beforehand. Odds are you'll get nothing but encouragement.
