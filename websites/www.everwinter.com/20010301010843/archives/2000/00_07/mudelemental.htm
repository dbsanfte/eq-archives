The Mud Elemental

Posted by Strengitan on July 14, 2000:

Tan frowned and rooted around in his bag of stones. He was trying to figure out the magics of this land and so far, he had not determined what made a strong pet and what made a weak pet. The stones, for sure had their varying degrees of flaws but that did not seem to correspond with the ability of the elementals they summoned. He picked a particularly round bit of malachite and tossed it on the ground. He uttered the words that would shape the next Potster and breathed life into the inert rock.

Moments later a hulking lump of earth regarded the small gnome silently. Tan grinned and greeted the looming elemental.

“Welcome, Potster VI! Let’s see what you can do eh?” The gnome looked about for a suitable target. In the distance the sail like wings of an enormous bat crested over the tops of the hills. “That one! Get him!” The elemental did not move. Tan furrowed his brows and scurried closer to the immense flying beast. “Here, attack him, that bat there.” He pointed. “Bat! Potster, get the Ba-no, the BAT…HEY WAIT!!” Tan bounced about as the lumpy conjuration lit out after a gnoll. “Where are you-AHHHICH!”

The gnome quickly gathered a spurt of flame and launched it at the gnoll. Within moments, the dogish creature lay dead and it’s coin in Tan’s purse. The small magician stalked over to the waiting pet. “We gotta talk, Potster, sit down.” The elemental did a weird shimmy as it sat as best as it could and waited for Tan’s instruction. The gnome paused, startled and amused that the chunk of rock could sit as well as did. Now if it would only listen to more important matters.

“Well, that’s a start. Now, here’s the way of it. I choose what we fight, you fight it, I burn the critter when he is thoroughly preoccupied with your…fists?” Tan shrugged as he tried to find a better term to describe the arm like projections that hung from the elemental. “The creature dies, you live, I live, and we reap the profits of a good kill. Understand?” The rock sat and said nothing. Tan peered at it, hands on his hips as he tried to figure out how much of his speech, if any, had sunk in. “OK, from the top. We are hunting mostly bats, so lets find one and grab it’s wings. They make tasty treats you know.”

The minute magician patted his earthen friend and set out for the hills to search for more flying rodents. He had his fishing pole and small spit along and planned to try and make fish rolls for dinner. So far, he had been rather unsuccessful but he was getting the recipe down a little bit better each time he messed up. It was going to take a lot of bats and a lot of fish, he feared, before he had enough rolls to last him a while. In the distance he sighted the tell tail beat of the wanted wings and sped off to catch up with the critter. Potster followed dutifully behind.

Tan grinned from ear to ear. His bag was bulging with batwings and Potster had preformed admirably. It was time for a well deserved break and he thought he knew just the spot to lie down and think of this that and whatnot. Somewhere in the hills there was a lake and Tan intended to get some fishing in that eve. After a few wrong turns, both magician and pet crested the hill that overlooked a long lake. A shanty and dock sat at one end and a couple of northern men sat around talking to each other. Tan bounced up to the barbarians.

“Hail, friends. How’s the fishing in these parts?”

The merchant barbarian sighed. “Th’ fish don’t bite as well as they used ta. I would suggest fishing somewhere else lad.

“Well, I just wandered out here so I might as well try my luck. Fare well friend, and may the fish start leaping into your baskets.” Tan waved good bye and started up the shore towards a fleet of small fishing boats. He hopped into one and set his gear down. There were no oars or no visible means of moving the boat that the gnome could see. He scrunched his face up in concentration and stalked about the craft looking for a way to make it go. Potster floated obediently behind him.

The frustrated gnome tapped his foot on the prow of the boat. “I wish this thing would move!” Suddenly, the craft gave a lurching heave and slid into the glassy waters of the lake. Tan tumbled backwards onto the floor of the boat, Potster tumbled overboard. The gnome sat up and looked about, grinning at his success.

“Potster, we’re sailing!” He turned around to the ever vigilant lump of earth. The back of the boat was vacant. “Potster?” The magician peered overboard. “Potster, get back in the boat.” The elemental came dripping up from the bottom of the lake to hover in the rear seat of the boat. “Very good. Stay here and we will have a bit of fun!”

Tan puttered the boat around the lake until he got board. He beached the boat at one end of the pond and got out his tackle and bait. It looked like a grand spot to relax and catch dinner and that’s just what he intended to do for a few hours. That and try to create the perfect fish roll. He sighed in contentment and cast his line. It was a beautiful day and so far, hadn’t once rained while he was flitting about in the boat or now that he was parked on shore. He closed his eyes and hummed a little tune to himself.

Hours and many fish later, the gnome decided to call it a night. He had not succeeded once in perfecting his recipe and the ground lay littered with burnt batwings and charred fish. He at least had had fun. He called to Potster as he packed up his things. There was no answer from the elemental. Tan stopped his futzing with his fishing gear and looked around. His constant companion had disappeared while he had been lying about on the shore. The gnome frowned in worry and looked to the hills for his pet. He had heard of random elementals wandering off and bringing back a whole slew of nasties at the most inopportune times.

“Potster?” The gnome glanced nervously about. He shrugged, assuming that something had eaten his stalwart friend as he quickly dug out another rock. He cast the spell to bring Potster VII to life. The spell caved in on itself leaving the bit of malachite unaltered. Tan stared at the stone. As best as he knew the magic of this world that could only mean that the former Potster was still lurking around. He called again for his pet.

The gnome frowned and sat down to puzzle out the disappearance of his pet. If it were to come back, it would have already done so he reasoned. Therefor, it was either already here and invisible, which he had never heard of the creatures being able to do, or it was hiding somewhere where he couldn’t see it. He looked about but other than in the bottom of the boat, there was no where for the rock to hide. Tan got up quietly and stalked up to the boat. He stood on tip toes to peer into the bottom, but to no avail. The boards held no hiding elemental.

Tan grew impatient. He hated to leave the creature all alone in the hills but this hide and seek was growing tiresome. He grumbled as he stalked up and down the lake shore. He had specifically told the obliging lump of earth to stay by him when he got off the boat. Tan looked out over the lake as he stopped his wandering. Did he tell Potster to stick with him or stick by him? These creatures sometimes took things too literally and if he had told the elemental to stay while they were out on the boat, then it was most probably….

Tan laughed as a string of bubbles floated to the surface of the water. In moments, the little gnome had jumped into the lake and swum down to where the earthy pet was dutifully staying put. It’s poor soggy arms were waiting for some word from the little magician to attack whatever fearsome creature happened by as it forlornly looked across the bottom of the lake. Tan beckoned to the creature but the water had eroded it so that it no longer recognized the little gnome as its master. He sighed and swam back to the shore to cast the dismiss spell for Potster. At least he had found the unfortunate pet and had learned that they don’t swim overly well. Next time, he would be more careful in saying things to the elemental, least it park itself somewhere more interesting than the bottom of a lake.
