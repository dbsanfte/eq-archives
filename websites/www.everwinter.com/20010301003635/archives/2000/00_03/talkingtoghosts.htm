Always Talking to Ghosts

Posted by Shiver Hel Despana on March 31, 2000:

The dark elf sneered, dropped to one knee, and removed the bottle of Kalish the small gnoll had been carrying. It was an easy kill and he had grown used to expecting nothing beyond their scalps. His natural inclination would be to sell the bottle to Sneed, but the merchant had conveniently forgotten Shiver’s alternate identity, despite immutable proof. It didn’t really matter. The guards would never allow him to slip through the gates of North Qeynos, anyway. Given his options and his already stuffed backpacks, he uncorked the bottle with his teeth, spit the cork into the long grass outside the city, and drank the fruit-wine in entirety.

His tolerance to spirits had been lost with the Orb Winter. His mind clouded immediately, but staying away from the guards was all he had to concern himself with. The gnolls were no threat. Only the Queen Klicknik would pose an issue, and she was easily eluded. Queen indeed—

The image burst into his mind, an uninvited visitor: the hardened, noble eyes and a slight smile. Mischief incarnate, he thought. He knew the face of that impish lady intimately, and imagined her each day—often when he chose to enter his reverie. They say the elven people need no sleep, because they meditate and dream of their lives, becoming innately confident of their own identity. The drow had no such practice. They didn’t care to know the depths of their own depravity, the shallow and hopeless sum of their days. Shiver, on the other hand, had learned from House Neirdre, and found the reflection addictive.

No fond recall of days past danced in his mind. He remembered pain, reliving the agony of each broken vow and betrayed comrade. Then again, he knew it didn’t matter in the end. He was defiance given form. He would choose the hardest path, no matter what opposed him and how lucrative the outcome of the alternative. Some believed him wise, having seen so much, but the dark elf knew himself better than that. He had the instinct for survival, memory and reason that facilitated his long years… but every day was suffering. Today was no exception, as he tried to steady his feet and concentrate on the objective.

Two hundred dead gnolls would force them to tolerate his life within the walls of Qeynos, at least most of them. The bounty for three hundred of their slain foes would see the merchants opening their stores to him. Others were not so kind, and no guard—corrupt or not—would cite them with a violation. The mages’ guilds, the bards who claimed to follow Veeshan, and most obviously the temples of Karana and Tunare… all were sworn foes of the power of necromancy, though Shiver would have been the last to drawn his power from Innorruk.

Such was the tedium of his life as a dark elf, but he was conformable in the form. Unlike his dragon brethren, he actually preferred the seeming, though by no means had he wanted to give up his true nature. No doubt Olam, Saidar, and perhaps some of the others suffered more than the deep. He didn’t wish the discomfort upon them, but dragon’s had grown very arrogant and relied far too heavily on the gift of claw, fang, wing, and breath. Shiver had never taken that for granted, and his many duels in the arena of Triboar, or the Lost Hills and Neverwinter Woods were also done in his dark elven guise.

But Norrath was different. Frail and seething, he had the grace of the Teir’dal, as they called the dark elves of the land, but so too was he weak and easily winded. That was something he missed about the human form, but it was difficult for him to tolerate it for long periods of time. He doubted even his fellows within Winterborne understood how deeply his hatred for servitude ran. Like it or not, being called druid required many sacrifices. It was easier to move about, but he did so with a shackle about his neck. Slavery to the discrimination of humanity… slavery to the Lord of Thunder… even if the end result would be his victory, it was too easy a path.

He made his way through the darkness with the ease dark elven sight allowed. His human form was nearly blind in similar conditions, though the mists affected all. He proceeded dangerously close to the gates, then found the illusionary wall, the entrance to the aqueducts of Qeynos. He slipped through the vines, ignoring the illusion.

The place was empty aside from a well. He knew that a short swim would lead to the underworld of this land, but he wasn’t ready. They didn’t like him there, either, and a defeat would be too costly at this juncture. A week in the future, perhaps, things would be different, and he would begin to explore. He knew of a temple dedicated to the Plague Bringer where he hoped he would be welcome to train. They saw him as a dark elf, and that was supposedly enough. But that was a plan, not the moment.

He sat down, leaning the skull-topped Staff of Writhing against the wall, over his shoulder. The fire of vengeance within him had waned for the eve. He closed his eyes, allowing the reverie to fully claim him.

The gaunt Erudite he knew as Erondis sat before him, cloaked in intricate black robes and mage’s mail, immaculate in the rented office. The air of nobility died as Shiver looked to the man’s eyes, open wide with distress and fear. Shiver stepped towards him, and he jerked abruptly backwards like an trapped animal. A feral screech erupted between bared teeth. "Little Erondi wants his auntttteeeeee!" and Erondis—sage and enchanter—dissolved into the form of a panicked infant. He shrieked as the tears streamed down his face, clenched his tiny fists, and began to turn purple under the strain.

Shiver narrowed his eyes, contemplating the situation. He needed the man—boy—alive, yet he was certain he could not care for the changed Erondis. "Daharil, come!"

The door to his private suite opened and out stepped the lanky form of his daughter, Shar, clad in bloodstained mail. There was a mad gleam in her eyes, the look a drunk has when he’s thwarted from drink. It was not unlike the glare of bantam Erondis, but feral and twisted. She grinned, showing pointed teeth and pulled a wide-bladed dagger from her belt. "Yah want me shut up the little thing?"

Shiver was never one to let surprise slow his actions, but there was a thick fog upon his mind, slowing his reflexes. He reached for the short, skull-topped stave that hung at his belt, but the Pale Lady closed the distance between his desk and the door, pouncing on screaming Erondis. He reached out, but the steel flashed across the dark skin of the juvenile erudite’s throat. A spray of hot, acrid blood fountained across his desk, the tomes he had been reading, and dripped from his daharil. She turned to face him, her mad smile broader.

The dark elf clenched the staff tightly, stepping back. Erondi’s head tilted back on a ravaged neck, and the blood steamed against the coldness of the room. His eyes were open and vacant, his mouth contorted into a weird smirk. Shar leaned over her prey like an animal feasting, her ice-blue eyes meeting his puzzled violet gaze. "Awwww," she cooed, "is that better, daddy?"

She brought up the knife, slick with gore, and drew it across her tongue, then pouted with stained lips. Her eyes closed in delight. "Mmmmmm…" she almost purred. "Winter certainly had good taste!"

He hummed against the unreal, calling on the dirge within him. The word of power was simple. "Enough." And the vision continued, matching the tempo of his lament, the dissonance rising against his melody.

Shar leapt, cat like, onto the perch of his desk, driving the dagger into the wood, through the parchment records of House finances. "I think I’m still hungry, daddy? What else do yah think I should eat?" She bit down lightly on her own lip, her azure tattoo’s seemed to twist and flow into a symbol, a rune that was on the edge of Shiver’s mind.

He pulled on the hate welling inside him, bringing the hoar-frost in a wave around him. Their breath froze between them, jagged crystals of ice coating their skin. "I have given thee a taste of death before, daharil. There is a cure for all things, even madness. I have thy medicine."

Laughter burst forth from the barbarian lass, her insanity given sound in the jarring cacophony. "Yeah! Yah need to do it right this time, old man! Finish me or I shut that bone-box of yours fer good!" She shot forward, blurring into the shape of a pale leopard, a snow cat, but the dark elf had stepped through the shadows, vanishing to the landing outside the apartment.

Shiver screamed out in defiance to the vision. "End this torment. I know thee, Lady Vox. Do nae play games with me." The rushing sounds of the chaos abruptly ended, leaving only his identity, floating in a void and the soft sound of the dirge, ever-present despair.

The voice echoed around him, company in the still and darkness. "Greetings, Lord Dirge, once-dragon. Have I earned your attention?"

His answer was dead-calm. "Ye have earned only my contempt. Ye know I’m nae capable of pity, dragoness."

Her laugh boomed. "I am disappointed in you. My servants continue to fail. It will not be long before I must call upon your House to search out the mad goblins in my own lair. They have delusions of divinity."

"So ye hinder my efforts? A brilliant notion from she that defies her mother. As ye well know, the House has pain enough without your meddling."

"Meddling? I send you news of other animations, and one of my greatest vassals, and you relegate her to weaving songs in taverns instead of assisting you, as the malfunctioning Erondis was meant to."

"I know little of this Aulura, save that ye have thy claws sunk deeply into her, and she grows in power. Perhaps this play thing of yours should be closer t’ me. I would be very amused should she surprise thy arrogance and impale thy grasp."

Again, the Lady Vox laughed, but their was a tension this time, apparent even in the disjointed dream. "Think what you like, once-dragon. Many servants have tried to defy me and none have succeeded. Aulura is no exception. Sit her in little Erondi’s place. With his keepers pursuing love instead of a solution, he will fail faster. Aulura is strong enough for the tasks you have in mind."

He understood the vision clearly. Erondis was not fit as his Assistant, though the enchanter certainly had been the best when his mind was whole. Shar was the implied future should Shiver fail to find a cure to the afflictions, personalized. His mind seethed and recoiled at the shackles forming. "I will appoint Aulura in his place, but listen to me closely. These mind games will stop. Even golden shackles chaff me, and my discomfort can lead to ready betrayal. Think ye not that Lord Nagafen and even the Lord Mistmoore approach me? There are many that would seek this knowledge I hold, and I react to threats very poorly."

"You would betray the only one who has a mutual interest in your success?"

Shiver smiled, his teeth brilliantly white against the midnight-blue of his Teir’dal skin. "I lost my honor long ago, lady. I hold the tattered threads of a once great tapestry. They are not whole enough to strangle me."

"Very well. Appoint Aulura and I shall loosen my grip upon her. I need to see progress, and soon. The Tribunal grows restless and a crusade could destroy my home, readily. If I die, what do you think your chances for success are? The barbarians have no love for the servants of Miragul, nor one of their own who has gone feral in the face of the Tribunal."

He nodded. "I have eluded a harder taskmaster than all the powers of Norrath could ever hope to rival, but Aulura shall be seated as my Assistant. She is a worthy successor for Erondis and I her hate for thee is far greater than mine. Now leave me. My time is precious."

"Go serve the people of Qeynos and impress them with your tails of valor against the mere children of Black Burrow. We both know the truth."
