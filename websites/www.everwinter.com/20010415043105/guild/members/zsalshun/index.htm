Who am I to claim Winterborne? By what right do you ask? Truth is, I do not know. I have my doubts that I am even one of these so called "animations" although the odd device held in Flutrose’s hand attested to my being one. Any gnome worth their salt can fashion such a device to dupe the unwary and guile the gullible. So why do I come at all? My answer is simple: Ambere.

My earliest memories were of my father and the church. He was not a priest, nor was he a magic wielder of any sort that I could divine. He did not fight well either, although he served as a guard in Neriak. What he did do was hang about the Blind Fish drinking and pushing me to become better than my peers. He hung about me when I came home from the temple and grilled me on what I had learned. He was quick to see that I was not brainwashed by the brotherhood of Innurak but told me that I should never voice these thoughts to anyone but himself. I lived my young years this way, spending time between the temple and the Blind Fish, which I grew to call home. Of my mother, I never knew.

My father would often ask if anything peculiar happened and I would always answer nothing out of the ordinary. Which was the truth as I saw it. Every few months I would have a "vision", but that too was not particularly odd. I had been having these visions since I was a very young child and I had always assumed they were gifts from Innurak. My father asked with such intensity and scrutiny and was vastly relieved when I answered "no" that I continued to do so. I rationalized that these messages from my god would only upset someone who had very little faith in them. That and I was uneasy by the calculating look he always gave me.

My first accomplishment as a young adult was to acquire the deed to the inn I called home. It was a simple means to dupe and kill the current owner and I did it well enough to not get caught. The hired help was suspicious of my sudden ownership but I am a fair master and soon eased their fears. I took pride in the Blind Fish, perhaps to my detriment, but it earned me the coin I needed to buy weapons and spells. That and the underground windows were the best remedy I had found to relieve stress. There is nothing quite so amusing as a swimming leatherfoot deputy viewed as a fish would see him.

My ownership progressed well as did my studies. I was almost ready to join the ranks of the defenders of our forest. The temple will give it’s apprentice clergy a small badly balanced club for beating heads in and 2 spells and send them out with the blessings of our god to certainly die at the feet of the guards or guardians. The Fish enabled me to purchase a fine quality weapon, my first ranking of spells and a piece or two of cheap armor to protect myself. And thus I adventured, considerably ahead of the other novices who were tossed out naked and weak.

I learned much. I learned that I had a penchant for undead and spent weeks at the obelisk that they called home. I met an enchanting young rogue with a sharp wit and tongue that made the hunting more bearable and of course, the enchanter Makaz. Makaz made the Fish as much a home as my father and I did. He was much older than I and at first was not entirely certain of my talents. I proved to him that the clergy of Innurak were not to be scorned and we adventured well together. He became one of the few people I could "trust" if there is such a word in my language. At least, he became one of the few people I knew who would consider selling any information he had gleaned from me at a higher price. That is considerable trust in the underdark world of my home.

And so I learned. I gained in power and wealth, sharing much with Makaz in hopes to sway him into friendship. He was a remarkable smith and aspired to become a jeweler. Both of which I needed access to but did not quite have the coin to spend on the open market. Shamelessly, I chipped at his capitalistic nature and bargained a suit of armor from him at a very good price. The proceeds from the fish adequately compensated my spending.

I was still quite young when the elf and his wife were brought in. I never saw the male, he was dispatched before I entered the third gate, however the female I had the distinct displeasure of seeing. She was a pathetic thing. She cried and whimpered in her hauntingly familiar tongue and looked about with large, frightened and uncomprehending eyes. We laughed and the guards took turns abusing her in many different and exotic ways. She just lay there, confused and shivering like a small pup or kitten as the guards taunted and beat her. Not once did she fight back. It became a spectacle of the guards having their way in such an easy fashion that I grew disgusted. What is the challenge of torturing a bear cubling? Or even a ratling? For that is what it was like to watch. My respect for my fellow Tier’Dal was lessened that day as they prolonged her misery for their own fun without a hint of challenge or danger.

I said as much to the gallivanting warriors, putting all the scorn and disgust I felt into my words. I made no friends as I pointed that this one was hardly worthy of their skills, yet they dallied over her as if she were the most deserving of foes. I wondered out loud at their prowess that they enjoyed such an easy fight. The arena was still, save the quiet sobs of the woman. I shook my head at them and offered to kill her so that the next prisoner could be brought forth. The head guardsman smiled wickedly at me and said her life was for sale only and after I bought her I could do with her what I pleased. I agreed to his outrageous sum and left to gather my coin.

It was far more than I had ever earned, but I had one recourse left to me. The deed to the Fish would cover the sum with just a little left over for me to continue to live off of, plus, I had a ready buyer in my father. He had always been surprised of my acquisition and jealous of the income it earned for me. He would pay the money and gloat over how he had won the deed. It did not matter much to me anymore, my faith in my god was always week and now my pride in my race was tarnished. He bought the deed, I bought the slave and considered adventuring out of the city never to return.

Unfortunately, I was still not wise enough to brave the far off lands at that time and the Slave woman was now my charge. I did not expect her to live through the night, but I made her comfortable and tended her wounds as best as I could. She surprised me by living. She surprised me again by showing that she knew something about gems and how to fashion them into jewelry. I smiled, aware that with her I had another source of income to replace that of the Fish. Who my customers would be was another problem. My fellow Tier’Dal did not take kindly to my pointing out their episode in such a bad light and I found that doors that were always open began to close. My father even decided to charge rent, which I paid off by working at the bar. I spent even more time out in the wilds practicing my skill. I never understood why Innurak stayed with me even as my own temple turned their backs.

It was on one of these trips just after that first night that I found the babe. It had been in the woods for perhaps a day, maybe two and had survived. She was a tiny thing, quiet as a grave but alert to her surroundings. She was also dressed in the same house pattern as the slave woman. I never knew what possessed a high elven couple to cross the forests of my people with an infant child but it appears as if they had tried. I sighed and picked up the babe.

The mother was horrified to see her child. I suspect she thought the babe was safely away with the father, and seeing the small bundle hidden in my cloak told her that the male did not make it. She cried for days afterwards and clung to the child like it was her lifeline. My father did not know about the child, and as the child was flawed and could not utter a single sound, there were no cries to give it’s presence away. I did not trust this to last very long, as my father had a habit of bursting into my room for whatever reason, so I pried the child from the mother’s arms and quietly went to see Makaz. I needed him to help me hide the babe away from my father who would probably kill it on site. Only my diligence and a stipend of my earned money to Makaz kept the child and the slave woman safe.

Both measures were not quite enough. Upon returning from a hunt of the desert with Makaz, I found the slave woman dead in my room, her eyes bulged out and her black tongue swollen and falling out of her mouth. Makaz also lost the servant who was watching over her. The child remained undiscovered, as did her mother’s killer for quite some time. Only after I stumbled upon the diary kept by my father did I begin to understand what had happened. He kept volumes of books which he wrote his thoughts and deeds down on in a secret room in the older recesses of the Fish. He also kept books of magic and various powerful items and scrolls. I sat for hours uncovering the secret truths about him, myself and the life I had been brought up in.

In his earliest books, well before even my mother’s mother was born he talked about a winter device. Much of the writing was in a tongue I could not read, but I have always been a quick study and an apt pupil and I copied down the few diagrams I found along with the words they contained to research later. I also discovered that this man was not my true father, but a necromancer of great power who had decided to create the ultimate undead fighter. He talked about his experiments, always named Zsalshun, who worked well until they started to loose their minds. I never found the book that told what he did to us to make us this way, only a reference to finding another suitable babe for his experiment. This time, a dark elf. I also found the entry that told of how he had eradicated the distraction to his newest Zsalshun and that I should return to my studies shortly now that that was over.

I left his small study as I had found it, save I had gained a determination to kill the man who posed as my father. I harbored this for years as I grew in power. My "father" was a drinker and a fine gambler, something which supported him more than the Fish did. However, Makaz was slightly better at cards and fit well into my plan. I told the enchanter nothing but watched for an opportunity when he and my "father" would be at the same table with the stakes fairly high. It came to pass that both were involved in a game of cards that lasted well into the small hours of the night. Both were drinking and with help from me, Makaz was feeling the liquor less and my "father" more. I served, they played. I kept my presence on the fringes of my "father’s" conscience as he began to loose more and more frequently. I reminded him by my very person of the bar he had bought. As the last hand came into play, he had run out of finances save that one wrinkled piece of paper that made the establishment his. His hand was good, but I wagered that Makaz’s was better. The enchanter played his cards close but I had a feeling that my "father’s" luck was about to run out for good. The deed went up, Makaz laid down and my stunned "father" watched as his establishment was swept off of the table into the arms of the enchanter.

It took not more than a few seconds for him to react and when he did, it was with the drunken lunge of a desperate man. Makaz wasn’t quite fast enough to retreat unharmed, but quick enough to leave with his life. He will always have the scar where my "father’s" knife bit into him and I will always have the satisfaction of seeing my "father" bested and having a small hand in it. If Makaz’s own knife hadn’t ended my "father’s" ambitions, the slow poison I had been feeding him all night would have.

I had nothing left for me in the underground city and a small child who’s life remained in danger every minute she stayed in this town. I finally decided that I had gained what knowledge I needed to know in order to leave. I had no idea where to go, but I knew that Neriak was not the place to raise the child elf. Makaz suggested to me to try and find a group known as Winterborne. The name made me uneasy, as it was very close to the Winter’s Device that I had sketched and translated from my "father’s" books, but whether Makaz knew about this or not, he never said.

I and the child set out the next day with Makaz’s help and the three of us journeyed across the land to the city of Qeynos. Amber, for that is as best as I could come to pronouncing the name her mother called her, and I stayed while Makaz journeyed back home. And so it is for the child as well as some questions I have of my own that I joined this house. I wonder if my visions are what the man whom I called father was looking for as signs of me loosing my mind or if these are truly gifts of an interestingly patient god. Perhaps these questions can be answered, perhaps not. Time will tell.
