Checkout Games reviews MS GameVoice
Catalyst [9/8/2000-6:37:27 PM]  Comments[2]

Checkout.com Games has put up a new review of Microsoft GameVoice. With the recent announcement of EQ's voice recognition support, I thought some of you might want to see what it has to offer. If you currently own GameVoice and use it for EQ, or any other voice software feel free to add your opinions to the user comments section. Thanks to Gigantorr for pointing out the review, here's a snip from the page:

Besides chatting, at the tap of a button you can switch to Command mode. Rather than transmitting your voice to other players, the computer actually listens to you, and does what it's told. The software includes profiles for the most popular online games from UT to Everquest, and you can create new ones for games that aren't supported, assigning voice commands to keyboard shortcuts. Say "Ripper" when you're backed into a corner in a UT deathmatch, and you'll automatically change weapons; say "Build Archery" in AOE2 and you won't have to bother hunting down a keystroke. It takes a few minutes for the computer to learn your voice, but it's surprisingly intuitive, and new game profiles are easy to make.

The priciple point the testers and Gordon are missing

You missed the point completely ! There is a huge amount of work that still needs to be done in balancing the classes.

That's certainly a valid opinion, but it remains an opinion. As I've mentioned before, regardless the status of the classes, and no matter what changes we make, there will always be people who have opinions regarding the relative power of the classes. The only way to avoid this would be to make all classes the same, at which point we wouldn't have a class-based system and thus the game wouldn't be EverQuest.

As you mentioned in your post, nothing is exactly black and white. The concept of balance fits this perfectly. Balance is not a point that we aim for, but a range. Some classes might be at the bottom of that range, other classes might be more towards the top. It could be said that we should keep making adjustments until everyone is in the middle, but it should be obvious to anyone looking at these boards that we're not going to get universal agreement regardless.

Right now our biggest concerns at higher levels is can you get a group. No class is so disadvantaged that they cannot get a group, providing that they've cultivated the friendships necessary to do so. Does this mean that a character that has soloed from 1 to a high level might not be able to get a group? Certainly, but that is OK. That is one of the prices of choosing to do nothing but solo.

I haven't yet heard from anyone who will say that they have a bunch of friends in game, yet all of them say "sorry dude, I can't group with [your class] any more." People choose their groups not only based upon a specific need, but based upon who is "cool" and whose company they enjoy.

-Gordon

Abashi: What happened to the question of the week on fun?

Thanks for running the questions of the week. For the camping question, you posted a summary of what you learned. Are you planning to do this with the topic of fun? I'd like to see what you found.

I think that if anything, the thoughts presented supported my original post where I spoke about the diversity of EverQuests players, and the fact that many people see different things as fun. Some spoke, for instance, about trade skills being fun, while some others said specifically that they weren't fun.

The task before us is to provide more. More options. More content. More things to do. We need to provide as many avenues to fun as possible, without infringing on other players' idea of fun. For instance, there are those who find it fun to cause other players grief. We do not wish to support that playstyle.

EverQuest already has many avenues to fun available to the players, leaving it up to you to decide which avenues to take. We can always provide more, as long as those avenues do not cause other avenues to break down as mentioned above.

- Gordon

Monk unbuffed running speed (EQ + D&D)

In D&D, like EQ, a monk receives AC bonuses for maintaining a set low weight. However, they also receive the advantage of a faster rate of unbuffed running speed which increases as the monk's level advances. Like the AC bonus, the running bonus vanishes once the limit of weight has been crossed.

We'd decided long ago that a faster running speed wouldn't be a part of a class-specific skill, but would be handled via spells and such.

-Gordon

Hot Topic: Improving EverQuest
Catalyst [9/9/2000-6:32:14 PM]  Comments[75]

I've been receiving quite a few suggestions lately for idea's to help improve EQ. While some are well thought out, others are simply good idea's that don't have the proper structure to be implemented into the game. With this in mind, I open up This Weeks Hot Topic in hopes we can develop some good idea's with a proper structure to submit to Verant and discuss way's the team could implement them.

Also there is no need for bashing/flaming; this is a constructive discussion to try and help better the game as a whole, so if you want to flame take it to another thread.

Anyway, let your voice be heard, and try and keep an open mind and truly think through your idea's.

A realistic look at upcoming MMORPG's and the future of EQ (long)

There seems to be a lot of misinformation and false expectations on this board concerning EQ and future MMORPG's in development. I've been following a number of games being built and thought I'd pass on the information I have gathered and give you my own interpretation of where this genre is heading.

The best estimate I currently have on the number of subscribers of the Big 3 (EQ, AC, and UO) is 600,000 with EQ having about 300,000 subscribers, UO 200,000, and AC 100,000. For those who seem to want EQ to die out it should be stated that the continuing success of EQ is vital for the funding and development of future games. MMORPG's are enormously expensive to build (currently around 8 to 10 million dollars) and are considered risky investments. The failure of AC to draw the same numbers as EQ and UO has scared off some investors and publishers. If EQ's subscribers were to drop off sharply it could very well kill some of the MMORPG's in development who still haven't gotten all of the funding necessary to complete their project.

We have Sovereign (www.station.sony.com/sovereign) in development, the first RTS game set in a massively multiplayer persistant world.
