Everlore - Ask Milo

Introduction
 

Milo D. Cooper is the man behind the models. Most of the 3D characters from the strong and hardy Dwarves, to the elegant and graceful Elves, and even the rare and powerful Dragons of EverQuest; all of which were beautifully produced by Milo's skilled hand. As such because of Milo's excellent work in EQ he's become highly respected in the 3D Modeling industry.

Milo is the 3D Character artist at Verant Interactive, and he allowed us to ask him a barrage of questions, about the models in EverQuest to character modeling in general.

Ask Milo here on EverLore has been discontinued. You may read any of the past columns in our Archive Section. Milo's website can be found at http://www.milospace.net/

Milo has done a great job doing the columns and he will be missed!

Many readers of this site have gotten on my case about chiding people for their poorly written e-mails to me. As some of you are aware, I often reprimand my readers for bad spelling, incorrect grammar and syntax, and other manifestations of poor English usage skills.

Recently, a friend of mine (who will remain nameless, so his current employer won't know that he doesn't like his job), who has expressed an interest in game design, sent me the URL of an article that includes the following page:

www.gamasutra.com/features/game_design/19980320/hiring_designers_02.htm

Let this be a lesson to all of you. What's written at that link is very true for lots of professions, not just that of game designer. It's a fact: if someone can't write well, he/she is probably going to come across as unintelligent (like some of the people who write to me), and I'm not going to say that such a perception isn't warranted. As the Gamasutra article suggests, the ability to write well indicates lucidity of thought and cohesiveness of reason better than just about anything else, while poorly written text betrays puerile, fragmented, and/or uncommitted thought processes, particularly without the benefit of other credentials that prove the writer's intelligence.

Someone who wants to be heard will want to be understood, and that requires structured thought and communication. There's no way around it. A few mistakes are forgivable, but come to me with excessive slang or insufficient punctuation or egregiously spelled words or inefficiently communicated ideas, and I'll treat you like the fourth-rate intellectual slob that you are. Then, I'll go eat my lunch and do it some more.

Period.

Milo Says:
Good questions, Mr. Wright.

Most of us artists use commercially available software -- Maya, 3D Studio MAX, Adobe Photoshop, etc. Our world builders use in-house software to build dungeons.

As far as I know, our graphics engines are also cooked in-house. Our programmers write plug-ins that allow us artists to export our work to formats suitable for their custom code.

The models are WYSIWYG.

I got into the gaming industry a long time ago (1991), and all I had to show my potential employer at the time was some of the two-dimensional, 4-bit color work that I did on the Amiga CRPG game that I, Brad McQuaid, and Steve Clover were completing in our spare time. Nowadays, one has to have a much more impressive portfolio, and I've come to know so many people in this industry over the years that I've never actually had to put together anything like the five minute MTV commercials that some artists use to sell themselves.
