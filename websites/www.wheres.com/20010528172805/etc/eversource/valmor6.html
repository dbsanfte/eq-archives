Chapter 6

    We strode through the city gates, and I was amazed at the sheer number and variety of folk that wandered through the streets and lanes of Qeynos. I often found myself closing my gaping jaw when seeing someone (or something) particularly extraordinary. Malachi chuckled just as frequently, having a hard time understanding my wonder.

    "Really, Valmor – you must learn to close your mouth," he said with a smirk. "It appears you’re trying to catch flies.

    My jaw snapped shut. "Aye," I replied. "I just had no idea there were so many different types of people in the lands. Humans and half elves are all I’ve ever seen – I didn’t have the upbringing you did." I stopped speaking as a particularly strange fellow passed me by. "Malachi," I whispered. "Do you see that man over there?"

    He glanced in the direction I indicated with my chin. "Aye," he said softly with a lingering hiss of breath. "’Tis a Drow, or dark elf, disguised very well. You see his image shimmering? It is some enchantment to hide his true form." Almost as if the dark one had heard us, his image changed completely and he blended into the crowd. We immediately lost sight of him. "Blast," Malachi cursed. "Watch your back, friend, or you might find a knife in it."

    "From where do such come," I asked, walking along briskly while looking over my shoulder.

    "Not far from here – Neriak, to the north. I have never seen the city, but I hear it is a hive of villainy. Shadow knights, necromancers and dark clerics of the worst reputation inhabit the city, and some say the sun never rises within its walls." Malachi also glanced around carefully, his eyes narrow slits as he concentrated on each passerby. "We would do well to stay far from such a place."

    "Aye, well spoken," I said.

    A short distance inside the city, we came to a building labeled Crow’s Pub and Casino. Fancying a drink and perhaps a meal, we stepped through the doors into the smoke filled interior of the place. "An ale would hit the spot mightily," I said, although no such words needed to be spoken.

    We sat at an empty table close to the long, oaken bar and drank several of the warm, delicious beverages down, washing the road dust completely from our memories. It    wasn’t long before we were feeling little pain, and we laughed, sang, and cheered with the crowd at the various gaming tables – though neither of us partook in the games ourselves. We had no coin to spare, which didn’t seem to stop some of the poorer looking folk from wagering all they could. Some fared better than others, but it always seemed that the casino fared best of all.

    The hours passed quickly, and as we stumbled out of the place the sun had begun to set and the shadows lengthened. "How long were we in there," I asked my teetering friend while rubbing my eyes with my fingers.

    "Too long," he smiled toothily. "Now we need an inn, or I shall surely fall asleep right here in the street!"

    After walking down the same alleys at least for or five times, we finally had to break down and ask a city guard where the nearest inn was. Qeynos is a very confusing city, to say the least, and getting lost became a bad (but frequent) habit for us. We headed southeast towards the docks as we had been directed, and it wasn’t long before the Lion’s Mane Inn and Tavern loomed before us. Lanterns were being lit on the outside of the inn, and the innkeeper hailed us with a smile as we entered.

    It was a warm and friendly place, with many people filling out the tavern tables and stools. The smell of warm bread freshly baked overwhelmed the senses; we realized that we hadn’t eaten all day. Without ceremony, we devoured a large loaf and some roast chicken. My head started to ache with the sight of ale passing us by, so we paid for our meal and adjourned to the cheapest room we could rent in the place. We drew straws for the bed and the luck was with him; I got the hard floor that evening.

    ***********************************************************

    Morning came early, and we rose with clouded heads but happy memories. We were afforded a basin of brackish water with which to clean our hands and faces, and when we finished with it, it was passed to the next room for their use. After a quick breakfast of a bit of sausage and hard rolls, we continued our exploration of the massive city. It was a glorious morning, full of bright sunshine – completely enjoyable once our heads cleared. And clear they did, with the first full breath of the fine salt air from the docks.

    "Ah! Smell the sea, Valmor. Isn’t it magnificent? Look! A ship approaches!" Malachi walked quickly towards the docks and watched the vessel enter the harbor. I was amazed yet again, as I had never seen a ship before. "Come! Perhaps we can set foot on her before she leaves." I shuddered, but followed.

    "Where does this ship go," I asked a deck hand as I strode majestically on board. Hopefully, no one would see my quaking legs.

    "Erudin, sire. Isle of the mages," he replied while tying a firm knot in a frayed rope connected to something I was very sure was important. "Have ye been?"

    I shook my head. "No, I have never traveled across the water."

    "Ah, yer missing a great trip, sire. You should venture out onboard one day," he said, waving his free hand across the deck. "She’s a fine vessel, eh?"

    "Indeed. Perhaps I shall go someday, but not today, friend." I smiled at him and waved as we strode off. I was happy to place my feet on the firm land again.

    We wandered for most of the day through the various marketplaces, looking for things that we could afford. Hucksters called from small canvas lean-tos, trying to get your business any way they could. Most shouted the types of items they were selling with an inflated price, while others cursed their competition outright: "Why would you buy from Engert? He’s without a doubt the WORST tailor I’ve ever seen! This garment would suit you far better, at half the price!" Most of it seemed harmless enough, but we were sure a bout of fisticuffs would break out at least once.

    After some serious bartering (or what we considered serious), we each bought a few pieces of patchwork armor, taking the time to get the fit just right; it wouldn’t do to be wearing poorly fitted stuff for days on end. After our shopping spree was completed we were pretty well spent, in more than one sense. We had just enough coin left to feed and house ourselves for another night, and perhaps enough left over for another ale or two. Provisions would not a problem for us – the land would provide what we needed once we left the city. We were just splurging now; tasting a bit of the good life before we hit the road again.

    We returned to the Lion’s Mane and seated ourselves at the bar for a cheap dinner. The bartender approached us and took our order.

    "What is the word on the street, friend," Malachi asked of the tavern master while pushing a few coppers his way. "Anything interesting happening here in Qeynos?"

    Our new friend slid the coppers across the bar into his other open palm with a jingle. "Aye, perhaps, perhaps." He paused for effect, while looking around carefully. "Let me see to your dinners, and then we’ll have a chat." With that, he strode off towards the kitchen.

    We were served a bit of lamb, blackened almost beyond recognition, and some potatoes that could use a few more minutes in the pot – but we were satisfied. Our bellies full, we sighed happily and waited for our friend to return. Our patience was rewarded, as he finished with another customer and returned to us.

    "Have ye been into the aqueducts," he asked, polishing his bar casually.

    "Nay, sir. What of them?" I pricked up my ears and listened intently.

    "From what I hears from me sources, there’s a lot of smuggling going on in the underbelly of the city. Illegal trade, possibly slaves as well. Constables don’t seem to concerned with the whole thing, as well. Very strange." He took our empty glasses and refilled them without hesitation. I thought of stopping him, but didn’t want to interrupt his speech.

    "Aye, rumor has it that there’s some corruption in the upper ranks of the city guard, but I don’t know nothin’ ‘bout that. Guards have always been right kindly to this alehouse owner, that’s fer sure. But I believe the bit about the ducts. Hear there’s right odd monsters down there as well. Giant, living cubes of jelly. Alligators and sharks. Rats, snakes, and the occasional member of the walking dead! Agh, gives me the shivers just thinking about that place!" He did visibly shake, and I could see a bead of sweat forming on his brow.

    "How does one gain passage to such a place," Malachi asked.

    "Just take a dip anywhere in the city, lads. Mind, I hopes ye can hold yer breath longer than this old man! I’ve also heard of a secret entrance outside the city walls, but ye can’t trust everything ye hears now, can ye? If ye enter within the walls, don’t let the guards catch you at it – they’re right unpleasant about people entering. Perhaps because they are involved, somehow. That’s all I can tell ye – I’ve said enough." With that, he strode off to serve another thirsty customer.

    "Talkative man, eh?" I looked at Malachi to see what he thought of the whole story. "Do you believe him?"

    "Aye. I have heard of other strange goings on here in the city recently. This rumor does much to confirm them. Perhaps we should pay a visit to the underbelly of this place, my friend." Malachi got up from his stool, which scraped across the wooden floorboards. "But not until a good night’s rest."

    "Aye. And I get the bed this eve," I said with a grin.

    "Fair enough," he replied, and we turned in.
