Chapter 7

    Entrance to the Qeynos aqueduct brought with it a certain amount of difficulty. Malachi and I were told by several “reliable” sources that we would be able to access the area by taking a quick dive in any of the various pools spread throughout the city. Swimming in these areas was not looked upon as a worthwhile activity by the guards, however, so we elected to make our first attempt under the cover of the Norrathian darkness.

    Because the pool nearby the Qeynos bank looked the most hospitable (which meant that it had the thinnest layer of green slime atop it), this was going to be our first target. We were equipped as lightly as possible to facilitate our ease in swimming, but we guessed that our weapons and the light pieces of armor we wore would make the going difficult. Arriving in the dank tunnels uninformed and unarmed, however, seemed a far worse alternative than a tough swim – and that issue was a difficult one to debate.

    It was a great stroke of luck for me that my companion carried a lesser lightstone; a small, round gem that cast a very useful glow when uncovered. The adventurers of Norrath used them everywhere, prying them from the corpses of slain willowisps all over the lands. We really had no idea how our infravision would operate in such a place, as we had very little information as to lighting sources below. It was good to have a viable backup in the stone. Also, the lightstone would prove irreplaceable while trying to navigate the dark waters into which we were about to plunge. We had only rumors of how long we needed to hold our breath before emerging in the tunnels below, and taking a wrong turn somewhere would prove fatal. We could only hope that the information we had been given was true.

    “Let me go ahead,” I said. “I can hold my breath for a decent period. I’ll take a look at what’s around, and then return and get you. There’s no need for both of us to be flailing around down there the first time.” My words were no more than whispers, and we spoke tucked in an alley, avoiding the moonlight.

    “Aye, if you wish,” Malachi replied. He did not seem at all disappointed with my offer, and he handed me the covered lightstone carefully. We both glanced around as we exchanged the item, as if we were partaking in some illegal transaction. “Godspeed, Valmor,” my friend said seriously.

    I lowered myself into the dark water slowly, with nary a sound. Our daytime scouting of the pool had shown us that there was a passage that shot almost straight towards the center of the earth to one side of the wretched pool. The passage had looked very intimidating even in the daylight; that was one benefit of traveling by night, I supposed. The water was fairly cool, but not terribly so – I believe the chill of my fear brought the temperature down a few degrees that night.

    “Wait,” Malachi hissed softly. “Guard!” With nowhere else to hide, he slipped into the dark pool beside me and we did our very best to hide ourselves, keeping mostly submerged in the water.

    The city guard came into view, the polished plates of his armor shining in the combination of the moonlight and rays cast by street lanterns. He seemed almost as an apparition as he strode slowly towards us, mail clinking and clanking with each approaching stride. I could not make out his face, but his helmet swung round in circles as he surveyed the area. My heart caught in my throat as his head stopped with him looking directly towards us, and a rippling shudder passed through me as his right hand slid down towards the blade at his belt.

    “Halt! In the name of Antonius Bayle,” he bellowed as he whipped the sword from its sheath with a schiiiiing. Without any hesitation, he began to move quickly towards us.

    My mind was reeling with possibilities on what we should do – run? swim? surrender? I was trying to make up my mind as quickly as I could when Malachi shoved me back to my senses.

    “Go! Now!” he said softly, but with considerable emphasis that the command should be carried out immediately and without perceivable delay. I took as deep a breath as my frightened lungs would let me, and submerged into the inky blackness.

    My first prayer as I dove downwards was that the guard would not enter the pool with all of that plate armor on his back, but this thought passed from my mind as the terror of being underwater in the dark sank deeply into my psyche. I quickly uncovered the lightstone with quivering hands and was rewarded with a full view of the passage downwards. I was so relieved to have my vision back that the stone slipped from my hands carelessly. Cursing myself, I swam as quickly as I could after it, and managed to almost keep up as the stone hit the bottom of the tunnel.

    So disoriented was I that I could not even think of what had happened to my friend, but I knew something was back there, as my feet were kicking something as I swam.

    I grabbed the lightstone from the bottom and we swam on and on, horizontally now as the passage had turned. I realized that I was now past the point of no return – I would die before I could go back the way we came, and my body would rot within the watery tunnels of the city. This was one of the more pleasant thoughts that passed through my mind during this experience. As I began to lose hope, the passage angled gently upwards and my pace increased with the anticipation of fresh air greeting my lungs. Within a few seconds, I broke the surface, gasping and sucking in a welcome combination of water and air – at this point any air was better than what could have been. Malachi arrived right behind my, and I could see him grinning broadly.

    “Kill the light,” he whispered. “And don’t kick me in the face any more.” I took a quick look at my surroundings and covered the magical stone.

    There was a dim light being given off by some sort of mossy growth throughout the place, and it seemed to be present on most of the stone walls that we could see from our vantage point in the pool. All looked clear, so I pulled myself out of the water and helped Malachi do the same. We shook off as much water as we could and looked around carefully.

    The tunnels were all cut from the rock beneath the city, and were amazingly well preserved considering the amount of time they had been here. The walls were smooth and covered with both the mossy residue, as well as a slick layer of water and slime. We were in a large chamber, the entrance pool taking up a large portion of one corner of the room, and there were many passages leading outwards in a confusing choice of options. My spirits dropped as I considered the odds of keeping from getting lost.

    “Who’s idea was this again?” Malachi asked, fixing me with his dripping gaze.
