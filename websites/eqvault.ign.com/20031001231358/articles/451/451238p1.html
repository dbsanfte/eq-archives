Ask SOE #4 - Our EQII Q&A
With Moorgard, EQII's Community Manager


Lepidus: Guilds are a huge part of the original EverQuest. Can you explain how the Guild will fit into EQII and what new spins the game will put on them?

Steve "Moorgard" Danuser: We currently have three types of social structures in EverQuest II: Guilds, Families, and Fellowships.

Guilds function much like they do in EverQuest, but with an additional political element. When a player founds a Guild, it will be registered in whichever city he or she is a citizen of. Guilds must pledge to support one of the five factions in that city (see our Lore page: http://everquest2.station.sony.com/lore.jsp). Essentially, each Guild will be competing against other Guilds to raise its prominence within its home town. The Guilds that achieve the highest standings will receive tangible benefits as long as they continue to maintain that standing.

Where the Guild is registered will not impact who can join it. For example, a player from Freeport is free to join a Guild that is based in Qeynos, but that player will essentially be contributing to the strength of the opposing city. This won't hurt how that player is perceived in Freeport, but it does mean that this player wouldn't receive all the benefits that a citizen of Qeynos would.

Families are registered in a city, but they don't vie against each other in the political meta-game that Guilds play. Members of a Family enjoy the camaraderie present in a Guild without the atmosphere of competition. When a player founds a Family, he or she creates a unique surname that will be adopted by any player who joins it. Seeing a character with a surname will instantly tell you which Family that player belongs to. Players who quit one Family and join another will see their surname change accordingly.

Fellowships are limited to six members. This is a small social structure that helps friends stick together in the game as they level up. Fellowships can pool a certain percentage of experience, which is then distributed among all members. It is designed to allow friends who want to play together keep their progress fairly consistent. Shared experience will be distributed even when players are offline.

Fellowships are not a substitute for gaining experience on your own. A level 40 player who forms a Fellowship with a level 3 player will not be able to help that character gain levels at an accelerated rate. Experience will be distributed proportionately, so the level 3 player would only receive experience as if he were killing monsters of his own level. Fellowships are designed to provide the most benefit when its members are relatively close in level and invest a similar amount of time to the game.

A player can be a member of any or all of these three social structures at the same time. One person can be in a Fellowship, a Family, and a Guild simultaneously, because the three structures don't compete against each other.


Lepidus: Obviously some degree of character customization is necessary in these games. Apart from what was mentioned in the FAQ, in how much depth will you be able to make your character your own in EQII?

Steve "Moorgard" Danuser: As we have already stated, our facial customization options will allow you to create a truly unique visual appearance. We will also have a wide variety of different armor and clothing available that will provide another level of individuality.

Additionally, the choices you make in game affect much more than just how you look. Our extensive class tree lets you select a profession with which you can closely identify. As a player, you will choose to support a faction within your city just like a Guild does. Which faction you support will in turn provide access to unique spells and abilities. This means all members of a particular sub-class won't be carbon copies of each other, but we will make sure that uniqueness doesn't negatively impact game balance.


Lepidus: Your FAQ boasts that a main goal of EQII is "to design methods of gameplay that will facilitate both casual and hardcore players." What types of in-game systems and mechanics will make this possible?

Steve "Moorgard" Danuser: The introduction of new types of social structures certainly helps support varying styles of play. If you enjoy competing against other players, a Guild is definitely for you. If you prefer to be more laid-back and just want to group with like-minded players, a Family may be just what you're looking for. If you have a small group of friends you want to play with on a regular basis, a Fellowship will help you stick together.

We are designing the majority of our content with a single group of four to six players in mind. This allows us to create a deep, meaningful experience that players can share, regardless of their play style. Our raid content is built for two to four groups working together, depending on the encounter. This means fun isn't limited based on your ability to amass a huge army of players to fight mobs, but rather on being able to assemble a tightly knit group of highly skilled players.

Lumping players into "casual" and "hardcore" categories has in the past been based primarily on how many hours a week they play. We believe that even people who can only invest a couple hours at a time still want an adventure that is challenging and fun, and we intend to provide it. Our game is extremely challenging, but that challenge is based on how well you play your class, not how many hours a week you play.
-- Lepidus
