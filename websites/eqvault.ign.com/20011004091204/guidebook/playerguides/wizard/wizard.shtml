Player Guide: Rudimentary Wizard Guide

Author: Damiano (N/A)   Organization: N/A

Forword
These are my personal notes regarding play of the Wizard class, and should not be taken as the end all, be all definition of how to play. Everyone has their own perception of the class, and how it should be played: I’ve endured more passing fighters and rogues telling me “Use your Root spell. You’re no tank.” than I care to count, for example. As with everything, in and out of real-life, find your own style, within your own tolerances, and stick to it. You’ll be happier in the end. To that end, I should point out that my style of play is essentially that of the loner: I do a lot of solo hunting (80% of the time, minimum). A player with a solid group to lean on probably will develop along different lines and use different techniques, as would a player with a more gregarious (outgoing) personality. Other wizards will have different tips and opinions, I’m sure. This document simply provides some insight as to one way of playing the class with some degree of success, as proven by my own progress from 1st to 18th level so far: nothing more.

Introduction
-=-
So. You desire to be a Wizard. Hmmm.

You see the great mages wield their might, and wish to be as they are, but you know not the trials they have endured. Do you really think you have the strength, the intellect, the sheer will, to succeed where so many before you have failed? Do you understand the difficulty of the path you have chosen to walk?

I think not. But, with time, you shall.

Yes, you shall…
 -=-

The common player preconception of the Wizard class is that of the classic high fantasy magic wielder, a taciturn and stand-offish one-man demolition squad, hurling fireballs and lightning bolts at will to the utter destruction of any creature foolish enough to stand in the way. Not quite the accurate perception to bring into EQ, I’m afraid: at least not right away. This is not to say that the Wizard is not capable of solo play, but the class is not exactly designed to accommodate your desires in that arena until much later in life.

This guide should give the prospective wizard a fair taste of the promise and pitfalls of playing a Wizard up to about level 20. Perhaps someday I will be able to expand upon these observations for higher levels as well, but this should be sufficient to help the fledgling newbie wizard conquer the initial stages of advancement.

Character creation
You have several options when choosing to create your character: Erudian, Human, High Elf, Gnome, and Dark Elf. My own experience is as a High Elf, but much of the general strategy and tactics are similar regardless of your initial race. You need a high Intelligence. Of the remaining stats, I would emphasize Charisma, Stamina, and Agility to as great an extent as possible, but truthfully, the end all and be all of your existence will be your Intellect (both that of the character and the player). However, you can use the guidelines below to make your own determination:

DEX: how quickly you learn weapon skills; how often weapon will proc; how quickly you learn rogue skills; how hard you hit with bows
STR: determines how much you can carry; influences maximum and average damage; influences how quickly you learn many offensive skills
STA: affects how many hit points you have, how long you can hold your breath
INT: directly affects how quickly you can learn most skills; affects the amount of mana for bards and all mages, as well as shadow knights
WIS: affects the amount of mana the priest classes have, also rangers; also affects how quickly you can learn many skills if your wisdom is higher than INT.
CHA: affects amount you will be paid for goods by NPC merchants, and how much they will charge you; affects the saving throw on certain bard and enchanter spells (charms in particular)
AGI: affects how quickly you can learn some defensive skills, how difficult it is to hit you as well as how much damage you take when you are hit

Now, using the base Intelligence score as a guide, the following would seem to be the general rule with regards to how the racial choices compare to each other, with regards to being a Wizard. Erudians (117) are tops, Dark Elves (109)/Gnomes (108) are nearly tied for second, High Elves (102) come in a lowly but still respectable fourth, and Humans (85) are dead last. There are mitigating factors, to be sure, but that those are the baselines for comparison.

Initial Preparations
I will not go into a huge amount of detail on initially preparing your character for play, as this is done in many other places and ways. However, I will strongly recommend two things: memorize the two spells you start with on scroll prior to doing anything else, and perform the quest as outlined in the note you similarly start with before looking for any trouble. The quest will yield you an Apprentice’s Robe, which is at least minimal armor, and at the start, you need all the help you can get. Explore your city thoroughly, find the places you get spells, food, and supplies: you’ll have to do it sometime, may as well do it now.

A note on newbie play: try not to beg for this, that, and the other thing. If someone is standing there and willing to help, great, but otherwise take your lumps as the game gods intended, and move on. Everyone’s been a newbie at some point, and worked their way out of it, often alone. Politely asking for aid is smart: a shouted demand for sympathy is not. Of course, you can always try to pull the telemarketer ploy and be so obnoxious that people give you what you want simply to make you shut up: but I wouldn’t suggest it as a way to build a great reputation.

Low Level Tactics
To be successful as a low-level Wizard, it is crucial you keep one idea firmly in mind: you are a complete wimp. At first level, you have less physical ability to deal out and take damage than the weakest creature in game, and it only slowly gets better as time goes on. Your magical abilities are only slightly more potent. In short, if you are out of mana, you are nearly helpless. If you can’t deal with that, start over and make some other class: warrior, cleric, druid, etc. You do have one advantage, however, and it is this one thing that makes the class playable at low level: if you are careful and smart, you should almost always have the opportunity to get in the first (and occasionally second) shot. You can, on occasion, take a half-dead large rat in raw hand-to-hand fighting (now there’s power!) This is the advantage you need to work as hard as you can, because it is the only way you will ever progress in solo play at low levels, and it greatly speeds advancement at more advanced ones.

For your first forays into the wild, it is generally best to stay as close as possible to some form of backup: this may be the town guards, a friendly warrior or priest you grouped with, whatever. Grouping is by far the most secure way to reach your next spell rank in the shortest amount of time. However, it is not impossible to solo your way to level in a respectable amount of time, as long as you are careful.

At these lowest levels, one on one, hand to hand, without some special advantage like getting first swing or having a couple of shielding spells up, you will lose: count on it. Every combat should start with you “considering” and selecting a distant target. At lower levels, you can usually defeat creatures which “con” white/black, a color that is meant to signify a level of strength approximately equal to your own. Be aware, as you advance, the sphere of safety slowly shrinks: in the 5th to 9th level range, whites are a 50/50 proposition even at full strength; at 10th and up, restricting any solo activity to blues or less becomes crucial if you want to make any progress.

Once your have chosen your target, hit that creature with your best, hardest spell at the maximum range possible, and start another casting of that same spell as soon as possible as the creature rushes toward you. With luck and practice, you will have a fair chance of getting the second casting off before your target hits you for the first time. Be sure to be in auto-attack mode before the creature closes with you, regardless: it never hurts to select a target, hit auto-attack, then cast your first “pulling” spell. Once melee begins, keeping casting as fast as possible, watching for the multiple interruptions you will suffer as your target hits you, until you are either out of mana or until one or the other of you is dead. As you progress and gain experience, you will begin to notice rhythms with various creatures which maximize the number of spells you can get off in the shortest period of time: however, at the start, it’s generally just best to click the spell “stone” as fast as possible.

Also: learn to watch the attack results more than the battle on screen. There is nothing worse than triumphantly finishing off a creature in a “close battle”, and suddenly realizing that it was “close” because two more of the same were/are attacking you from behind! If you suddenly see a marked increase in the frequency of attacks, it might be worth your while to simply turn and run, if only to die in a place you can more easily get to in order to retrieve your corpse.

A final word of caution: be sure you actually select the target you wish to attack: your spells, while harmless to other characters, are damaging to you. Accidentally casting a shock or Area of Effect (AoE) on yourself is one sure way to spend even more time staring at the inside of your spell-book than you already will.

Death
Always remember that death is a mere inconvenience in EverQuest. If you have experience in traditional pen-and-paper role-playing games, this can be a difficult change of viewpoint to handle. Death in EQ has all the significance and impact of waiting in line to pay off some parking tickets: it’s irritating, annoying, and a waste of time and money, but not exceptionally important in the grand scheme of things. This is especially true at the lowest levels, since you do not even lose any experience from death until you reach level 5.

If you have any other mindset, get your expectations worked around into this point of view before you walk out the city gates, because you will be dying. This will occur quite frequently at the start, and continuing at a somewhat slower pace for quite some time thereafter, no matter how intelligent and cautious you think you are. The game is designed around this concept: for example, there are wandering “sure death” encounters which will attack you at random, scattered in every area you have an opportunity to gain experience in while hunting, at every level of play. Also, up to level 5, dying is the cheapest way to re-provision your food and water: remember that all other equipment and gathered loot remains with your previous body, however.

Believe me: if you can find a way to deal with this underlying concept, you will be a lot happier going forward. If “dying” in any way feels like “losing” to you, and you can’t deal with randomly “losing” on occasion regardless of how carefully you play: take up another game, because EQ will have you randomly breaking expensive computer parts in your frustration. ‘Nuf said.

Target selection
Successful wizards become masters of intelligent target and spell selection very early in their career. Unlike other spell-casters, Wizards don’t get a pet to run interference for them if they make a mistake: it’s their precious flesh that’s getting bruised from the very start of the combat. And since you will have far less hit points, armor, and the like than the semi-/non-spell-users… choose wisely, or make friends with a Necromancer who doesn’t mind doing constant searches for your corpse.

When choosing targets, always be aware of your surroundings. Avoid creatures that have a tendency to support one another, like spiderlings and giant wasps, unless there are absolutely no others in sight or you are sure you can take them all. “But it’s so far away…” is no excuse: you can put money on the fact that it will choose to wander your direction the instant you pull that other lonely specimen over on the horizon.

Also, be aware of special abilities a creature has, and factor them in when deciding whether to attack or not. Creatures which stun, poison, or cause disease are often poor targets for a soloing Wizard to choose, since a single unlucky break can leave you at a severe disadvantage either during the fight or afterward. Special caution should be taken with creatures with Poison capability: it is hardly advantageous to defeat a creature, then die of poison shortly thereafter even as you frantically attempt to meditate or bandage yourself through the duration of the effect.

Grouping
Some high-level Wizard players have written in the past that an intelligent Wizard PC should be the defining character in a high-level group. This statement, if true, is probably most valid at upper levels, however it has a certain tactical validity at all levels of play. Wizards have exactly two main abilities: the ability to quickly do a lot of damage, and at upper levels, the ability to evacuate an entire group if things get out of hand. However, despite all this, you will often find it difficult to get into groups because many other PCs have abilities that are more obviously useful than your own. Clerics, shamans, and druids are constantly sought by groups for their healing and buffing capabilities. Magicians can summon supplies, and Enchanters have their “crowd control” spells, in addition to the ever-present possibilities of pets as a backup “tank” in a pinch. Warriors, Paladins, and Rangers are often sought out as front-line troops (i.e. Tanks). All this often combines to leave wizards, along with necromancers, shadowknights, and rogues, out in the cold when being weighed by other classes as influential group players.

If you can get in with a group, however, you can quickly become one of the most influential members in that group through intelligent play. In essence, your role should be that of the “traffic cop”, similar to other spell-casters: however, you achieve this in a markedly different manner. In short, you throw the weight of your spells in to soften up the opposition and prevent concentration of fire on any specific group member. Also, since your role is basically a support role, you are the eyes and ears of the group during combat. If you insist on simply blasting away, you are doing yourself, your party, and your profession a grave disservice.

Healers are generally kept very busy during combat keeping an eye on the health of the group, while the tanks are generally consumed with blunting the enemy rush and attacking as fast as possible. Your most appropriate role as a Wizard is to watch the overall battle, and apply your special talent for laying waste in as strategic a manner as possible. Elements of this strategy should include:

  • quickly drop any creatures approaching death to free up warriors to acquire another target;
  • soften up any opponents which seem to be gaining the upper hand on a party member;
  • Lacking a ranger or druid, who have better spells for these duties, use Root to nail down possible runaways, and throw O’Keills Radiation on your tanks as soon as possible before combat starts…
  • Out in wide open spaces, Oasis for example, you can use occasionally give your Ranger or other “puller” a breather with judicious use of your bolt spells, if you’re feeling a bit suicidal…
  • In large battles, if everyone is engaged with a different opponent, throwing a couple area of effect spells may work to maximize your effectiveness. You need good teamwork and a bit of pre-planning to really pull this off, however. If all of your tanks are concentrating fire on a single opponent, this is a BAD tactic to use. All of the unengaged targets will turn to beat on you, and things will get very, um, messy…
  • if things get desperate, use a Root or Shock to “pull” combatants off of a tank who is getting double or triple teamed, evening the odds and giving them a chance to dispatch their current foe and (hopefully) eventually come to your rescue.
As you progress in levels, you will also become the logical person to call the retreat when the party gets in over it’s collective head: good practice for when you achieve mastery of the Evacuation line of spells at advanced levels. Inspired and conservative play by a wizard can keep a party going strong for a long period of time, and minimize downtime to a surprising degree.

Fizzles and Resistance
These two concepts are collectively the bane of a wizard’s existence. Fizzles are simple failures to cast a spell. They not only waste time, they also cost a portion of the mana which would normally be used to power the spell: 4 or 5 fizzles can easily wipe out a bubble or two of mana, depending upon the spell. As a low-level wizard, you will quickly achieve mastery of the styles of magic central to your existence (Evocation, Abjuration) through simple routine use. However, Divination and Abjuration, important magic types at later levels, may be good choices for occasional training expenditures simply to keep them viable and prevent excessive fizzling later in your career. The best way to reduce fizzles is practice, practice, practice: the higher your skill in the associated realm of magic, the fewer fizzles you shall suffer.

One thing with fizzles: watch your mana bar! The most absolutely idiotic death I have ever suffered occurred while attacking a lone Orc Centurion in Misty Thicket. I started a bit low on mana (75%), but nothing drastic. Let’s try out this new spell, Lightning Bolt… Fizzle. Fizzle. Dang it, go! Fizzle. Fizzle. You WILL fire! Fizzle. Fizzle. Bang! Finally! Cool effect… here he comes… now, a Lightning Shock… “You do not have sufficient mana to cast this spell!” Doh! This is gonna HURT…

Resistance is generally more dangerous to you in the long run than fizzles. Resistance is a relatively random occurrence in which your spells either do much less than normal or fail to do any damage at all. Resistance is essentially what makes attacking creatures that con “white/black” a losing proposition, and makes you basically useless if your party accidentally pulls something too far over your own level. As a guideline, if it cons white or yellow, your spells will likely do much less than damage than normal. If it cons red, save your mana and go hand-to-hand: you will probably have more effect, sad as that sounds. This situation does apparently improve at upper levels: I can’t speak as to the relative improvement as of yet. There doesn’t seem to be much you can do about this except choose your targets carefully and be cautious: you can get partial resistance results even on green targets, and if you’re low on mana to begin with…

Spells
Wizards have essentially 6 types of spells at low level, and a couple of special additions. These are:

Shocks:
The workhorse spell of the wizard. They have relatively short casting times, are not blocked by mere terrain, and do a fair amount of damage per point of mana spent. Shocks are blocked by walls, a fact which can be quite frustrating in dungeons like Blackburrow and Crushbone, as your target conveniently wanders around a corner just before your latest spell goes off…

Bolts:
The ultimate pulling spell in many ways. Slightly longer casting times and the fact that trees, rocks, other PCs, even minor differences in elevation can intercept the effect often make this most effective as your first and last spell in combat, as opposed to what you cast during the fray.

Storms/Spirals:
Area of Effect spells. These are the “look at me, I’m a Wizard” spells, very showy, lots of flash, but with a somewhat muted punch. These can be useful to do a fair spread of damage across a wide selection of targets, but they are also a good way to bite off far more than you can chew if cast at the wrong time. Casting times are generally comparable to that of Shocks and Bolts, but unless you’re in a real bad spot or performing group support, these probably won’t be your first choice.

Shields:
These are personal buffs, adding crucial hit points and armor protection. In general, these can add 10% to 15% to both of these critical stats, and cost a minimal amount of quickly recoverable mana: so why would you ever run around without one active? Note that these spells do not “stack”: multiple castings simply renew or replace the existing effect. One exception to this rule is Resistant Skin, a level 12 spell: this spell is actually a different type of shield, increasing your poison and disease resistance, and does work in tandem with your more familiar Shields.

Your first spell requiring a spell component is a shield as well, the Shieldskin spell obtained at level 16. A Catseye Agate is required to cast this spell, and each casting consumes a gem. Shieldskin is a nice little buff, a bit expensive for continuous use, but as it essentially adds 55 non-recoverable hit points to your total, it can be nice for that little extra bit of insurance when facing an especially tough opponent. It stacks with the normal Shield line as well, as an additional bonus. In general, at this level, it only lasts through the first couple of sets of attacks, and your spell casting can still be interrupted by such impacts: i.e. don’t get cocky.

Teleports:
Fade, Gate, Shadow Step, Bind Affinity. At first glance, these resemble spells that would allow you to escape from combats that are going against you. Don’t bother trying to use them in that capacity, however, unless you are working with a group and don’t mind running out on them when things get hairy. In solo play, these are at best long shots (personally, I consider them false hopes). Fade and Shadow Step move you a completely random distance and direction from your foe: this can be 1 foot or 1 football field. Now factor in the little proviso that despite your lack of knowledge of where you’ve ended up, your attacker knows precisely where you are. In short, unless you get quite lucky and know the area very well, neither spell is likely to move you far enough away that your attacker can’t get to you before you get your bearings and start heading for zone. Bottom line: dang near useless.

On the other hand, Gate is an excellent spell for general travel, although it is far too slow to hope to cast it while being attacked. It is much more useful for getting you close to the merchants by teleporting to your starting bind point when you are so encumbered with loot that you can barely move. Bind Affinity allows you to change your bind point to anywhere, or to bind other group members when in a town zone: it makes Gate an even more useful and convenient travel spell than it already is.

Despite the limited usefulness of some of these spells, I do suggest that you still pick all these spells up when you have a little extra cash: they are still needed and useful for practicing the Alteration magic skill, which is the underlying basis of all your future teleport spells.

View spells:
True North, Glimpse, Sense Summoned, Gaze, Bind Sight, Invisibility, Heat Sight. These are all Divination spells, and since Invisibility is grouped in with them and available at level 16, you’ll want to pick these up for practicing Divination as time and cash allow. In general, these spells are not exceptionally useful during play, although they can be nice for selecting targets and doing your initial survey of a new zone. Invisibility is the obvious exception here, of course.

Greater detail regarding the “special additions” is probably in order:

Root: A stand-alone spell, which certainly looks like an after-thought when reviewing the Wizard’s spell lists. It appears to be a relatively weak attempt to appease the soloing Wizard, and give him/her some ability to perform the type of “crowd control” available to many other casters. The basic concept of the Root spell is to hold a creature in place while you decide what to do with it. A good concept and a generally useful spell, but don’t be deluded into thinking this gives you license to attack with impunity.

First, Root is both easily resisted and acts to draw (“pull”) the creature into combat. Second, a Rooted creature can and will still attack if you wander into reach: this isn’t paralysis by any stretch of the imagination. Finally, even if the Root does take hold, the creature gets another chance to break it with every damaging attack that hits it. Therefore, attempting to use Root as part of your initial attack sequence is generally less effective than simply blasting away, since one of two events are 90% likely to occur:

  • Root will often simply fail and pull a completely undamaged creature to you;
  • Root will simply give you the first shot you would have had anyway for many of the remaining attempts, as the first major damage spell causes the creature to overcome the Root
This situation gets a little better as you advance in levels, thankfully: by the mid-teens, you can start to lean on Root a little without doing constant corpse runs as a result. At this point, it’s sticking more often and holding longer, and the creatures you are holding away are doing proportionately more damage per attack than they were earlier in your career.

All of the cautions above are not meant to imply that Root cannot be a useful spell. It can be a better choice for a last ditch escape spell than the Fade/Shadow Step series, since if it does take hold, the creature cannot chase you down for a bit, and you know approximately where you are with limited re-orientation required. Root is also nice for controlling runaways, which can be crucial in crowded zones like Blackburrow and Crushbone, where there can be a whole bunch of reinforcements just itching to jump into the fray around the next corner. Finally, Root can be convenient for crowd control, both while soloing and with groups, holding one or more creatures out of the battle until their friends have been dealt with.

O’Keills Radiation: This is another apparent after-thought, but one which I have kept in my spell arsenal since first trying it out. This spell is the one “buffing” spell the Wizard gets during his entire lower level career. It quite simply causes damage to any creature that strikes the target of the effect. The amount of damage done per hit is minimal (2 hit points), and the effect does not last for very long, but it costs relatively little mana and can easily be the slight edge which gives you or the player you are assisting the victory. It is quickly surpassed by the Druid’s line of damaging shields, but it remains useful well into the upper teens whenever in solo play. My advice is to get this spell, and use it.

This pretty much covers your spells at lower levels. Since in general, the only things you routinely spend cash on are food and spells, there’s rarely much reason not to have all of the next rank of spells available in your spell book shortly after achieving the appropriate level.

Equipment:
Your choices of in the realms of armor and weaponry are, shall we say, limited.

Daggers and staves are your weapon options. You will probably want to get a staff as soon as possible: these generally do more damage per blow than daggers, and there are several nice magical staves available via quest and/or auction once you get yourself established. However, daggers are not that bad an option, if they fit your style: they are quick, do fairly decent damage, and are generally quite light. If you do decide to use daggers, look for a Giant Snake Tooth as soon as you have couple of extra platinum to spend: these are the best of the non-magic daggers available at a reasonable price, and have a quick attack rate to boot. A common magical option in the Piercing category is the Dragoon Dirk: a good solid weapon with some attribute bonuses and nice stats, usable by all spell casters. It results from a fairly lengthy and high-level quest, so you’ll likely need to acquire one through guild membership or auction to have one of these prior to 20th level.

As far as armor goes, you get clothing: period. This is not as worthless as it may sound, however: every little bit helps, and at least clothes are relatively light and cheap. As you get a little more established, look for a tailor (or take up the craft yourself) and get the raw silk versions of your clothes. Some specific things to look for include: Upgrade to a Senior Apprentice Robe as the cash becomes available. These robes look identical to your starting equipment, but have a slightly higher AC rating, and a +1 mana to boot (+2 for the Gnomish version). Again, every little bit helps… There is an item available in Qeynos from one of the tent vendors, and possibly elsewhere, called a Silk Evening Tunic. This is a +5 CHA item, and while it takes the same inventory slot as your robe, a +5 CHA translates into saving a copper per silver piece when purchasing equipment, and similar gains when selling off your loot. This item pays for itself in no time: if you have the opportunity, acquire one ASAP. Put this on as you walk into town, and switch back into your Robe as the last step before heading out the gates.

Your limited options in armor and weapons should leave you with a fair amount of available cash at times, which can be well spent picking up small pieces of magical jewelry from merchants or experimenting Enchanters. 5 to 10 platinum can get you a ring or earring with bonus INT, AGI, Hit points, or other useful powers. What good does all that cash do you sitting in the bank, unless you have some specific high level magic items you are saving up to buy?

Nearly any tailor with a relatively low level of skill can make Raw Silk clothing, but it requires a fair amount of silk swatches. Where do you get silk swatches, you might well ask? Well, you make them by simply combining two Spider Silks (not Spiderling silks, only Spider silks will do) in a small Tailoring kit. Spider Silks are a fairly common drop from Giant Spider kills of many sorts. You can expect an average of 1 silk per 3 or 4 spider kills, occasionally better. You can probably get a fairly good deal on a full suit of silk if you bring a good number of pre-made swatches to an accomplished tailor, since simply making silk is trivial (i.e. waste of time) to them at that point. Also, it is instructive to note that while Spider Silks are not stackable, silk swatches are…

At level 16, you get to start working on your Spell Research. There are several fairly well written guides on this endeavor available elsewhere, so I won’t go into great detail, but be warned: this is an expensive and time-consuming skill to develop. It’s also crucial to your development during later levels. Do your homework, and work on it.

Food and drink:
As a wizard, you are one of the subset of classes which does not have some convenient (and free) method of obtaining food and drink while adventuring, either through summoning or foraging. For myself, I found Fishing to be a viable and eventually money-saving alternative to simply buying foodstuffs, since bait sells for 3 to 4 copper, while fish can be sold back for over 2 silver. The fishing poles are rather expensive at low level (over a gold apiece) and they do have a marked tendency to bust at lower levels of skill, so don’t expect this to be quick money-making scheme. However, achieve a reasonable level of skill (20-30) and you’ll be pulling waterlogged sandals, fish scales, even rusty daggers out of the water to supplement your income along with the fish to supplement your diet.

If you’d rather not go through the hassle of staring at lakes and streams for long periods of time, remember that the cheapest form of liquid is milk, and the cheapest form of foodstuff is the muffin (6-8 and 3-4 copper pieces apiece, respectively.) Water is insanely priced as a drink alternative, probably because it is a component in Smithing and Pottery: reminds me of the “sparkling waters” at the convenience store, it does. As for Rations: there’s a money sink if I’ve ever seen one… go with the fishcakes if you can’t find muffins, much cheaper. Save your money for the spells.

Skills:
My own instinct has been to shy away from the crafting skills, since they are incredible money sinks at lower levels, and I am more of an introvert than is required to recoup the costs at advanced levels of skill. Based on my limited experience, I would suggest pursuing the tailoring, smithing, and brewing skills if you are interested in that sort of thing: tailoring is obviously useful (Silk anyone?), while smithing and brewing can be a nice way to get in good with the warriors and priests.

With my wizard, I have simply developed the basic skills of Bind Wounds, Sense Direction, Swimming, and Fishing, and would highly recommend them all. They are (relatively) easily mastered, require only small levels of investment, and have straightforward, practical applications. A couple of notes: Bind Wounds is rather expensive to master, since bandages cost 5 silver or more apiece, and it doesn’t work any miracles, regardless. However, bandaging with a relatively low level of skill (25 or so) can be enough to keep you alive against some fairly nasty poisons… if you have enough bandages on hand. Also, bandaging can be amazingly effective on lower level PCs: with a mere 30 skill, I was recently able to restore over half a bubble per attempt on a character 10 levels less than myself who was near death and suffering from poison. In combination with her meditation, it kept her alive until the poison wore off. Not bad for a mere blast-master, if I do say so myself.

Which brings me to the skill central to maintaining your sanity as a player: meditation. Not much to say, except that it’s insanely useful, and if you don’t get it ASAP, you must be a masochist. It allows you to restore mana and memorize spells much faster than otherwise possible, and seems to slightly increase your healing rate to boot. Unless you truly enjoy flipping through the pages of an empty spellbook or running in circles for long periods of time…

Paths of Progress to 10th level or so:
I have played low level casters on both sides of the world, so I can give some limited direction to Wizards of each of the likely races, although my main experience has been as a High Elf.

High Elf:
Felwithe and Kelethin are within spitting distance of one another: take a right at the fork outside the town gates, run for a couple of minutes: voila. Plenty of good potential targets as well: giant wasp drones, decayed skeletons, and bats are your best bets. Watch for the orc pawns, and be cautious when hunting wasps in crowded areas. Your bolt spells are relatively useless here: too many trees and hills. Stick to Shocks and you’ll get a much better return on your mana. There are several low level quests available to you: Black Wolf pelts (undamaged) go to the Wizard trainer, bat wings (in groups of four) go to an isolated Magician in the rear of that training area, and skeleton bones (in groups of four) go to the head paladin in the temple nearest the lake. All three of these quests return small amounts of experience and a first level spell which can be sold for cash. However, if you really need cash, it’s better to just sell the items directly.

Do not overlook the bard near the exit of the right hand tunnel into the town proper. This is a small fragment of the overarching mail quest, and is basically easy money for you, since a couple of minutes of travel can earn you 1 to 4 gold pieces. Hail the bard, say “I am interested”, respond “deliver Kelethin”, deliver the letter you get to the appropriate bard in Kelethin… 1 to 4 gold, and a miniscule amount of experience. It’s a great way to fund your initial spell acquisition and get familiar with the basic layout of Greater Feydark.

Once you hit fifth level or so, you will probably be looking to change hunting grounds. Orc pawns are probably the only targets regularly giving you experience in the old spots, but you have another tough but generally fun alternative near at hand, the “orc hill”. This is a spot north of Kelethin, between the “orc lift” and the entrance to Crushbone, which can provide hours of nearly constant combat. Be wary, though: this is really above your head up until at least eighth level, when you get your third rank of spells. Hunt this area in groups, or at least in crowds, and choose your targets very carefully (avoid oracles and “big” centurions like the plague). They’ll be choosing you often enough without begging for trouble. Run for the Kelethin “orc lift” whenever things start to get hairy. And think of it this way: at least it’s easy to find your most recent corpse.

Another fairly good option in the same vein is to head to the eastern end of Butcherblock: take that unexplored left fork when exiting Felwithe, and follow it to the end, past the “spires” (the Feydark Gate for Wizards). Again, this is actually a bit above your head for solo play, but a small group or good crowd can make it quite enjoyable. Note that there is no love lost between High Elves and Dwarves: the merchants in this zone will generally just tell you to get lost, or sell at terrible prices if you can get them to barter at all. Head back to Kelethin to dump your loot.

Gnomes:
My experience in Steamfont and Al’Kabor is limited at low levels, but I know there is a fairly good area off to the left of the exit from Al’Kabor, with spiderlings, rats, and kobold runts. Avoid the diseased rats and all their nasty plagues until you can quickly drop them. Spiderlings are both numerous and nasty in this area: not only are they tough to kill, they have a tendency to congregate fast: that one on one “discussion” you arranged can easily turn into a 4 on 1 bloodbath in no time.

I’ve also been told there is small “dungeon” of sorts in lower depths of Al’Kabor itself which is actually a better place to hunt at extremely low levels. I’ve never been there, so I can’t really speak to the truth of that assessment, but from the creatures listed, it sounds pretty good: lots of decayed gnome skeletons and the occasional spiderling and “obsolete clockworks”. Again, a dungeon doesn’t sound like a great place to be using your bolt spells, but the hills outside of town are rather nice for watching the fireworks fly. Remember, fire down at your targets: bolts take a somewhat low trajectory if fired upward, and can easily be intercepted by even a small bump in the terrain.

Al’Kabor itself is a fun and friendly place (if you have good faction). The Guide constructs not only tell you where places are, they actually lead you there… a pleasant surprise if you’ve ever tried to find your way around Kelethin. The magic guild in Al’Kabor also has the widest selection of spells available: High Elves in particular are well advised to check here for hard-to-find scrolls.

Once you get bored in the immediate environs of Al’Kabor, it’s just a quick (if desperate) sprint through Lesser Feydark to get to Greater Feydark and all the opportunities there. Follow the road straight out of Al’Kabor: it takes one left turn at some distance from the town gate (do not take the branches, stay on the main road) and leads directly to the zone to Lesser Feydark. Beyond the turn, the environs are pretty deadly to you at this level: stay well back from anything, and run straight for zone if anything takes an interest in your viability as a snack. Stopping to try to root or fight is simply picking a place to recover your corpse. Lesser Feydark is even worse, but if you take a right upon entering the zone and stick to the boundary, it’s a relatively short and safe trip past the “corner” to the entrance to Greater Feydark. Follow the road to the right… Felwithe and the rich hunting grounds thereof.

Be warned: some of the High Elven merchants may thumb their noses at you as a Gnome. The priests are especially bad about that, in my experience. To the best of my knowledge, no one ever actually attacks anybody, but don’t bother with the Skeleton Bone quest, for example.

Speaking of quests, if you are going to head to Greater Feydark, be sure to run your leg of the mail quest. My briefly played little Enchanter has regularly been getting 6 to 8 gold for making the run from downtown Al’Kabor to Kelethin: nice pay for some quick (if rather dangerous) leg work.

Dark Elves:
If you chose this race… you’re into raw pain, aren’t you.

Neriak is a great town, don’t get me wrong. For one thing, it’s huge: three zones, pretty much everything is available somewhere (with a few notable exceptions). Good spell selections at the guild, too: probably the best on Norrath.

On the other hand, it is three zones. Getting from hunting grounds to guild house is three full zones in, another three out, which takes a fair amount of time.

My only experience in Nektulos is as a Necromancer, but I do know that the hunting is not the best. The creatures stay a long ways away from the town entrance, and there’s not much distance between the low level spiderlings, beetles, and decayed skeletons and the “death on the hoof” bears and normal skeletons, all of which are quite belligerent (or hungry, who can tell?) and attack without provocation. There are wandering guards and stone guardians, but they do wander: no guaranteed safe spots within easy sprinting distance.

That having been said, being a low level Dark Elf Wizard shouldn’t be all that bad. The East Commonlands aren’t far away, which means that if you can make it past the first few levels, there are lots of potential targets both in Nektulos and the Commonlands quite near at hand. Take your vengeance upon the above mentioned bears and skeletons for a while, hunt orcs out in the Commonlands when bear pelts start getting old. Watch out for the Freeport guards, remember: you are kill-on-sight (KoS) to a fair percentage of them (like nearly all of them, I believe). Never tried interacting with any of the merchants: not sure what their reactions are to you.

As with many low-level areas, Nektulos is not the greatest place to be using your bolt spells, too many trees and other potential obstacles. Not quite as bad as Greater Feydark, but not great. However, the easternmost edge of East Commons is almost sheer desert… not too bad at all for perfecting your long range targeting.

Humans:
If the Dark Elves have the most restricted starting location, Humans have the widest choices. You can start in either Qeynos or Freeport, you lucky dog. Now if you were just a wee bit smarter…

If you choose Freeport, there are copious hunting opportunities just outside the gates of both East and West Freeport. South of East Freeport you have North Ro, the northern part of which is quite comfortable at low levels. South of West Freeport is East Commons that you share with the dark elves as a nice low to middle level hunting ground. Both East and West Freeport have large contingents of guards that are ready at hand, assuming the high level “guard hunters” aren’t being hyperactive at the moment. Don’t be too hasty in judging these guys: first, the Freeport Militia is basically corrupt, and second, if you’re nice about it, you can probably stay buffed to the hilt the entire time you’re on line with a few politely phrased requests. Work your way south on both sides of the mountain range as you gain levels, zoning into Oasis and West Commons more and more frequently as you get into your middle teens and start approaching “world-traveller” status.

If you choose Qeynos, you’ve again got a large hunting area right outside the gates of town with gnoll pups, klikniks, bats, snakes, etc. Obtain and keep a Fire Beetle eye as soon as possible, of course: this is basically the easiest, cheapest light source to obtain in the entire game, and as a human, you probably need it. As you get further along in levels, you can start exploring the Qeynos Aqueducts (watch out for the gelatinous cubes, yuk!) or venturing out into Qeynos Hills, eventually making your way to Blackburrow at level 7 or 8, which will keep you pleasantly occupied slaughtering gnolls of various types well into the mid-teens. Forays into West Karana and Everfrost, especially in groups, can be extremely profitable and fun, as well, especially such activities as hunting baby mammoths in Everfrost and blasting bandits out at the farm in West Karana.

Quests are hardly in short supply in either Freeport or Qeynos, although many of them are a bit out of your league, especially at the start. These two towns receive a great deal of the focus with regards to quests: it is worth your while to simply talk to everybody and search for opportunities, more so than in most other cities on Norrath. Of special note in Qeynos are the bandit sash and gnoll tooth quests: these can be quite profitable in terms of both monetary and experience with persistence. These are detailed quite well elsewhere on the Web, so I won’t go into great detail here and now in this document.

Erudites:
I have spent precious little time on Erud, and the zones of that continent have underwent some massive changes of late to my understanding, so I can’t offer much in the way of detail for the Erudite Wizard that wants to stay close to home. I can say that Toxxulia Forest shares many characteristics with Greater Feydark and Nektulos Forest, in that bolts are fairly useless because of all the trees. I also found that the hunting was a little poorer than in other zones, a fair number of kobolds and decayed skeletons about, but not much else that I ran into. My short stint as an Erudite Cleric didn’t reveal all that many low-level quests which offered much in the way of returns: I can tell you definitively that you do not want to go swimming in the harbor, however… (BIG fish…)

Obviously, there is always the option of taking the boat to Qeynos and adventuring there, as well: however, you are not exactly greeted warmly by the merchants or guards on that side of the Crossing, and it will be a fairly time consuming process to retrieve your corpse if you die in Qeynos but are bound in Erud. Paying someone for a bind at the Qeynos gates is probably your best bet if you decide to travel.

Progressing beyond 10th level:
By now, you are probably doing a fair amount of travelling, so I won’t bother separating by race any more. There are actually a wide variety of places to spend your time on both sides of the map: I list some of the ones I’ve thoroughly wandered in the text below. Some areas you may want to check out in these level ranges:

Eastern Butcherblock
Goblins, basilisks, dwarf skeletons, and Undead Pawns are some fair challenges throughout the lower half of the teens. Err on the side of caution with the basilisks: that petrification ability can be a real pain in the derriere. There are a few places that are pretty nice for solo/small group play beyond 15, but not many. There are a couple of rare spawn ogres which occasionally get trained to zone, the occasional Goblin Wizard, and an “orc runner” which is an integral part of a Kelethin-based quest, but there generally aren’t that many “random sure death” encounters here, as I recall.

Crushbone
Crushbone remains challenging well into the upper teens, despite losing the ability to get xp for slaughtering the ever-present centurions. Head closer and closer to the castle as you advance in level. My suggestion would be to avoid the normal route to the castle, the right hand passage around the huge stone platform in the center. Jump up on the ledge skirting the obelisk and follow it around clockwise to reach the castle in a shorter distance, and with much less chance of being run over by a passing “train”. Grouping with a solid and diverse 5 to 6 member group can have you taking and holding the main throne room for massive xp gains in the early teens, but be careful not to attract too much of an Emissary’s undivided attention…

Steamfont
In the low teens, the area near the zone in Lesser Feydark is a nice place to hunt Skeletons, pumas, Lesser Ebon Drakes and the like for fair experience. Plague Rats are also available. The area out near the windmills boasts more of the same. Beyond the Windmills, you will find Ebon Drakes, Harpies, Minotaurs and Earth Elementals to play tag with in the mid-teens. Unfortunately, by 17th or so, everything is low green and no longer granting xp. Watch out for the Minotaur Lord, unless you don’t mind being a horn ornament.

North Ro:
The southern half of the area is good solid hunting up to the low to mid teens, even solo. You can work your way up from scarabs and the smaller rattlesnakes through orcs and mummies, mixed with some serious group work against the dervishes.

Oasis of Marr:
Great place to hunt in the mid- to upper-teens. Crocodiles, orcs, madmen, and undead. Remember to load up on the liquids before heading in: you consume water more than twice as fast as normal in Oasis. A useful tip: madmen generally carry 2-3 water as treasure. My associates and I have taken to referring to them as water-boys. Not the safest area, however: there are more than enough wandering Sand Giants and Spectres to keep you on your toes.

Misty Thicket:
This place is like a dream come true, except for the fact that you have to go through Kithicor or Runnyeye to get to it. You can easily spend levels 1 to 15 happily hunting in this one zone without ever leaving except to run into the Halfling hometown to get supplies and dump loot. On the “near” side of the wall run the newbies, on the far side you can find the more accomplished PCs. Orcs and goblins abound. If you feel like taking the risk and have a good guide, this can be a fun and profitable area to work.

Blackburrow:
This place is great for nearly every one from level 7 on up to the upper-teens. Stay near the zones to Qeynos Hills or Everfrost at low level, and work your way down and in as you gain in ability. When you start ignoring the Elites and searching for Commanders to give you a challenge… time to move on.

West Karana:
A good place to hunt in the teens, especially if you don’t mind camping bandits. The farm is somewhat overworked on my server, but there are other camps in the nearby hills that can be equally enjoyable. The lions and bears can and will steal kills on occasion: they have to eat, too, you know! Avoid going too deep in unless you are really ready for a challenge, since there are scarecrows, ogres, a ghoul messenger, wandering hill giants, treants, the occasional named cyclops, etc. Also beware the werewolf who often prowls that long canyon which is the zone into Qeynos Hills during the night-time hours. Quite a vicious cur (30 to 50 hit points a shot…)

Conclusion
Many people look down on the Wizard class, even those who choose to play them, due to the relative lack of depth to the class. You can do basically one thing: damage. In some ways, there is a kinship between Wizards and Rogues in that respect, in that both classes generally can deal out far more damage than they can take, simply in different ways. However, being a Wizard does truly hold the greatest potential for raw power if you can simply endure the long climb to greatness. If you ever get the opportunity to watch a master Wizard in action, you’ll see what I mean: spells which do 1000 points of damage per casting, teleporting entire groups of players around the world in a blink of an eye. This is what the future can hold for the persevering Wizard. No, you do not start with the breadth and depth of Merlin or Gandalf: but in the end, you will be mentioned with the same hushed breath, the same tone of wonder and awe.

Sounds good to me.
