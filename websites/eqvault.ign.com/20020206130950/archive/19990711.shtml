New Patch Message
The EverQuest Patch Server was updated with the following message:
July 16th, 5:00pm
-----------------

The following changes and game play enhancements have been made to the Test Server,

- The "r" key will now initiate the "/tell" command.
- The /surname command has been added, which allows a player of 20th level or greater to issue his or her character a last name.
- If a character is knocked unconscious, and has no stamina, the player will still regain consciousness.
- Players are no longer able to cast Shadowstep next to a zone point and teleport to an area past the teleport region, which is inaccessible to other characters.
- If a player is too far from a corpse while attempting to perform the /loot command, a message will now be given.
- If the user was under the effect of either Treeform or Minor Illusion, then had Spirit of Wolf cast on them, the player would be able to move in their altered state - This is no longer possible.
- If a character is blinded, the chat box will no longer flash.
- The Eye of Zomm now allows the caster to travel up and down.
- If a shuriken or throwing knife is equipped, then unequipped, the weapon will no longer remain on the character model.
- When player dispels minor illusion, the character's weapons will no longer disappear from the character model.
- Monk trainers will now train Instill Doubt.
- The EverQuest Team

New Producer Letter - New Zones
This week's Producer Letter has been published on the official EverQuest site. In this edition of the letter, EverQuest Producer Brad McQuaid reveals that a few new zones will be available to download for free. Also noteworthy is the fact that new additions and modifications to the Rogue-class have been made and are now implemented on the Test Server. Here's the complete letter:
Welcome Back!

First, I’d like to thank all of you who’ve been helping out on the Test Server – the testing of the PvP Teams code is coming along quite nicely. We’re probably looking at about another two weeks or so of the test server being configured that way, after which we will bring Tallon Zek online.

I wanted to talk a little about the upcoming zones we’re working on. The first of these new, free downloads will be the Temple of Solusek Ro, a smaller dungeon of sorts which will be rich in quests. The second will be Paineel, home of the Heretics, situated south of Toxullia Forest. Once this zone is online, Erudite Necromancers will start in their city. And finally, the Hole, which will be a mid to higher level dungeon underneath Paineel. I expect to see the first zone come online in a month or so, followed by a new zone every month or two after that. Oh, and one of the issues we’re debating is whether to make these zones patch automatically, or whether to make them optional downloads – let us know what you think.

Which brings me to the new Voting screen in the EverQuest front-end. How we will patch add-on zones is just one issue of many with which we can conduct a poll now. And we really do appreciate all of you taking the time to read through the questions and casting your vote. This is a great opportunity for all of you players to be heard, and it really helps us understand the feelings and opinions of our player base.

You may have noticed a Guild button appear suddenly in the front-end as well. Very soon we will be activating this new functionality which should allow for the maintenance and approval of Guilds on a more global basis – it should provide for quicker Guild approvals as well as making it easy to administer Guild related activities. And look for additional Guild functionality both in the front-end and in-game soon – we’ve only scratched the surface.

Before I close I wanted to give the Rogues an update. Make Poison is still underway and coming along quite nicely. We’ve also enhanced your class in some additional ways, and we encourage you to check it out on the Test Server. Backstab now has a minimum damage it can do, and we’ve also modified both Sneak and Hide such that you can move while hidden (albeit at Sneak speeds) and it also let’s Rogues know if their Sneak and Hide attempts were successful.

Oh, and it looks like there will be an EverQuest players get together at GenCon on Thursday (the first night) night – we’ll post more details soon, but it’s looking like we’re going to rent out a pizza place and we’ll be showing both the Expansion and Sovereign™ there as well.

See you next week,

–Brad

Brad and Raph Debate
EverQuest Producer Brad McQuaid and Ultima Online Lead Designer Raph Koster continued their debate, which we reported earlier this week, on the Usenet newsgroup alt.games.everquest. A reminder, Brad's response is in italics while Ralph's words in in normal text. Here's the update:
Yes. By saying "common" I didn't mean to suggest it was an absolute. After all, there's always systems like EQ's where there are actual level restrictions on what you can attack!

Good tactics on the part of a player, given a system that actually permits the attack, can overcome extremely large power differences. Some of these tactics might be called "exploits," certainly, and most of them are usually due to poor tactics on the part of the victim (be it insufficient AI on a mobile or lack of smarts or expertise on the part of a human player)... even in level-based systems, it's pretty common to see this happen. Indeed, in designing level restrictions like EQ's, you actually assume it occurs and formalize the expected power differential at which it can occur (eg, a guy 10 levels lower can take out a level 20, if he's smart--that's the point at which you put your hardcoded restriction in).

Agreed.

[Responding to Brad's comment stating that Verant will upgrade Lady Vox before people are powerful enough to solo her] Well, of course! You're a diligent maintainer. It comes back to the point about the support staff working to correct this (and "correct" is perhaps a bad word--it's natural for it to occur, so it's not like a mistake was made). The problem there is that as deflation occurs because of the database growing, you then have a larger amount of data that needs to be revised. Eventually, you stop trying to revise the entire database and leave in these older creatures because you get more bang for the buck in building new tougher ones than in upgrading the old ones that are no longer competitive.

I agree that it is a lot of work, but I think it's worth it, especially if that's what it takes to keep your player base around. And I think if the resources are sufficiently there then even though the database grows, you are still able to revise it in its entirety.

I call this "standard of living." The actual standard of living for the playerbase as a whole has risen, and was use to be expensive is now common, what used to be rare is now frequent... it's a natural process given static, non-evolving datasets.

So in your opinion, SHOULD that Lady Vox or vampire be upgraded or not? Letting it remain in its overtaken-by-events state actually makes for interesting conversation too ("wow, I just soloed Lady Vox...! Anyone remember when she was the deadliest mob in the mud?")...

Lady Vox in particular I would upgrade, based on the premise that a few people should never, regardless of how long EQ has been online, be able to slay a dragon. But I certainly think there are other areas/quests/NPCs that would be left the same on purpose to provide the nostalgia.

Tradtionally, designers haven't been able to keep pace. :) We will, of course, have to see how it does with the new breed of paid maintainers and admins.

I've seen some of the better MUDs keep pace, meaning they controlled the deflation such that the game was a blast for over three years. The key here is simply to control the rate of deflation such that you have an interesting and challenging game for the duration of its lifespan, not to attempt to keep deflation at zero. In any case, we certainly believe it's both very possible to keep pace and also essential.

If it's not then I think there is a serious problem with this emerging genre we are so interested in. One of the premises is that these games are persistent evolving worlds and if we can't keep up with these first or second generation MMORPGs then I really fear for the future. Players expect worlds that grow over time, with content being changed, added to, and deleted. In the process of doing that, the game's maintainers simply have to control the rate of deflation at the same time.

[Responding to Brad's comment stating that typically Mud GMs aren't paid and aren't able to devote 40+ hours a week to maintaining their MUD] True, but they generally have a much higher (orders of magnitude) admin to player ratio than the commercial endeavors do. It'd be interesting to run the math on admin hours devoted to maintenance. I would guess that most muds are still higher than the commercial games on a per capita (hours spent per player in playerbase) basis.

Hrm. Without in any way meaning to brag, my team pretty much lives and breathes EverQuest, and because they are paid for it, they don't have to balance maintenance of the game with their day job (because, of course, it is their day job). No matter how devoted and committed a nonprofit MUD admin is, I don't see how he/she could compete unless they were financially independent or some such. (No offense MUD admins -- I definitely do not mean to diminish your efforts in any way -- you guys are awesome).

Unfortunately, always less than you expect. :) The Internet is just such a *great* tool for communications--and the most important thing isn't the items, it's the knowledge...

Agreed -- spoiler sites and such certainly aren't going away. A game designer must always assume that knowledge will spread rapidly and plan accordingly.

    [Brad's comment in previous post] You lost me on this one Raph -- and don't worry, I know you're not bashing EQ :)
Heh. Well, what I was getting at was the notion that adding a character type or an expansion actually alters the gameplay in a significant manner. This is of course something that varies according to the tastes of the player as well. For me personally, playing most of the character classes in a level-based XP-for-killing RPG is pretty much exactly the same gameplay experience. Yes, I'll try a couple of classes or races to see the differences, but the central play mechanic, killing for XP, is the same. I won't sacrifice my character identity (which I have built up over the course of decades of RPGing) multiple times to play different classes that have the same play mechanic. It'll pall on me, and it demands a higher level of sacrifice of identity than I care to make.

But some players are of course less attached to their character, and see the differences in play between a wizard and a druid as being very significant. My personal feeling is that the longer they play a game, the less significant those differences seem.

It definitely depends on the player and his/her tastes. We have many players who concentrate on one character and one character only, and probably just as many if not more that jump from one character to another, perhaps bringing 8 different character race/class combinations to level 10 or 15 and then going on to another. We also have an amazing number of players who immediately jump to new servers when we launch them in order to obtain a fresh new start, abandoning their characters on the previous server.

    [Brad's comment in previous post] Why does the 'form of play' have to be altered by additional character types or world expansions? Did T2A do this?
Nope, T2A didn't change a damn thing in UO play mechanics. As a UO example of an addition that DID--vendors created a completely new play mechanic for people that didn't entailstarting their character over...
    [Brad's comment in previous post] If the world was left alone, yes. If the world is modified such that it keeps up with the deflation we are talking about, then not necessarily. The rate of deflation, of course, must be monitored and controlled, but it is not in and of itself a bad thing.
Yeah, the issue is the workload (and the consistency of the work done, too, since estimating the amount of deflation is very difficult when you're talking in units of orcs and magic gloves. ;)

The hidden issue then becomes budgetary, naturally--something we generally do not bother the players with. :)

Agreed, but again I submit that if the budgets aren't there to support maintenance teams with enough resources to keep these games interesting, then the genre is in trouble. This definitely applies to PvE games, and perhaps less so to PvP games, but it's still applicable as long as the game involves a persistent virtual world.

    [Brad's comment in previous post] The alternative is a totally closed entropic system, which I'm not sure I've ever seen work, and I'm pretty darn sure wouldn't be too fun even if it did work.
Hurm, not sure you need to go to totally closed entropic system. There's also the "floating level" notion, which I've never seen done but is an interesting thought experiment.

In the old Encyclopedia Brown books there was a mystery involving a map on a boat that supposedly got wet when the tide came in through an open porthole. Ludicrous, of course, because as the water level rises, so does the porthole--and the map stays dry.

If your definition of "level one" or of "magic item" were not based on hard numbers but instead were the result of an equation analyzing the actual current day capacities of players--then rabbits would actually spawn stronger to match the current capacities of newbies killing them. Balance would remain exactly the same--a level 1 mob matching a level 1 player. The water level raises not only the water but also the porthole...

Never seen it done in a mud though. Standalone games often do it. Especially platform games, where the enemies will be smarter or dumber based on how well you are doing, or the game will supply extra powerups if you are doing poorly.

-Raph Koster
Lead Designer, Ultima Online

Aren't you just talking about algorithmically keeping pace with deflation as opposed to having game designers manually doing it? I'm not sure we're at the point where this could be programmed, and even if it could, I doubt the program could even come close to competing against a hand tuned world in terms of providing compelling, fun, and interesting content. There's quite a big difference between algorithmically increasing the speed and quantity of enemies in a Pacman maze and algorithmically generating quests, story lines, and adjusting the difficulty level of dungeons and outdoor areas in a MMORPG. I'd rather allocate programming resources towards providing my game designers with better and better tools such that they could 'hand tune' the world more quickly, allowing them to be creative and yet still efficient.
