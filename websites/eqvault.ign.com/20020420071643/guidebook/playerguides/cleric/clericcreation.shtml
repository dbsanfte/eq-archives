Player Guide: Cleric Creation Guide

Author: Ailamil Faithbringer (N/A)   Organization: Clan of Shadows

Introduction

In Everquest, a game focusing on grouping together with your fellow adventurers, a cleric is the most sought after and important part of a good group. A Cleric’s specialty is healing and, in all of Norrath, we have no equal. We are essencial to a group at high levels and a great benefit to a group at any level. With the benefit to a group that we provide, we also rely on grouping more than most classes out there. It is very difficult to solo your way into the mid levels as many melee classes, necros, wizards, druids, shaman and mages can. This is also in your favor, as you will find out who you like and who you don’t earlier in your “life” than the classes that primarily solo. Don’t get me wrong, clerics can solo, we just work much better with a partner or five.

We are not limited in the armor we can wear, as “pure casters” are, but we are limited in the weapons we can use. We are limited to blunt weapons only (one handed and two handed blunt). We also get higher melee skills than the pure casters, but lower than the pure melee classes or hybrids.

Now, I’m going to dispel some myths from the beginning. We, along with druids and shaman, are not tanks. We don’t even come close to the amount of damage that the melee classes can put out with a weapon, which will make us very much like the pure casters at higher levels. There are a few reasons for this. The first reason is that our melee skills are not as good as that of a warrior, ranger, paladin, shadow knight, rogue or bard, although they are not that far off (all weapons skills max out at level * 4 +5 and offense and defense are level * 5 +5, all melee classes are level * 5 + 5). Another reason that clerics are not a melee contributor at higher levels is that there are no blunt weapons that produce the amount of damage necessary to take down monsters over level 30. The biggest reason that we don’t deal out the necessary damage is that we do not get dual wield or double attack. Combine this with the fact that the only defensive ability we get (dodge) caps out at good, and we end up hitting for 19 while the monsters are hitting us for 35-100 with multiple attacks. Not a good thing, however, this is to your advantage, trust me. The higher level monsters HATE healers (just like we hate monsters that heal), being in range and adding to their hate list by doing melee damage to them is not in your best interest.

You will eventually become part of the “back row” who do not engage in melee, but stand back and cast spells while letting the melee classes keep the various critters off you. Until you reach that level, however, you will be engaging in melee, casting DD spells, healing, buffing, stunning, rooting, and anything else you can to take down those monsters.

With that said, let’s look at some options you have for becoming a member of the healing elite.

Creating your character

There are all kinds of reasons for creating a character a certain way. The role-playing aspects of this game are limited only by your imagination. The different races have different statistics and starting locations, which can be an advantage or a disadvantage. We’ll look at both the statistics and the starting location for evaluating the races, but I must stress that you must play a race that you think would be fun. All the races that can be a cleric can make good clerics.

Wisdom is the key to being a Cleric. The more wisdom you have = the more mana you have, this translates into the more healing you can do. It doesn’t mean that the race with the most mana is best (there are no “winners” or “losers”), as timing of healing and casting of other spells often influence the balance of a battle, but wisdom will be the most important statistic you have. You can be a good or bad cleric, no matter what race you choose. Becoming a good cleric is something you learn. There are enough statistic enhancing items out there to (almost) make up for any beginning racial statistics you have, becoming a good cleric is up to you :)

What the statistics mean

**Note: there are several large “milestones” where increasing a certain statistic will return a proportionately larger amount of benefit. The largest noticeable effects are for strength, dexterity, agility, and charisma. These milestones do not effect mana. These milestones are at 75, 100, 125, 150, and 175. You will see a larger difference in AC if you dexterity increases from 74 to 75 than you would if you increase dexterity from 75 to 76.

Strength- With higher strength you’ll hit harder (strength bonus affects damage after level 20), you’ll hit for max damage more often with your weapon, and carry a whole lot more. If you dream of one day wearing a full suit of steel plate mail, you should have around 85 strength. Most high level clerics that can afford a full set, often do not wear all steel plate as you generally will have a number of wisdom enhancing items that are used in place of the pieces of plate armor.

Stamina- The higher stamina you have the more hit points you’ll have. You’ll also be able to swing your mace/staff longer without tiring, and be able to jump or swim longer.

Agility- This directly effects your armor class (AC). It is key that you get this statistic to 75.

Dexterity- This statistic increases the number of times a “processing” weapon will “process” (it’s special effect will go off). There are not very many good cleric 1HB or 2HB weapons that process.

Wisdom- High wisdom means more mana. A higher wisdom also helps to decrease the chance of you being interrupted while casting (although this is debatable).

Intelligence- Higher intelligence will help you learn skills at a faster pace.

Charisma- Charisma affects how NPCs view you (after racial, religious, and past history bias). High charisma can help you buy items for a lower price or sell items at a higher price (from NPC vendors).

The Races

As a cleric, you have the option of being a human, dwarf, high elf, erudite, halfling, gnome, or dark elf. Each of these races has a different set of starting attributes, different innate abilities, specific racial and religious hatreds and different starting locations.

The Dark Elf
The starting city of Neriak is one of the most difficult to learn. There are 3 zones in the city and no monsters to hunt (you can zone 3 times before getting to the starting hunting grounds). As a truly evil creation, clerics from the dark elf race have a truly difficult journey ahead of them. This is true of all the evil races (Troll, Orge, and Dark Elf), not just clerics. However, the dark elf clerics must worship Innoruuk, the Prince of Hate. Being evil and worshipping Innoruuk makes getting faction up with any of the “good” cities very difficult.

Dark elves have a great starting wisdom, wonderful agility, and decent dexterity, however, strength is very bad, as is charisma. I’d recommend putting 5 into strength or stamina and the remaining bonus points into wisdom. You may also want to put a couple points into charisma, but I would not recommend this as you need at least 75 to make an impact on the prices you will get from vendors. If you get a change to get a crude stein (people hunt Ogre Guards in Ferroot and may end up giving them away once they all have one) get one, this will put you at the 75 limit.

Once you find your “newbie” training ground, Nek Forest, you’ll quickly want to leave, even though there are plenty of undead to kill which clerics do very well. Not only is the forest very hard to navigate in, there are different creatures that wander around that are much higher than the most of the creatures you will be hunting, many of these are aggressive to you. On top of that, the loot in this zone is horrible. You also can not depend on the guards to be there for you, as many have taken it upon themselves to slay your protectors (“good raced” PCs). In time you can also repay the favor to their guards, but first you must grow in power. I would suggest that you head over Innothule Swamp and bind in Grobb after you hit level 4. The Foglocks and Kobalds have great loot, you will increase your swimming ability (it is a swamp after all). You can hunt in this zone until level 12, then it’s a quick trip to South Ro. After you reach level 16, you can start hunting inside of Guk, continue hunting in South Ro, or head to Oasis. All of these zones are very close to Grobb. Remember the crude stein that I recommended that you get? Ferroot is right next to Innothule Swamp. One disadvantage is that you will have to travel back to Neriak to buy all but a few of your spells, which can be bought at the shaman’s guild in Grobb.

One main complaint about this race is that they have a difficult, if not impossible, journey to complete class specific quests. My thoughts on this, having played many different character/race combinations, is that there are just too few cleric quests in general, not a specific slight on the dark elf cleric. Of the quests that are available, the dark elves are left out of a number of them. I guess that goes with being “evil”.

The Dwarf
Dwarfs clerics start in Kaladim, with the cleric guild being in North Kaladim (2 zones in this city). The starting hunting grounds are excellent, as Butchblock can take you from level 1 to around level 12 or so, with lots of undead. There are many good quests in the city, which give incredible experience for low level players, as well as some great rewards.

Dwarfs are from a hearty stock of the short folk. They have the highest strength of all clerics, a great starting wisdom, very high dexterity, medium agility, a low intelligence and are just plain ugly (low charisma). I would recommend that you put all your points into wisdom and the remaining points into agility. Dwarven clerics tend to maintain their combat role for a number of levels past other clerics. They do this by hitting for good damage pretty consistently.

The Erudite
Erudite clerics start in Erudin, on the far continent of Erud. City is two zones with some very nice graphics, however, the newbie hunting grounds are horrible. It’s difficult to see very far in any direction and there are few landmarks. It gets easier when you get your sense heading up a bit, but I’d recommend you take the ship to Quenos and get bound right at the front of the city there. Quenos has all the cleric spells and great hunting grounds for a new cleric.

Erudites are humans breed for intellectual superiority. They are the “High Man”, much like High Elves are the intellectual superiors of their wood elf cousins, both are smarter but weaker of body. The other races do not particularly like the Erudites, although they are not an “evil” race. They do not like the Erudites’ “superior” attitude, however, this is a minor faction difference (unlike the Dark Elves). Erudites are blind as a bat at night, just like their human relatives.

Excellent starting wisdom, the best starting intelligence of all clerics, good stamina, and average dexterity, agility, and charisma. Strength is a weakness, and the agility could be a bit higher. I would add 5 points to strength and 5 into agility, putting the remaining points into wisdom.

The Erudite Cleric will find that his/her combat role fades pretty quickly in the late 20s, early 30s.

The Gnome
The gnomes start in the city of Ak'Anon. They have a racial trade skill, called tinkering, which has some useful items and many items that have not been found yet. This could end up being one of the best trade skills in the game, if it becomes explored (there needs to be some fixes from Verant too).

Good starting hunting grounds as you can hunt here from 1 to about 16, getting good loot and experience. This zone will make you group much more in the beginning stages of your character as many monsters will join the fight when you are trying to kill something, but this game is about grouping so it gets you ready for future adventures.

Gnomes have the lowest starting wisdom of all the races, they also have the lowest strength, and pretty bad charisma. They do have great agility, dexterity, and intelligence. I would recommend that you put as many points into wisdom as you can and dump the rest into strength.

The Halfling
Halflings start in the city of Rivervale. Excellent hunting grounds, some nice quests available in the city too, but probably the best reason for starting in this city is that it is pretty much in the center of the main continent. You do not have far to travel to reach anywhere. Halflings also have the ability to hide. This is a nice ability for meditating, but this skill will never increase (max is 40 I believe). Moving when you are hidden will cause you to appear (you can stand, sit or turn).

These little, furry-footed fellows have the highest agility and dexterity of all clerics, good stamina and strength and a good starting wisdom. Their intelligence is low, as is their charisma (who really likes furry feet?). I would recommend maxing out wisdom and then throw the rest into strength, intelligence or stamina.

The Halfling cleric, along with their Dwarven counterparts, can maintain a combat role for a number of levels past that of many other clerics. They do this by not getting hit as much.

The High Elf
The city of Felwithe is the starting place of high elf clerics. There are two zones in this city. A good, although somewhat crowded, newbie hunting grounds as you will have to share the same space with your wood-elf cousins. Lots of great quests for low level characters. Somewhat similar to the Erudites, some races think that the High Elves are a bit too snobbish (a VERY, very minor faction change).

The High Elf has the highest starting wisdom of all the races, a high intelligence, good agility and charisma, medium stamina and dexterity, but the lowest strength of all the clerics. I would recommend that you put as many points into wisdom as you can stand, then put the remaining points into strength. Any remaining points should be put into agility. This is one race where you can get away with not putting the majority of your points into wisdom, however, many still put the majority into this (it will max out at 130).

High Elves, along with Erudites, will be one of the first races to remove themselves from combat.

The Human
Humans have the most balanced set of starting statistics. As a human, you can start out in Quenos or Free Port, two cities with many quests, a very good starting (“newbie”) zone, and the benefit of being on the “mainland” or central part of Norrath. The can not see at night or in enclosed areas without some sort of light.

All of the human’s statistics are average. I would recommend putting all that you can into wisdom, then split the remainder into agility and strength.

*****

I have stayed away from making a recommendation of which class to pick, however, I will say the top four that I recommend are High Elf, Dwarf, Halfling, and Dark Elf. They all have their advantages and disadvantages, but I truly believe that you can make each race a great cleric. I also did not follow the formula above when creating my character, but if I were to start a new cleric (with all that I know now) I would. If your cleric happens to have the advantage of equipment and platinum that is not attainable by your particular level, then the beginning hunting grounds above matter little.
