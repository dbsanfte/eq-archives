Developer Chat Logs for June 20  [ Comment ]
Print News
You can find the log of the developer's chat for last night here at Stratics.

Some highlights:

Server crashes:
"Our code team is well aware of the problem and has been working on a solution, but the root of the problem has been very difficult to track down. Once they’ve got that nailed down, we’ll be able to get a fix in place."

On raids in the next expansion:
"The majority of raid content with the next expansion will be aimed at players who have exhausted the current raid content in the game. There are some raids that will be doable by mid-tier guilds as well, but those guilds have a lot of older content still at their disposal so it’s less of a focus. ...

We’re taking a “one step back, two steps forward” approach to raids in the next expansion. The raids will essentially start one “step” back from the current high end and then progress forward so there is a bit of a ramp for mid-tier guilds to use to get into the top-tier areas.

In addition, we’ve got some plans for the single player game that will make transitioning from a primarily single group player to a raider considerably easier. That should go a long way toward helping those mid-tier guilds out."

There's lots more info in the chat concerning various things in the game, so check it out.

  "The Life of a Chinese Gold Farmer"  [ Comment ]
Print News
Curious about how gold farming works? Even if you aren't, you shouldn't miss Julian Dibble's article for the New York Times this week, "The Life of a Chinese Gold Farmer."

"...For every 100 gold coins he gathers, Li makes 10 yuan, or about $1.25, earning an effective wage of 30 cents an hour, more or less. The boss, in turn, receives $3 or more when he sells those same coins to an online retailer, who will sell them to the final customer (an American or European player) for as much as $20.

The small commercial space Li and his colleagues work in — two rooms, one for the workers and another for the supervisor — along with a rudimentary workers’ dorm, a half-hour’s bus ride away, are the entire physical plant of this modest $80,000-a-year business.

The polite name for these operations is youxi gongzuoshi, or gaming workshops, but to gamers throughout the world, they are better known as gold farms. "

It is common practice to despise gold farmers, but you may see it from a different perspective after reading this article!

  Server Crashes  [ Comment ]
Print News
The "elephant in the room" problem lately with EQ seems to be the almost daily server crashes on Firiona Vie that have been happening for quite some time now, and apparently, unstable servers elsewhere. I call it the "elephant in the room" because even though the problem is pretty obvious to the players, the developers are not posting any comments or explanations on any threads concerning the crashes, even the recently stickied one.

The rumor mill has it that the crashes are related to guilds being in the Solteris instanced zone, with evidence given that when a guild entered Solteris on Firiona Vie, the problems started, and that Drinal is stable because no guilds are in Solteris at the moment. Since this has not been confirmed, at the moment it's only player guessing.

Why the developers have not come out publicly with any comments is a big unknown. Perhaps they just have nothing to say on the matter. The problem is most acute on FV server, because the whole game doesn't crash, just the individual zones one by one, according to player accounts.

  MMOGData.com Site Founder Interview  [ Comment ]
Print News
Our readers may recall the site MMOGCharts.com, which gave credible, independent subscriber numbers and statistics for online games. The site stopped being updated last year, and a new site has taken its place, MMOGData.com, which just released its first batch of numbers earlier this month.

IGN's RPG Vault recently published an interview with the new site director, Phil "Vortal" White. White is a veteran of the gaming industry and took on the task of gathering this data with the goal of expanding on the original idea of publishing accurate information on the market size and growth of the MMO industry.

This kind of data gathering, which is considered by most MMO publishers to be proprietory, is a challenging task. Phil White writes in his article:

"It has been a bigger task than I thought, not only trying to gather all the data - there is just less and less public information available now - why is this? In the movie industry, you can get the box office numbers from lots of different sources, but not for MMOGs; it is still all hush-hush, and with World of Warcraft redefining what is a commercial success, it gets even more difficult.

Ensuring the numbers people send me are verified also takes time, and Sony's Station Pass, which allows you to play almost any of their MMOGs, further increases the level of difficulty. However, I can say that if I cannot get two sources for the data, or it is not from official sources, it will not go into the spreadsheet - I have not, and I will not ever make any of this up just for the sake of it, but at the same time, I cannot guarantee how accurate the data is."

The article also refers to two new acronyms for revenue that MMO players will start to see more of: buy to play (B2P) and free to play (F2P). White plans to track data from these games as well. SOE President John Smedley in a recent New York Times article stated that SOE will be introducing the B2P model in their newest venture, "Free Realms," aimed at young girls. The concept of a "velvet rope," or an aspect of a game you cannot enter without paying for additional content, seems to be a hot trend for future economic models in the gaming industry. The current pay to play (P2P) model that Western gamers are familiar with may become less common as these new ideas are implemented outside of Asia, where they have had tremendous success.

White's efforts to make transparent subscriber numbers and other data for the MMOG industry is laudable. Although current trend for publishers is to keep the numbers hush-hush, most likely due to the fear that low numbers will send subscibers fleeing or expose waning popularity of titles, the industry can only be strengthened by the knowledge of what people are playing. For players, facts -- not rumors -- can help boost loyalty to a title.

As White mentions, getting accurate data that reflects the actual number of people playing a game can be very difficult. Between the efforts of game publishers to keep numbers secret and the tendency to inflate subscriber numbers, the inability to track active players versus idle accounts, the current trend of people using multiple accounts for "boxing," and other factors, the numbers will probably never be exact. That doesn't mean that careful estimation and data gathering isn't helpful and in fact, probably does represent a fairly good snapshot of what's happening in the industry.

So where does EverQuest stand, according to White's figures? Specifically, according to the numbers from his site in June, EverQuest has 1% of the market share for the genre. With an all time high of over 500,000 subscribers in September of 2004, EQ now hovers around 150,000 players in May 2007.

  President Smedley Comments on Future Direction for SOE  [ Comment ]
Print News
On June 11, 2007 SOE President John Smedley gave an interview to the New York times giving his ideas about how SOE would like to approach the future online market. Entitled "Sony's Other Games Push in a New Direction," the article explains that SOE is looking in the direction of a "pay-for-play" model, found most commonly today in games distributed in Asia.

Some interesting quotes:

"But tucked within the relative obscurity of Sony Pictures Digital Entertainment, the online entertainment unit has grown into a profitable business with revenue in the $150 million range (the company does not break out exact results for the division) by creating and publishing a variety of subscription-based online computer games."

"The problem is that the company’s first huge hit — EverQuest, released in 1999 — remains its only genuine blockbuster, with around 250,000 users. (The company used to report subscriber figures, but stopped a few years ago.)"

It's a shame that EQ's success is considered a problem!

and

“Right now our revenue is almost all subscriptions,” John Smedley, the unit’s president, said in an interview. “In two years, we would like to see no more than 50 percent of our revenue coming from subscriptions, and five years from now we think less than 10 percent of our revenue will come from subscription sources.”

At the same time, Mr. Smedley said he wanted to diversify his customer base, which is 85 percent male and 32 years old, on average. Women have become the major driver of the casual games business (games like Bejeweled and Bookworm), and Mr. Smedley wants a piece of that action.

“We want to get our average age lower, probably into the low 20s, and I’d really like to see the gender breakdown go to 50-50 or even slightly more women than men, to reflect real life,” he said.

Sony Online’s new direction can perhaps be seen most clearly in a game the company intends to announce today called Free Realms.

In general, Mr. Smedley wants to replace subscriptions with a combination of microtransactions, advertising and what he calls the “velvet rope” approach [in other words, you have to buy content within the game -- Ed.]. All three concepts may come to bear in Free Realms, which the company hopes to release on PCs this coming winter and on the PlayStation 3 next summer."

In the crossed signals department:

[Smedley's] boss, Yair Landau, vice chairman of Sony Pictures and president of Sony Pictures Digital Entertainment, said: “We are clearly moving beyond men in tights with broadswords. Women in cocktail dresses and stilettos and men in tuxedos with silencers is a very natural place for Sony Online to be expanding.”

Umm, if "Women have become the major driver of the casual games business ... and Mr. Smedley wants a piece of that action," then why don't you give them silencers and a pant suit, since most gamer women know you don't usually chase after the bad guys with a long dress and 6" heels. Hello, Mr. Landau, gamer women don't want to be just the hero's ornament anymore .... /sigh.

  WarCry Interview with Producer Worley  [ Comment ]
Print News
Warcry has posted an article, "Status Report, Look Forward" based on an interview with Clint Worley, current producer of EverQuest.

"'EverQuest has always been a hardcore game,' Producer Clint Worely told WarCry. It has a steep curve and while they have made efforts to correct this, the new player experience is not really their current focus. Specifically, he characterized the churn of players out of the game as extremely low, a major reason for them to focus on keeping those long lasting subscribers happy."

So expect more from the end game, and not as much newbie stuff, if these comments accurately reflect the focus of the producer. It's perhaps a shame they have given up on attracting new players, but not surprising. EQ has a niche, and it epitomizes the hard core, raiding game MMO genre. That's what it's best at, and why not do what you do best? People who grow bored with easier MMOs will hear about EQ through word of mouth, and perhaps Station Access, and give it a try. It's been painfully obvious that they are not putting any emphasis on marketing campaigns in print or in stores, with the shift to digital download and no presence in gaming or big box stores for their product.

Worley states that they have 40 developers working on the game, which is about the same as the other titles they publish (EQII, Vanguard) which indicates they are supporting EverQuest, and it's not "dying" as so many people would like to believe.

Concerning the newest expansion, Secrets of Faydwer, which will go live November 15:

"...live events have become increasingly important for the developers. These events build up to and introduce new expansions and are totally free to the player. He doesn't believe these should just be throw onto the pile. Instead, he wants to introduce them to the players, give them a taste of what it offers, and then ask them to plunk down their money and make a purchase.

This expansion offers players mid to high level raid content and employs a clockwork theme. Players should expect lots of gears and gadgets in their enemies this time around. They even have a raid inside a giant Zeppelin and a robot that players can not just enter, but quest inside.

To introduce it, they plan an epic live event. Worely kept his secrets as best he could, but he did reveal that they would literally move a mountain to set things up. As it flies in the air above, mechanical creatures will rain down on the players. There is more, but what he told us was just enough to illustrate his philosophy of showing players what they can expect in an expansion pack through live events."

By introducing the expansion in the live game with an epic event, Worley can achieve both the team's marketing goals and bring in some of the later expansion adopters earlier. In every expansion, it seems that people wait to buy; this strategy may generate more sales for the expansion earlier and involve players in a more active way. In the early days of EQ, such as the opening of Plane of Hate, the entire world was involved in the live event that made for lasting memories (some better than others, it was quite a newbie slaughter). Bringing back that kind of GM and development team involvement in the launch of an expansion should have a positive effect for the game.
