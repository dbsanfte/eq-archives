  The EverQuest Designers  [ Comment ]
Print News | Mail News
EQVault Series: The EverQuest Designers

You can enjoy a painting, or a book, or a movie without knowing anything about the author or the artist that created it. In the same way, you can enjoy playing a video game without knowing anything about its designers or the way it’s put together. But if you really want to appreciate EverQuest the game, and the zones you adventure in, then you want to know something about the designers and the zones they create for you.

EverQuest appears to be a seamless game for players, and it is easy to overlook that each zone has been designed by a creative individual who merges game ideas with artwork and programming skills. Each person on the development team works to bring something fun, creative, and engaging to the player base for each zone and instance. They each imagine a zone: what it might look like, how the non-player characters might act in such an adventure, what items could drop there, the storyline behind the zone, and how the playerbase will react to the encounter.

In fact, the each person on the design team is like an artist creating a work that we get to play in. And just like you might appreciate the work of an artist, or film maker, or songwriter, so you probably appreciate each zone for what the designers bring to each one.

The Producer orchestrates the game, of course, and makes sure all the zones in an expansion fit together in a cohesive whole. Without that person, the game wouldn’t work.

The artists make it all happen from the designer’s vision. The itemization designer and the spell designer make the game’s mechanics work. The coders actually make the game playable on your pc.

But it’s the imagination and personality of the designers that make the game of EverQuest the world in which we like to spend our time. The work they do is so tangible in the virtual sense that we come to know a zone in all its complexity – the landmarks, the inhabitants, its structures and features. The designers make it possible to learn how to manage to survive in a zone so we come to know it, like a hummable tune. And just like good art, we take their ideas and make them our own, finding new creative ways to make the game work for us.

I imagine a world in which each designer on the team is recognized as an individual creative force, where we appreciate each person for what they bring to the game. Just like any artist, we could get to know what kinds of adventures each lead designer creates on an individual level, as well as the effort the whole team puts in to make a seamless game that all works together. Just like an author that writes a favorite book series or a song writer that puts out multiple albums, the EQ designers work on zones each expansion that reflect their own personal visions of what is a great place to adventure in.

This series will feature one-on-one interviews with designers on the EverQuest team, with an emphasis on the zones they have designed. Like the director’s cut on a movie DVD, The EverQuest Designers series is an alternative view of the game, highlighting how each developer contributes to the virtual world of Norrath through their unique creative ideas.

My first interview is with Holly "Vahlar" Longdale, Assistant Lead Developer for the SOE team. I hope you enjoy Holly's "Strong Vision" as much as I enjoyed talking to her about her design work. You will also get an exclusive glimpse of two of the newest zones in the Serpent's Spine expansion!

Monday, 11 September, 2006


  IGN Previews Serpent's Spine  [ Comment ]
Print News | Mail News
You can read a positive, solid review of the Serpent's Spine expansion on our parent site, IGN.

"When it releases on September 19, the rest of the EQ universe will undergo a few worldwide changes separate from the expansion content. The developers at Sony Online are trying to make their world more user-friendly, akin to what they dubbed the "modern" MMORPGs out there. In an attempt to minimize downtime out of combat, the regeneration rates of health and other parameters will be dramatically increased. For high level players, this will mean standing around for about three minutes, as opposed to the exorbitant amount of time required before for inter-battle breaks. Death is also being given some attention; it'll now involve less needless zoning. As another tweak, the skill purchase system, previously available only to high level players, will be accessible to lower levels, appropriately scaled down.

The build we saw was running The Serpent Spine's beta server, and we got a chance to check out bits of Crescent Ranch. Players can expect a bunch of new quests and merchants there. It's apparently an abandoned ogre city, so much of the architecture is colossal compared to the players running around. Players can even visit some titanic dragon lords who've taken up residence in a section of the city. Most of those running around had picked the Drakkin race. Being the first characters created using the updated graphics engine from two years back, they're more detailed than most other EQ races. Each has scale-like patterns along their ribs, horny protrusions along their foreheads, and significant tattoo patterns. The color patterns of all the features can be altered in the character creator, depending on which dragon sire players choose to align their character with.

More than anything, Sony Online was stressing how this expansion is poised to bring together lower level characters for intricate boss and dungeon encounters. We checked out a dusky, craggy zone called Direwind where a gnoll stood amid a series of totems. This wasn't an instances boss encounter, rather an outdoor, static raid or party target. In order to take down the gnoll, players had to interact with the totems while conducting the battle. These kind of fights where parties are forced to manipulate environmental objects while in combat is repeated in numerous other areas. In the Blightfire Moors, a zone just outside Crescent Ranch, there's a tower encounter, again non-instanced. This time, it's a bunch of torches against the room's walls that need to be manipulated in order to prevent the phantom boss from summoning more allies."

Sounds good, doesn't it? You won't have to wait much longer to check it out for yourself.

  'Resurrecting EverQuest' Article  [ Comment ]
Print News | Mail News
Does EverQuest need a rez? Slashdot.com's Michael Zenke has gone from "frustration and disappointment" with EQ to "I've seen the light."

After taking a tour with Rashere around the new expansion, his article refers to the TSS expansion as "nothing less that a relaunch of EverQuest" and "EverQuest 1.5."

The main point of his article is that TSS is targeting players that subscribed in the past and will want to return given the content being released in just a couple of weeks. Rather than focusing on a lot of new features for the game, they've concentrated on "focusing on the players," according to Zenke. "SOE hopes that, by providing a clear quest path incorporating all of the innovations and upgrades the game has received since launch, players will be able to experience the best of what EQ has to offer," he explains.

I agree that TSS is a great idea, giving players another Kunark-type expansion, plus some time saving features and a new race to have fun with. However, Zenke's comment, "SOE seems finally to have come around to the idea that they should be spending time and effort on the players," seems gratutious at best, since as an eight year veteran I have seen the SOE Team focus on the players since the game was launched, probably more than any other MMO out there. The SOE Development Team has always listened to the players, steadily improving EQ and making it better every day. But, if this new expansion brings back old players from where ever they wandered off to, that's fantastic. The Progression server began the process, but a new player-oriented expansion on the current servers is even better.

If anyone can deliver "a love letter to the players," as Zenke puts it, it's the current SOE development team. In my opinion, TSS is just another dozen roses in the on-going romance.

Tuesday, 29 August, 2006


  Rashere on General Upcoming Changes  [ Comment ]
Print News | Mail News
Rashere has announced a number of upcoming changes to the game:

AA
We’re moving the AA system to a more relative system so the amount of AA experience you gain will be based on the difficulty (con level) of the NPC you killed rather than its level (quest XP and other ways of getting AA are likewise converted over to the new system).

Spell Resists
We’ll be introducing a new spell resist formula that will make spell resists much more lenient than they have been in the past.

Death
We’ll be changing death so that when you die, you will be “hovering” over your body and can choose to stay that way while waiting for a resurrection instead of immediately zoning to your bind point. This is being done to help players on slow connections who take a big hit from having to zone twice when they die.

Character Creation
We’ve changed character creation to make it more graphical, allow characters to be created from a single page, and update it to include features associated with the new Drakkin race.

Endless Quiver
We’ve introduced the ability to create new arrows that are not affected by Endless Quiver. These arrows will specifically be marked as “Expendable”.

Rashere on Upcoming Downtime Changes  [ Comment ]
Print News | Mail News
Along with the new expansion to be released in September, the design team is working on some fundamental changes in the combat/non-combat states, which may or may not be pushed live when the expansion is. The basic idea is to reduce the amount of downtime in EQ. Read below for all the details, but here is the executive summary courtest of Rashere as posted on EQPlayers:

"Once you have been out-of-combat for a sufficient amount of time, you can sit down (or be on a non-moving horse) and begin to rest. While resting, you enter an accelerated regeneration state that will quickly recover your mana, health, and endurance. For most single group content, once you are out of combat for 30 seconds you can rest. While dealing with multi-group content, that time is 5 minutes.

There are a series of icons that will display by your character’s name to let you know what your character’s current state is.

Crossed Swords – You are currently in-combat.

Hour Glass – You are out of combat, but not yet in a resting state. In addition to the hourglass, there is a timer bar beneath the endurance bar that will count down as you get closer to being rested.

Poison Drop – You are no longer in combat, but cannot rest because of a debuff that is affecting you. You must get rid of the debuff before you can rest.

Void – The void is the default icon and indicates that you are not in-combat and you can rest at any time.

Energy Swirl – The energy swirl indicates that you are currently resting."

It seems that these changes are partly to address the endurance regeneration problems, as well as mana regen for the large pools of mana at the higher levels.

Woes of the Non-Raiding Player  [ 2 Comments ]
Print News | Mail News
Loral, who runs Mobhunter, an excellent EQ site, has posted a provacative video entitled "Prophecy of Ro Single Group Progression Video." The video is very pointed statement by Loral in direct rebuttal to the Township Rebellion video of the submission of Deathknell, and expresses his position on the lack of ability to find groups in the newest expansion.

Loral is a level 70 cleric, a self-proclaimed "non-raider." His commentaries on the lack of equivalent gear and opportunities for players who do not raid with a guild have been an important counterpoint to the raiding emphasis in the current game environment. For the non-raider to succeed, pickup groups are paramount to the game experience. His video makes a poignant statement about the difficulty in finding a group to play with if you are not raiding.

Loral comments, "Pickup groups are a very important part of the game. Not everyone can rely on a tight knit group of friends with the right classes, right levels, right equipment power, and right time frames to meet these challenges. WOW solves this with solo content but at the expense of good group dynamics. EQ can do better."

Without the ability to easily form groups when any individual logs on, the game fabric indeed will unravel. Two, three, and four boxing is not the answer to conquering group content to ensure character progression. The fun of EQ is in meeting and playing with other people. If the game is to attract and retain players, there have to be opportunities outside the raiding environment for people to enjoy the content. One type of play style is not enough.

But is this now a non-issue? Frodlin, the warrior community representative, a member of a top raiding guild, and a self-proclaimed "SOE cheerleeder," comments on the EQPlayers board:

"[Loral's] biggest problem is that he got everything he asked for, and is now a crusader without a crusade. PoR was unequivocally THE least raider friendly, and THE most casual friendly expansion ever released outside of Legacy of Ykesha, he got his chance to deprive raiders of a good time, while enjoying everything he claimed to be asking for: A method to get Qvic level armor without organized raiding, entire zones devoted to casual grouping and easy to finish quests for gear that are significantly easier and represent a no-brainer to receive in relation to the same stuff a couple expansions ago, and no group content which benefits raiders in any way. It was the perfect expansion if you listened to what Loral wanted... and it resulted in what he got, which is the LFG movie.

One day, perhaps he'll learn that challenge is what drives people, and not "catering to the bottom". Until then, we'll see more and more of these silly antics like the rallying of the troops because of 2 hard zones, and the "LFG because I got everything I wanted in an expansion" movies."

The best we can say is that PoR may be the most "casual friendly" according to the raiding crowd, but if the zones are empty, there's a larger problem than the design of the zones themselves.

Is creating content that is not raid oriented "catering to the bottom" as Frodlin would like us to believe? Has the creation of a large amount of single group content that does not require a raid force solved the problem of lack of opportunities and gear for the non-raider? Many players now feel without a doubt that this issue is yesterday's news, and the issues that Loral has raised are no longer valid. Others would say that the video does express their frustration with getting a group.

EverQuest's design and sheer size has created a huge universe of play style opportunties, much more than any other game in this genre. It is probably inevitable that the debate over content emphasis between the non-raider and the raider will go on as long as the game keeps growing.

Whatever side you fall on in this debate, kudos to Loral and Frodlin, as well as others, for posting opinions and talking about this very important fundamental issue of game design -- the way people play the game. This debate means we are all better informed and can form our own opinions on whether there is enough content for how each of us want to play the game.

August 23 Dev Chat Logs  [ Comment ]
Print News | Mail News
Missed the dev chat last night? We have the log right here.

Some highlights:

Rashere on AA changes: "I plan to get a post up on the live forums within the next couple of days discussing the planned changes that are going in along with this expansion, including the AA changes. Specific to the AA changes, if you're at level 70 you shouldn't notice much change but lower level players will gain AAs at a faster rate than they have in the past."

Ngreth on TSS spells:"You will be able to get any spell that you can buy in the original EQ, Plane of Knowledge, and Serpent Spine itself. Additionally you will be able to get any spell that you can make using spell research. The Serpent's Spine will add LDoN and OoW spells to spell research."

Prathun on raids and pets: "I've got several abilities and spells planned for the upcoming expansion that should help address pet survivability in raid situations. Rhagnell on the expansion claim item: "It is a scepter that will teleport you to Crescent Reach (the home of the Drakkin) once a day."

Brannoc on endurance regen: "The upcoming downtime changes should address the extensive amount of wait associated with regenerating a full bar of endurance." Prathun: "By resting for a few minutes between combat, you can restore your entire supply and be ready to go again. We're not planning on adding endurance regeneration to items."

  Classes Available for Drakkin  [ 1 Comment ]
Print News | Mail News
An interesting thing about the newest race for EQ, the Drakkin, is that they have a color associated with their class choice and an innate resistance that goes along with the color of their hair and scale markings. Obviously, this is all subject to change when the expansion goes live, but currently on the official website the classes available for a Drakkin to specialize in are:

Red Drakkin, Masters of War and Fire, have an innate fire resistance:
Warrior, Shadowknight, Cleric, Rogue, Bard, Enchanter, Wizard, Mage, Necromancer

Black Drakkin, Masters of Shadows and Secrets, have innate disease resistance:
Warrior, Shadowknight, Cleric, Rogue, Bard, Enchanter, Wizard, Mage, Necromancer

Green Drakkin, Masters of Nature and Creation, and have an innate resistance to poison:
Warrior, Ranger, Monk, Cleric, Druid, Rogue, Bard, Enchanter, Wizard, Mage

Blue Drakkin, Masters of Serenity and Peace, have an innate resistance to magic:
Warrior, Ranger, Monk, Cleric, Rogue, Bard, Enchanter, Wizard, Mage

Silver Drakkin, Masters of Wisdom and Spirituality, have an innate resitance to cold:
Warrior, Paladin, Monk, Cleric, Bard, Enchanter, Wizard, Mage

Gold Drakkin, Masters of Enlightenment and Knowledge, have a slight resistance to all forms of magic:
Warrior, Paladin, Monk, Cleric, Bard, Enchanter, Wizard, Mage
