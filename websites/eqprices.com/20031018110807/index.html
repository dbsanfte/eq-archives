9/13 LDoN Launched

 

            The Lost dungeons of Norrath are now live on all servers, and the first groups of brave adventurers are exploring the dank caverns of deepest Guk and the haunted halls of Mistmoore.  The flavor of the expansion is somewhat innovative, using instanced dungeons, as well as providing several different scenarios for the same dungeon.  LDoN can be seen as an interesting alternative method to the grind of experience, although for all intents and purposes that is what LDoN is: a glorified exp spot, with the notable exception of the raid type encounters. 

 

The augmentation system provides interesting toys and combinations, and is based on the slots that an item qualifies for.  The short explanation is, an item has a certain amount of slots, each with a number, and the various numbers (from 1 to 8), signify a different type of enhancement for the items.  Type 1 is stat bonuses and resists, while type 5 adds dmg to a weapon, and so on.  Each dungeon also has four different types of settings: assassination, collection, slaughter, and rescue.  The descriptions pretty much explain themselves.    In the rescue type, you escort an npc located deep inside the dungeon to a safe location near the exit.  In collection, you gather a set number of items from the mobs you slay.  Yet another neat addition is the introduction of interactive objects, such as barrels, treasure chests, etc.  There is even a leaderboard that keeps track of the missions and allows players to compete.  All these missions are designed to amuse and entertain while simultaneously distracting you from the fact that all you really are doing is killing mobs like always!

 

While the premise of the expansion has very few negatives, one irksome quality that I noticed was that the immersing element of the game was somewhat sacrificed by the instanced dungeons.  The impermanence of each dungeon, coupled with the fact that the layout changed each time, gave the missions an artificial feel, as well as disconnecting from the redundant surety of the Everquest landscape.  While some may consider this a benefit, I feel that it removed some of the immersing feeling of the game.

     LdoN provides some interesting new content, yet the changes and impact to the game aren’t nearly as major as a full-fledged expansion.  The new explorations will keep hardcore gamers at bay for now, but they are no doubt turning their eye on the next installment of EQ

8/21 Small change to Tribunal and Tallon

taken from the EQ website:

Relocation of The Tribunal and Tallon Zek can affect character settings.

We are relocating the Tribunal and Tallon Zek Servers.  This won't affect access to these servers once the move is finished, but the "number" of these servers has changed.  This will mean that if you have characters on those servers you will need to rename the .ini files for those characters to retain your settings (like hotbuttons and such).

Renaming those files is easy.  Here's how.

1) Open My Computer and locate your EverQuest directory.
2) Find the .ini files for the characters you have on The Tribunal or Tallon Zek.  The file will have your character's name, then the server number - like this:
 Absor_84.ini
3) If the number after your character name is 84, change that number to 56.
4) If the number after your character name is 85, change that number to 55.

Once you've done that, all of your specific setting should return.

7/1 Internet Tax on EU Digital services.

 

     Beginning today, a new internet service tax will be enforced for all European citizens (VAT).  This tax applies only to services which do not take tangible form, and is calculated at the point of consumption, i.e. the EU member country itself.  This of course is bad news for the european online gaming community, and it creates a tricky problem for foriegn internet service companies, who now must try and account for a tax if they wish to remain competitive with their european counterparts. 

   

6/30  Star Wars Galaxies- A Rocky Release 

 

     With much fanfare and hype, Star Wars galaxies debuted on June 26th to a public clamoring to get their hands on the game.  Few franchises in history hold as much prestige, and SWG seemed to be the shoe-in for the title of the "Top" third generation MMOG. 

 

Unfortunately, things didn't go exactly as we had all hoped.  In what is now being called the Worst MMOG launch in history, the immediate future of SWG seems dim.  Plagued with massive stability problems, incomplete content, serious and commonplace bugs, amidst a host of other calamities further exacerbated by a new launch, Sony Online Entertainment (SOE) and Lucas arts? are now facing an angry and disappointed customer base. 

 

The most confusing question that everyone is asking is: "How could "they" let this happen?"  The Star Wars movies (at least the original three!) are beloved by nearly everyone who watches them, and universally recognized as one of the most enduring fantasy worlds across a spectrum of media.  So it naturally begs the question, with all SWG had going for it, why why why!!?  There were early warning signs from the beta test community, at first cordial, and as launch date grew closer and closer the pleas became more insistent.  Eventually the cries of "incomplete!" and "Don't do it!" became so loud, that SOE refused to lift the NDA of SWG until only two days before the actual launch date.

           

     For Sony Online Entertainment, their future in MMOGs seems a little more uncertain.  Facing an increasingly jaded customer base  after assuming command of the award winning game everquestTM, SWG was a chance for them to do it right from the start, using all the experience gained from their years at EQ.  This alone should have assured a smoother launch then what occurred.  If the problems in SWG aren't handled effectively, and customers begin to lose faith in SOE, it might have consequences for their highly anticipated Everquest 2TM game scheduled for launch in Q3 2003. 

      

     One of the most disheartening truths of this whole fiasco is that the public truly want to like SWG, truly want to see it succeed, and yet perhaps this simple fact of perception may be its saving grace.  The customer base may overlook the fact that they are now in effect paying monthly fees to beta test an incomplete game, and paid 50 dollars for a game they didn't get, merely because it IS star wars.  Only time will tell if SOE can get the game tested and fleshed out before the power of even the Force is overwhelmed by frustrated customers.
