"Look Lina! Thrombrir cast spell again! Thrombrir is great warrior wizard!"

Young Thrombrir looked down at his new Minotaur Axe in wonder. The ground around the goblin camp was littered with the stinking corpses of dead goblins. At Thrombrir's feet lay the schorched body of their shaman leader.

Lina laughed. Her spell, which had barely saved the dwarf's hide, had struck at the exactly the same time as his final blow. "You sure did Thrombrir! And a mighty spell it was! I'm very lucky to have such a mighty dwarf as a companion!"

"Thrombrir lucky that Lina give Thrombrir great axe of wizardry! Thrombrir not know how to cast spell until Thrombrir get axe."

Lina laugh affectionately at the dwarf's stupidity as he rattled on. He ran about the battlefield (very slowly due to his considerable injuries) pointing out where his amazing new wizardry had ripped goblins to shreds. In actuality, only one of the six dead goblins was due to the young warrior's axe -- the other five had been slain by the powerful high elf in frantic attempts to save the dwarf's life. But Thrombrir did not realize this, and as he continued to rave about his newfound power, Lina summoned some magical bandages with which to bind his wounds.

The setting of the story is, of course, the fantastical world of Everquest, and the events are as real as any can be in a role playing game.

In my opinion roleplaying is by far the most fun aspect of Everquest. The interaction that my young creation has had with other characters has led me to a lot of laughs and more than a few entertaining stories -- not to mention a wealth of powerful items. If you're interested in enhancing your Everquest experience through roleplaying but don't really know where to start, or if you're looking for a way to improve your current roleplaying abilities, this guide is for you.

Character Creation
The first step in roleplaying succesfully is the creation of a character. Without a solid background for your character you'll have a hard time interacting with other people and with your environment. Unfortunately, the creation of a good character is also in my opinion the most difficult aspect of roleplaying. But if you can come up with solid ideas for a few aspects of your character, the rest will fall into place.

The first thing you'll need to choose for your new character is a background. Will you play a powerful but unintelligent barbarian warrior? A highly honorable human paladin? A spiteful, vengeful dark elven necromancer? You need to decide on a race, class, and general demeanor for your character. Keep in mind that race and class have a large effect on the demeanor of a character -- you'll have a very hard time playing an attractive ogre or an elegant dwarf. There are no rules that say that all barbarians are barbaric, and no rules that say that all elves are intelligent and refined, but in both cases they usually are. There is nothing wrong with playing a stupid elf, but it'll probably be a lot harder to pull off than playing a stupid ogre.

Next you should design a background story for your character. This need not be very complex, but the more detail you put into it the better. The basic things you'll need to know have to do with your character's motivation for becoming powerful. Why did he choose to be an adventurer instead of a merchant? What does he hope to accomplish during his journeys? The answers to these question can be as simple as a few words and as complex as a novel, and will effect a great deal of the story that you create in the game. There are many things about my character that noone knows about but me -- they are details that will present themselves slowly to my companions as I continue to play, and are a great part of the fun of roleplaying.

Another important thing to think about at this stage is what makes your character interesting. A dull personality leads to a dull roleplaying experience. Does your character speak with a refined or lower-class accent? Does he walk with a limp? If so, why? Does he believe that he is part of high society, and look down on the average swarthy adventurer? If he has spent his entire life studying in the monastary, does he even know how to properly set up camp? More on interesting player quirks later...

The last thing you should choose for your character is a name. Try to choose a name that sounds as if it belongs to an individual of that particular race. Elves often have refined names, dwarves have very gruff names, and humans often have names that sound as if they belong in shakespeare. Also try to choose a name that fits your character's personality. A name can often convey an idea of the temperment of a character -- some names sound more regal than others, some more moody, some more jovial. I find the selection of a name to be the most difficult step in character creation, and I've spent many hours brainstorming names on more than once occasion.

Playing Your Character
Playing the character that you've created is often much easier than creating the character. You already know a lot about him, so you should be able to judge his reaction to events (especially significant ones) based on his background and temperment. The only really hard part about roleplaying is learning to defeat the nasty Everquest interface. If the was more like real life, you'd judge the meanings, reactions, and general mood of your companions through body language. In Everquest, the only way for you to know these things is for your companions to actively tell you. Emoticons greatly enhance the roleplaying experience -- no good roleplayer goes without them.

Emoticons (emotes for short) are the roleplayer's main method of communicating facial expressions and body language to his companions. Emotes should be used to display things that could be seen by other characters in the game, but not to convey internal thoughts. A common misconception is that emotes are also good for communicating ideas; I commonly see people emoting things like "Thadur hates goblins". Instead of ":hates goblins", use ":scowls angrily at the goblins" or simply say "I hate goblins!". Instead of ":is angry" use ":appears to be angry"; one is an internal statement that companions might not know about, the other is a description of body language. As a general rule, use emotes as often as you think about your character's internal state and how it would be visible to your companions.

In addition to emotes there are a few other useful tools for roleplayers to keep in mind. There is a description box available to all characters in which most roleplayers put a detailed description of their character. It is visible by others when they inspect your character by right-clicking. To set this description, right-click on your own character's picture and then click and type into the darkened box to the right of the main dialogue box. Most roleplayers put their character into roleplaying mode by typing /roleplay into the main dialogue box. This option causes a character to become anonymous (class and level information will not be displayed on a /who command) and causes the character's name to be purple instead of blue.

It is also important to think about how your character speaks to his companions. Often roleplayers try to take on a particular dialect. Dwarves try to speak in a scottish accent, Ogres and Trolls in a very dumbed-down version of standard english, and Elves in a very high-society dialect with a large vocabulary. The dialect you choose, if any, is entirely up to you and your character's background story.

In a game such as Everquest, issues of game mechanics often arise and need to be spoken about in-game. Since your character would have no knowledge of these mechanics, it is important to be able to let other players know that you're speaking out of character. DO NOT use the /ooc out of character speech built into Everquest. This speech will be broadcast to everyone in the zone, which is almost never necessary. Instead, preface your statement with the letters "ooc", for instance "ooc I just made level 12! woohoo!".

Advancing the Story
The most enjoyable aspect of roleplaying for many people is character and plot advancement. Adventuring with a group of friends who also like to roleplay can lead to significant changes in your character. The moral here is simply to be adventurous. Some of the best roleplaying situations I've been in began when I acted like a total fool. Some people will laugh at you -- there's no way to get away from that -- but most will laugh with you. You'll find great stories that seem to just materialize when you do something as simple as play in character.
