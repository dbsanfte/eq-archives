Becoming A Druid

Forward

Hiya!  I’m Bobbins Rumblebelly, a Druid on Rodcet Nife, and for the next few pages I’ll be your guide through this intriguing and powerful class.

This guide is geared towards players who have a good working comprehension of EverQuest game fundamentals, but wish to achieve additional information on class’s they might want to play. If you are a new player in EverQuest, then I suggest reading the EverQuest Manual and looking at our new player section before continuing.

If you their are any errors or comments for this guide please send them to: bobbins@eqanswers.com

Overview

Druids are the guardians of the natural balance of life in Norrath.  Above all else, they treasure the peaceful coexistence of nature with the more ‘intelligent’ races and fight those that would harm nature with no consideration of the consequences of their actions.  Many Druids are neutral, in accord with their strive for balance.  In a struggle for control of the land between the forces of nature and a nearby Human city, the druid would side with whoever is losing--if the Humans are mercilessly hunting the local animals, the druid will see to it that the Humans are stopped.  If the animals are indeed becoming overpopulated, and a risk to the humans, the druid will aid in the control of the animal population (as long as proper respect is paid to the dead animals).

There is an ongoing struggle in the ranks of the Druids--a new faction, known as the Unkempt Druids, have emerged.  Many young druids choose to ally themselves with this faction; others remain fierce defenders of the original faith.  A true druid’s path lies somewhere in the middle--the path of balance.

Unkempt or not, Druids possess a wide variety of spells.  Be it healing, nuking, buffing, or any other of a wide variety of abilities, Druids can do it and, often, do it quite well.  We are also one of two classes in EverQuest that have the ability to evacuate a group (known as ‘evacing’ or simply ‘evac’ - our Succor line of spells) - which allows an entire party to escape from a deadly situation, whisked away to the (relative) safety of a Druid Ring.

A large disadvantage is that although we have many abilities, we are rarely considered the best in any one area - for each trait that we have, another class is usually specialized around (such as clerics for healing, enchanters for general charming, as opposed to our animal-specific charm spells).

Myths and Controversy

Druids are the ‘god’ class
This is both true and false.  Due to the large range of spells that we get (everything from nukes to teleports to powerful buffs, etc.) people assume that this is the class that can do everything - which is simply not true.  While we do possess traits from many other classes, we will never be as good at those things as each other class.  Druids will never be as good at nuking as a wizard, we will never be as good at healing as a cleric, and we will never be as good at buffing stats as a shaman is.  Our role in groups is to fill in what is missing.  We can often handle more than one role (backup healing and nuking simultaneously, for example), but we will never be able to do everything.  The class itself may be a ‘god’ class, but the people playing the Druid class are not ‘gods.’

Druids are ‘k3wl d00ds’
Unfortunately, this is both true and false as well.  Druids are very capable soloists, which has prompted many so-called ‘k3wl d00ds’ to play this class - we level up quickly and are good at ‘farming’ items from low-mid level dungeons.  On the other hand, many casual gamers (with motives completely opposite those of a ‘k3wl d00d’) choose the class for the same reason - if a person can only play for an hour at a time and is unable to find a group, he will still be able to solo and earn decent XP.

Druids are useless at higher levels
False - The Druid class is a jack of all trades, and a master of none.  As such, at higher levels, when class abilities become more specialized and unique (clerics get complete healing, wizards get amazing firepower, etc.), druids tend to get left behind.  We are stuck with Greater Healing for many levels (29 to 53), and after the mid 40s are incapable of being the primary healer for a group.  Likewise, we cannot nuke on par with wizards. 

So, what are Druids good for?  Aside from evacing (which wizards can do also), all of our abilities are shared and for the most part surpassed by other classes.  The catch, however, only applies in a perfect group; in EverQuest there are so many class possibilities that no group will ever be considered ‘perfect’ or ideal; Druids can fill in for many positions which makes them valuable.

Character Creation

Creating a Character can be a daunting undertaking, for it can be straightforward and complex at the same time. You can create your character depending on what type of situation or role you want to classify in.

Race

Another large factor in creating your Character is deciding what race to play.  This is not only a personal choice depending on how you want to role-play, but it will also effect how you play.  Each race has diverse starting attributes, location, and disadvantages over others.

One thing to keep in mind is that although stats are very important in EverQuest, the most important thing is that you choose a race that you like, and most importantly, have a roleplaying reason for choosing.  Never choose a race simply for its stats - there are dozens of wood elf and halfling druids out there (many of which were chosen simply for stats) - an adventurous Druid may choose to be a human or half elf, for the sole purpose of being different.

Half Elf
Half elf druids are a rarity in Norrath.  They have the lowest base wisdom of the four races, and the agility, which being good, is not the highest.  However, due to the large number of wisdom enhancing items in the game, supplementing your wisdom as a half elf should not be difficult.  Half elves also posses infravision, which allows them to see in the dark - essential at lower levels.

Halfling
Halflings have excellent starting statistics.  They are tied with wood elves for the highest maximum starting wisdom and agility.  They also posses infravision.  A disadvantage of being a halfling is the very low charisma (50) that will make charming animals more difficult.

The halfling newbie area, Misty Thicket, is one of the better newbie zones in the game, allowing for quick leveling up to 14 all in one sheltered zone.

Human 
Like half elves, human druids are a rarity.  They posses decent stats, though not as good as a halfling or wood elf’s, but they lack infravision, making adventuring the night more difficult.

Wood Elf
Like Halflings, wood elves posses excellent starting statistics.  In addition, they also suffer from the same problem as halflings - the size of the population of wood elf druids is staggering.  Wood elf is probably not the best race to choose if you want to be able to be distinguished by your race.

Attributes

One of the most significant parts of character generation is choosing where to put your bonus points. This will let your further modify your characters role, to be more helpful in diverse situations. A Warrior might add more to his Strength so he can cut deeper into his quarry. While a Necromancer would be much more interested in increasing his magical powers and spreading the evil deeds with intelligence. A thing to keep in mind, you can even further increase your attributes with magical items found inside the game, so incase you make a oversight, it is not very hard to correct.

Strength (Ineffective for Druids) 
Influences your maximum and average damage ratios, the more strength you have, the more likely you will hit harder.  Additional strength will let you learn your offensive skills faster, and let you carry more weight.

This attribute is less important.  Being able to hit more often and carry more weight is appealing at lower levels, but at higher levels, Druids use their melee skills less and less and have no need for the additional strength.  Our strength buff spells also reduce any problems of being encumbered due to low strength.

Stamina (Ineffective for Druids)

Affects how many total hit points you have, and how long you will be able to hold your breath under water.

This attribute is less important for Druids.  We naturally have low total hit points, and the increase made from this is not worth sacrificing other, more important, attributes.

Agility (Effective for Druids)

Agility affects your armor class, resulting in your druid being hit less often and therefore taking less damage during combat.  It also affects how quickly you learn some defensive skills.

This is an important attribute for Druids - I would recommend putting 5 points into this.  The AC bonus is very useful at higher levels.

Dexterity (Ineffective for Druids)

Influences how often your weapon proc (spell) will engage, and how quickly you learn weapon skills.  This attribute also affects how quickly your channeling skill raises (channeling allows you to continue casting a spell despite being hit - which would normally interrupt your casting).  However, dexterity does not affect your success rate with channeling - only your channeling skill affects that.  This attribute is less important than others - the channeling skill goes up rapidly without the added dexterity.

Wisdom  (The Primary Attribute of Druids)

Wisdom affects the amount of mana your Druid has - a crucial attribute.  It also affects how quickly you can learn many skills if your wisdom is higher then intelligence.  The formula for calculating your total mana is ((wis/5)+2)*level.

Intelligence (Ineffective for Druids)

Directly affects the amount of mana of magi classes including bards and shadow knights.  Also affects how quickly you can learn many skills.  This attribute will only be important to a Druid who wishes to develop their trade skills; with the large number of intelligence increasing items in the game, it is more practical to put points into other attributes first - especially since intelligence will not affect the day-to-day life of most druids.

Charisma (Varying effectiveness, depending on play style)

Affects amount you will be paid for goods by NPC merchants, and how much they will pay you.  Also effects saving throw on certain bard and enchanter spells (charms in particular). Druids get several animal-only charm spells.  Charisma is useful in situations where an animal is being charmed because a higher charisma will make the charm spell more likely to succeed.  In most other situations, however, charisma will not have a large affect on a Druid’s life.  For these reasons, a Druid’s starting charisma is best left as is.

Combat

Being a jack of all trades, there are many different tactics for a druid to learn. Much of your time spent inside EverQuest will be involving combat situations. To be prepared for combat, one must know the basics of it and EverQuest itself, which we won’t describe here, for obvious reasons. What we will divulge, is common tactics used to take down foes more hastily and safer.

Solo

Druids are very capable soloists.  There are several different tactics for soloing, all of which are effective in their own way:

Kiting: Kiting as a druid consists of snaring the creature, running away from it, casting a direct damage spell, running a little further, casting again, etc.  It is relatively simple to learn, however, it is important to properly estimate the time your spells need to cast versus the time the monster needs to reach you.  If you don’t leave enough distance, you could be in trouble - the monster will catch you before your spell is finished and will most likely take a few hits (more important at higher levels - after level 30, monsters start to hit hard).  Leaving too much distance is usually not a problem - it might even allow you time to get two spells off before running again.  (Note that in Kunark, the range at which monsters give up and stop chasing you has been decreased - if you run too far out of spell range, the monster may give up and stop following you.  When it does this, it goes into ‘super heal’ mode, during which it regenerates hitpoints at an extremely fast rate.  When you go back to find it, you could find that it is already at nearly full health).

Root-DoT’ing: In order to get full damage out of a damage over time (DoT) spell, the monster must be either immobile or running away from the caster.  Root-DoT’ing is a popular method of soloing, especially since the recent patch that fixed Druidic root spells.  Start by rooting the creature.  Then, apply your DoTs (the Stinging Swarm/Creeping Crud/ etc. line stacks with the Flame Lick/Immolate line).  Meditate and watch for the root break notification message.  When you see the message that your root spell has broken, get up immediately, run back a ways (so the monster doesn’t reach you while you are casting), and reroot it.  It may take several minutes of Root-DoT’ing in order to kill the creature, but if you’ve been meditating the whole time, you will be at near full mana, and ready for another monster.

A note: at level 35 this gets much easier, as you gain the ability to meditate without using your spell book - you can watch the monster the entire time and react quickly when root breaks.

Snare-n-Scare: This tactic is effective solo against animals, and if grouped with someone who can cast regular fear, it is effective against normal creatures as well.

Solo Snare-n-Scare (animals only): Snare the animal and fear it (panic/terrorize animal).  Then, apply your DoT spells.  You also have the option of running behind the animal and attacking it in melee--this is nice for practicing weapons skills and taking the animal down faster, but it puts you at risk when the fear spell breaks--you will be within melee range of the animal and will take more damage than if you had been safely out of range (and with enough distance between you and the animal to cast the fear spell).

Partnered Snare-n-Scare: The tactics are the same as above.  Snare the creature and fear it.  If it is an animal, you should fear it.  If it is anything else, the other fear-capable group member should fear it.  Where to go from here varies - if you are grouped with high melee damage capable partner (rogue, monk) simply let them run behind the animal and attack it.  If you are partnered with a necromancer, you both should stack your DoTs on it - with combined necromantic and druidic DoTs, the creature will go down fast.

Porcupining: This tactic is great for keeping your melee skills up, and is effective up until level 35 or so (at which point monsters start hitting so hard that it becomes impractical - if not impossible - to porcupine a mob from full health).

Start by fully buffing yourself - Skin like ***, personal damage shield, regular damage shield, strength buff, firefist, wolf form if you have it, and regeneration, if you have it.  Now, find the mob that you want to kill, and engage it with a DoT.  If you have a second, stackable DoT, apply that as well.  Now, wait for the creature to get to you, and melee it.  The combination of the full damage from your DoT spells, the damage from your damage shield (you’re likely to get hit a lot - which is a good thing), and the damage from your weapon should take the creature down quickly.  This method is also very mana efficient.

Group

As fun and rewarding as soloing can be, there are also many advantages to fighting in a group.  High-level dungeon environments require groups - and many of the best druid items are dropped deep in dungeons.  Simply put, soloing will only get you so far - grouping will take you further.  (Yes, it is theoretically possible to solo all the way to level 60 - grouping, however, will be much more rewarding in terms of items and playing skills).

There are many roles that a mid-level Druid can play in a group.

Don’t Panic: This is possibly the most important lesson to learn in a group environment.  When grouped, you are capable of taking on multiple monsters at a time with little or no trouble.  When you see the puller returning to the group with 3 mobs, don’t panic and gate/evac (unless it is clearly a bad pull, in which case you should evac - the group will thank you later).  Also, no matter what role you are playing in the group, you will get hit at one time or another - that’s a simple fact.  When a monster does aggro on you, don’t run around screaming “/g ge ti tof fme! Goonnna die!!!!” - doing that will get you killed faster than nearly anything else.  Simply stay calm, don’t move, and wait for the tank to taunt the monster off of you.  Remember that taunt has a very low range - if you’re running around, it will be impossible for the tank to taunt the creature off of you.  If you’re outdoors, you will probably also be running out of range of the backup healer (heal spells have a much shorter range than nukes, DoTs, etc.).

Primary Healer: Being a primary healer is just what it sounds like - you take the majority of the healing responsibilities.  If you’re going to be the primary healer, make sure you have a backup healer.  Having a backup healer is always important, but it is especially important when a non-Cleric is the primary - Clerics can wear plate and have a much higher AC than Druids; they can take a few passing blows in a fight.  Druids can’t wear plate - their lower AC makes them much more vulnerable.

Time Your Heals: Learning when to heal and when to sit and meditate is crucial.  If the puller brings back 4 mobs, and you immediately heal him, the three mobs that the puller did not hurt are going to jump onto you.  This will get you killed very fast.  Even if the puller is losing health, it’s better to wait than to heal right away.  If you are grouped with an enchanter, wait for them to mezz the extra mobs - this will remove the danger of pulling the extra mobs onto you.

Coordinate Multiple Heals with the Backup Healer: If two group members’ health begins to drop, call which one you are healing (“/g Healing B”) so that the backup healer won’t heal the same person - nothing is worse than to have one person get two GHeals while the other tank dies without getting any.  This also helps to spread the healing aggro (each hitpoint you heal adds that much damage to your position on the surrounding monsters’ aggro lists) out among several healers, making it less likely to have one healer jumped by multiple mobs.

Backup Healer: In this mode, you act as a backup for the primary healer.  Feel free to toss in an occasional nuke, but always keep more than enough mana to keep the primary healer healed, as well as healing anyone that the primary can’t (if two tanks’ health starts dropping simultaneously, for example).  Also, always be ready to take over as primary healer if the primary goes OOM or dies.

Snare Everything: Allowing a runner (a mob that runs away when it gets to low health) to escape will get your group killed quickly.  Your group will be forced to chase after it, most likely picking up extra mobs on the way.  Or, if the group decides not to chase it, it will soon return with several friends and often a large train.  Snaring every mob you fight is the solution - when they get to a low enough health to run, they will be at ‘walk’ speed - snaring reduces their speed to zero, making it easy to kill.  This is not the same as rooting - root doesn’t last as long as snare, and is more likely to break before the monster is dead.

Also, make sure that you inform your group that you’re going to be snaring - root overrides snare, and there could be problems if people root the mobs before you get a chance to snare them.  Also, if people in your group are using swords that proc root, warn them about the problems it can cause - and, if possible, convince them to use a safer weapon.  (This is just an opinion, but it is true that swords that proc root can have a deadly effect on groups.  The root spell is very unlikely to last for the entire life of the creature, and when it breaks, the creature will resume running - most likely requiring your group to chase after it, getting trained in the process.)

Nuking:  Although we are not as skilled as other casters in the realm of direct damage, we are still quite capable.  This role is similar to being the primary healer (though it doesn’t seem so at first) in that timing is key. If you nuke too early, you’ll pull mobs onto you.  If you nuke too late, the mob may die before you finish casting (resulting in a waste of mana if you don’t interrupt your casting in time).  Also be sure not to overburn. It is tempting to nuke again and again, but you will soon find yourself OOM with multiple mobs on you, and the tanks will have a difficult time taunting them off of you.

Evaccing: This is an important role at higher levels (though you will never find yourself on evac duty and nothing else - in a good group, evacs will rarely be required).  When grouped, keep an evac spell up at all times.  And, even if you are not the designated evaccer, save enough mana for the evac spell - when evac is called, anyone who is capable of evaccing should cast it.

Always work it out with your group before hand.  If you are grouped with evil characters, you should not use Succor: East - the NPCs at the druid ring in East Karana will attack the evil characters on sight.  Make sure that the group knows the evac protocol, so that when you call the evac, people won’t run to the zone (it happens to me all the time).

Certain roles are hard to mix with being the designated evaccer.  If you are playing primary healer, it is generally not a good idea to also be the main evaccer - if an evac situation occurs, you are likely to have several creatures attacking you (aggroed because of your heals) and will find it difficult to cast the evac spell without being interrupted (8 seconds is a long time when you’re trying to survive a train).

Mixing Roles: Many of the roles listed above can be mixed. It wouldn’t make much sense to only snare mobs or to only be an evaccer. I usually find myself doing a little bit of everything: backup healer, snarer, primary evaccer, and some nuking thrown in.  The most important thing is to keep your group alive by doing whatever the situation calls for.

Special Information

Channeling (level 4): Unlike int-casters who start with this skill, we get this skill at level 4 and have to train in it.  This spell is crucial - with a high channeling skill, your spells will get interrupted much less during melee combat.  Many druids forget this skill (I didn’t train in it until 21) - it is truly essential, and should not be forgotten.

Meditation (level 8): This skill allows us to regain our mana much faster than normal - crucial for reducing downtime.  Unfortunately, we don’t get this skill until level 8.

Weapons skills: Druids have 3 weapons available to them: 1 hand blunt, 1 hand slash, and 2 hand blunt.  Although we become very poor melee fighters at higher levels, we are still able to hold our own until level 25 or so. It is important to keep as many of your weapons skills maxed as possible.  I neglected 1hs and 2hb, and am now regretting it, as I am unable to use anything other than 1hb, if I want to be useful in a melee situation.

Forage (level 5): We get this skill at level5 (wood elves start with it).  At lower skill levels it goes up slowly, and rarely yields any food, but at higher skill levels it becomes a very useful and reliable source of food.  Once you become a master forager (skill 101+) you will be able to forage special items such as Steamfont Spring Water, the infamous Plains Pebble, and other unique items.  The new Kunark zones have a larger number of ‘special’ forage items, including Mud Crabs, gold nuggets, and stink beetles.

Also, many items needed for baking are foraged, making this skill important if you ever wish to become a baker and don’t want to have to rely on others for your ingredients.

Dodge (level 15): Druids get this skill at level 15.  If successful, it allows us to ‘dodge’ an attack, thus avoiding the damage.

Tracking (level 20): We get this skill at level 20.  It gives us a list of nearby creatures, allowing us to select one to track.  This is useful for locating NPCs and PCs, though there seem to be many problems with using track to find PCs - more often than not it gives ‘ghost’ locations.

Specialization (level 30): Specialization allows you a chance to cast a spell faster than normal and to use less mana that you normally would.  There is a specialization skill for each branch of magic - one of your skills can go above 50, and all others are capped at 50.  You will only see a noticeable benefit from the skill that goes above 50.

The two most common choices for specialization are alteration (heals, snares, evac spells) and evocation (DD spells).  Both are good choices, and your choice depends greatly on your style of play.  If you find yourself  kiting mobs, solo, more often, evocation would probably be a better choice.  If you find yourself playing as the primary healer or evaccer in a group, alteration would be the better choice. (Note: See our Specialization guide for more information.)

Spell Information

Here we will describe the first two ranks of spells you are eligible to buy including which ones to buy and the ones to avoid.  You can find all these spells at your local guildhall or near it.

First Rank (Levels 1-4)

Minor Healing

This is the first healing spell that Druids get.  It heals 10 hit points of damage.  This spell is useful during combat up until level 5 or so - after level 5 the monsters start hitting so hard that this spell makes very little difference in combat.

The next upgrade to this spell, Lesser Healing, comes at level 9

You start with this spell, so there is no need to purchase it.

Skin Like Wood

This is the first hit point/AC buff that Druids get.  At level one, it adds 3 AC and 11 HP and maxes out at adding 5 AC and 20 HP.

The next upgrade to the spell, Skin Like Rock, comes at level 14.

You start with this spell, so there is no need to purchase it.

Endure Fire

This spell decreases the damage that you take from fire based attacks.  Although it is useful at higher levels, at low levels there are very few creatures that attack with deadly fire attacks - this spell should be one of the last rank 1 spells that you purchase.

Flame Lick

This is the fist DoT spell that druids get.  It is a very weak DoT (10 damage) but it also reduces the target’s AC by 4 - very useful at lower levels.  It also makes a nice taunt spell if another member of the group is in trouble.  The damage that this spell does is not powerful enough to lower the health of the creature - it simply counteracts the regeneration of the creature, keeping it from regaining health during long periods of misses (which will happen a lot at lower levels). Additionaly, flame lick requires a fire beetle eye to cast (the eye is not consumed during use and can be reused).

This spell is highly recommended for purchase.

Burst of Flame

This is the first rank Druidic direct damage spell.  The damage it deals is minor (3-5 damage), but it is still useful - it is the best DD spell we get until rank 2.  It is also quick casting, allowing it to be used during melee combat without too much risk of interruption.

It is highly recommended that you purchase this spell - it should be the first spell you purchase.

Snare

This spell reduces the movement speed of your target - by 41% at level one up to a maximum of 55% at level 15.  It is essential for kiting as well as stopping runners.

It is highly recommended that you purchase this spell - it should be the second spell that you purchase.

Dance of the Fireflies

This spell summons a globe of light, which you can place in an inventory slot, and have it act as a small light source.  This spell is useful for humans who do not have infravision and for other races who wish to raise their conjuration skill.

This spell is not very useful unless you are a human - it is not recommended to immediately purchase this spell unless you have the extra money.

Sense Animals

When cast, this spell points you in the direction of the nearest animal, and targets it.  This is useful for finding animals to kill in newbie zones… although the act of killing the animals is not very Druid-like and will get you killed in Qeynos Hills (Holly Windstalker and Cros Treewind fiercely defend the animals there).

Lull Animal

This spell reduces the aggro range of animals that would otherwise attack you.  Note that this spell is only useful if cast before combat; it will have no effect if you cast it after the animal has aggroed on you.

Panic Animal

This spell causes the target animal to flee in terror.  This is useful if you get attacked by a higher level animal - it gives you time to escape safely.  Also, at higher levels, this spell becomes very useful when using Snare-n-Scare on animals.

Second Rank (Levels 5-8)

Cure Poison and Cure Disease

These two spells cure poison and disease, respectively.  There aren’t many low-level creatures with disease or poison attacks, but they do exist - these spells are recommended for purchase.  In order to make more efficient use of your spell slots, find out beforehand which creatures have which type of attack - it is a waste to keep both of the spells memorized at all times, as they are rarely used.

Burst of Fire

This is an upgrade to the Rank 1 spell Burst of Flame.  At level 5 it does 12 damage, and increases gradually to 15 at level 8.  It is highly recommended that you purchase this spell.

Ward Summoned

This spell works only against summoned creatures - NPC pets, elementals, and other similar creatures.  It is very powerful (45 damage for 30 mana) but is also highly specialized - there are very few summoned creatures in Norrath.  This spell is useful if you know you are going to be fighting summoned mobs - otherwise it is best left unpurchased.

Whirling Wind (outdoors only)

This is a conjuration-based DD spell.  It does 24 damage, and ‘jolts’ the target - I still use this spell at 36; NPCs are very difficult to interrupt, and this spell does it well.  It is also useful for raising conjuration.  Note that its resist rate is higher than other DD spells, due to the added ‘jolt’ effect.  This spell is highly recommended for purchase.

Invoke Lightning (outdoors only)

This is an area-effect DD spell.  It does 24-25 points of damage to creatures within a small (~3 foot) radius of your target.  It is useful for damage multiple targets at one time, but make sure that you are not caught in the area of effect - you will be damaged.  I did not find this spell to be extraordinarily useful, and would recommend purchasing this spell after you have purchased the other more useful spells.

Grasping Roots

This spell holds (roots) the target in place and does a small amount of damage to it (10 damage).  This spell is useful for ‘root-parking’ a mob (rooting it out of range of the group so it won’t be able to fight until root breaks), and for Root-DoT’ing.  This spell is recommended for purchase, though its uses are specialized.

Camouflage (outdoors only)

This spell makes you invisible to most creatures.  It is extremely useful for sneaking past creatures, and for corpse runs - this spell is highly recommended for purchase.  A note: this spell is outdoors only - an indoor version comes at level 19 (Superior Camouflage).

Harmony (outdoors only)

This spell lowers the aggro range of all creatures, making it extremely useful for pulling only one creature out of a group.  Another great feature of this spell is that it is unresistable - unlike other lull spells that will aggro the creature if they are resisted. This spell has no chance of aggroing the mob and is essential at both lower and higher levels - it should be one of the first spells you purchase.

Gate

This spell returns you to your bind point.  It is extremely useful for escaping deadly situations - simply cast it, and as long as the spell is not interrupted, you will be whisked away, out of harm’s reach.  An essential spell - it should be one of the first spells you purchase.

This mana requirement of Gate is extremely high at first - it will most likely take most of your mana bar at lower levels, which makes it less useful for escaping situations.  At higher levels, it becomes much more reliable.

Conclusion

The Druid class is a powerful and fun class to play.  It fits many playing styles well - if you only have an hour a night to play, you can easily solo for that time.  If you have many hours to play, we are very useful in groups.  It can be difficult at first because of the wide variety of tactics that we have at our disposal, but once you get used to it, you will find this class very enjoyable.
