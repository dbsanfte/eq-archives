Syrneus wrote:

A Column over the importance of 989 Studios role-playing with the other gamers.

Simply stated, and yet a very good subject to discuss. Thank you for your submission!

When speaking about 989 Studios role-playing with other gamers, there are two ways to think about it. The first is having the designers play as regular characters, without anyone knowing they are actually GMs. I was a developer of a MUD in which this practice was suggested. One reason is, it keeps the developer up to speed on what everyone else has been creating, and how their piece of the pie fits in with the rest of the game as a whole. The benefit here is obvious, and can go a long way toward providing an in depth consistency throughout.

As a player, the GMs can also see the tactics people use, and problems they encounter. This can give them direct, first-hand feedback about whether or not their intentions have become reality. Imagine if a developer created a nasty trap that an unsuspecting player would have to skillfully avoid, and only if they had a high enough dexterity to do it. But players discovered that if you scoot along the wall, you can avoid the trap completely. Perhaps this was intended, and perhaps not. Unless the developer goes out and sees what players are doing, and how they are doing it, they will not know if they were successful.

There is also the whole market research aspect to this. If you put yourself in with the players, you can discover what they like, what they don't like, and what they wish they could do but can't. This way, the GMs can learn what to do differently in the next area, or what to fix in the next patch.

Some of this can be done merely by invisibly watching (snooping, in MUD terms) a player as he goes about his or her adventures. However, I believe actually doing it yourself with other players gives you a better perspective on the reality of the situation the players are in.

The second, and perhaps more interesting way of role-playing is for GMs to jump into the role of an NPC and create dynamic events and quests for players. Examples of this have been given by Brad in the past. Perhaps your favorite shop keeper will "come alive" and start talking to you and responding to your actions. Imagine finishing selling your freshly gathered loot, and as you turn to leave the shop keeper stops you.

"Wynn!".

You pause for a moment and wonder what is going on.

"Yeah you.. the short one with the pointy ears."

"Umm, yeah?"

"You have been one of my best suppliers. I was wondering if you could get me one of those flaming longswords. I've had a few... inquiries. I'll pay you double!"

Wow, that would be great. Interacting with NPCs as if they were PCs. It generally adds a sense of realism and makes the environment of Norrath more believable. It provides a greater sense of immersion and general game enjoyment. Next time you approach a NPC, you will ever be wondering if you should stop and talk to them for a moment before moving on. Perhaps there is something special behind those eyes.

Quests like that are good to a point. But they can be severely limited by the time GMs have to interact with only a select few individuals who stop to chat and choose to accept the challenge. Another nice aspect the GMs can take on is to visit the world as a special character that one would not normally see. And I don't mean Aradune. Instead, an in character power, such as one of the Norrath gods. Imagine if the evil dark elf lord appeared outside the gates of Qeynos, shooting lighting from his fingertips to blast the guards. He shouts to all that they will be destroyed unless they worship him. Suddenly in a bright glow, the paladin god appears and summons his loyal followers to gather for battle.

Through their GM powers, they could physically move players from anywhere in the game into this area, and it would be completely believable. The two player groups could then engage in a massive holy war, with the winners being granted some special boon by their god. Even if the gods merely clashed with each other in a flashy display of magical effects, as the mere mortals watched on in awe, I'm certain it would change our impressions of the game.

In general, having the GMs role-play is every bit as important as adding new features to the game. By injecting a new character into the game, that is, in essence, a new feature as well. If nothing else, it can provide a temporary break from the typical monotony of repeatedly killing the same things that is so often seen in CRPGs. It has been stated in previous chats that things like this will be done, and the effect it has is bound to be spectacular.

-Wynn
