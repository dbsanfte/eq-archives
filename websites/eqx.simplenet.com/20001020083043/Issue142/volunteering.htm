ISSUE # 142

 September 23rd - September 29th, 2000


ARE THRIVING ONLINE COMMUNITIES IN DANGER?

This past week a friend send me an icq message with a link to an article on a new lawsuit filed in Colorado against Ultima Online on behalf of its volunteers who supposedly were enslaved in a virtual sweatshop, laboring unpaid on behalf of Electronic Arts’ bottom line. Not long ago, I also followed the links on the Vault to articles about a similar suit against AOL alleging it too had mistreated its volunteers by not paying them in order to pad its already fabulous bottom line. While the suits so far seem focused on creating economic gain for the “used-to-be-volunteers” plaintiffs and their lawyers, it seems likely that other suits will follow, each attempting to apply non-virtual law to limit nearly all aspects of virtual community, impoverishing the thriving environments we creatively participate in. It seems as if yet another battle has been joined in the war between those who would try to create and support lively and free communities online (even using for-profit sites to do so) and those who are trying to remold the internet into a strictly business environment using a variety of motivations, such as the revenge and greed shown in the above volunteer cases, to invoke laws and governmental restrictions solely for economic benefit with no regard for the impact it may have on the communities that are trying to develop and grow online.

It seems to me that what is happening is that those involved in these moneygrabs are not distinguishing between the corporate body that develops and maintains a site, such as a game world or chat rooms, and the community body that creates itself within the boundaries of a particular virtual environment. They are, however, two distinct bodies. A company such as Verant can provide all the coding and content it wishes and sell it. It cannot, however, simply create a community and sell it too. A community such as the one growing among the players of EverQuest, has its own distinct existence and its members should not be assumed to be concerned with Verant’s profit when they bring the site to life with their presence and contributions. Members of the community may choose to beta test, report bugs, build support sites, create content ideas and give them to Verant to implement if it so decides, volunteer to help players, write articles, poems, stories and make art and music about the world they inhabit. There is no requirement that they do so in order to use the game they bought, but these are the very kinds of acts that develop community in real life as well as on the internet. The members of a community such as that on EverQuest have chosen to participate in a particular virtual world and to accept the rules and restrictions that bind that virtual world. Everything an individual adds to that fantasy world, whether it is simply play and character development or whether it includes solid committed volunteer efforts, rests on a foundation of freedom to choose whether or not they wish to contribute to the development of the separate community.

This individual choice to contribute to community so far exists, although somewhat hindered, in the real world. People can and do contribute volunteer efforts to for-profit, non-profit and governmental bodies on behalf of their surrounding communities and they have done so for centuries. Why then, are online communities, as well as the companies providing the sites where virtual communities grow, under these particular attacks? The issues raised in the two lawsuits seem to hinge on the idea that a for-profit environment should not be allowed to also include within its boundaries a thriving and contributing community. With that in view, the lawsuits say that all activity taking place within the environment should be regulated as part of a singular corporate entity, essentially relegating all those who volunteer their time and energy within a perceived community to a state of mindless and irresponsible peonage whose contributions must be measured only by a minimum wage scale. Imagine the old days when a barn-raising would call in all the surrounding neighbors for the eventual profit of one farmer. What if those neighbors had been required to receive a day’s wages as the sole repayment for supporting and helping each other. Imagine, today, the volunteers in for-profit and non-profit hospitals around the country contributing to the wellbeing of their community members. What if they are then told that their contributions can only be measured against an economic standard for the hospital, not on the benefits their friends and neighbors have received.

What bothers me is that these lawsuits, in their rush to apply economic tests and laws to a volunteer ethic, seem to be assaulting the right of each person to choose the communities they wish to support and help build. In the long run, if suits like these succeed, they will change the enjoyment of and connections available from our online communities. They will quite likely further harm even our real life opportunities to add our individual creativity and concern to the communities that offer so much to both ourselves and our fellow players. These suits certainly put a chill on our potential involvement with our online environments.

Put in online terms, these suits are saying that a person who goes on-line to help other people enjoy a virtual environment they mutually share, particularly if the site is provided by a for-profit company, must be paid because his or her contributions may benefit the company that created and maintains the environment. The lawyers claim this is so even if the reason those contributions are made is for the benefit of the community and not the profitability of the company. No attention whatsoever is being paid to the fact that the person volunteering, in whatever capacity, might be doing so for enjoyment, for altruistic reasons, for developing skills in one environment that might be useful later on in another, or for finding in the environment a creative muse. None of this has anything to do with a motivation to enrich the company which has provided the online environment. Even if that company chooses to support volunteer and creative efforts made on behalf of the community existing on its site, it is still crucial that people have the right and ability to decide where they will spend their extra time and whether they choose or not choose to give of that time to others with similar interests.

Some professors of law were quoted online as saying that these suits do not hold enough substance to prevail because labor laws were designed only to protect those who economically rely on an employer. As I look around and consider what I have seen online in years of mudding and playing EQ, it seems to me that I have not observed players who volunteer expecting to make any money from their efforts (noting, of course, the E-Bayers who have chosen to create an illegal black market in selling game attributes and whose activities should not affect the economic reliance question). What I have experienced are players who enjoy a game community and environment so much they want to add their own ideas, time and energy to building and supporting it.

What these lawsuits are also attempting to say is that any online companies should either have to pay for or else turn down all of the ideas and offers of assistance made to benefit the internet communities developing on their sites. Thus, the very ethic of sharing and contributing that has made the internet, as a whole, strong and vibrant is under attack because a few people have decided that the words “community” and “volunteer” have no other definition other than "I wanna get mine or I’ll close your site down"! It seems important that both the companies who sell virtual environments where communities thrive and the people who care about those communities have some way to stand forth and simply support an internet where volunteers can continue to act to help our communities grow and do well. I would hope that those who seek to limit our opportunities to do so would stop and think about the damage they are doing to the online expressions of the volunteer ethic.

    Essay by Nepenthia
