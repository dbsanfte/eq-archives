EXPRESSions

Morodin wrote:
I'd like to hear your opinions how to make a rogue into something more than an annoying little pickpocket. How would you play a rogue in such a way as to gain respect from those around you?


The stereotypical rogue is a thief who robs everyone blind in order to amass wealth, without regard for laws or the effects imposed upon the victims. In fact, when a character meets a thief in an online game such as EverQuest, his first instinct is to be very cautious and to continuously check his inventory during the conversation and after they part. This presents an inherent difficulty in appearing to be more than a simple thief to the public eye.

First, we must understand why rogues have such a bad reputation. The main thing is their primary skill is often seen to be pick pocketing. In all online games I have witnessed, this is only ever useful against other players. The game creators did not generate a huge two-headed giant with a magical hammer and resistances to all forms of magic just for a thief to sneak in undetected and take it away with no time commitment, effort, or risk of death. The idea in online RPGs is to gain equipment and money through combat and bloodshed.

Against players, however, it is often used as a form of balance. Because thieves often tend to horde powerful equipment, there will be less of it in the game. And if sold, the recipient may think twice about using the equipment if they may have it stolen out from under them. Obviously this is a poor method of balancing anything, but the point is the game masters have little incentive to prevent player vs player stealing.

The end result is a lack of trust. Unless someone is very familiar with a particular character (and sometimes not even then), they will tend to avoid rogues at all cost. So what can be done? The first step is to counter the marks against all rogues. Ignore that pick pocket skill in your choice of training. As I will mention later, there are plenty of other things to train. This way you wont be robbing from other players and wont have a reputation of being a thief. Earn people's trust with your personality and actions. Of course, being a rogue offers a fun change of pace in role playing a darker personality, but if you are approachable or silently confident, it is better than being shifty-eyed and quick-fingered.

All is not against the rogue, however. There are several popular fictional characters who were thieves and were looked up to by the general populace. Robin Hood and Ali Baba come to mind. If you want to be a hero, you can try to emulate these examples. If you do use the pick pocket skill, target the evil players or neutral monsters that no one cares about. Be sure to give away money and small items to beginners. While they are still new, they will be telling others what a great rogue you are. When they grow to be a strong player, they may be a powerful ally or friend who will support you when you are in need, and can be a character reference if questions arise.

These suggestions are not specific to rogues, though. Any character can build their reputation like this. So what can rogues do differently? A respected rogue will be a useful rogue. Their true power does not come from robbing trinkets from the local population, but rather in their ability to sneak undetected through dark areas and do great amounts of damage through surprise attacks from the rear. Their agility and speed can make them a formidable ally in the battle field. Rogues can also be extremely advantageous for disarming traps that could otherwise injure the party and picking locks that can lead to new and exciting adventures.

To give you an example, let me share a story in which I was able to play a character with some very rogue-like qualities.

Several years ago, I was introduced to a game that my friend made up when he was a kid. It is so fun that he continued playing with a regular group up through college, until everyone moved to different states. It is an RPG, but not played with pen and paper. The game is called Vampire and is played at night, outside, on a golf course or park area with trimmed grass and lots of nice pathways through wooded areas. Naturally these places are usually closed at night and are probably illegal to play in, but that merely enhances the spooky atmosphere. One person is chosen to be the Vampire. Another person is chosen to be the good Healer, leader of the villagers. The Vampire speaks to each of the rest of the players (villagers) one by one, and selects a secret Minion who will masquerade as a normal villager.

Play begins with the Vampire running off into the woods to hide or plan an attack. Everyone else splits up into smaller groups and heads off in different directions to hunt the Vampire. As they travel, they will inevitably be very cautious about when the Vampire may jump out of a dark shadow, and very paranoid about which of their companions may turn on them when they least expect it.

The game is played with Wiffle Ball bats as weapons (swords) and striking a body part rends it unusable. Thus, if you get hit in your sword arm then you must switch hands, and if you get hit in both legs then you better be good at fighting on your knees. Anyone who is killed becomes a ghost, and can be converted to a minion or villager by the respective leader. If the Vampire dies, or all villagers are dead, the game is over.

I remember being chosen to be the secret Minion in one particular game. I needed to begin the game by acting calm and traveling with a smaller group, which just happened to include two veteran players, including the creator of the game. Stalking through the darkness caused me no fear, as I knew there were no secret minions or vampires waiting to jump me. In fact, I enjoyed the knowledge that I was the one in control and could strike at any moment.

Eventually the time came to make my move, and as my group began running toward the distant sound of clacking yellow bats, I quietly ran a little slower than the rest, which allowed me to lag behind and follow from the rear of the pack. I cautiously moved in closer, and with two swift motions I tapped the veterans on their backs with my sword and whispered, "You're dead." I grabbed them, now ghosts, each by the arm and, so the others ahead would not grow suspicious, told them to keep running. A short distance ahead, I veered off and brought them to the lair of my leader, the Vampire. The look on his face as his jaw hit the floor was enough to make me proud of my accomplishment. But, after he converted the fresh recruits, the veterans' words of awe and surprise instantly placed me in as one of the elite players, and went down in history as one of the best moves in the game.

This, to me, exemplifies the ideal positive rogue. Using skills such as stealth and backstabbing, being one with the darkness, and using finesse above strength to manipulate your foes will make a powerful rogue. And using these skills for the benefit of others, such as your party, will ensure a reputation of popularity and above all, respect.

    EXPRESSion by Wynn Orill
