Chinese Tea Room Revisited

        John Searle argued against the strong version of the AI theory with his Chinese Tea Room thought experiment. In this thought experiment, a person in a room is given a set of instructions in English to deal with a stream of incoming Chinese symbols by producing a stream of outgoing symbols from a row of "symbol wheels". An external individual might suppose the person inside can comprehend Chinese. Searle claims that this thought experiment countered the view that the human mind was just a complex automaton that processed information without truly understanding it as is the case with computers. This is because every Chinese speaker understands what he is saying or writing. But how deep does this understanding go?

        Let us first take English linguistic examples. How would you pronounce the nonexistent word "VAPORON"? Do you pronounce the first syllable as "VEY" or "VA"? Most would say the latter even though "VAPOR" is pronounced "VEY-PUR". The reason for this is also the reason why "NATION" becomes "NATIONAL". But can you articulate the reason?

        Now try to pronounce the nonexistent word "CABAHAX". Which syllable gets stressed? The first, right? What about "CABAHAXAL"? Which syllable gets stressed? This time it's the third. Why does the stress mark jump from the first to the third syllable? Do you understand why?

        Let's take a look at compound formation. Do you say "mice-infested" or "mouse-infested"? Do you say "rats-infested" or "rat-infested"? Why did you choose the plural noun in the first set, and the singular noun in the second set? What is the underlying rule?

        Give me a definition of the English verb "to drink". You can only drink something if it is a liquid and if you don't chew it. Something that is partially liquid, like clam chowder, is eaten. But why is it that we swallow solid medicine, but we don't drink liquid Tylenol? - we take liquid Tylenol. Also, people swallow poisonous liquids such as lighter fluid. They don't drink it. Does that mean we can only drink things that keep us healthy or alive? No, because we can drink tequila till we pass out. Moreover, we can construe a situation so that a person can "drink" lighter fluid. The person doing the drinking has to be doing it deliberately. So we can say, "Crazy Uncle Tom is drinking lighter fluid again." Another famous historical case is Socrates. Socrates did not die by swallowing hemlock. Socrates died by drinking hemlock. So a complete definition of "drink" must be the act of orally ingesting a liquid, excluding liquid medicine which is "taken", where the actor knows to a good degree how the ingested liquid will affect his or her own mental and physical state. Before anyone can use the word "drink" correctly, their minds have to acknowledge these rules and filter through different situations unconsciously.

        And yet these phonetic, morphological, syntactic and semantic rules are hidden from our conscious mind. We do not realize most of that is going on when we make a simple sentence. The word "drink" poses rigid boundaries on when it can or cannot be used, and "drink" is just one common word. How much of why we speak what we speak do we understand?

        Let us now consider an example from daily life. Suppose you are in a conversation with a friend and in the course of the conversation they seem to stare at a spot behind you. What do you do? Now ask yourself why you did it. And did you know that reason before or after you turned around to follow their gaze?

        Here lies the crucial part of the whole dilemma. Does our understanding come before or after our words and actions? If it comes before, then we know exactly what we are saying or doing, but if it comes after, then we are merely interpretating our own actions to give a hind sighted rationalization.

        To connect this back to Searle's thought experiment, an a priori understanding means a module inside the room that sends out the commands to the symbol wheels, while an a posteriori understanding means a module outside of the room that sees what the room does and then tries to interpret why it did just that.

        This is analogous to the James-Lange theory of emotion. They claimed that our emotions are interpretations of our physiological changes. Our happiness does not cause our blood to flow faster or heart to beat faster, but rather, the interpretation of these two give rise to the emotion of happiness. This would explain why sometimes we feel depressed or happy, but we don't know why and we try to understand why and give it a reason.

        The difference is very important - between a true understanding and a rationalized understanding. The former means we know exactly why we do what we do; the latter means we really don't and can't. The evidence seems to weigh towards the less sobering end of the question, but it is not clear how much we are merely interpretating and how much we direct because we clearly have an understanding of what we are saying and we have a control of what we want to do. But is it an illusion? Is the greatness of human minds born out of a superior interpretative module that tries to make sense of behavior that it has no control over? Is this what we call humanity?
