Making a Case For Universal Standards

In this section, I'm going to argue that standards must exist. And I'm going to do this in a very roundabout way that uses a rule of inference to prove my case. The main backbone of the proof is a contradiction proof.

The general formula of a contradiction proof (from Geometry) is that we start by assuming a proposition. We do not care about the truth or falsehood of the proposition at the beginning. If by following the implications of the initial statement, we reach a contradiction, then it must be false. This kind of proof is used to falsify a statement, thereby proving its opposite. A contradiction cannot be used to verify the initial statement however.

So let us begin with an initial statement. It is that absolute standards do not exist for any value or moral. Theories that ascribe to this statement are either nihilists or relativists. Relativism states that no one culture's or person's values or morals can be better than any other's because there are no real standards.

This second statement is an inferrence from the initial statement, and it would be so regardless of whether we pulled the relativists into this. But by doing so, we will be able to show the absurdity in relativism when we show the falsehood of the initial statement.

So the second statement is that because no standards exist, no one person's values or standards can be better than another's. Now if this were true, then let person A be a relativist, let person B claim that relativism is absolutely wrong and booboo. Now since the relativist must believe that relativism is true for him to be a relativist, he cannot say that person B is wrong. In other words, he is forced to accept person B's judgment. Therefore, he has to accept that relativism is both right and wrong. In fact, given person B and C, they can generate any number of statements where they are all both right and wrong. Moreover, person B and C do not need to use reason to argue. They can claim they think so because they think so. Since there are no standards, their method of reaching an answer must be equally valid as one where reason is used rigorously. They can claim that logic is invalid and relativists could say nothing about that.

And yet not only have we shown that logic is valid, but we have shown in the beginning that something cannot be both X and not-X at the same time. Our initial must therefore be false because we have reached some very serious contradictions. Therefore, relativism must be false as well, because it forces you to accept illogical statements as being correct.

Now, our initial statement was that absolute standards do not exist for anything. If this statement is wrong, then it must be the case that absolute standards exist for somethings. Think about this for a moment. This statement is profound because it raises our certainty even further. Not only can we know things, but there are things out there that are absolutely true and cannot change. This implies that somethings are absolutely right and other absolutely wrong. What these things are exactly, we will all have to find out ourselves through a lot of mental labor.

Categories of Moral Certainty
Theory
Summary
Nihilism Humans create good and evil and thus there is no real foundation for what we call good or evil. Nothing can be better or worse than anything else. Moral truths do not exist.
Skepticism There may or may not be moral truths, but we may never know them.
Relativism Moral truths depend on culture, time and context.
Pluralism There are multiple, though not unlimited, moral truths.
Absolutism There is one fixed set of moral truths that are unchanging through time and context.

Existing Theories of Ethics
Theory
Summary
Egoism What is right is what is in an individual's best long term interest.
Hedonism What is right is what is in an individuals' best short term interest.
Utilitarianism What is right is what can amount to the greatest amount of measurable good for the greatest number of people involved.
The Categorical Imperative Act such that you would be willing to have your actions become a universal law. Do unto others what you would have done unto yourself.

End Chapter Questions

1) Is it necessary for a society to have rules and laws? Is it ethical to force unborn citizens under a set of rules they were never given a chance to vote for or against? What are the limits of democracy?

2) Considering capital punishment: If the criminal was a murderer, then won't the executioner be performing the exact same "crime"? Can killing another healthy human being purposefully be both a moral and immoral at the same time?

3) In judging the severity of a crime, is the motive or consequence more important?

4) Would the world be a better or worse place if everyone could only tell the truth?

5) Evaluate the following statement in the context of community service and charity work in general: " And when someone accuses you of being selfish, just remember that he's only upset because you aren't doing what he selfishly wants you to do." - Harry Browne

6) You are a railroad switch worker, and one day, your loved ones are tied on one side of a track, and the other runs off a cliff. A train is quickly approaching the split. What should Chris do? Would it make a difference whether the train were a passenger train or a livestock train?
