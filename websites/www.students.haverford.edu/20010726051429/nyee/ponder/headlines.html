Headlines
by Nicholas Yee

Student Group Labeled Racist

The administration of a local liberal arts college has been inundated for a week by the phone calls, emails and letters of support and complaint of a newly-founded student group. The student group, known as the White Students League, was founded last week by sophomores Rick Walsh and Andy Peterson.

According to Walsh, the group was founded because he felt that "students at liberal arts colleges are being made to feel that white is not a culture". Peterson added that "just because someone is white doesn't mean he cannot add to the diversity of a college or society". Both felt that the word "diversity" at liberal arts colleges in the US had come to exclude whites, as if being white meant an utter lack of cultural or ethnic background.

The founder of the Black Students League, Steven Morris, has coordinated a protest against the White Students League which he labels as a "racist group that bears an unmistakable resemblance to the Ku Klux Klan". Another member of the Black Students League commented that "it was a form of reverse-reverse-discrimination". One student, whose race she wanted to keep undisclosed, remarked that "to claim reverse-reverse-discrimination is occurring is tantamount to claiming that reverse-discrimination was happening. But hey - what's new?".

Kay Suffield, a senior at the local liberal arts college, commented that "You can get through college here without taking one class on Western history or culture. But somehow, you have to take a course on minority history or literature or some course under the guise of a social justice requirement, which is really the same thing, except in the latter case the professors feel like they have a right to make you feel guilty for something you didn't do yourself. It really makes no sense."

Born to be Lazy

Over the past year, a discovery by two American scientists has caused sweeping changes in the American education system. Gail Flannagan, one of the scientists, explains that "It's not that we've found a laziness gene, but we have isolated a cluster of interacting genes that together moderate motivation in human beings." Paul Moss elaborates that "this cluster of genes influences how likely someone is to set goals and complete projects without procrastinating."

Parents of children diagnosed with Lack of Motivation Disorder (LMD) have petitioned schools to make sweeping changes to accommodate students with LMD. These parental committees are pushing for grade handicaps, untimed testing, and scholarship quotas for students with LMD. Debra Cesara, mother of a child diagnosed with LMD, commented that "schools should not discriminate against children who are born with this handicap. They should try to do everything they can to compensate them." Many school boards across the nation have already adopted policies such as the ones suggested by the parental committees.

Brighton College was the first private college in the nation to adopt LMD policies. Chris Thompson, Provost of Brighton College, commented that they "do not discriminate students on the basis of gender, ethnicity, or sexual orientation, and there is no reason why we should discriminate against students who are born with something they cannot change." Under a new quota system that will take effect during the next admission process, 10% of the slots for the incoming freshmen class will be available only to students with diagnosed LMD.

Students at Brighton College rejoiced at the College's decision to adopt these policies. Ian Donovan, a senior at Brighton College, said that he was "glad to be at a college liberal and open-minded enough to accept individuals with LMD without prejudice." Amy Paslow, another senior, concurred and added that "it is clearly wrong for students with LMD to get lower grades just because they have LMD."

Who's Sweating?

Last semester, the student group Students Against Sweat-Shops (SASS) initiated a campaign to generate letters to be sent to clothing companies operating sweatshops in developing countries. Founder of SASS, Elizabeth Yarning, spoke at a campus-wide broadcast and said that "it is unbelievable that brand name companies are operating sweatshops in these countries. It is a shame to the nation. They must be stopped."

Letters were not only sent to these brand name companies, they were also sent to several factories in Vietnam where SASS claimed the workers "were often women and were working under atrocious conditions." These "letters of sympathy" were sent to the workers in these sweatshops.

Overseas reporter, Vylinh Nguyen, visited these factories to gather the reactions of workers. One worker said that she "thought it was a joke at first. But then when the letters kept coming in, we got really angry and scared." Other workers expressed the same sentiments. Another worker commented that she "was really afraid they would shut the factory down because of the letters. I tried really hard to get a job here. If they shut down the factory, where will I get a job to feed my family?" Her sister asked "Do these Americans want me to become a prostitute to feed my family?"

The factory manager, who wanted to remain anonymous, commented that "Americans exaggerate the working conditions and make up names such as 'sweatshop' so they can campaign to shut these factories. But it's all really to make themselves feel better. These American liberal arts college students really enjoy fantasies of 'saving' less fortunate people."

Nguyen asked local workers what they would like the students group SASS to know. Most workers replied, "Please stop writing letters. We need this job. If the working conditions were that bad, would we be here in the first place?" One worker calmly remarked, "I'm not sweating. Are you?"

Color-Blind Artists Liberated

Founder of the Color-Blind Artists Coalition (CBAC), Peter Veil, spoke yesterday at a press conference after the National Endowment for the Arts claimed they would fund a 2 million dollar initiative into developing arts programs for children who are color-blind. Veil commented that this program is "one great leap in liberating color-blind people from the prejudices they have had to endure since the creation of art".

CBAC vice president, Michael Stokes, argued that "color-blindness is a genetic trait like race or gender. It is absurd, and indeed backwards, for self-proclaimed modernized nations such as the United States to discriminate against those who are born color-blind. We advocate a society that treats men and women equally. We advocate a society where whites and blacks are equal. But where is the society where color-blind artists can compete with their non-color-blind colleagues?"

As evidence for the discrimination, Veil points to statistics showing that color-blind people are under-represented in the artistic world. At the press conference, he challenged people to name even one color-blind artist. He was met with silence. To this, he shouted out "And you think women and blacks are prejudiced against?"

Beginning next May, programs across the nation will be funded through the NEA grant and directed specifically to people with diagnosed color-blindness. Color-blind students in middle-schools and high-schools will be allowed to participate in high-tech classes designed to "unleash" their "stunted creativity". Stokes said that they were trying to "train first-class color-blind artists". When asked whether children with normal vision would be allowed to take part in these classes, Veil replied "That would be like giving money to the rich. Only greedy bastards would take the money."

Opticians around the nation have also reported a significant increase in color-vision testing. New tests had to be developed to counter parents coaching their children to incorrectly identify what they saw in the traditional tests. Wendy Tetherstone, an optician in Boston, commented that she had "never seen parents coaching their children to be color-blind".
