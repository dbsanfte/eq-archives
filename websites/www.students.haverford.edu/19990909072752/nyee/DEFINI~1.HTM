What Does It Mean To Define Something?

To define something is to limit what it can be, to make it clear what the word means, and to show how it is different from certain other closely related words

Well, how does one do this. One method you can use is to start with everything and chip away what the word is not. When you take away everything a word is not, what is left must be part of the word you are trying to define. (1)

The failure to define words properly can lead to logical fallacies in argument. A fallacy is a mistake in logic. Here are examples of Fallacies that come from definition problems.

Fallacy Name
Definition
Example
Simple Tautology

(Circular Definition)

Using the word you are trying to define in its definition.

An animal is human if and only if it has human parents.

Conflicting Conditions

The definition contradicts itself.

A society if free if and only if liberty is maximized and people are required to take responsibility for their actions. How can you maximize liberty by requiring people to do things?

Equivocation

The same word is used with two different meanings

Criminal actions are illegal, and all murder trials are criminal actions, thus all murder trials are illegal

The Ship of Theseus Paradox

A shipmaker in Ancient Greece builds a ship and names it the "Ship of Theseus". He then takes the ship on its maiden voyage around the world. When he returns to Greece many years later, the ship has become deteriorated, and so he decides to renew it. However, he does not have enough money at the moment to make a new ship or replace all the parts, and so he decides to use what he has and renews the hull. The following year, he renews the masts, and so on until every part of his ship is now made of newer parts. But he is a little eccentric and he kept all the old parts in his warehouse. He now puts all those parts together and builds a ship following the original blueprint. The question is: Which ship is the 'Ship of Theseus"? (2)

Listing the Alternatives

There are 4 possible answers to the question, and each has difficult questions it has to answer.
Answer
Questions
Old Ship

Did the ship exist before the old ship was rebuilt? Did the old ship ever cease to be as it was being taken apart? Can gaps occur between existences? Can an object exist multiple times?
New Ship

When did the identity transfer from the old ship to the new ship? What percentage of the ship had been changed before this was so? Why this percentage?

Can one ship turn into a fleet? Can two identical things exist at the same time?

Can something disappear forever? When does the ship become nothing? Can something turn into nothing?

Untangling the Paradox

Before we untangle the paradox, let us define what a paradox is. A paradox is a story that starts off logically, proceeds logical, but ends up with a conclusion or question that seems absurd or illogical. Now let us try to answer the paradox by defining key terms that were allowed to remain vague throughout the story.

The vagueness of the paradox stems from two different kinds of names. We use a collective name in everyday speech to label things that are alike. Symbolically, they belong to a category that has limits. An example of this is "chair". There is not just one chair, but many objects that can qualify as chairs. Whether they do depends on if they fit the definitions and limits of the category "chair". Another kind of name we use is a proper name. Proper names are given to specific objects within a collective name category. Proper names are a layer above collective names. For example, "human" is a collective name, but "Adam" is a proper name that is being applied to a specific "human". The reason why people argue in circles is that they are using both definitions of name at the same time.

The most significant difference between the two is that when one labels an object with a collective name, we are comparing it to an ideal of what the category is. We all have a concept of "chairness" and when something looks close enough to it, it is labeled a chair. This allows for one object to change categories, which is convenient, because they do in real life. When does a clay ball on a potter's wheel become a pot? Conceptually, it is when there is more "potness" than "clayness" in the object. Where the line is drawn exactly is less important because in this model we are accepting a gray area. We accept that there is more than just black or white, and we are not worried when one object is halfway between two categories at the same time. (3)

When we use a proper name however, the situation is different. Proper names do not have categories and we do not allow two objects to occupy the same exact proper name. Does this mean that there cannot be two Adams at the same time. No, I mean that even though the word is the same, the conceptual proper name is different. A proper name is a collection of that object's past history and experience. When we know one Adam, in our mind he is "Adam .. with black hair, cheerful attitude etc.". When we know a different Adam, in our mind he is "Adam .. who is short and sort of plumpy etc.". Even though we label both Adam, we are quite aware that they are not the same person because of these mental tags which come from our experience of the object. Notice that collective names do not depend on past history. A pot is a pot regardless if the potter shaped it into a vase, bowl or urn before hand. Secondly, notice that a proper name can remain with an object even after its collective name has changed. If I name a piece of clay "Bungo", it is still "Bungo" when I shape it into a teddy bear, and when I shape it into a bowl (I don't know why I would name a piece of clay "Bungo" though). (4)

Consider now a time machine and we brought Adam from three years ago to the present and placed him side by side with the present Adam. What then? Are there now two identical Adams? Certainly not. We have in front of us the present Adam, and an Adam from three years ago. Time and age are a function of past history and experience. The two Adam's should have different mental tags. This is common sense though and we can see this when we look at photographs of ourselves: "Here is me when I was 2 days old, then here when I was 5 years old etc.", but the main point is that there is no contradiction when there are so many "me's" in front of me in these photographs. Time resolves any problems. (5)

If we chop Adam into two equal halves with a big cleaver (philosophy is not for the faint-hearted), what happens? They are certainly not both Adam, because neither half has the properties that our mental tags ascribed to the proper name. It should be intuitive that what we have before us are two halves of Adam. Can Adam ever disappear completely? No, because there are pieces of Adam flying everywhere (his skin flakes, his hair, his breath) that become other things and people. When we name something, we are naming all of it, even those parts that are constantly moving in and out. A piece of Adam's hair that fell off two weeks ago is not Adam, it is a piece of Adam's hair that fell off two week's ago. It is in such cases that mental tags and history are most important. (6)

What if Adam fell apart piece by piece until there was nothing concrete left? Then the physical core of Adam has disappeared, but we know that pieces of Adam are flying about in the world. There is no more Adam as a whole though. We judge present proper named objects by the mental tags they used to carry from the last moment. We account for changes, alter mental tags if necessary and then move on.

Let us go the actual ship problem now. (7)

The Old Ship is built and we name it the Ship of Theseus. This name we use is naturally a proper name, not a collective name. When we replace a part, it is naturally the changed ship that becomes the Ship of Theseus, the way that when Adam eats, digests, and becomes his food he is still Adam. We accept constant change. Also, we should be comparing an object from moment to moment, not from now to 2 years ago, because of our mental tags that incorporate past history. Thus the changing ship and changing Adam inherit their names. When every part of the old ship is replaced with new parts, the complete ship is naturally still the Ship of Theseus, because it is not as much that everything has changed but that continuity of identity has guaranteed that very little has changed. Remember we are judging the identity from moment to moment, not from now to when the Ship was just built. (8)

What about the ship built from all the old parts? Well, it is obviously a ship made of parts of what used to be the Ship of Theseus, but it is of itself not the Ship of Theseus. The real Ship of Theseus is the ship that has carried on through time from the beginning. Thus now we have the present Ship of Theseus which has gone through periods of renewal, and a ship that is made of variously aged parts of what used to be the Ship of Theseus. (9)
