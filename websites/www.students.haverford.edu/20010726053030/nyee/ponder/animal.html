Do Animals Deserve Rights?
by Nicholas Yee


        The controversy surrounding animal rights is a dense and almost impenetrable one because arguments are often so entangled with subjective emotions that it is hard to tell where emotions begin and where reason ends. It is hard to answer such questions as "How can you justify torture as research?" because it is laden with assumptions. Catch-phrases such as "animal abuse and wholesale slaughter" are capable of ending discussions altogether not because of their logical force but because of the weight of their emotional implications. Even the seemingly more rational arguments for animal rights are often founded on unspoken subjective assumptions. Starting with a close examination of Peter Singer's arguments and then a careful construction of my own, I will try to demonstrate that animals do not deserve rights.

        Peter Singer is well-known for his book Animal Liberation where he argues for the need to bring animals into the moral calculus because they have the capacity to feel pain and pleasure. His main argument is founded on the concept of equality. According to Singer, "the basic principle of equality does not require equal or identical treatment; it requires equal consideration. Equal consideration for different beings may lead to different treatments and different rights." He then points out that racism and sexism are wrong because they deny this equality. And yet to use the measure of intelligence or language as the admission ticket to this equality community is an arbitrary one, and Singer asks: "If possessing a higher degree of intelligence does not entitle one human to use another for his or her own ends, how can it entitle humans to exploit nonhumans for the same purpose?" For Singer, the yardstick for admission is the experience of suffering - "If a being suffers, there can be no moral justification for refusing to take that suffering into consideration."

        To shed light on one of the problems in Singer's argument, let me rephrase a question of his: If possessing a higher degree of intelligence does not entitle one human to use another for his or her own ends, how can it entitle humans to exploit plants for the same purpose? There are plants and animals, such as oaks and oysters, that have no pain perception mechanism. Does this mean that they can be massacred without reason? In fact, most living beings would not be included in this equality community because most of the beings alive today are bacteria and single-celled photosynthetic organisms. This shows that "the ability to suffer" is just as arbitrary a line to draw as the ability to reason or to communicate.

        Furthermore, evolution has guaranteed that for every organism, there is another organism almost like it. There is a chain of organisms that connects animals to protozoa to plants. To be truly fair, shouldn't the line be drawn at "the living"? The logical conclusion of Singer's suffering argument is that all living beings would clearly suffer if they died. Thus all living things deserve equal consideration. There is only one thing the normal human individual can do to preserve as many lives, and thus prevent as much suffering as possible, and that is to kill himself. This is because, in his lifetime, a normal human "slaughters" billions of bacteria and micro-organisms. Singer claims that animals are the same as human beings, but he refuses to see that the chain of similarity does not end there but extends to the insects, micro-organisms and plants. He accuses others of drawing arbitrary lines, but he is guilty of doing so himself.

        Singer wants to claim that all suffering must be taken into equal consideration, and thus the suffering of a crippled ant deserves equal consideration as that of a crippled human child, and if we could only save one, Singer has to say that we should flip a coin and decide or else we would be speciests. The major flaw in Singer's argument is that not only has he chosen the arbitrary measure of suffering, but he assumes that all suffering is equal. He wants us to believe that the suffering of a human child is comparable to that of an ant's. This is clearly absurd. The suffering that a Holocaust victim endures is more intense than that an ant will ever endure because human beings have the cognitive and emotional capacity that other animals do not have. Shoot a son in front of his mother and the mother feels anguish beyond imagination that will scar her for as long as she lives. On the other end, when male langurs kill the infants of captured female langurs, the female langurs seem to become attracted to the male langur and promptly resume estrus. Singer is trying to avoid pulling intelligence and human cognition into the equation, but he fails to see that it is impossible not to. It is human intelligence that can inflict and understand the kind of overwhelming agony that is part of human existence. It is also human intelligence that allows us to bestow and understand the kind of awe-inspiring joys that are also part of it.

        I want to take this point one step further. To feel physical pain is not necessarily the same as suffering. For example, we know that masochists take pleasure in pain, and when a child takes a vaccine shot, we do not say that the child is suffering. We also know that the mother suffers even though it is his son that died and no physical pain is done to her. The most important aspect of suffering is the mental one, not the physical one. To suffer requires cognitive and emotional capacities that animals do not have. I contend that all, if not most, animals do not suffer because they do not have the necessary mental components to process mental pain into suffering. They merely feel pain. And the distinction is necessary because the two words have very different connotations. Suffering is unique to beings that have the mental capacity to understand pain beyond the physically inflicted ones.

        Animals do not suffer beyond what we claim them to suffer. We are merely personifying animals, giving them qualities that they do not have. Rephrasing Shakespeare: There is no animal suffering, but thinking makes it so. Singer's arguments are not sound because they do the same things that Singer claim to be wrong: drawing arbitrary lines. He also fails to realize that there is more than one kind of suffering. Even if his equality community based on suffering is tenable, we would still have reason to treat animals differently from human beings because we can suffer and derive joy under much more circumstances and to greater degrees than they can. Singer claims that speciests violate the principle of equality because they "allow the interests of their own species to override the greater interests of members of other species." I would claim that Singer himself is a speciest because he is allowing the lesser interests of other species to override the greater interests of man.
 
        He also argues that the concept of rights is nonsense because "the real weight of the moral argument does not rest on the assertion of the existence of rights, for this in turn has to be justified on the basis of the possibilities of suffering and happiness." He claims that to argue that animals do not have rights is thus "misguided". After our discussion of the different kinds of suffering and happiness that humans and animals experience, I feel that a venture into whether animals have rights is not misguided but necessary.

        Even Singer himself recognizes that "the principle of the equality of human beings is not a description of an alleged actual equality among humans; it is a prescription of how we should treat human beings." Equality and rights are not intrinsic; they are bestowed. Rights are the artificial constructs defining how different parties agree to treat each other. Beings that have not developed enough to fully understand rights do not have access to their full set of rights. This is why parents have jurisdiction over their children until they become independent. Organisms that do not have the lifetime potential to understand rights, however, do not deserve to be given rights, because to do so would jeopardize ours. You cannot reason with a leopard to not attack you if it's hungry. To give the leopard rights would be absurd because it will not respect yours. Rights are a responsibility that the individual holds to the community and other holders of rights. Animals that do not have the capacity to understand rights or responsibilities cannot be given rights or that would destroy the whole concept of rights altogether. Rights are contracts held between rational and accountable parties, and animals do not deserve to be in our moral community of rights because we cannot punish them for violating our rights - the same way it is absurd to punish a new-born infant for breaking a vase accidentally.

        The crucial reason why animals do not deserve rights is not because they cannot reciprocate them, but because the rights that we give them must be taken from ones that humans have. There is not an infinite pool of rights to be given. Rights are balanced between parties involved. To give rights to individuals who do not deserve rights means taking them away from people who deserve them. The right of the whale to live means taking away the right of the Eskimo tribes to hunt them. Thus the main reason why animals should not have rights is not because they do not deserve them, but because they infringe upon our rights.
 
        Humans have a right to luxury, provided they gain it rightfully through work, that animals do not have because we have the capacity to enjoy more of life than they can. For example, one of Leonardo Da Vinci's paintings or Mozart's arias will mean nothing to a pig or falcon, but they might be very important to a human individual. Because of our mental capacity, we can experience pleasures far above that which an animal can. And animal rights activists must recognize this much because it would be absurd to argue that pigs deserve to be housed in fully urbanized apartments just as humans do. It would also be absurd to argue that a human child should live the way pigs do on a farm since it would keep him alive and well-fed. Human existence is much more than just survival. Our cognitive abilities have opened a new world of higher pleasures to us and we have a right to them.

        Does this justify the unnecessary suffering that animals go through so that we can have them on our plates? - the animal rights activist will ask. We've seen that suffering is not the right word to describe it, and I will argue that evolution has guaranteed that life be full of necessary suffering. Life feeds on itself to survive. Should we save the gazelle from the hungry African lion? Should we save every gazelle from every hungry lion? But for something to continue living, something else has to die. This is a fact of life and to claim that it is wrong is naïve.

        Do vegetarians think that nothing dies to feed them? What of the millions of "innocent" green lives that are "harvested mercilessly"? If omnivores slaughter animals, then vegetarians massacre plants. The flaw in both is attributing human qualities and connotations to non-human organisms. The right to luxury, the circle of life, the conclusion that animals do not deserve rights, and the wisdom of a balanced diet are sound arguments for consuming meat.

        Vegans and vegetarians often try to confront meat-eaters with images of animals being killed in slaughterhouses. They argue that if a meat-eater is not willing to go and kill a cow for his steak, then his buying it at the supermarket is immoral. However, the same argument can be used in human surgery. If a patient is unwilling to operate on another to remove their appendix, then it is immoral for this patient to ask the doctor to perform the same operation on him. Repugnance and disgust are not valid moral arguments. Maggots feasting on a human corpse is disgusting, but are the maggots immoral? Thus, this confrontational question that animal rights activists pose has nothing to do with the morality of the dilemma.

        Does this mean we can slaughter animals without reason? I contend that we should not for the same reason we should refrain an infant or an insane patient from swallowing their own waste. We do it because it is revolting and causes us unnecessary discomfort. We should prevent inhumane or unnecessary ill-treatment of animals not because they will suffer, but because we will. If swallowing one's waste is not morally wrong, why do we feel impelled to prevent it? For the same reason why we should prevent inhuman treatment of animals even though there is no moral reason why we should do so. Disgust is our natural emotion to ward away thoughts of death and there is no reason why we should immerse ourselves in unnecessarily disgusting situations if we can prevent them.

        A thought experiment may drive this point home. Imagine a computer program that generated a virtual dog in a virtual home where the computer user can interact in realistic ways with the virtual pet, including slaughtering it with a chainsaw. Is it immoral to kill the virtual dog with a chainsaw? No, then why do we feel impelled to prevent it from happening? Inhumane treatment of animals should be prevented not for their sake, but for ours, because we find it difficult to not attribute our agony, anguish, and despair onto them and this in turn causes us discomfort. We have to ask ourselves whether suffering is in the eye of the beholder with regards to animals and plants.

        The major flaws in arguments supporting animal rights is that their loud objective statements are based on subjective assumptions. For example, Tom Regan proposes that animals have rights because of inherent values. He asks us to "consider that you and I, for example, do have value as individuals - what we'll call inherent value.", but he is silent as to what this value is, where it comes from, and why we have it in the first place. Peter Singer falls in the same trap with his equality and suffering that he does not carry to their logical conclusions.

        We hear slogans such as "All animals are equal", "All living things have rights", "Every living thing is inherently valuable". And yet we never hear it explained what makes all animals equal? - What are rights? - Why do all living things have rights? - What makes all living things inherently valuable? Some have proposed rights as the concept that will create equality, but they fail to see that there are many gradations of rights and that there is such a thing as partial rights. They feel that anything that has rights must be equal, but this is not true. Others have proposed souls, and argue that because animals and unborn fetuses have souls, it is wrong to claim the lives of either. They fail to see that if such an abstract concept as soul can be proposed, that it is equally easy to propose "soul races", "soul ages", and "soul inferiority". Proposing such abstract, ungrounded concepts create more problems than they can solve.

        In the end, what they are trying to achieve is to give animals something they do not have. Singer tries to make animals capable of human suffering. Regan tries to claim that animals and humans are equally valuable. But concepts such as suffering and value are human concepts. Agony, anguish, despair, joy, achievement and inspiration are human emotions and they are used to describe animals by animal rights activists. They claim that those of us who fail to grant animals equal moral status are anthropocentric because we are singling ourselves from our equals. I claim that animal rights activists are anthropocentric because they are trying to ascribe human concepts and emotions to animals that do not have them. Animals remain animals until we try to make them human, and this anthropocentric mistake is where the controversy of animal rights truly begins.
