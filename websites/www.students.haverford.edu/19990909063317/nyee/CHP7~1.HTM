Through the Eyes of a Thought Experiment

Before we leave Ethics altogether, we are going to now focus on a specific topic under ethical controversy - abortion. We will deal with this subject by using what are known as thought experiments, and we will not deal with abortion as directly as people do in normal arguments. A thought experiment is a hypothetical analogy or story that has the essense of the elements under debate but sheds new light on certain aspects of the debate.

A well-known example of a thought experiment is Newton's "proof" that all masses with different weights fall just as quickly when air resistance is removed. Newton asked his friends to consider what would happen if a 5 pound weight were tied to a 10 pound weight and dropped together. Now the prevalent theory at that time was that the speed at which masses fall depended on their weight. Using this theory, two answers are possible. One could argue that the two weighs would fall faster than either would alone because they are tied together. But one could argue equally as well that because they are tied together, the lighter mass will slow down the speed of the larger mass and the total speed should be somewhere in between. Newton showed that because of this contradiction, it cannot be that falling speeds depended on the object's weights, and thus it must be that all objects fall with the same speed.

I am now going to present three thought experiments for abortion. Each focuses on a very different aspect of the debate and each is provocative in itself. None of the three is as strong or as conclusive as Newton's but each allows us to think of the debate in a different light. The following thought experiment has come to be known as the Unconscious Violin Player.

But now let me ask you to imagine this. You wake up in the morning and find yourself back to back in bed with an unconscious violinist. A famous unconscious violinist. He has been found to have a fatal kidney ailment, and the Society of Music Lovers has canvassed all the available medical records and found that you alone have the right blood type to help. They have therefore kidnapped you, and last night the violinist's circulatory system was plugged into yours, so that your kidneys can be used to extract poisons from his blood as well as your own. The director of the hospital now tells you, "Look, we're sorry the Society of Music Lovers did this to you-we would never have permitted it if we had known. But still, they did it, and the violinist now is plugged into you. To unplug you would be to kill him. But never mind, it's only for nine months. By then he will have recovered from his ailment, and can safely be unplugged from you." Is it morally incumbent on you to accede to this situation?

Thomson, Judith J., (1971). "A Defense of Abortion," _Philosophy and Public Affairs_, I, 1, 47-66.

From the title of Thomson's book, it should be obvious what her take on the issue is. Nevertheless, her thought experiment is a sound one. When reading through though experiments, you should focus on several things:

1) What elements are being highlighted in the story? Are the elements being represented accurately?

2) What is the underlying point? Which direction is the author leaning towards?

Let us consider Thomson's story more. Is her representation of pregnancy fair? What crucial part of pregnancy is Thomson leaving out? Do people get pregnant suddenly regardless of what they've been doing? Why does Thomson choose a famous violin player? Is this a fair comparison to a fetus? When you have a child, do you automatically know what they will be in the future? What is the difference between potential and certainty here? And certainly there is no conspiracy theory involved in normal pregnancy.

Let us turn to our next thought experiment. This one is from Paulos book, A Mathematician reads the Newspaper. Imagine if the world were suddenly hit with a high amount of radiation and all women were very likely to have 50 fetuses when they got pregnant. Now we have the medical technology to allow all fetuses to come to term, but without any medical attention, all fetuses and the mother would die.

The weight of the argument focuses on two things: 1) the rapid and unsustainable population explosion if all babies were allowed to come to term, 2) the need of intervention. It also forces us to weigh the value of a potential life against that of the risk of overpopulation and the starvation that must follow. If we will not have enough food to feed a child anyway, why should we let a baby come to term? Americans may argue that this is not the case in their country, but if the world were condensed to a village of 100 people (keeping all ratios the same), 50 people in the village would be suffering from malnutrition. Starvation and malnutrition are very real problems. Certainly, when we choose between people who have realized their potentials and those who have yet to, we should choose the former if we could only keep one alive.

If we would shrink the Earth's population to a village of 100 people, it would look like this:

- 57 Asians, 21 Europeans, 14 from the Western Hemisphere (north and south), and 8 Africans.

- 70 would be non-white; 30 white

- 70 would be non-Christian; 30 Christian

- 70 would be able to read

- 50 would suffer from malnutrition

- 80 would live in sub-standard housing.

- Only 1 would have a college education.

- 50% of the entire world wealth would be in the hands of only 6 people. All 6 would be citizens of the U.S.

Now let us consider one final thought experiment. This one is more elaborate and dramatic than the previous ones.

Imagine that a woman in coma is raped while in coma. She regains consciousness the day she gives birth to a child. The doctors most certainly knew she was pregnant, but they were in no position to abort the child without the mother's consent. When the woman bears the child, she realizes that the child cannot be hers. Furthermore, the child is severely deformed and will never be able to walk properly or have an IQ of higher then 60. She also recognizes that she cannot take care of the child because it would constantly remind her of being raped. After talking with the doctors, she concludes that if the child lives, it would live in pain and suffering even in adoption, and asks the doctors to kill the child. Should they?

The thought experiment is directed at pro-choicers. It questions why infanticide is illegal when abortion is legal. If pro-choicers believe in the rights of the mother, then certainly her rights remain when the child is born. Mothers abort their fetuses because they are undesirable. Why can't mothers decide to kill their undesirable children (humanely of course)? Is there some magical divide between a child one moment in and out of the womb? What real difference is there? Is one dead and the other alive? Having a soul won't help you here either (Occam's Razor). Furthermore, if it DNA testing of fetuses becomes legal and parents want to abort deformed infants they will be allowed to. Is there really a difference between this scenario and killing a deformed child once it's born if we didn't know before hand. This thought experiment forces us to recognize that a mother's rights must have limits somewhere or even murder can be easily justified.

End Chapter Questions

Here are three thought experiments unrelated to abortion:

1) To see how crucial this excess baggage of ours is, imagine that musicologists unearthed a heretofore unknown Bach cantata, definitely by the great man, but hidden in a desk and probably never yet heard even by the composer himself. Everyone would be aching to hear it, to experience for the first time the "qualia" that the Leipzigers would have known, had they only heard it, but this turns out to be impossible, for the main theme of the cantata, by an ugly coincidence, is the first seven notes of "Rudolph the Red-nosed Reindeer"! We who are burdened with that tune would never be able to hear Bach's version as he intended it or as the Leipzigers would have received it.

Dennett, Daniel C., (1991). Consciousness Explained, Boston: Little Brown, p. 388.

2) Since some of you may be a little weary of Putnam's Doppelganger on Twin Earth, let me tell my own science fiction story... Suppose lightning strikes a dead tree in a swamp; I am standing nearby. My body is reduced to its elements, while entirely by coincidence (and out of different molecules) the tree is turned into my physical replica. My replica, the Swampman, moves exactly as I did; according to its nature it departs the swamp, encounters and seems to recognize my friends, and appears to return their greetings in English. It moves into my house and seems to write articles on radical interpretation. No one can tell the difference.

Davidson, Donald, (1986). "Knowing One's Own Mind," Proceedings and Addresses of the American Philosophical Association, 60 (3), p.443.

3) Suppose that I'm locked in a room and given a large batch of Chinese writing. Suppose furthermore (as is indeed the case) that I know no Chinese, either written or spoken, and that I'm not even confident that I could recognize Chinese writing as Chinese writing distinct from, say, Japanese writing or meaningless squiggles. To me, Chinese writing is just so many meaningless squiggles. Now suppose further that after this first batch of Chinese writing I am given a second batch of Chinese script together with a set of rules for correlating the second batch with the first batch. The rules are in English, and I understand these rules as well as any other native speaker of English. They enable me to correlate one set of formal symbols with another set of formal symbols, and all that "formal" means here is that I can identify the symbols entirely by their shapes. Now suppose also that I am given a third batch of Chinese symbols together with some instructions, again in English, that enable me to correlate elements of this third batch with the first two batches, and these rules instruct me how to give back certain Chinese symbols with certain sorts of shapes in response to certain sorts of shapes given me in the third batch. Unknown to me, the people who are giving me all of these symbols call the first batch a "script," they call the second batch a "story," and they call the third batch "questions." Furthermore, they call the symbols I give them back in response to the third batch "answers to the questions," and the set of rules in English that they gave me, they call the "program." Now just to complicate the story a little, imagine that these people also give me stories in English, which I understand, and they then ask me questions in English about these stories, and I give them back answers in English.

      Suppose also that after a while I get so good at following the instructions for manipulating the Chinese symbols and the programmers get so good at writing the programs that from the external point of view-that is, from the point of view of somebody outside the room in which I am locked-my answers to the questions are absolutely indistinguishable from those of native Chinese speakers. Nobody just looking at my answers can tell that I don't speak a word of Chinese.

Searle, John R., (1980). "Minds, Brains, and Programs," The Behavioral and Brain Sciences, reprinted in Douglas Hofstadter and Daniel Dennett, edd., The Mind's I, New York: Bantam, p. 355.
