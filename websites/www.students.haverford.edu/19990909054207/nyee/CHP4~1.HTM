An External Reality?

There are many important questions in Metaphysics, but in this brief section we will only consider whether an external reality exists. Many people have never asked themselves this question and for them the question is absurd, but for those who have, the thought itself may be quite unsettling.

The question stems from a very basic fact that we will never know how a color really looks like to someone else. It wouldn't do to ask them, because it is obvious that our color names have no real correspondence to the essence of the color itself. We could not know even if we could open their brains up and hook our senses to theirs because as soon as we perceive their color, it is already ours. The same is true not only for colors, but for taste, smell, beauty, love, sadness, and consciousness. The fact is that we can never be sure that anyone else is conscious. (1)

At this point, I cannot help but include a diversion to a story about colors and god. A color-blind person cannot tell the difference between two colors. This implies that he is missing certain color palettes from his color vision. To him, those missing colors are unimaginable because he cannot synthesize them from his existing colors. (2) Let us take a broader view though, and we realize that a concept of a missing color should not trouble only those who are colorblind, but also those that have normal vision. This is because since you can never be sure your color palettes match up perfectly with someone else's, you can never be sure that the two of you have different color palettes and are actually seeing colors the other cannot see, a color they cannot even imagine. The colorblind person can never find his missing colors and neither can we. And if we cannot even begin to even fathom this missing color which we know must exist, how can we even begin to prove the existence of god whose existence we are not sure of.

When I thought of and wrote the pseudo-argument down several years ago, I was arguing for the limits of reason and a need to make a leap of faith. Now, I realize that the argument could be used both ways. You could use it to argue that faith, not reason, connects you to god. Or you could use it to argue that god doesn't exist, and so there's no use looking for one.

The argument itself is naturally not logically sound. The most important flaw is that it implies at the end that because you cannot disprove the existence of god, then such a being must exist. One can show the absurdity in this claim by reversing the argument - because you cannot prove the existence of god, therefore he does not exist. The lack of a proof or disproof has no bearing on the truth of the matter. (3)

The lack of a proof, however, should have bearing on our beliefs though. Given two propositions trying to explain the same phenomena, it is logical to choose the simpler and therefore more economical one. This reasoning is referred to as Occam's Razor. For example, I could choose to explain that when people die, that is the end of the story. Or I could choose to say that some go to heaven, some go to hell, and some limbo, depending on the judgments on a family of omnipotent beings. The strength of the first theory is that it has nothing more to explain. The second, on the other hand, has to explain where and what heaven is, the same for hell, and how the omnipotent beings came to be in the first place. The second theory answers the death question by assuming things that are even harder to explain. The beauty of Occam's Razor is its simplicity. When you can explain something simply, why would you choose to invent superhuman beings? (4)

I assure you that all this is connected to external reality, but before we get back to that, let's conclude this business with god, which is also a big part of metaphysics. Let us apply Occam's Razor now to the dilemma posed by my pseudo-argument. The argument left us with the problem of god, which we could neither prove nor disprove. But the problem is only there because of a assumption that god might exist. People who are agnostic choose to give equal weight to both positions: does exists and does not exist, but Occam's Razor would argue that the "does not exist" theory is simpler and therefore has more weight and validity. By putting equal weight on both sides, one is saying that both are equally simple and economical, where this is clearly not the case. A truly rational person who felt that there was neither a good proof or disproof regarding the existence of god should choose to not believe in a god.

Some of you have undoubtedly heard of Pascal's Wager before, and are at this point slightly perturbed as to what you ought to believe. Pascal's Wager was formulated by the mathematician Pascal. It goes like this: Say a god exists, then you ought to believe in this god, or else you would be sent to Hell. Say no god exists, then you would have only wasted a small amount of time and this saves you from risking your afterlife since we don't know for sure whether god exists or not. This line of reasoning may seem very solid, but it is not. This is because once we allow the existence of a god, it opens the door for all other sorts of supernatural beings who we could not prove. So what, you say. (5)

Here's Nick's Anti-Wager: The being we call god is merely a pawn working for a powerful and rational force in some far-off galaxy. This force is trying to weed out people who are irrational by seeing who would be stupid enough to believe in his god illusion so easily. Those that believe in this illusion, he will send to eternal damnation and he will deliver the rational beings, those who stoically refused to believe in a god, to heaven. So say this force exists, then you should not believe in god or you will be sent to hell. Say this force doesn't exist, then there would be no god or hell, and you would have only wasted a small amount of time. So in the end, it is better to not believe in god. (6)

Now with all this background, we can get back to the issue of an external reality. Let us look at the two sides. If an external reality exists, then it puts us as ephemeral perceivers in a world that is infinite in information. If an external reality does not exist, then we are the makers of this infinite amount of information. Not only that, we ourselves are the creators of all the laws in the universe, and are aware of many of this laws. We are the authors of all the literature we have read, makers of all history, architects of all buildings and so on. More importantly, we are the creators of the universe which would be revolving for us alone, and every other human being is our creation whose desires, beliefs and emotions are created by us. Occam's Razor would certainly ascribe to the first theory. It would take a very conceited person to believe in the second view and claim that they are the only conscious being in the world. (7)

What do we do though when we meet such people who believe that reality exists in their minds, and their minds alone? Well, first of all, I have to say that if you knew two people with such thoughts, it would be interesting to put them together for a while and see what they said to each other. Secondly, if they believe that they control the world, then they certainly wouldn't mind you pushing them off a building. Thirdly, if they control the world, why would other people be arguing with them about their thoughts in the first place? Lastly, if all else fails, tell them that evil exists in the world and the only reason why this would be is because they are evil.

Existing Theories of Metaphysics
Theory
Summary
Materialism Reality is physical "stuff". It is clear-cut and we can understand it through objective methods.
Objective Idealism What is real is what can be thought or understood. Patterns, laws, and mental structures control the physical aspects of the world, ie. mathematics, physical laws, etc.
Subjective Idealism What is real is what I think. And something is real only if I think it is. The most extreme form is known as solipsism.
Pragmatism Reality is what works. What exists is not necessarily real. An chair is a chair if it can work as a chair.
Phenomenology Reality is the interaction of our perception with the external reality, it is the part of the entire reality that we can experience as human beings.
Existentialism Meaning is what defines reality. When you assign meaning to things, they become real. Your life only becomes real when you apply meaning to it.
Realism Something out there determines what is real. It is what it is no matter what I perceive it or think it to be.
Anti-Realism Everyone's reality counts. Everyone has their own reality. We need to acknowledge multiple viewpoints which are equally valid. Reality is multidimensional because it is multifaceted and contrasting theories can be equally true.

End Chapter Questions

1) A big question in Metaphysics that we will not have time to dwell on is whether free will exists. Free will is your ability to control your life and do what you want to do. Are your actions controlled by your genes, parents, society, culture or do they come from your own volition? (We will encounter this question again later in Classical Conditioning under Psychology).

Things to think about: Do a few factors or do many different factors contribute to your behavior and actions? Do you always know why you did what you just did? Do you always have control over your actions? How much influence do your parents and peers have on you?

2) Evalate the following statement in the context of spirits or soul: "A wheel that turns nothing is nothing."

Hint: What does a soul do?

3) When we see another person cry and we think to ourselves that the other person is sad, where is that sadness really coming from? Why can we never be sure that other people are actually sad?

Hint: Do you necessarily need to know how to get to the right answer to answer correctly, especially on multiple choice tests? Distinguish between overt behavior and actual knowledge or emotion.

4) To explain how we are different from animals, some people postulate that we have a soul while animals don't. This invention of a soul is also used to explain why racism is wrong. Why does a concept of "soul" fail to really solve either problem?

Hint: If you can invent the existence of souls, what else would you be able to invent? How about “soul races and cultures"?

5) In many kinds of divination (fortune-telling), a cure or ward can be acruired to ward off something bad that would otherwise happen. Or alternatively, you are told to do something so that the bad thing won’t happen. Usually these wards or actions cost you money that goes into the pockets of the fortune-tellers.

In fact, it is illogical and paradoxical to accept that one can know the future and be able to change it at the same time. Why?

Hint: Think about it in the context of a story. John goes to a fortune-teller who tells him he will die in a car crash tomorrow. So the next day, John is obviously not going to go out of his house (let us assume that the fortune teller is able to know the future). And since he doesn’t go out, he doesn’t get into a car accident. At the end of the day, he calls the fortune-teller and tells her how she saved his life. What is wrong with the logic of all this?
