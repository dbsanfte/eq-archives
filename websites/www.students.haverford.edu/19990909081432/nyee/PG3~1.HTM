Introduction to Philosophy

What is Philosophy?

Philosophy is a Greek word that means a "love of wisdom". It is a pursuit of wisdom and meaning.

There are many situations in our world where questions can arise:

Should we follow laws that are imposed on us? Does anyone have the right to decide what is right and what is wrong? What is justice? Why are we here? Does our existence depend on a god? Why do pain, sorrow, jealousy, misery and grief exist? Can science tell us anything about the real world? Is there life after death?

Philosophy begins with questions about the world around us, but it does not end there. It allows us to find answers to these questions for ourselves, to depend on our own cognitive abilities to understand the world around us and not answers given to us by authorities. Philosophy examines the beliefs we have held onto as children and asks why we hold onto them and whether we should continue to hold onto them.

The goal of philosophy is autonomy, the freedom to formulate answers to the questions of identity, meaning and life.

Philosophy is not a field that is restricted only to a small subject area. In fact,, philosophical questions are raised in every subject and occupation area. Here are some examples:

1) Neurophysiology: As a scientist investigates the biological underpinnings of our behaviors, he asks himself whether the "mind" is distinct from the brain?

2) Nuclear Physics: Quantum mechanics tells us that most of an atom is made out of emptiness. Is the world made out of form or emptiness?

3) Behavioral Psychology: If our behaviors are shaped from a continuous process of conditioning, do we have free will?

4) Law: What constitutes an obscene art form?

5) Anthropology: Do different cultures have different boundaries for moral and immoral? Are they equally valid?

6) Linguistics: Is our thought bound to our native language?

7) Government: What makes a group a religious one?

8) Biology: Does a human clone have any rights?

9) Computer Science: Are humans just an elaborate processing system?
