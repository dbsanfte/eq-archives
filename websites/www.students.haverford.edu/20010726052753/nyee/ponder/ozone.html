The Ozone Scare
by Nicholas Yee


Environmentalists have threatened us with an ozone depletion catastrophe scenario for quite some years now, and there have been skeptics who have questioned whether reality has been exaggerated in an attempt to mislead the general public. In our age of information technology, we no longer have to rely on the media for truth, not especially when we can find the data ourselves.


Preliminary searches on the net produced the oft-reproduced images of a depleting ozone layer over Antarctica, such as these:





Source: http://see.gsfc.nasa.gov//edu/SEES/strat/class/Chap_11/index.htm


And indeed, the graphical data seems compelling and worrying. However, all these three graphs of varying graphical sophistication seem somehow preoccupied with the month of October. A little more research tells us that a yearly polar vortex concentrates high concentrations of man-made CFC's in the Southern stratosphere due to several meteorological reasons and this causes severe ozone depletion. The peak of this polar vortex is around October. So far, our story seems to confirm the environmentalist's hype.


The hype ends when we look at other months of the year. Raw data was available on a British Antarctic research team's webpage (http://www.nerc-bas.ac.uk/public/icd/jds/ozone/). The data contained monthly averages of ozone values (measured in Dobson Units) from 1957 to 1998 for the months August to April measured from Vernadsky station. Analyzing the data in Statistica, we can produce the following graph for October:


which seems essentially similar to the ones we had before. Now, if we did the same for the month of February however, we get this graph:


where we see that February averages haven't really changed at all. In fact, the averages of 1957 and 1998 are almost identical. Looking at the standard deviation of monthly averages, we see that the Jan-Mar months have exceedingly stable ozone values. This is because CFC's break down ozone into oxygen and UV constantly changes oxygen back to ozone. The more oxygen there is from ozone breakdown, the more ozone will be produced by UV reaction. So, what happens is that the environment balances things out.


Now, is the ozone hole getting bigger? The answer clearly depends on what month you're talking about. In October, yes. In February, no. Now you see how easy it is for environmentalist fanatics to misrepresent the issue and create misleading graphs. Is the ozone depletion situation getting worse? Yes. Is ozone replenishing getting better? Yes. And since the ozone hole is confined to the South pole because of meteorological reasons, will the entire ozone eventually disappear? No.


The situation appears that even though manmade CFC's are destroying more ozone than ever before, more ozone is being created than ever before as well. What about the UV problem? It appears that less UV is getting to the ground than before as well, and this is because of the increased cloud cover due to global warming.


In conclusion, the ozone hole is getting bigger, but only in October, and it is not causing any harm, and it will never be severely depleted as a whole. Are environmentalists spewing out more fact or more hype? You have access to the Internet. You decide.
