Negative Information 
by Nicholas Yee

            Information intuitively seems to necessarily be positive in quantity. Zero information is possible from a source. For example, if we knew something ahead of time at 100% certainty then we would gain no information if it occurred. An illustration of this is a two-headed coin. It has to land heads so we gain no information when it inevitably does. But is negative information possible? 

            Something that qualifies as negative information means that it is giving us information, but that information causes us to lose information we already had. This seems totally absurd and only possible in worlds where probabilities of events can exceed 100% certainty. But it is not. 

            Consider a computer hard drive that can store a maximum of 100 megabytes of information. Let us say that it is filled with 4 different 25 megabyte files. Now I have a new 5 megabyte file that I need to put on my hard drive, so I delete one of the old files and copy this new one onto my hard drive. In the end, I lost 20 megabytes of information by gaining new information. 

            Let us transition now to a human mind model of what just happened. The human mind has a finite memory. Each particular memory or piece of information we have takes up our memory space. There must come a point when our memory is almost full. But at the same time, our sensory organs and mind are constantly generating new information and this information has to go somewhere. What inevitably must occur is that old information is systematically erased so that there is room for new information. The difference with the hard drive model is that the human mind probably does not need to delete entire memories at a time but only as much as the new information needs to take up, so the net gain is close to zero.

            This is entirely different from the zero information case however, because we did gain new information that we could not have predicted beforehand, and yet this new piece of information caused us to lose previously stored information. This new information then is actually negative information.
