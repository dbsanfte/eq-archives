Introduction to Philosophy

The Three Main Divisions of Philosophy

Philosophy is traditionally divided into three main fields:

Metaphysics

The field of metaphysics is concerned with the nature of reality. Metaphysical questions ask what is real and what isn't real. This may seem like a naive question, but it brings up many thorny issues. For example, if we can only perceive the world through our mind, how do we know that all reality does not start there? Is reality made of mental constructs or is it made of physical objects? Does an external reality, separate from our consciousness, exist? This last question led several past philosophers to consider the nature of god?

Should dreams be considered to be real? How about hallucinations? Apart from wondering what is real and what isn't, metaphysics also asks us about the true nature of objects. What constitutes a chair? Several years ago, an artist used high power air vents coming from the ground that were strong enough to support a man in a sitting position. He labelled it "Chair". Is this really a chair? (1)

Epistemology

It seems counterintuitive to ask people how they know an apple in front of them actually exists. They would probably find the question absurd. This question, however, turns out to be exceedingly difficult to answer.

Epistemology deals with how we gain knowledge of the world around us, and whether we really know anything at all. Are there things that we know for certain, or is it the case that we cannot be sure of anything at all? Is knowledge something stable and constant, or does it change depending on culture and context?

An important question is whether objective knowledge is possible, by which we mean knowledge which is not biased, subjective or that can be changed easily under different interpretations. (2)

Ethics

After thinking about our sources of knowledge, we can turn to Ethics, which asks us how we know what is right and what is wrong? Is it even possible to know what values or morals are more correct?

Ethics questions why we believe certain things are right, while others are wrong, and whether it is possible that contrary actions can be both right at the same time. Is abortion or capital punishment moral? Like epistemology, it asks whether such things as universal rights exist, or whether such lofty concepts are manmade illusions.

The main question is whether one method of living or choosing is more righteous than another. Should we be selfish, or generous? Should we make rules and follow them, or should we judge according to the situation? Is consequence or motive more important in determining guilt? (3)
