Disguising Dead Animals
by Nicholas Yee

        In English, cows become beef or steak once they have been prepared and served on a plate. In the same way, sheep become mutton, pigs become pork or ham, and deer become venison. This is not the case for all animals. It seems only to apply to large mammals. All fish, fowl, and small mammals keep their original names. Salmon is salmon, chicken stays chicken, and rabbit is still rabbit.

        Why do large mammals need to have different names when they have been cooked? One possible reason is because the larger the animal gets, the more difficult it becomes to eat it without thinking about the carcass and its slaughter. To make it psychologically easier to handle this dilemma, romance languages gave these larger mammals different names when they are served on a plate.

        In all dialects of Chinese however, all animals are referred to by their original names even after they have been cooked. Cow becomes cow-meat, pig becomes pig-meat, and sheep becomes sheep-meat. Why don't the Chinese have alternative names if such a psychological fear exists? One explanation is that Chinese cultures serve meat in a very different way from Western cultures. In most dishes where meat from large mammals is present, it is either shredded (as in rice or noodle dishes) or grounded/minced (as in dumplings or buns). It is often very difficult to tell what meat is being served by merely looking at it. Fish and fowl are often served whole, or in larger, more recognizable pieces as in Western culture.

        Thus, one possible reason why Chinese cultures did not develop alternative labels is because large mammals are prepared in a way that makes it difficult to connect the food to the living animal. Western cultures, on the other hand, usually prepare these larger mammals in fragments significantly larger than in Chinese dishes. They usually come in slabs (such as steak, ham or roast beef), or with bones attached which clearly link it to a once-living animal (such as spare-ribs). In both kinds of cultures however, it seems that fish, fowl and small mammals do not have the qualities that elicit this psychological reaction, and thus do not need to be concealed linguistically or physically.

        Two theories have emerged and both have predictive power. The first is that a psychological reaction, which may be fear or disgust, exists which makes it difficult for us to consume large mammals unless they are disguised physically or linguistically. Fish, fowl, and small mammals do not trigger this reaction.

        We would predict that very few or no cultures have alternative names for fish, fowl or small mammals. We would also predict that if a culture has alternative labels for fish, fowl and small mammals, then it also has alternative labels for large mammals. Thus, there should be no culture or language that has an alternative name for fish or fowl, but not for cows or pigs at the same time.

        The second theory is that one kind of concealment is enough. Thus we would predict that cultures which physically disguise meat from large mammals do not need to disguise it linguistically. And also the other way around. Cultures that linguistically disguise their meat do not need to disguise it physically.

        Eating utensils must come in the picture somewhere, but they are probably not the cause of concealment. This is because the food must be prepared before you can use utensils to eat it. Cultures probably did not invent eating utensils before there was food. Chopsticks cannot slice slabs of meat into smaller pieces, but this is not why Chinese culture shreds and minces meat from large mammals. This would be backwards thinking.
