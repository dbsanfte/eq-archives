There Is No Castle
by Nicholas Yee

        The movie, The Cell, presents one perspective of what another person's mind might appear to us, but the reality of what we know about neurobiology and psychology gives us a very different picture.

        The Cell presents human minds as beautiful visual theatres capable of astounding, seamless tapestries of unrelated memories and ideas. Our thoughts, as we all have experienced, on the other hand, are adamantly verbal. They are adamantly verbal, because it takes considerable effort to mentally construct a visual imagery of anything coherent and realistic.

        More importantly, the visual imagery our minds form is perceived mainly from underlying assumptions and not from what's really there. For example, imagine a guard in armor. Now try to rotate the image 360 degrees. Notice that you can't possibly fill in all the details of the guard as you spin him around, but you can get the big picture right and your mind assumes what the missing gray areas should be.

        Thoughts require a thinker. There are no other images other than the ones you try to conjure up. No reservoir of cinematic wonders exist in your mind hidden somewhere. To someone else, your 3D image of the armored guard would appear crude and unfinished. And this was just one object. Imagine the shabby appearance of your image of a 3D world to anyone else. They would peek around the corner of something and discover a gray slab at best. More often than not, they would simply find a void.

        Furthermore, one thought is seldom coherently linked to the next. And the reason why we change thoughts is unknowable to another person. Not only would they find no fully-colored images, there would also be no sustained image. Most of the time, fragmented sentences flicker by, leaving their importance to be guessed.

        A foreign mind is unable to decipher importance because the network of relationships of every person's mind is different. An image that triggers a cascade of memories for me will not trigger those same memories for you. Our minds are built from a network of neurons that have relationships built up from their past experiences. There probably is no correspondence between my concepts and yours at the neural level. Thus, there is no way for you to experience my mind coherently because our neural maps are different. Because you cannot perceive my mind without passing it through your network of relationships, my thoughts would appear to be encrypted. And I alone hold the network key to decode the triggered thoughts.

        Although mind travel might never prove to be very interesting, one consolation is that no one can ever eavesdrop or steal your thoughts and make any sense out of them … in case any of you were on the verge of developing schizophrenia.
