What Time Is It?
Posted on Friday, June 29, 2001 7:15 PM EST by Bailey

It’s really a no-win situation. You make a game, you hype the content way before it’s written in stone, and then fans get all pissy when you can’t deliver their stables of pet dragons and personalized castles with monogrammed drawbridges. Adversely, say nothing until a few months before release, and no one has any idea who the hell you are. There really has to be a happy medium in here somewhere.

It was a good long while ago, when I was still hyped about the Dec 2000 release of AO, when I heard of another little game. Dark Age of Camelot, essentially “another EQ” in premise, with some similar content. 12 races, 3 major sides in PvP, various classes… really nothing drop-dead original or amazing. But after playing the beta in two different phases, this clearly is not EQ. At least, as the saying goes, “EQ without the suck” is more accurate. Better spell effects, better class variation, better PvP, graphics in general, (naturally) and, quite honestly, a better dev team, when it comes to friendly, sociable, open people. That’s not really fair to say though. I heard Milo very rarely ate puppies. Alive.

Still quite similar in concept, unfortunately, as all genre mmogs are. However, in an attempt to bring some unique twists to the norm, everyone tries something different. Horizons dropped trolls and ogres in favor of orcs and goblins. Neocron added naked kinematics technology to enhance our lonely, lonely playing experience. Project Entropia decided to make their in-game credits exchangeable for real-world currency, and baby jebus wept. Atriarch… well, let’s not hit them before they get off the ground.

While not all of these ideas displayed cohesive marketing or even coherent thinking, they were an attempt by each to make themselves unique, to stand out. Mythic, however, broke through this shortsighted mold, with the introduction of a factor hitherto unfound in mmogs.

Kitsch.

Nostalgia is one of the highest selling factors in today’s marketing. Lots of well-off, stupid people want to buy a piece of their lost youth, mostly in the form of hideous décor or a stupid car that is vaguely bastardized off a classic. Reaching farther back into the collective childhood of mankind, Mythic is proud to bring us two pieces of this past, I, personally, would have rather forgot. Forget any preconceptions you might have of what trolls and kobolds are, or should be. Oh please, by the small and merciful gods, wipe those unclean images from your neuroglia. Mythic brings us the new and improved, kitschy swing.



This isn't neccesarily a good thing, mind you, in my opinion. Yours?

Correction on Going Gold
Posted on Saturday, June 23, 2001 11:28 PM EST by Quantum

"Going gold refers to the high quality master CDs that are delivered to the game manufacturing company for replication. These CDs are put in a master machine and their images are read. At that point literally a thousand or more CD burners fire up in parallel and begin writing CDs that have already been printed with the cover art. These CDs, once written, are packaged, stacked, shrink wrapped and prepped for delivery."

No sure who you're quoting here, but a small (but important) point of
clarification:

Large scale duplication of CD's occurs does not occur with cd-burners, but is actually a more physical process. The CDs are actually created using a stamping process, where the plastic substrate is formed then plated.

You can find more info here:

http://www.cds.com/process.htm

Binding Non-Assignable Keys
Posted on Saturday, June 9, 2001 4:45 PM EST by Quantum

Great bit of information sent in by Cambell:

I've found out a way to remap the keys which can't normally be fixed in-game (Keypad 0 for example). I put it up on the official formums at this link but thought you might be interested as its scrolled off the page now hehe.

***********************************

As a lot of people are aware, some keys are fixed and cannot be changed from inside AO. However you can change them manually outside the game by editing the log.cfg file found in the

\Anarchy Online\Prefs\YOUR USERNAME\

folder. You can use notepad to edit this file.

IMPORTANT

BACKUP LOG.CFG TO ANOTHER FOLDER BEFORE YOU MAKE ANY CHANGES!!

Each key is represented by a number. For example Keypad 0 (INS) is 70, Backspace is 25. If you want to find out what number represents a key (one that can be bound ingame that is) bind they key you want to Autoattack. Then go into the log.cfg and look at the number for the following entry:

KEY_BUTTON_ATTACK 25

The important number here is 25 which represents backspace.

To find out the number that represents a key which cannot be bound ingame you need to look for its entry in the list. For example, to find out what the number is of keypad 0 (ins) is I would look for the entry for Autorun (some of these can be a bit obscure!) which is:

KEY_MOVMENT_4EVER_FORWARD_TOGGLE 70

So we now know that Keypad 0 is represented by the number 70.

Now to change the keys. If I swap the values of these two entries, IE:

KEY_BUTTON_ATTACK 70

KEY_MOVMENT_4EVER_FORWARD_TOGGLE 25

Auto attack is now Keypad 0
and
Autorun is now Backspace.

If you check the keybindings in AO itself on the options menu, you will see these changes are also represented there.

Easy! The long bit will be to work out what all the numbers are for each key!

CAUTION!

Make sure you don't bind the same key twice (by using the same value for two entries) as I'm sure this will cause problems. Also be sure to bind all keys - it may not matter but better safe than sorry. I'd also recommend against binding keys such as CTRL, SHIFT, which are used in conjunction with other keys as this may also cause problems. Finally - I've tested this and things seem ok so far, but use at your own risk!!

Anyhows hope this is of help for those that need this kind of information.

*****************************
Hope it makes sense :)

QA
Posted on Friday, June 8, 2001 7:18 PM EST by soy

I've worked for a good amount of time in the Quality Assurance field. I've seen projects crumble because of the lack of QA staffing. When I was 16 I was hired as a QA guy at a company very close to where I lived at the time. Most of the time I took the bus to work, and I enjoyed the job immensly. I was not however, at all proffesional or knowledgable.

I tried to invent my own methods of Quality Assurance, taking advice from co-workers and using the projects we worked on as testing grounds for new methods.

During this time, I figured out a great deal of things about Quality Assurance.

1 ) Developers will not go out of their way to read reports from inexperienced immature Quality Assurance guys.

2 ) The only way to get developers to fix bugs is to tell their managers, their managers managers, and their managers managers managers.

3 ) Orginazation is KEY, if the bugs arent presented to the developers in an orderly fasion, which is fast and reliable, the bugs will not be fixed.

4 ) Regression testing is extremely valuable. (Regression testing means at the end of a span of time, take all previously reported bugs and re-test them.)

5 ) Quality Assurance departments need to know as much about the product as the Programmers themselves. It's a lot easier to isolate areas of a program if you know how the program works.

About 3 months before pretty much half of the company was laid off, a man who previously worked for Symantec (Norton utilities, etc) was hired. By this time 2 other QA guys had been hired (I say QA guys because at the time we didnt know the difference between an analyst and an engineer).

This man was like a shining light of knowledge. In those 3 months our productivity quadrupled. We developed highly advanced methods for testing, tracking, and following through with defect reports. It was then that I realised how poorly the game industry's QA section was doing.

I don't really blame the gaming industry for this.. Quality assurance is a difficult area. I've had people describe it to me as sort of a religion, and I'd completely agree. You will never know how important QA is until you have a working QA department.

A good number of game companies these days were created sort of on a whim. Mostly by friends, friends of friends, etc. This means that the sort of corporate development process was avoided. Sometimes by choice, sometimes by arrogance, and sometimes by both. This definetly needs to change, and I encourage you to help them along.

Quality Assurance departments pay for themselves. The payments come in the form of press relations, customer relations, and reputation. Sure it may be impossible to make a completely bug free product, but you can damn well try.

For developers:

Theres a good number of us floating around right now. Most companies have been taken over by moronic Ebusiness executives who don't know shit about software. The first department they tend to lay off people from is usually Quality Assurance. Some how they think that their product will still be completed without us. You shouldent have a problem finding QA people on job search sites (hotjobs, dice.com, computerjobs.com, etc.)

Also, here are a few books that you can read if you want to train your own QA guys, or if you just want to know what questions to ask applicants:


Customer Oriented Software Quality Assurance
by Frank P. Ginac



Internal Quality Systems Auditing
by Paul F. Lewis



Handbook of Software Quality Assurance
by G. Gordon Schulmeyer (Editor), James I. McManus, G. Gordo Schulmeyer


Reading this books is helpful, but experience in the field is more helpful. Eventually you should be able to give your QA department a product, and they should be able to effieciently write a build report within any specified deadline. Note that the extensiveness of the build report will vary depending on the amount of time they are given.

I hope this helps, if you have any questions on the topic feel free to email me.

The Joys of Camping and the Agony of Questing
Posted on Sunday, June 3, 2001 10:47 AM EST by Kampfschwein

In their main page propaganda, Mythic claims that they have the cure all for two of the more controversial aspects of gaming. Well, the War Pig decided to “test” their salt on these two things yesterday.

In my mind’s eye, there are two forms of camping, the experience based camping (ala the ‘sweet spot’) and the item based camping (ala kill-the-mob-with-ueber-toy) or, as you may think of them, hunting and questing. Now, let me be honest here, I love camping so much I have a sponsorship from Coleman. Hell, my high score in gems would make all you fans of the fatman jealous.

First, the experience based camping. It did not take long to find several that were decent for my level. It was the customary “pull and kill” method. It worked like a charm for a while, but had several drawbacks. If a spot is over-hunted for an extended period, the game will start spawning higher-level mobs. At first, this was cool; several of us just grouped and poured out a bowl of hell on the poor critter. Soon, it got harder, so, we moved on. Therefore, I give Mythic credit for originality (an ueber-quest mob that was at least four levels above me just casually walked by me, scared the hell out of me while I was fighting a social mob that was screaming for back up). Overall, Mythic has not eliminated this type of camping, just made it a little more fun and interesting.

Honestly, I’ve seen questing handled in two ways, first the EQ method, second, the AC method, both, in my opinion sucked. For those of you just joining us, in EQ, you simply settle in line and hope that some high level dress wearing geek did not steal your place in line. In AC, you gave item A to NPC X and run for an hour or so to fight for a grand total of two minutes. Which sucks the most? Well, I do not bother comparing the suck value of things. That is like asking me if I would rather be shot or stabbed. The answer is a resounding “hell no!” Now, the quests that I have worked on for DAoC seem to be equally as bad. All were along the lines of “find obscure bastard NPC in the middle of nowhere, get the quest, and find an equally obscure, mobile mob with a traveling jones and a penchant for grouping with a mass of buddies”. I give Mythic credit for addressing the problem, but they lose a lot of cool points for the way they failed to solved it.

EQ 1.5: First Impressions from the War Hog
Posted on Saturday, June 2, 2001 6:49 PM EST by Kampfschwein

Well, after a month full of more foolishness than an extended stay in the nut house, the War Pig is finally in this damn beta. Therefore, I will skip the formalities and get to the chase.

We know that the longer wait will be something of a disappointment to many of you; this might make the wait more worth it. We've decided to add a few things to the Beta version that we had previously not planned to introduce until later. When the Beta version "knocks on your door", you can expect to see it with the full trade system installed, for example. Not to mention the full range of grouping possibilities. And perhaps most importantly of all, we'll be testing the Player vs. Player system in its entirety, with four new options added to the arena combat rules, including plaver vs. NPC and party vs. party.

So, hang in there. The studio will be putting out quite a few things in those weeks before the Betas, and we promise that the wait won't be a day longer than we need to make sure that the version you get will be the best possible. Thanks for your patience, and we'll be in touch with more news as soon as it comes in.

The Tannhauser Gate team
