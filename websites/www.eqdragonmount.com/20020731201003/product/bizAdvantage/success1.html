KeyWestShrimpHouse.com Carries On Tradition - Online

Scott Koerner's business is steeped in tradition. The building that houses his Madison, Indiana restaurant was a button factory in the late 1800's. Mussel shells dredged from the Ohio River were brought into the factory and punched into buttons. In 1968 it became one of five Key West Shrimp Houses in Indiana. Scott's parents worked at the Shrimp House in Indianapolis, his mother as a waitress, his father as a bartender. In 1974 his father became the manager of the old button factory turned restaurant on the bank of the Ohio River. In 1976, at the age of 13, Scott went to work at the Key West Shrimp House and by 1981 his father had purchased the restaurant.

Scott and his wife now own the only Key West Shrimp House. "We still make some of our items basically the same way they did when they opened (the first restaurant) in 1950," he says. "I started in the business because my dad needed help. I stayed with the business because it's in my blood."

Two years ago Scott thought it might be fun to have a Web site. "I had no idea what an effect it would have on my business," he says. And what an effect! The site has increased sales by 20 percent, according to Scott. "During the summer months almost everyday a customer would tell me that they were here because they saw our Web site."

Scott began developing his site using Microsoft's FrontPage and found that developing a Web site wasn't as hard as he thought. He was attracted to BizLand at first because it supported FrontPage extensions.

KeyWestShrimpHouse.com will celebrate its second birthday this month. "BizLand has been great to me," Scott says. "The Web site has been such a boom for business. The thing I value the most about BizLand is the people. Everyone I talk to cares about me." Scott has taken advantage of many of BizLand's tools: "I love the guestbook, the e-mail list manager, e-mail forwarding, the e-commerce and recently I installed Human Click. Wow!"

Additionally Scott's online marketing prowess has helped make the restaurant a tourist attraction. "Madison is a small town of 10,000," Scott says. "Using the Web site, we are able to catch people's eye before they ever even reach the city limits. I've seen customers who have printed out the whole Web site and used it as a tourist guide."

Now that he's the owner, Scott has started traditions of his own at the restaurant. Customers celebrating birthdays, anniversaries and other special occasions have their pictures posted on the Web site. "They love to go back and see their picture, and they forward the address to friends. We have had parties of 20 people come just so they can have a picture of the birthday boy, in the lobster hat, posted on the Web site."
