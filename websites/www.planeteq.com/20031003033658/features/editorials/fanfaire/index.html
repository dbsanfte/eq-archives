Fan Faire Notes by Kwill

First of all, I want to thank everyone who was on my Live Quest Team for doing such a great job! Our team was Moongazer, Rokq, Jassal, Ommelf, Kwill, Schott, Sweet William, Pakse, Seka and Cenddar. I think we had a lot of fun!

The Art in EverQuest

I attended part of the art panel, which was very interesting, if a little technical! What you might not know about the art in EverQuest is that it is very much dependent on the game engine. The game engine is robust, but does have limitations so the artists have to be aware of that when they design environments and creatures for the game. The art in EQ must respect the engine's limitations so the game is still playable. The art and design teams take into account what it will be like to play in a zone filled with people, and design their creatures accordingly.

EverQuest artists keep in mind that EQ is a role playing game, and not a first person shooter, and so their artwork reflects the mood and tone of the game. The process goes from the design document to the first sketches. The design document lays out what is important in an environment, and the way the characters should look and feel in a particular zone or area. Then the lead artist assigns work to the art team to make the idea of the zone, creature, or artifact come alive. For instance, Dulak was a sleazy pirate port, and the artist that drew the conception of what Dulak should look like took all those things into account when he first drew out the zone. He drew buildings made of driftwood, and floating barrels in the water, and even dead bodies laying around! He wanted to get the feel for what Dulak would look like. Once the concept was drawn, then the actual modeling could begin.

There are two teams of artists: environment artists and character artists. They work on their own, but together with the art director to make a place come alive with a feeling of beauty, horror, or whatever the designers think the zone should be. The types of artwork needed to make a creature or environment come alive are modeling, texture, and animation. All of those jobs are done by different people in the art department, using software specifically designed to make pencil sketches come alive into a place where we can play.

The ideas always being with pencil sketches, which are often more detailed than can be used with the game engine. Then the sketches are fleshed out with color, and the details are finalized after they are scanned into the computer. Once the details and colors are finished, then the model can be made three dimensional, using software that works with polygons to make movement as natural as possible. Again, the game engine puts limitations on the amount of detail on a model, and the type of movement a model can do. Next time you see an NPC in humanoid form, look at the shoulders and how they move. Shoulders, we were told, are one of the hardest things to model and make work correctly with the game dynamics the team has to work with. Seeing how complicated making a sketch into a monster, NPC or PC race is was very impressive! I came away with a new appreciation for all the work the art department has to put in to make the game so immersing and realistic.

EQ2

One of the most well attended panels was the EQ2 panel. Although the game is still under development, we were able to see some of the environment at the panel presentation, and the graphics are quite amazing. Some of the NPCs had been populated in the environment, but not all. The level of detail and realism with the new game engine for EQ2 makes it a true step above graphically from the original EQ.

Some of the highlights of the discussion included:

-System requirements will include a base requirement of DirectX 9. There is an all new graphics engine. They are designing environments to get decent frame rates no matter how many people are in a zone. There is a dedicated pathing engineer working on pathing issues for the game.

- EQ2 is not meant to replace EverQuest. EverQuest will remain more guild oriented, and EQ2 will be geared more toward smaller groups and raids. The maximum raid at this time is 24 people in EQ2, with content for lower levels and groups of six. The idea is to have two games that are similar, but not to take players away from EverQuest to play EverQuest2.

- There will be facial customization but no body customization. Racial archetypes will remain, and artisans can be both good and evil. Race will be a "flavor"; archetypes will be the most important attribute of a character. Religion will also be important, but in a different way than EQ. The religion will be more "new agey" -- most of the gods are not around and have been silent, according to the designers, so this will lend a different feel to religion in EQ2.

-EQ2 will support ways to find solo play as well as ways to find groups. There will be adventures from a starting city for even one or two people.

-Quests will be meaningful and there will be in-game quests than can be completed in a short amount of time by groups.

-There will be no in-game maps at release. Exploration is a key element here, and player made maps will be encouraged.

-Instance play, which has been successfully integrated in Lost Dungeons of Norrath, will be a part of EQ2. Instances will be integrated into the game play. Ships on the oceans in EQ will not just be for transportation -- instant technology will be used and adventuring will be offered on sea-going voyages.

-Weapon decay will be in the game, possibly as part of the death penalty. This was not clearly explained, and we will have to see how this feature ends up.

-Trade skills will be an important part of the game. Trade skill items will include the ability to make artifacts for the player houses.

-Loot distribution will be such that you can't monopolize the economy.

-Player housing will be customizable. Players can purchase floor plans, and customize floors, walls, trim, and more. There will be storage space and the ability to put player made artifacts into a player home.

-There will be no centrally located bazaar. Any merchant can offer wares, sort of like "catalogue shopping."

- Teleport spires will be used to travel around the world. No mounts are planned for release.

Lost Dungeons of Norrath Panel

The LDoN panel was a question and answer session. The LDoN team was open to listening to questions and concerns from the audience. They are working on the bugs that exist in the expansion, of which there are a few, but because the instance technology has worked so well, this kind of thing will be implemented in the future for EQ. Highlights included no change in the level range for players in adventures (too hard for the software to make an equitable adventure for a huge level gap in players); no change in the leaderboard lists (people complained it caused hurt feelings for people who could not get groups if they were not on it -- the team indicated that although it seemed elitist, in fact most people were getting groups as there were more than 100 people in adventures at any given time on a server); no solo adventures are forthcoming; and there will be no choices in adventures, you have to decline and re-request. Why does it take so long to get a new request? Because when you request an adventure, the server actually creates one for you on the spot. When you decline, it has to be destroyed and a new one created. So they put in a time gap so this system could work efficiently.

Live Team Panel

Scott Hartsman, lead Technical Director, was so personable, open to listening to player concerns, and informed, that I was quite impressed. The game has gotten so big and so complicated that it is difficult for any one team member to know all the problems, and so they rely on player reporting of bugs to let them know when things aren't working (/bug). They are working on balancing the melee classes, and they feel this will take care of many individual problems in the game. Casters, you are done for the moment. They also know about pathing and graphics issues, and will be working on fixing some of the strange things that happen because of these (for instance, a shaman in bear form will show up with the hanging lip graphic if they are wearing anything but a leather hat). Now that LDoN has been released, they can concentrate on fixing many of the little things that people have been telling them about. And the funniest moment came when someone asked why helmets never show up. It's not a bug -- the hat designer doesn't like helmets! Scott and the team got a good laugh out of that, joking that it was an issue for a therapist!
