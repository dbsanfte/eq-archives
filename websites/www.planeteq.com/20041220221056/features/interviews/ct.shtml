Crimson Talon: "The Strength of Our Guild is Our People"

Crimson Talon is another "old school" guild on the Cazic Thule server. CT demonstrates how a stable guild can bring good experiences to its members without requiring huge amounts of playing time. The guild has been steadily growing over the years, with many of the original starting players still in the guild, and with the same guild leader that originally founded Crimson Talon over three years ago: Altus, a 61 monk. I interviewed him over e-mail in late December 2002.

Kwill: When did Crimson Talon start?

Altus: We started when a group of real life friends who had all been roped into playing decided that we wanted to have a centralized way to chat with one another. Unfortunately, there were not ten of us at the time so we had to solicit help from others to even get the guild formed. Fortunately, the people who we have recruited and our friends who joined us along the way turned out to be very welcome additions. We actually formed around this time of year in 1999. At one point I almost gave up on getting the tag because Valshire, who was our server GM, kept telling me to meet him at various times and places, and then for one reason or another he would not show up. In retrospect it is kind of funny now but it was not then.

Kwill: How long have you been playing?

Altus: I have been playing since July 1999. I had never heard of EverQuest until I happened to chat with Sanglant, now a member of Divine Alliance, at a friend's wedding. He spent a couple of hours telling me how cool the game was. I picked up the game the next day. As a result, I doubt that Sanglant will be on my wife's Christmas card list anytime soon.

Kwill: How is the guild organized?

Altus: Once we started growing as a guild and formalizing some goals, we established a fairly simple philosophy. The purpose of our guild was to increase the opportunities for our members to do things that they otherwise would not be able to do without a disproportionate increase in frustrating BS. Therefore, our organization is a bit on the minimalist side.

In addition to myself, we currently have five active officers: Khallin, Tomwin, Groovit, Brewall and Greymask. Each of us has certain strengths and weaknesses and areas of guild management we tend to gravitate to, but we do not have any specific job titles. Our officers get no special perks in regards to loot priority or any other tangible benefit to compensate them for the administrative functions that they must perform.

The fact is that our guild structure is set up to encourage each person to be a leader, within reason. Any member of our guild can call a raid. Any of our seventy five or so members might be called upon to mediate a dispute between members or to help with the administration of the guild. Our officers define our guild goals and policy very broadly and it is the duty of each member to do what they can to help us meet those goals.


Kwill: What do you think the strengths of CT are?

Altus: It is certainly a cliché, but the strength of our guild is our people. In terms of guild census, we have grown pretty slowly. The reason we have grown slowly is because we have not been willing to mortgage our group identity for the possibility of achieving things a little bit faster. Our people are by and large builders more than they are consumers. Almost every one of our members has received an invitation or multiple invitations to other guilds that are higher on the EQ food chain. We are people who enjoy the journey as much as the destination. We enjoy experiencing new things together for the first time. We share our successes and our failures, and have enough of a shared history to get some enjoyment from what we have built cooperatively.

More importantly, we try to accomplish as much as we can without adversely affecting our real life obligations. By and large, we are made up of people who have somewhat limited playtime. We are adults who have jobs and families and other important obligations. This helps keep this game a game to us.

Our biggest in game strength is probably planning and execution. Proper planning and execution has allowed us to conquer some challenges that others told us we had no chance to conquer.

Kwill: What has the guild done that you are most proud of?

Altus: One thing that I am pretty proud of but which is pretty dated by today's standards of success is the amount of epic quests we have completed. Even the epics that have meant very little to the guild in terms of helping us increase our abilities mean a lot for the people who want them. When epics first came within our reach I promised that we would complete the epic of any person who would put in the work for their epic and who would help others meet their goals. Over 80% of us now have our epics, and the vast majority of people who do not are newer members or people who have taken long breaks from playing. In a personal sense, it makes me very proud that I could keep my word.

Our greatest accomplishments as a guild are united by one common theme, constant advancement. We continue to move forward and face new and greater challenges.

Kwill: What is your take on guild politics on the Cazic Thule server?

Altus: Politics are inevitable when you have groups of people competing for limited resources. Trying to manage a political agenda for my guild is not something that I enjoy, so our policies are a little isolationistic. We try very hard not to be involved in server politics. Frankly, it is pretty easy for us to do because we are not one of the guilds competing for the very bleeding edge of content. Most of the guilds who hunt the same spawns that we hunt are reasonably easy to get along with. The powers that be have made things quite a bit smoother by increasing the amount of available content, thus decreasing the amount of desperate competition that occurs. I may be naive, but things seem to be a lot less rancorous than they used to be.

I do want to stress that we respect the other guilds on the server, and admire their accomplishments.

Kwill: What are your goals for the guild?

Altus: I want to play together with our members as long as we can. I want to have as much fun as we possibly can. I want to defeat every challenge that we have the will and perseverance to defeat.

Kwill: What are your recruitment policies?

Altus: Currently, we have a "needs" recruitment list as well as an open recruitment policy for any class. People can become probationary members of our guild by meeting certain class and level requirements, attending a certain number of raids with us, and securing an appropriate number of sponsors from the ranks of our current members. Probation lasts about thirty days. At the end of thirty days, probation can be extended, or the person can be granted or denied full membership. During the term of probation, a person's sponsors are directly responsible for the conduct of that person. Our level minimums are level 55 on our needed class list and 57 otherwise. I expect both of these numbers to be raised in the near future because our average level is above 60 and I do not believe in recruiting people who are not at least nominally ready to participate in the activities of the guild.

Kwill: How do you distribute loot?

Altus: Someone once derogatorily accused us of taking our loot system from a low level role-playing guild. There is some truth to the statement because we have had our system when we were a low level guild. Since the time that I formalized the loot system, I have fallen out of love with it and subsequently learned to love it again. It is simple and objective in its administration, although likely not fair in every instance.

For most loot, we use a need before greed system. People who could and would use an item on a full time basis get first crack at the item. People who would use it situationally but not all the time get the second shot. After that, the item is fair game for anyone. If there is more than one person who qualifies for the item, they roll for the item. Any droppable item that they replace with the new item is then distributed in the same manner. That way there is a trickle down effect from a single drop. Items won in open rolls may be sold, traded used to twink etc. Some items of particular value may be reserved and distributed by the guild in the same manner at a later time, or sold by the guild for cash.

Quest items are distributed to the person who is closest to completing a quest.

Of course, the dice have no memory, so in theory some people could win everything and some people could never win, even if they were present for every item that dropped. In reality, that does not seem to happen. Over time, everyone seems to get what they need. From the standpoint of maximizing our guild's capability, it would be nice to have one main tank with every good item that we have every gotten that he or she could use. If the person cast in that role ever leaves the game or the guild, that advantage turns immediately into a disadvantage. I guess you could say that our loot system tends to create a bunch of adequately well equipped to modestly well equipped people which works well with our guild.

Kwill: Why do you think guilds break up?

Altus: I think guilds break up for the same reasons that voluntary relationships break up in real life. Sometimes, they blow up spectacularly because of perceived lies, greed or any of a million other slights that one person thinks someone else has done to them. I imagine these problems start small and fester for a while, with nobody being overly concerned about them. Then, some event lights the fuse to the powder keg and all hell breaks loose.

The other main reason is apathy and a lack of a coherent group identity. If people do not all care about their guild, and share the same general vision and values in regards to what they expect from the guild experience and what they are willing to contribute, then at some point being in the guild becomes more of a burden than a blessing. People perceive the guild as moreeffort than it is worth and they leave or it withers on the vine.

Kwill: What is the hardest thing about being a guild leader?

Altus: I am not sure that it is the most difficult thing, but the most frustrating thing is when I have to deal with someone who is unhappy about something that I have done or failed to do and who has not told me about the issue until they are really angry about it. I sometimes feel like I am expected to read minds. That is a talent that, sadly, I lack. Most problems presented to me in this way could be easily solved if people would just talk about them when they were small problems as opposed to waiting until they become big problems. I think we are all guilty of unconsciously assuming that the people we associate with know the things that we know and feel the same way about things that we do while we know rationally this is not true. I wish I could get everyone to communicate with one another more effectively.

Kwill: Where do you think Crimson Talon fits as a guild on the Cazic Thule server?

Altus: In terms or what content we have completed, we have not accomplished the things that the "first tier" guilds on our server have. Some of the things, due to time constraints, we never will accomplish. I sometimes joke that we are about an expansion and a half behind. We are just now settling in to what was end game content before Luclin. I do not know whether that makes us second tier or third tier or 45th tier, and frankly I do not really care. I think we have some very exceptional players, planners and leaders in our guild. We continually meet and overcome new challenges. We raid often, and can usually field a reasonably complete raid force. I have a lot of respect for those people who have done the things we are now doing before us, but I do not feel like our accomplishments are cheapened by the fact that someone did them first.

I imagine some people on our server like us, some people dislike us, and the majority of people are pretty apathetic about us. I do want to say that I appreciated Thornsong's comment in the last one of these interviews that you did when he said that he had respect for our guild. It is gratifying when someone outside of your guild recognizes what you are trying to accomplish. Respect, in some sense, transcends like or dislike.

Kwill: What about EQ addiction? Do you think that's an issue? Do you have any players that you would consider addicted?

Altus: I think that some people do become dependent on EQ. In my mind, some hallmarks of dependence might be consistently playing more than you intend to; spending all your time immersed in an EQ culture, or planning what you are going to do next when you get online; giving up or reducing important occupational, social and other recreational activities; and finally continuing playing the game even when it is not fun anymore. These are similar to some of the clinical diagnostic criteria for substance abuse disorders. When I hear tales of people losing their jobs or having their
spouses leave them over a game then I have to conclude that those people have a problem that goes beyond a mere lack of responsibility or setting priorities. When I hear people bitch, piss and moan about how much they hate the game but I see them online when I log on and they are still online when I log off then they, too, would seem to have a problem to me.

Games like EQ are somewhat insidious because they are like a playground or a nightclub that never closes, where you are likely to see friends at anytime day or night and where no preparation is necessary to attend. You do not have to get dressed up to play. Hell, you can skip brushing your teeth and no one knows unless you tell them. It is really not surprising to me that people prefer that over the rigors and frustrations of real life.

From time to time I have had to talk to people in my guild about taking the game too seriously. That is as close to directly confronting the addiction issue as I have ever come.

Kwill: How would you describe your leadership style, and how do you keep your guild together?

Altus: The guild stays together because we constantly try to talk to one another and make certain that everyone participates in the business of the guild. We have an active and lively message board, and our members are mature enough to realize they will not get their way every time on every issue and not take it as some personal attack.

I try to use whatever techniques I can to get us from where we are now to where we need to go efficiently and intact. I try to explain the reasons for doing what I do so people do not have to guess at those reasons. Sometimes reasoned discourse and praise work the best. Other times, I have to scold or chastise people. I am very direct in my leadership style. Some people have accused me of being a bit of a control freak, and I probably have been from time to time. I try to remember, however, that this is all a game and do not take anything too seriously.

Thank you Altus for giving us a glimpse into Crimson Talon!
