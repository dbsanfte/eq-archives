PlanetEQ | Interview with Chris Cao, Lead Developer for EQII

Game Theory: A Conversation with Chris Cao, Lead Developer for EverQuest II

At the Fan Faire in Las Vegas, 2004, I had a chance to sit down with Chris Cao, one of the two lead developers of EverQuestII, and talk to him a little bit about gaming design and philosophy. This was long before EQII was in beta, let alone released, so no one knew much about it. We were at a Star Trek themed restaurant, and if there is anything that is yet an “undiscovered frontier” it’s the universes that game developers are creating for all of us to play in. EverQuest is five years old now, and the field of massively multiplayer online games is just beginning to blossom. The EverQuestII team is working to be again on the cutting edge of this genre, just as EverQuest was. Chris and I talked about what kind of theories and ideas game developers have to consider to create an engaging and sustainable game world that’s fun to play in, ideas that turned out to be more complex than you might at first imagine them to be.

Chris worked on the live team for EQ before moving to the EQII team as lead developer. He came to gaming after being a science teacher in the public schools, finding that his physics background lent itself naturally to scripting of game code, and his life experience lent itself to designing fun, challenging games that have the complexity to sustain themselves over time. He has thought a great deal about complex issues that impact a game of the scope of EverQuest and EverQuest II, including building community, the economy, playability, and how to make it so people want to log in and keep coming back over time. Right now, his job consists mostly of making lists, he told me, keeping the project on track and making sure that everything gets done the way it should be. But his first love is game design and the details that go into creating a world that people want to adventure in.

My first impression of Chris was how thoughtful he was about game design, and the importance he stressed of balancing the game between fun and sustainability. Game designers have to make decisions about how they are going to treat the players as the game progresses. The tone that the developers take makes a huge difference on how the game ends up being played. Will the developers, and later the live team, take a very strict authoritarian role, forcing players to play the game as they envision it, squelching any deviation from the assumed gameplay with nerfs and changes? Or will they be permissive, and change things as the players ask for them, realizing that some of the changes may ruin overall gameplay in the long term? Where do they go when suddenly a new play style in the game shows up that completely differs from the original design idea and changes all the encounters? Chris was very earnest in explaining that many times developers and designers seem to be making changes that at the outset seem unfair, but in fact are making sure that the game remains playable and enjoyable over time. If the game can be “won” in a short period of time, players will leave and find other games to play. Live team changes are a result of many discussions about what will keep the game fun and hard enough to be satisfying to the players over time, without making it too difficult or frustrating. A tough challenge! We both agreed that even the most brilliant group of programmers and game designers can never outguess 450,000 players, using all their skills and imagination to make the game into their own personal playground. It’s not possible for the developers to guess every trick and technique good players come up with to conquer a difficult encounter, as they found as EverQuest progressed. Every time the players come up with something new, the developers like Chris have to decide how that is going to impact the game now, and in the future. What changes, if any, should be made to make sure the game stays fun?

Chris is also very interested in fostering a sense of community and friendships among players, working to put elements into EQII that will encourage interactions between individuals. Players of MMOs place value on their player avatars: they have individual personalities that may or may not reflect the personality of the person at the keyboard, and exist strongly as individuals in the game world. Interaction between players is what builds community within the MMO. One important part of EQII that Chris feels will draw on this concept is the player housing feature. In some MMOs, the player housing can be described as a “trophy case”, something a player constructs to show off his or her accomplishments. In EQII, the housing is very personal, in the sense that players can design the layout, the placement of the windows, and the design of the interior, but more importantly are also made for sharing. The house is an expression of the avatar’s personality. A key element is that friends can come into the house to enjoy it, move objects around, and even contribute to the design. The house is interactive, not static. EQ has not survived and prospered over five years just because of the monster encounters; it is because of the relationships player characters have formed in the world of Norrath. Chris hopes to encourage friendships and altruism in the game by incorporating more elements to make cooperating with others rewarding personally. I can’t wait to see what he comes up with in this regard.

Because of the emphasis on friendships and camaraderie, groups and raids are going to be designed to work on smaller scale. There is a theory behind these decisions, as well. Chris feels that large raids end up marginalizing some players. Most people don’t feel very important in a group of 50 to 100 players zerging the mob. In EQII, the emphasis is on smaller raids, up to 4 groups of 6, and many of the encounters can be easily done with just a small group of 6 people. So in changing the game design, he has changed the focus from huge raids to more personal adventures with groups of comrades.

We also discussed the economy of an MMO. The economy in a game becomes very important in the success of gameplay, as a player’s in-game money and gear needs to have value. Chris has looked into economic theory, which helped him to decide which elements can make the economy stronger, thus making the game more playable because buying and selling items actually works.

People’s play styles also come under scrutiny. Chris thinks often about what kind of playing experience disparate people want to have. Some just want the hack and slash of a good fighting encounter. Some want the challenge of quests and puzzles. Others want a different type of game, where player characters work at trades or skills within the game world. Chris, as a former schoolteacher, uses his knowledge of different learning styles and diversity to incorporate some of these ideas into his game design, with the goal of making the game interesting and fun for many kinds of people.

Fan Faire is important to him, as it is to all the designers and programmers, because they really get to see what the game fans are like, up close and personal. It’s not just the 18 – 25 year olds playing: it’s the over 50 crowd, professionals, women, men, children, whole families. Designing a game that’s fun and a rewarding use of leisure time for all those types of people is not easy, to say the least. For EQII, Chris has tried to put in a more open ended game, with less linearity than the traditional RPG, to appeal to a wider audience. Tradeskilling, player housing, and other elements are in place to offer alternative playing experiences. He also took into account the casual gamer, who does not want to raid for 5 hours each night. Chris has considered gender roles and the ways men and women might play differently, and incorporated those ideas into his design elements, with the goal of making a game that can be fun in different ways and blend differing player personalities into a complex MMO that is really enjoyable to spend time in, day after day.

We briefly touched on the issue of player-versus-player. Chris personally feels that although PvP can be fun, it also can foster a gang mentality amongst certain players. He hopes to develop an alternative system of PVP that incorporates the excitement and skill of challenging and defeating a real person, but does not end up with a bullying group of players ruining the experience for everyone else.

The bottom line for any game designer is how to keep people coming back who feel that the monthly subscription fee gives them entertainment value. To keep customer loyalty, the design and development teams have to be responsive to customer concerns and yet have a strong sense of where the game is going and how the game universe, in this case the land of Norrath, should function. The game has to be fun, for a lot of people. It has to foster community and a sense of belonging. It has to give you a sense of accomplishment and personal satisfaction. There has to be a desire to log in today, and the day after. To that end, the designers play themselves, and read message boards and fan sites. How does their theory of game design really play out in the real world? The grass roots commentary and complaining is not ignored; the developers really do want to know what the players care about. If players are complaining and asking for changes, that means they care passionately about the game. And there is nothing more rewarding for a designer to log in as just a regular character (which they do!), join a group, fight, enjoy a challenging encounter, and watch their group members have a great time too. Yes, Chris admitted, he cringes when he hears his group members complaining about something he designed, but he doesn’t say anything. He just waits for the next moment when people are having fun again.
