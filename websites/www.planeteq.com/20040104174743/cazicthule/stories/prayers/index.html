Prayers to the Gods
by Phrost of the Disciples of Innoruuk Note: This actually happened. I had screenshots of the event, but had to format my hard drive, so lost them. I wrote this to show that not all roleplaying is about saving the princess and killing the bad guy. Sometimes it’s more fun to BE the bad guy.

The decaying blackness of a Nektulos evening greeted me as I passed out of the valley from the commonlands. Closing my eyes, I inhaled the night and smiled. I was home again.

With bags full of booty from the carcasses of my victims, I strolled down the path to Neriak. My feet knew the path well. It had not been long since I had regularly run down it, chased by a variety of creatures that wanted my young blood, headed for the safety of the bridge guards. My mind wandered off to debate with itself the virtues of a good brandy over elven wine when the sounds of swordplay broke my reverie. I ran to the source as fast as I could, ignoring the clanking of the captured weapons and armor in my backpack. What I saw brought fire to my ice blue eyes: A barbarian, standing over the corpse of Guard T’Quetal, his yellow teeth smiling as he pulled an axe from the lifeless body.

Quickly, I ducked into the shadows of a nearby tree and reviewed my enemy. I have known many barbarians in my short time. My traveling companion, Flatfoot, who renounced his gods to follow Innoruuk, had taught me their ways. Whereas Flatfoot had learned the values of bathing from us, this barbarian apparently hadn’t. His spittle was caked on his face, and his dull eyes wandered lazily around the landscape. He never saw me standing there however, close enough to hit him over the head with my runed staff, as he checked for further dangers. I continued examining him, arriving at the conclusion that this foul creature could best me in physical combat, even with the use of my necromantic powers.

After a short time, the barbarian snorted and left his kill. I then ran to inform the other guards and Dragoons about this invader, although knowing it was hopeless. The guards were trained to ignore all save the presence of threat, and as such would not listen to my warnings. I ran on, to the gates of Neriak, and on the way I informed my brethren in the forest. Their outrage matched my own. There was little we could do, however. I tried praying to Innoruuk, but the prince of hate had other matters on his mind, and ignored my insignificant pleas.

Gathered at the city gates, my fellow Tier’Dal and I decided to beseech the powers of those that were above even our gods: the elusive and powerful “Giemms” as they were spoken of, in hushed tones. We sat there, repeating the archaic mantra of invocation to the Giemms: “Fawhad-slaashh, peie, eie, tee, eye, tee, eye, ohh, enn……..fawhad-slaashh, peie, eie, tee, eye, tee, eye, ohh, enn”, hoping that we would hear from these omnipotent beings.

An hour passed.

One of my young blue-skinned brothers suggested we pass the time by slaying the local halfling clan that was trying to encroach on our forest. We all agreed. I shouted to the forest, the stone cliff face of Neriak echoing my words for all to hear: “We shall have our revenge by slaying the women and children of the sons of Rivervale, meet at the bridge if you wish to join us in this endeavor!”

From the bridge, a loosely organized group of eight or so of us set out. The first camp, fell to our might in less than a minute. The dark magics and sword art of the Tier’Dal vanquished our undisciplined foes in such a manner it is not even fit to mention here. They were appetizers for our bloodlust. We set our sights on their shrine.

From the banks of our pihrana infested river we saw them, the initiates of some puny halfling diety, grouped around their Questor leader by a crude shrine. We could see from their faces, he was relating a tale of mirth and happiness to them, two things which had no place here in Nektulos. I eyed my companions and they nodded. We would kill the Questor first.

With a flash of light the short battle began. I wove my incantations and the elderly halfling’s blood began to boil. A wizard’s spell brought him down. However, instead of fleeing at the site of their teacher’s death, the initiates fired back at us with their good intentioned, but feeble magics. In no time, we had slain them, however. I particularly enjoyed healing the small wound I had received by draining the life out of the last initiate that was standing.

It was a satisfying battle, yet we were still frustrated that we hadn’t heard from the Giemms. Had we recited the incantations properly? I recited them once, to satisfy my curiosity, and discovered that yes, they had reached the mysterious beings as my incantations were cut short by their mysterious powers.

Then, at the moment of my greatest frustration, came a voice: “Greetings traveler, my name is *********.(no matter how I try, I cannot penetrate the enchantment that prevents me from repeating the name) If you would like further counsel on this, use this simple mantra, and speak freely.” She disclosed the mantra to me, which was similar to the one I’d use to contact her, and that it was called to her kind, Fieedbaaak. As she left my consciousness, I felt a cloud lift from my mind. In the moment of clarity I experienced, I saw things from above. I knew then that nothing would change. The knowledge both frustrated and relieved me. I would now continue with my life.

As I sat down at the bar in the foreign quarter of Neriak, I related my tale to Chon Chon, the Ogress. She listened with a bemused look on her face, but I could tell that she understood none of what I told her. Sighing, I raised a glass of brandy to my lips, and put pen to parchment as I sat there. Maybe someone else would understand this tale.
