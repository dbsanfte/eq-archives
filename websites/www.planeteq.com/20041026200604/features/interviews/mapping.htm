The Fine Art of Player Mapmaking

An interview with Brewall Rainsinger
Bard Mapmaker from the Cazic Thule Server

October 23, 2004

Brewall Rainsinger, a member of the Crimson Talon guild on the Cazic Thule server, has been playing his Bard since July, 1999, and is now level 67+. He has been making in-game maps longer than that, and started making maps of Norrath at level one! I was curious on exactly how those maps get made, so I asked Brewall about art of mapping.

Kwill: How did you get started?

Brewall: I’ve been doing maps in games as long as I’ve been playing them. My first computer game was Scott Adam’s Adventure (on cassette) on an Atari 800, and I drew a map of it using graph paper so I wouldn’t get lost. Since then, I’ve done dozens of other games from Bard’s Tale to Wasteland. When I was level 1 in EverQuest, someone told me that leaving the city was very deadly, so I stayed within the city walls -- literally. Foolishly, I leveled from 1 to 8 in the Qeynos Aqueducts, and did my first map there using the seams in the walls to make the grid. It’s funny when I think back upon it: no compass, pathetic sense
heading, and no knowledge of locs, but it wasn’t out of line from other games at the time.

Kwill: How do you go about mapping an area or zone? How long does it take? What tools do you use?

Brewall: The methods by which I map a zone have changed considerably over the years! The Kunark and Luclin era maps I did were all hand drawn onto graph paper and then drawn on the computer. To give an idea of what this is like, a zone like Katta might span 8-10 pieces of 8x10 paper just to get the level of detail for which you could draw a map on the computer. Outdoor maps were fairly easy to map, perhaps an hour (make one run around the zone to get max coordinates, make graph sheets up, then start the mapping at detail), indoor maps took considerably more time, perhaps five or more hours. Once it was drawn out on graph paper, I would spend an hour or two on the computer drawing it up using simple graphics programs. Jagged Pine forest was the last of the graph paper maps, and it spanned over 20 pieces of paper to get that level of detail!

With LoY [Legacy of Ykesha expansion], I wrote an Excel macro that would translate /locs, /say startline, /say stopline, and /say note to create a grid and draw a map for me. It is actually pretty sophisticated and I can give it a lot of setup information on how I want the map drawn, such as line widths, color, grid dimensions, etc. I used this to create Nedaria’s Landing and all of the LoY maps. When the mapping system was introduced, I rewrote the code to use the EQ map files. I take the output of the macro, clean, color, add other information, and then save it as a GIF for size. Generally, if the map file is clean, creating a final map takes about an hour.

Kwill: Do you go to all the zones yourself to make the maps?

Brewall: I make it a point to map all the zones myself and I love to explore every corner of every zone. I feel sorry for the people that level their way up as quickly as possible and miss out on the vastness of
the game. Some of the best zones are ones where there is a real atmosphere, like Mistmoore, where there are living quarters, kitchens, piano rooms, alchemy areas, gardens, and libraries. I love zones where you are wondering what lies ahead; versus LDoN [Lost Dungeons of Norrath expansion], where everything looked pretty much the same and unrealistic from what an army of monsters would need on a day-to-day basis. Some of the new GoD zones are pretty neat looking, but my favorites are the ones in true castle or dungeon motif.

Kwill: How does your character class help you?

Brewall: Bards definitely have an arsenal of tools at their disposal for mapping, and I certainly take advantage of them. Bard speed is probably the most obvious advantage, but other basic ones are invisibility, levitation (boots), eye of zomm (helmet), and bind sight (song). In difficult areas, I usually have to dig deeper and use faction (song), illusions, sneak/hide, mez/charm, gate a mob, and fading memories (lose all agro). With red con mobs, I’ve used bard divine aura song to map the room, exited the room, and then used fading memories. Many times, just when I think I am stuck, I figure out a way to map it. I keep a stack of invis undead potions, sow potions, and a few other things at the ready. Perhaps the only disappointing bardic tool is picklock, because bards are capped so low and I get stuck in some green-con mob dungeon.

Kwill: Does anyone else go out and explore zones for you or help you with your maps?

Brewall: Virtually all of my maps are done solo, if anyone was to accompany me, they would probably find it pretty boring watching me crawl the highest points on the walls around the edge of a zone. Or, when I was doing it the paper method, waiting while I labeled the grids on new sheets of paper. Occasionally, on raids to more difficult zones I will wander off to the walls and clean up maps that I think are too sloppy or are missing something, but as long as everyone gets their fix of AE mana song, no one seems to mind.

Kwill: How many maps have you made?

Brewall: I’ve published 19 maps to the internet, another 10 unreleased (guild-only) maps, and another 20-30 partial maps prior to finding a better one on the internet. Today, it seems that as soon as an expansion comes out you can find zone maps the next day, versus the weeks or months as was the case with Kunark and Luclin.

Kwill: Do you look at other people's maps?

Brewall: There are a lot of great map makers out there. I have an enormous amount of respect for the maps on EQAtlas, the detail, accuracy, and artistry are fantastic. I also consult a number of other map makers including Saeny Rangerheart and Draelon Stormsong. The one thing I will say for myself is that I have been doing them for a long time and a lot of other mapmakers have come and gone. For my maps, I always focus on the things I feel are critical for people to have: gridlines, coordinates (especially the 0 lines), easy printability, and all key features labeled (zi/zo/mobs/etc). I’ve looked at the official SOE map book at the store, but it paled in comparison to the three-ring binder of “best-of” maps that I keep.

An example of Brewall's mapping skills -- map of Plane of Knowledge

 

Kwill: How much time do you spend mapping, say in a week or month?

Brewall: I tend to do most mapping when expansions come out. But with the new mapping tool, the need for mapping has really dwindled. With the immediate availability on the internet, I now tend to explore and label versus the actual mapping and drawing. There are a lot of electronic maps of GoD [Gates of Discord expansion] out there, but I haven’t seen any drawings yet; there is just something about a real map versus an electronic one. If some don’t come out soon, I’ll probably break down and do some.

Kwill: What is your best map, in your opinion?

Brewall: The EverQuest World map is probably the best one I’ve done so far. It took an enormous amount of time just trying to lay out all the zones, accurately determine all the connections, and ports. Then to do the small thumbs of all the outdoor zones (and redo the layout). Personally, I print this map out on a plotter 4x4 and post it on the wall near my computer. Not that I need it, but it is just fun to see how big and complex the world of EverQuest really is! It will be a long time before another game has this much real estate.

An actual zone map that I consider my best would likely be the Temple of Veeshan. Now that Crimson
Talon is allied with the dragons since we killed the Shade and are working on Veeshan Peak quests, I will release this map to the general public. It is a single map of the entire zone that shows all mobs, named mobs with resist needed, and other information. It took a lot of time to develop, compile, and accurately draw.

Kwill: Is this just a hobby, or are you a map-maker in real life as well?

Brewall: Just a hobby, although I would probably be doing it in any game I was playing. The closest thing in real life is that I am the person everyone hands the maps to when I get in the car.

Kwill: What do you enjoy about making maps?

Brewall: I think it is helping other people that do not have an innate sense of direction or is new to the game and do not know where a zone is.

Kwill: What is most difficult about making maps? Were any areas or zones particularly hard?

Brewall: It has gotten much easier than it used to be! When Kunark was released, I remember making maps of the Mines of Nurga and the Temple of Droga. The Mines were a snap, but I really got burned on ToD. Typically when I map, I do a rule where I put my right hand on right wall and follow it until, eventually, I find the exit. Well, let’s just say that was a bad rule in this dungeon because after I fell down the in entrance area, it took me over 20 pieces of graph paper and over 20 hours to find my way out, and by then, I had mapped the entire dungeon! It was a very harrowing and exhilarating time when I finally emerged.

Kwill: Do you have any funny or interesting stories aboutmap making?

Brewall: When I was finally level 8 I went to Qeynos Hills to hunt. I was amazed at the outdoors and decided to see how big the world was. I was afraid of things attacking me, so I stayed to the sides, and ran up the west wall, ran to Jagged Pine, ran back out, fell through the world, and landed safely on the road on the south side of the zone. I thought is was odd, and ran all the way back north and accidentally fell through the world twice more! Years later, with the Direct X patch, I found myself back in the zone and realized I had an incomplete map of the zone. No problem, I thought, I’ll fill in the missing parts, I started the map tools, and promptly fell through the same hole in the world I fell through 57 levels earlier. Worse yet, I mistakenly fell through the hole twice more trying to find the exact loc of it! This time, I knew how to /bug and reported it.

Kwill: What is the URL for people to get your maps? Are they free to the public?

Brewall: All the maps I issue to the public are free and can be found on the Crimson Talon guild website at www.crimsontalon.org. There is a menu option on the left side called “Brewall’s Maps”. Typically, I release maps to the guild for a few weeks prior to releasing them to the general public. The guild also has access to some special maps the general public does not have access to.
