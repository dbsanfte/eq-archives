Ruldari - half elven ranger ("Extreme Forager")

 

Face it, no matter how you sugar-coat the truth, one fact remains. I am a bastard half-elf with no kin in the lands willing to claim me. Mayhaps it is this that makes me cautious of others. Mayhaps this is what spurs me to prefer the outdoors to the city. Or, mayhaps it was that fateful day in Blackburrow ...

Mother was a brewer. Unfortunately, she oft sampled a bit too much of her own brew. Whenever she got into one of those "sampling sessions", she'd go into maundering tales about the handsome human she'd tried to trap into marriage. When she was sober, she'd tell me nothing, but slowly I pieced together some of it. From comments such as "flying feet" and "hands of an artisan", I'd determined that he was a monk, and a potter of some skill. Apparently he hadn't wanted her ... hadn't wanted me. To be fair, I don't know if he ever knew about me. It does not matter anymore. There is no way I can find him from this single piece of pottery that my mother always kept hidden away. I will never know if she kept it hidden from me, or from herself, in fear of what she would do in a drunken rage.

One day mother heard rumors of a new brewing recipe in Halas. Nothing was going to stop her from getting that new recipe, not even the fact that she had a young child to drag with her on such an unsafe journey. I balked only momentarily out of panic, until her hand connected with the side of my head hard enough to make me see stars. Knowing more blows would come if I didn't behave, I resigned myself to the journey. Off we went from Qeynos, my short child's legs valiantly trying to keep up with her adult strides. I always desperately wanted to please her, so I didn't dare complain again of my exhaustion, much less my fear.

We had to pass through the chaos that is Blackburrow on our trek, and there I was lost. A mob of gnolls separated me from my drunken mother. I ran and hid from them behind a rock, biting my lower lip until it bled to keep from crying out. Finally, the gnolls were slain by others, and I made my cautious way out of my hiding place. I looked everywhere, dodging gnolls, hiding when necessary. My mother was nowhere to be found. Hours passed as I looked for some trail, some sign of my mother. I begged total strangers to help me. None heeded me, although some barbarian shaman who was hard-pressed in a fight somehow managed to throw a protection spell on me before being swept away by battle.

Finally, exhausted, I found a hollow tree and slept inside it. In the morning, I started the search again, tired, scared, and very hungry. I know not how, call it instinct, call it an innate tracking skill, but I thought I sensed my mother deep in the caves of Blackburrow. Quickly, I hurried on, finding faint tell-tales here and there that spurred me ever deeper.

I stopped dead in my tracks at the dreaded snake pit. There was my mother. Dead from numerous snake bites.

After several long minutes of frozen shock, I clambered down into the pit, sobbing. Mother smelled even moreso of alcohol than usual, and instead of a weapon in her hand, she clutched a crumpled piece of paper. The cursed recipe. Had she been trying to kill the snakes with paper cuts? The irreverent thought was gone as quickly as it came. My mother died looking for me. How could I live with the guilt?

Such a grief-stricken, guilt-ridden child I was. I probably would have died right then and there if not for "Unkle" Mortekai. With many soothing words, the kind shaman escorted her from that foul place, seeing that I was fed and cared for. Knowing I was poorly suited for life in his northern homelands, he found a couple in Qeynos who were willing to care for a young orphan, and promised to visit often.

I was not an easy child to care for, either. I became quiet and withdrawn, never voicing the belief of my part in my mother's death, but letting it fester instead. I spent many long days in the wilds, learning how to track even the faintest trail, vowing I would never lose a loved one like that again.

It was only years later, when I reached my teenage years, that I realized the significance of that piece of paper my mother had been clutching. She hadn't been looking for me. She had lost me and continued on her way to Halas to acquire the recipe. Then she had sampled the result and fallen, drunk, into the snake pit, to be bitten to death.

Finally, I can see clearly. My real mother never truly cared for me, only for her drink. My true father may never be found, and if he does, he may never care for me. The Spiritwalkers, however, led by Unkle, seem to have genuine concern for me.

They are all the family I need.
