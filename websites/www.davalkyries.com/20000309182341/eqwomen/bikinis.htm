Ode to Online Comrades
submitted by Dr. Chris Green

I play RPG online these days,
RPG, you know, role-playing games.
    Dungeons and Dragons,
    Wizards and warriors,
    Knights and necromancers,
Elves, and dwarves, and trolls, and humans,
    battling monsters, giant spiders, and the undead,
    sometimes side-by-side,
    sometimes toe-to-toe.

I'm living in the midst
    of a legend, a fairy tale,
    more complex, more fantastic
    than any of the many
    I devoured as a child.
In this world of magic and heroes,
    not all the races are human,
    so some folk have pointy ears and golden skin and can      see in the dark,
    while others are 7 feet tall and have steel-blue eyes       and blonde flowing locks.
But we all share a thirst for justice and knowledge,
    are all involved in the universal struggle
    of Good vs. Evil.
And what counts is not what divides us,
    but rather, what side are you on?

And I, Guerilla, level 5 Paladin,
    roam the forested hills
    and haunt the dusky taverns
    with my rusty bastard sword
    and my trusty brass buckler,
    looking for kindred spirits
    who will watch my back
    as I offer them aid and encouragement.

But, until I find them,
    I travel alone
    seeking to grow ever stronger and wiser
    so that I may be worthy of strong and wise                  companions.
I toil ceaselessly in the killing fields
    so that I may add to my modest store of
    silver and gold,
    food and drink,
    armor and weapons,
    in short, my all-important stuff.

I move further and further away
    from that ignominious day
    when I was born,
    like some kind of clone,
    an adult in size and stature,
    but ignorant and defenseless,
    no money in my pockets and no boots on my feet,
    vulnerable to the bite of the weakest snake or rat,
    and stone dead in seconds
    against mightier foes who ambush me in the dark.

Sometimes I take a break from all this killing and being killed
    to query innkeepers, merchants, and temple guardians
    who were set on my path
    to help me find
    arcane spells and magical helmets,
    amulets of invisibility and rings of invulnerability,
    weapons of great power and unerring accuracy.
I seek just the right words for my questions,
    words that will unlock their mouths
    and let them reveal to me my next quest.
Then I puzzle over their answers
    wondering how in the world
    they might mean anything of significance.

But the most fun of all
    is playing alongside
    the alter egos of my fellow gamers.
We try to get into the fantasy aspects of our characters,
    saying corny things to each other like,
    "Hail all ye brave soldiers of fortune," and
    "Where might a weary traveler find an inn in this fair         town?" and
    "May the gods grant thee gentle breezes and bless all          thy ventures."
Most of the time, though,
    we communicate like the 21st century geeks
    (translation - young white computer nerds) that we are,
    "Hey, dude, wazzup?" and
    "Let's go kill us some critters,"
and, most frequently, "HELP!"

What most amazes me about the game is
    how often my online comrades respond
    to my cries and the cries of others for assistance.
For no immediately discernible reason,
    for I know it keeps them from their own quests,
    they stop to explain the rules,
    show me the way through the maze,
heal me with a therapeutic touch from afar,
give me possessions just because I asked.
And I know this is quite macabre,
    but when I die,
    they help me find my corpse.
Somehow there's nothing quite as satisfying
    as reuniting with my empty shell
    and recouping all my worldly goods.

The last time a stranger helped me
    find my way out of the dungeon
    when I was helplessly lost
    and surrounded by merciless enemies without number,
I thanked him
and offered a few pieces of silver as a reward.
He merely replied,
    "No problem. I was helped out of there
3 or 4 times myself before I learned my way around. "
He asked me to pass on the favor when I could
    and I promised to do so.

If only the quote-unquote Real World
    worked like my fantasy world.
Would that the dangers were so clear
    and the evils so obvious,
    that we would all join hands to conquer them,
    forgetting our race and class differences,
    remembering only that the strongest party includes
        doers and thinkers,
        fighters and lovers,
        teachers and learners,
        leaders and followers.
Or, would that the quote-unquote Real World
    suddenly disappeared on day as we were all online
    and we played together,
        all day and all night,
        watching the sun rise and set,
        the seasons change,
        and kingdoms rise and fall,
        forever and ever amen.
