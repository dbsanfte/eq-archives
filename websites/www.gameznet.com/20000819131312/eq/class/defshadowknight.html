Shadowknight Class Guide
It has been over a year since I wrote my original shadowknight class guide and in that time much has changed. This evening I found myself in an odd position. My brain was still fried from this weekend's festivities and I could not find anything to occupy my mind while sitting on this laptop. As a result a new shadowknight class guide has been born. I hope that you enjoy it.

This guide does not follow the normal class guide structure found elsewhere on the site. I had much to say and it simply would not fit inside of the older style. This guide is a result of more than twenty months of play primarily as a shadowknight. Some of the opinions expressed in this article are based on my personal opinions and may not neccessarily be the standard practice of other shadowknights. I have attempted to make my best effort to display as many situations as possible.

Because of the extremely large size of this document it has been split into four seperate parts. The first part covers shadowknight basics. The second, third and fourth cover levels 1-25, 26-50 and 51-60 respectively. If you know where you are going then you can quickly skip to one of the other sections by clicking one of the links below.

Why A Shadowknight?

In the 19 months of EQ'Lizer's existance one of the most common questions I have been asked is, "Why should I play a shadowknight?" Class selection is definately a matter of personal preference. Many players enjoy playing a necromancer because of the versatility of the class and the ability to solo effectively when play time is limited. I myself can understand this argument but never have been able to play a necromancer past level 12 because they do not fit my personal style of play. So i'm not going to tell anyone that one class is better than another. Each class has their own pros and cons and in my opinion the classes of Everquest are more or less well balanced.

I will instead give a list of the reasons that I have chosen the shadowknight as my class of perference.

The first thing that I want to cover is the caster vs. melee issue. I have always been a player who likes to be involved in the action. I like to be involved in the fight from start to finish which is the primary reason that I moved from a caster(cleric through most of the beta) to a melee type. Before my cleric I had spent a decent amount of time as a dwarf warrior but in the longrun the lack of options is what made me move to a caster. By the final two months of the beta test I was certain that a hybrid would be the best choice for me.

Ranger and paladins are also very good class choices in my opinion. I have spent a decent amount of time playing both of those hybrids as well. The shadowknight remains my favorite class for a variety of reasons which I will attempt to explain below.

In my opinion the single largest strength of the shadowknight class is it's versatility. Shadowknights are the only hybrid which can cast both invisibility versus both the living and the dead. Lifetaps have proven to be an excellent addition to the class for reducing downtime and for healing yourself in an emergency situation. Shadowknights are the best soloing hybrids. Your dot's help you to dish out a sizeable amount of damage in a lengthy fight and only warriors make better tanks. Feign death serves not only to keep you alive but also makes shadowknights excellent pullers for groups without an enchanter or monk.

I have heard many players complain about the shadowknight class. It remains one of the most underused classes in the game. Much of this can be attributed to players not playing to the classes strengths or using improper playing techniques. Some other areas are legitimate gripes. When all is said and done however I feel that shadowknights are in the longrun one of the most powerful classes in the game. Do not expect to feel this power early on however. Shadowknights struggle through the early levels and do not feel their true power until much later in the game. If you have the persistance to play it through however you will be rewarded in the longrun.

In just a few paragraphs I am going to  attempt to walk you through playing a shadowknight from beginning to end. Before I can get to that however I need to clear up the basics of the class including racial benefits, starting stats and basic skills.

Which race to choose?

When choosing a race for your shadowknight there are two common schools of thought. The first is to choose a small race shadowknight(dark elf, erudite, iksar or human) because of the smaller experience penalty and the generally higher mana pool due to increased intelligence. The second is to choose a large race(troll or ogre) for their excellent melee stats. I personally subscribe to the second school of thought but I will try to show the strengths of both choices.

There are three steps of small race shadowknights. Erudites have very poor melee skills countered by the highest level of intelligence. Dark elves feature probably the best mix with slightly below average melee skills countered with very high intelligence. Iksars have middle of the line stats but have the advantage of regeneration. Humans are middle ground with no attribute being above or below average.

There are some who will tell you that intelligence is not important to a shadowknight. The reasoning behind this is that a shadowknight's do not generally expend a large amount of mana in a battle. In a typical dungeon battle you will most often cast your debuffs or dot's. Since our primary damaging spells are dot's we only need to cast each spell once per battle. Thus even if we stack all of our dot's and debuffs we will generally only use about 2 bars of mana(or less) per battle even as a large race.

While the above paragraph is completely true it does not tell the whole story. Mana becomes more and more important at higher levels because more mana equals more lifetaps. It is not uncommon for life leech to take a full two bars of a low intelligence large races shadowknights mana when they first attain the spell. In a single battle this still will leave you mana to spare. However a well tuned group will be pulling more or less nonstop and every bit of mana counts. More importantly you will often find yourself being the primary puller in a group. This will not leave you time to meditate and you will enjoy the extra mana.

Mana also makes a huge differnce when reverse kiting. This is a popular method of soloing employed by many shadowknights and necromancers. Because you are keeping your opponent feared throughout most of the battle you will not be taking many hits. More mana allows you to fear kite longer which allows you to take on tougher mobs. This allows small race shadowknights to hypothetically be much more efficient soloers in outdoor zones than large race ones.

With the exception of iksar shadowknights small race SK's also face fewer faction problems than their larger counterparts. This allows them to complete a larger number of quests and to travel to a greater variety of locations without worrying about being killed on sight.

That having been said I still prefer large race shadowknights for their melee abilities. There are two types of large races shadowknights: trolls and ogres. One could argue for hours about which of these two makes the better SK. If popularity is the decider then troll's are clearly the winner here. There is an old saying however that if a million people believe a dumb thing that it is still a dumb thing. I wholeheartedly agree with this.

In my opinion the choice of large race shadowknight depends on your personal playing style.

Trolls make better soloers because of their natural regeneration. Regeneration can cut downtime in half when soloing. Trolls suffer from a critical weakness to fire starting with only 5 fire resistance. One thing that should be noted is that troll shadowknights have by far the highest experience penalty of any class/race combination in the game. It should be noted that your party members pay this penalty for you when in a group which all but eliminates the problem so long as you aren't soloing.

In my opinion ogres are the better choice in a group however. They have naturally higher strength, stamina and intelligence which are perfect for a shadowknight. In the longrun I think that choosing between a troll and an ogre should depend on whether you plan on grouping almost exclusively or soloing a decent amount of time. 

Large race shadowknights have the advantage of using the slam skill. This allows you to bash without using a shield. As a result when using a two handed weapon(which you likely will for much of your career) you will have the advantage of a secondary attack where small race shadowknights will not. Slam is also great for interupting casters.

Now that I have given a brief description i'll give a few of my personal thoughts on the matter. I personally play a troll shadowknight. I do subscribe to the thought that shadowknights generally do not need the mana pool of a dark elf or erudite if it means trading melee effectiveness. I feel that we are 3/4 warrior and 1/4 necromancer rather than 50/50. I know all of the benefits of high intelligence and boost my intelligence every chance that I get. I however feel our ability to tank and dish damage via melee is more important.

I also enjoy troll shadowknights for their regeneration. While I feel that players should group as often as possible(for both fun and efficiency) I also am a player who suffers from time constraints. There are often times when I only have one or two hours to play. Under these time constraints it is sometimes hard to find a group without travelling for the majority of my playtime. The ability to solo without a large amount of downtime comes in extremely handy in these situations.

If you choose to go the subscribe to the mana school of thought I would recommend playing a dark elf. Iksars can not wear platemail which in my opinion is a serious drawback for a shadowknight. Dark elves have a high mana pool with less melee penalties than erudites pay. They also feature a very SK friendly home and ultravision which both will come in handy.

Beginning Stats

There are many useful ways to allocate your starting stats. For each race it is a bit different so I am not going to attempt to explain a base way of doing things. I will however cover the benefits of each stat really quickly here.

Strength is right up there with intelligence as the two most useful shadowknight abilities. Strength equals ATK which equals more damage in combat. Strength only affects your maximum damage not your median damage. Some will complain that strength does not increase your maximum damage by enough to make a difference. While this is a valid argument it is still the second most useful skill of a shadowknight.

Agility is an important skill because it increases your armor class. The most important thing to remember is to start your agility at 75 as a minimum. With an agility below 75 you will suffer a bad armor class penalty. Increasing your agility above 75 is not an imporant issue to me. Agility and dexterity are the easiest skills to raise by way of equipment. There are many items which give signifigant raises in your agility and dexterity.

One thing that should be noted here is the two types of armor class rating in Everquest. These both calculate into your AC rating but many players do not undersand the way that it works in Everquest.

There are basically two types of AC increases. The first is agility and defense based AC and the second is armor based AC(I am unsure which of these that  buffs fall under). Agility only affects how often that you get hit. Armor class only affects the amount of damage that is absorbed on a successful hit. Thus two characters with identical armor classes can be affected differently. Monks for example have a naturally high AC bonus. When comparing a monk and a shadowknights AC a monk will usually be much higher. Monks AC allows them to be hit less often. When they are hit though their armor will absorb less damage.

This is why a twinked character gets hit as often as an untwinked one but often only takes one damage per attack.

Dexterity affects how often your weapons will proc. This only enters play on weapons which have an effect. Many players would rate dexterity up there(or above) strenght in importance. I however do not feel it is as important for a shadowknight for various reasons.

First it is easy to increase via items. Strenght is not.

Second many of the best shadowknight weapons either do have a proc or feature a proc which does not stack well with itself. For example the dark reavers steal strength only needs to go off one time for its five or so minute duration to be effective. Any more than this is a waste. The proc on Soulbinder for example is a DOT lifetap. Thus if it procs twice in a short span of time it limits it's own damage potential.

Intelligence is probably the most important stat to increase at startup unless you have deficeincies in other stats. Intelligence equals mana. While you may not care about it much early on it will make a difference later in your shadowknights life. While 10 strength might equal 10 ATK at level 60 10 intelligence could mean a signifigant amount of mana. That mana could be used for a lifetap for example to do more damage(and also heal you) than the same amount of strength. Intelligence is also harder to increase without trading armor class. There are many strenght items which feature high armor class. There are far fewer items which increase intelligence. When it comes down to armor class or stats I would choose armor class in most cases unless they give a good amount of intelligence or mana.

Wisdom or Charisma are more or less throw in stats for a shadowknight. I wouldn't ever spend any points inreasing either of these.

Shadowknight Basics

In the next section we will cover skills and tactics on a level by level basis. Before we get to this we need to cover the basic starting abilities of a shadowknight.

Harm Touch

One of the defining skills of a shadowknight is the ability to harm touch. Harm touch allows you to do direct damage to your opponent once per game day. Once used it will recycle in exactly one day of game time(not actual playtime). It is important to note that harm touch can be resisted or partially resisted but that it is resisted less than a normal spell. The damage formula is (10*level)+1 from level 1-40. From level 41-60 it becomes (20*(level-40))+401.

Examples:

A level 1 shadowknight will harm touch for 11 damage maximum.
A level 23 shadowknight will harm touch for 231 damage maximum.
A level 60 shadowknght will harm touch for 801 damage maximum.

Harm touch has often been the cause of many shadowknight complaints. The common argument is that lay hands > harm touch. I personally agree with this assessement but I also feel that we should be accepting harm touch for what it is rather than comparing it to our goodie goodie counterparts.

At early levels harm touch is an excellent source of damage. At those levels it can often cut a monsters hit points in half. At these levels I recommend saving your harm touch as long as possible and only using it if absoleutely neccesary. There are many times during a fight when you think that you will lose. However by saving it until the end you will find that you often are able to pull away from these without wasting it. The pitfall to this tactic is that it can be resisted or partially resisted which may result in death.

As you begin to gain in levels you will find that your ace in the hole is becoming less and less effective as a damage disher. It is still a good source of damage however.At later levels I usually attempt to save my harm touch for one of three situations.

When solo I use it about midway through a battle if I think it is going to go bad. On monsters which run this might push them into running early which can save your life. Either way it allows you to get a good idea of whether or not you are going to win or lose before you reach the point of no return(when your run speed is too slow to escape) and then make the proper choice. 

In groups I usually save my harm touch for a dangerous situation. Harm touch is an excellent taunt. When a monster locks on a caster and they are going down fast this may be the only way to rescue them until the healer can pump them with enough hit points.

Harm touch can also be used as an emergency way to stop a monster. There are times when a monster breaks snare, or darkness or whatever method of slowing them down that your party is employing inside of a dungeon. Sometimes this leads to hairy situations where the mob will move into the aggro range of other monsters. Harm touch can be used in these situations as an emergency kill. They are usually low enough in health by this point that it will kil them or slow them to a near stop. This has saved my life more than once.

Bind Wound

Bind wound is a popular method of reducing downtime. It allows you to heal yourself to reduce downtime. It requires a bandage which limits it's usefuleness at early levels for an untwinked character. Magicians can summon bandages however which can save on bandage costs.

At later levels bind wound is an invaluable skill for reducing downtime, especially for shadowknights without regeneration. It heals (bind wound skill/4) per bandage rounded down.

Taunt

The taunt skill can be used to attempt to draw monsters off of other players. This skill is often used to draw monsters off casters or injured tanks in your party. It will not go up on it's own unless you train one point into it.

Slam

The slam skill is a special variant of the bash skill which you obtain at level 6. It is only available to large races shadowknights. It is a secondary attack which can be used in addition to your weapon skills. Until you obtain(and train in) the bash skill it will only do one damage per attack but does have a chance of stunning your opponent. After you train in the bash skill slam will do the same damage as a bash but it does not require a shield.

The slam skill is linked to the shoulder slot. This means that if a monster requires magic weapons to be hit then you will need magical shoulderpads to be able to slam them.
