Combat Strategies & Tactics

EQ Druids has some excellent articles on combat strategies, and uses at various levels.  I am providing a few additional notes, and some suggestions on hybrid strategies which I have found to be extremely effective.

Melee

I'm not going to say much about this except that if you are still relying on this as your main tactic after around level 18, then STOP IT!  I took a while to learn this, though Glenfinen tried to tell me time and again.  Sometimes it is a harsh lesson, but sooner or later you will realize that you made a caster, not a warrior.  I think I was level 18 for about 3 months as I kept refusing to accept this...

Now this is not to say that you can't step in and bash something.  Just realize it is not your primary means of dealing with an opponent.  Sure you can use your damage shield, just make sure you used DoT, snare, and maybe a DD first.  And if you are getting beaten (or clawed, gouged, mauled, etc) to death, back off and cast a spell!  If you are in a group, don't stand there and melee, you can do much better to heal, shield and snare (and sometimes other things too).

Pets

This is where I sometimes think that maybe I should have been a mage.  For one thing, it would be nice to have pets that don't try to eat you all the time.  Ah well, the life of druid is not bad at all, so I cannot complain.  I do not know many druids who use pets often, and in fact I also used to consider druid pets to be of limited use.  The problem is the unpredictable nature of druid charm spells.  Actually this is needed in order to maintain balance, though a guaranteed five minute charm would be quite nice indeed... 

What?  Only five minutes?  You mean it does not usually last that long?  And for the high mana cost?  That is correct.  But, have you seen the amount of damage a griffon can inflict in five minutes unopposed?  I know of no other pet of similar level that can come close to this.  And yes, sometimes it lasts longer, sometimes only five seconds.  Here are some strategies to maximize the effectiveness of your pets.  As I have only recently become proficient at pet control, I am writing it from a late 30's to early 40's level perspective.  I believe these strategies would have been quite effective in the late teen levels in east (or north) karana also.

General Tips:

  • Always have a good SoW, and make sure fear (terrorize) animal and snare (ensnare) are memorized.
  • Check your pet's health quickly by selecting yourself and hitting F1.  You can also do /pet report health (I think that is it)
  • If your pet is in trouble, or losing, target it and /assist.  Immediately snare whatever it is fighting, if not already done.
  • If charm breaks, fear your pet, split it and the monster up, then recharm it.

Pet Against Stationary Opponent(s) (Griffoning*): When you send your pet against a stationary monster, it will begin attacking your pet.  Often your pet is the weaker side of the struggle, so you need to help it, but you also need to protect yourself.  In short, damage shield your pet, then snare the monster (order can be reversed depending on health of pet and relative danger of monster).  If your pet is almost dead, just snare the monster and prepare to get away or switch to another strategy.  You can find a new pet if mana is available, after the monster is snared (Pet Kiting). 

If mana is low, forego the pet's damage shield.  If pet is doing well in the battle, sit and med while it fights.  You will receive message of when spells wear off, so don't worry too much.  At 35 and up this is much better, as you can see what is going on.  Do not worry about damage shielding a powerful pet (like a griffon).  As long as you do not melee it, the damage shield will not hurt you.  It also has normal duration, and does not reset when your charm breaks.  Trust me, you will not have the luxury of melee, you will need to fear and med in order to live (see Snare/Fear section).

If your pet is fighting two or more monsters, snare them ALL.  Some may break off and attack you.  When and if your pet dies, they will all come after you.  If one breaks for you early, use your speed advantage to keep them separated.  Your pet will still be fighting the other(s) so DON'T go near it, at least not for long.  The single monster will stay on you, and it is snared, so YOU control where it goes.  This will allow you to at least get one (and usually two) monster kills, rather than the pitiful "zoning with no mana and getting no exp" alternative.

This technique is great against the corrupt guards in WC, who just stand there at their post.  Also, you will look really neat sitting there with a griffin hovering over your head.

*Griffoning: The equivalent to the RL hunting technique of Falconing, though using a Griffon instead of the smaller RL bird.

Unsnared Pet Against Snared Monster(s): This technique also works against multiple opponents, but is most effective at level 29 or later, though could work at lower levels.  For this to work, you need to make something MAD at you before your pet attacks.  In fact, it is great for hunting hill giants in North Karana, though it useful in other places as well.  This is a very useful technique when your pet is much weaker than the monster that your are fighting (ex: griffenne against hill giant).  One final point: it is often best NOT to have a pet when you start this technique, but instead to pick one up after the fight starts. 

To start, locate a suitable adversary (don't get crazy, just find something that you might normally hunt, but that would be very difficult).  I will use the Hill Giant as my example.  Snare the HG, and throw your DoTs at it.  Do not nuke it more than about once (if even that).  Now it snared and you are SoW'd, so find an appropriate pet (use tracking).  Don't go too far away, because you need to keep tabs on the HG, and you don't want others to think it is available.  If DoTs wear off, rethrow them.  I will find a griffon if possible, but settle for a griffenne (which can be a little easier to control).  Once you have your pet, track the HG again and run back (do NOT snare your pet or this technique is worthless).  DoTs will wear off again, so rethrow, and have your pet attack the snared HG.  Basically this turns the griff into a HUGE DoT, as the HG will be so mad at you that it will ignore the griff.  If the thing turns on your pet, either make it mad again (sit or stand in front of it, be prepared to take some damage), or let the pet fight it (switching to stationary pet technique).  This is not good for your pet's life expectancy, but will eliminate the "hmm now what do I do with this griff" problem.  Personally I like to keep my pets if possible.

You may be amazed how fast a griffon can take out an HG, when the griffon is not being hit.

Snared Pet Against Snared Monster: This is not so much a technique, as a way to salvage an otherwise big waste of mana.  The problem is that most snared pets do not catch most other snared monsters, meaning that if a monster is not stationary (i.e. it's chasing you) then your pet won't do much good, and will turn on you before it can do much for you.  This situation normally happens when you are low on mana (and snared your pet in order to get away from it), making it doubly bad for you.

My usual technique is to run through the monster and pet, putting the pet between you and the monster. This will allow your pet to get in a few attacks, even if it is snared.  It has some risk, as YOU can be the target of many attacks if charm breaks while you are in the middle.  Do not attack while you are doing this, as you are trying to get the monster to aggro on your pet, so you can use stationary technique.  Sit and med whenever you can, you will need the mana.

Sometimes you can stand there and take some hits while your pet attacks.  This may be impractical, as some monsters can kill you before they will aggro on your pet.  Another option may be to use (yuck) root.  It will break way too often, but you may be able to stick the monster long enough to make it mad at your pet.  I haven't tried this, but it might work (let me know if anyone tries it). 

If charm breaks in this situation, just fear your pet and fight the monster.  Keep doing this (fear is very low mana cost) and basically just ignore your pet.  You may be able to fear and med when the battle is done, and pick the pet back up.  Sometimes it is better to just zone (if you can).

Pet Against Animal: In some zones (ex: The Overthere) it is easy to find animal targets for your pets.  Now I am not talking about nice little wolves, bears, or bunnies, I am talking about rhinos and sabretooth tigers who would just as soon eat a druid as they would anyone else.  This can be very effective, and allow you to take on multiple opponents at high levels (though I have also died doing this at level 40).

With one opponent, this one is simple.  Just snare something (so it follows you slowly).  Find a pet if you don't already have one, fear the monster and send your pet after it.  Of course don't snare your pet (see above).  If charm breaks, fear your pet.  It is best if you can recharm your pet, though you will often have to snare it to do so.  Another strategy for re-charming is to run way in front of your feared pet, then charm it at maximum range.  It will have passed you by the time the casting is complete, but it is the only way I have found for re-charming without snaring.

This can quickly turn into multiple opponents, as the feared ex-pets can become difficult to find again.  Often I just charm a new pet at this point, and send it at the original monster.  When the old ex-pet comes back, fear it and snare if you like.  If the new pet behaves, you can take out both opponents (monster and the ex-pet).

When this technique goes well, you can relax a bit and med, forage, etc.  Usually you will need to because much of your time is chaos when hunting this way (but you get lots of exp).

Pet Experience (Important): Make sure that if your pet does more than half of the damage to a monster, it is either charmed or dead when the monster falls.  If not (i.e. if charm broke and it is out there running around) you will get NO experience for killing the monster.  This is most discouraging when you have spent 10 minutes and nearly died multiple times hunting a "big one".

Snare/Fear

The combination of snare and fear is incredibly powerful.  It can make impossible battles seem almost easy.  As a druid, you can only use your fear spells on animals.  However, you can easily group with someone else who provides the fear component (Enchanters & Necromancers are good).  For "normal" snare/fear hunting, just make sure you use the snare first, or things can get out of hand quickly.  You can easily solo griffins (and griffons) with this technique by mid 30's (near 40 you can start with almost no mana and still make it). Just snare, fear and DoT.  You can practice your melee by beating on the beast, if you're not so low on mana that you just need to med when possible.  Snare/fear is also useful to keep something off of you while you regain mana. 

Snare is also a valuable tactic for splitting up two monsters.  Just snare one, then run far away.  The non-snared one will stick fairly close to you, while the other will drop back a ways.  When you have achieved the desired separation, snare the one close to you and fight.  Keep moving though, re-splitting them is a pain.

I have used this technique to solo Grimfeather at level 40.  He has also killed me twice.  Be careful.

Animal Crowd Control

This is really just a special application of snare/fear, but it comes up a lot when fighting with pets.  It also helps when that annoying level 8 lion is keeping your group from meditating.  Just fear the thing, it will run away for a long time (usually).  Sometimes it will just keep showing back up and you will have to kill it, but it gets it away from you long enough to do something else.

The same idea applies to multiple animal opponents in zones like The Overthere or The Emerald Jungle.  Get the things away from you, then prepare to fight or zone.  Either way, it gives you that much needed time, even a few seconds can make a big difference.

This is also useful to keep something off of you while you regain mana.  Just make sure nearby do-gooders know that you are not in need of help in killing the thing.  You may have to explain several times, but I have never had anyone kill me "pet" this way.

Kiting

Here I wanted to put down a few thoughts about the much discussed topic of Kiting.  It is hard to even read a message board without seeing the term "Kiting Druid" these days.  But what is it, and why is it such a big deal? 

In its purest and least accepted form, "kiting" is the practice of targeting a monster (usually one that is yellow or even 1-level red /con), casting your damage over time and snare spells on it, then running away.  Repeat until you or the monster are dead.  Personally, I have never been able to solo a yellow /con monster, much less a red one.  It may be possible at some levels, with some monsters, if you have LOTS of mana.  It may have been more possible back in the old days, before DoT damage was reduced by 1/3 when monsters are moving (the infamous "kiting nerf", which is still not mentioned in some circles).

It its more general term, kiting seems nowadays to be interpreted as fighting without meleeing (i.e. run and blast, run and dot, run and shoot with bow, run and let pet hit, etc).  Given this definition, you will be kiting most of your life (almost always when solo, and many times in small groups).  Why?  Well, to test this do the following:  cast your damage shield, then walk up to a nice Hill Giant or Griffon (or Griffawn, Griffenne, Sarnak, Drolvorg, etc as applicable), and start fighting it with your mighty 5/28 or 9/35 or whatever weapon.  Make sure you have a good SoW, so you can run away in 10 seconds when this doesn't work (if you are unstunned long enough to run).
