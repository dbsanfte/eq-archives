Patch Message
[ add comment ] Submited by Porticemaster

 *Level 1 Character Purge*

The Level 1 character purge announced several weeks ago will take place on Wednesday, September 27, 2000, at 7:00am PST (GMT-7). Any level 1 characters that haven't been played within the previous 30 days will be deleted. If you wish to keep any level 1 characters that you may have, please log in and play them between now and 9/27.


***Patch Day***

As a testament to our on-going goal to constantly provide more content for EverQuest players, today's patch primarily consists of *many* content-additions to the game.

*New Epic Quests*

We are pleased to announce that we have implemented new "Fiery Avenger" style quests for every class in the game (including paladins). The ultimate reward for each quest boasts a custom model with unique particle effects. We think that you will be pleased.

Good luck to everyone on your quest.

*Class Issues*

Rangers:

Last week we completed in-depth testing of melee and defensive skills for all melee-centric classes. In the course of these tests we determined that the ranger class was not performing to its intended level, and have made the following changes:

- The "Jolt" spell has had its casting time reduced.
- The Skillcap on Dodge and Parry has been increased.

Monks:

High-level monks can now carry increased weight without penalty. The additional allowance is granted at levels 55 and 60.

Cleric:

Celestial Elixir was failing due to multi-tick cure components that are incompatible with that configuration. We have therefore removed the cure components and increased the healing provided by this spell.

After a great deal of consideration, we have concluded that the Mark of Karn spell is imbalanced and requires correction. This spell's current function makes melee classes without dual-wield far less desirable members of the group. As such, the spell has had its duration decreased, its recast-time increased, and its "heal per hit" value decreased.


*Fungal Regrowth*

"Fungal Regrowth" is now a self-only spell. It was being used as an offensive spell by virtue of its "snare" effect upon the target. Furthermore, we ruled out making it "group only" as with other spells that have this issue, due to a class-balance concern. The spell was reducing the group's desire for classes capable of casting regenerative magic.


*Attack Speed Buffs*

In this patch we corrected a bug that would cause any "Haste" item combined with any "Haste" spell to automatically yield maximum haste, regardless of the level of "Haste" granted by each individual component. This fix increases the usefulness of better "Haste" equipment and spells. The downside to this is that those who usually have both items and spells active will see a reduced attack-speed unless outfitted with the best equipment and spells.


*High-Level Dungeon Balancing*

Veehshan's Peak and Sebilis have received loot adjustments in order to address disparities in risk and reward. For the most part, the quality of the "common" loot in Veeshan's Peak is at least as good as the "rares" from Sebilis.


*Low-Delay Weapons and Damage Bonus Changes*

In accordance with our announcement several weeks ago, two low-delay weapons can now only be equipped in the off-hand. Included in this change are the Mosscovered Twig and the Barbed Scale Whip. The delay on these weapons and the associated damage bonus to weapons in the primary hand made these weapons much too powerful for their availability and the risk associated with their acquisition.

In addition, two-handed weapons have had their damage-bonus modified. The damage-bonus for low-delay two-handed weapons (27 or below) created a problem similar to the weapons above and has been reduced. The damage-bonus has not changed for normal-delay (28-39) two-handed weapons. The damage bonus for high-delay two-handed weapons (40+) has been increased.


*Server Selection Screen*

It has come to our attention that new players often think that the high-population servers are better, based upon the logic that if everyone else is there, it must be a good server. However, in Massively-Multiplayer games, less populated servers are often considered superior for gameplay. Therefore we have marked servers that would benefit from new characters as preferred servers, listing them in "green" in the server selection window. The preferred servers have populations such that an influx of new people would benefit the server rather than cause overpopulation.


*Custom Emotes and AFK*

Animations with custom emotes have been implemented. Current canned emotes such as /point allow a target other than the current target to be specified. That will continue to work. So if you type "/point Baobob", the usual text will come up. However, you can add custom text to the emote, but you must specify the target as "say" as the target. For instance:

/point say points at a gnoll, indicating that he is ready to kill it.

...your character will execute the /point animation, and the text output will be: "CharacterName points at a gnoll, indicating that he is ready to kill it."

Finally, you can now set a custom message for when you are /AFK. Type "/AFK " (without the brackets) to set your custom AFK message. For instance, if you type "/afk Grabbing a snack", you will reply with "Grabbing a snack" when someone sends you a /tell.


*Skill Gain*

The skill-gain rate of many skills is now further modified by character stats. This will allow those with natural affinities towards certain skills to improve at a greater rate.


*Trade Skills Enhanced*

Many of the trade skills in game have received massive enhancements, including dozens of new craftable items, new fishing and foraging items, and other things to be discovered once in game.

In addition, trade-skill specialization has been implemented. Players will be able to exceed 200 points of skill in ONE of the generally available tradeskills. This includes:

- Baking
- Blacksmithing
- Brewing
- Jewelcraft
- Tailoring
- Fletching
- Pottery

Once above 200 points in one of these skills, no other skill will be able to exceed 200. Specialization restrictions do not include class or race-specific trade skills such as Alchemy, Make Poison, and Tinkering. Players with race or class-specific trade skills will be able to exceed 200 points of skill in that skill, PLUS any generally available trade skill.

The enhancement of trade skills is an on-going project. Further enhancements will be made in the coming weeks.

- The EverQuest Team

 Hello all,

I'm still trying to get together a list of changes for this Friday's patch and will have final data tomorrow, but either that one or the one after will include some new features in the in-game chat channels.

@C - Returns the class of the target (only works on Player characters)

So if you type "/point Abashi", the usual text will come up. However, you can add custom text to the emote, but you must specify the target as "say" (getting a bit complicated now, sorry). For instance, if my current target is "a gnoll", and I were to type:

/point say points at @T, indicating that he is ready to kill @O.

...my character would execute the /point animation, and the text output would be:

"Abashi points at a gnoll, indicating that he is ready to kill it."

Custom text for canned animations must be directed at a target named "say".

Ranger Upgrades!
[ add comment ] Submited by Porticemaster

 After a LOT of internal testing, interacting with the community, and major debate, we've decided to upgrade Rangers as follows:

We will be upping their dodge and parry to near Warrior levels. I don't want to get into specifics yet because we are still fine tuning, but the end result is that a Ranger should be significantly more survivable.

I'm personally happy with the solution because, rather than just increasing defense and making them more Warrior-like, we've upped skills that are more traditionally associated with Rangers (the view that they are nimble, chain-wearing fighters who often avoid attacks).

I'd also like to pre-announce a future goal and policy change:

Soon (we'll announce a date in the future), we're going to completely stop adjusting game balance as it relates to tweaking classes. Recently, we've made more changes than usual due to the level limits being raised from 50 to 60. Soon, however, we're going to come to a point where it stops.

We'll continue to add content (i.e. items, spells, etc.) and game balance will still be tweaked (and will continue to be tweaked as long as we do add content). But class balance changes as a whole will cease.

The Ranger upgrade is part of this. I'd say that hopefullly by early 2001 we'll be done.

Abashi or I will post more on this as we work out the plan in detail internally.

-Brad

Discussion of the Week: Dynamic Events
[ add comment ] Submited by Porticemaster

 I think that there’s a common misconception going around that, “All dynamic events consist of GMs going on a killing spree.”

This isn’t the case, as there are many different types of dynamic events, ranging from the typical raid, to story-telling events, to epic quests with a vast story-line behind them.
Raids are typically short on story and last anywhere from a half an hour to an hour or two. They usually consist of trio of actor NPCs or so entering an area and running amok until they are killed by a sufficient force of players.

Story-telling events typically have little-to-no fighting of any kind, and are usually used to tell a story leading up to or concluding an epic quest.

Epic quests often run several hours and boast a slew of actor NPCs that both tell a story and engage in combat at some point during the quest.

In addition to these, there are also “Invasions” which are more static in nature and deal with a temporary data reset of the zone. Examples of this have been the period undead invasions of Qeynos Hills, or the recent one in Najena that was launched with a small epic quest.

We realize and agree that there have been problems publicizing the story behind dynamic events, and will be launching a new section of our website in the next couple of days to deal with that issue. However, the question I would like to pose is, what do you want to see in the way that dynamic events are handled? It seems that no matter the event, or the story behind it, there are always some people who felt that the event was nothing but a “killing spree”.

What can we do to make these events more enjoyable for the players?

-Gordon

More on VP from Brad
[ add comment ] Submited by Porticemaster

 The following was my third post regarding the subject prior to the thread corrupting (*sigh*):

I don't mean this in a condescending way at all, but I honestly wish some of you would at least try to put yourselves in our shoes.

What options do we have in these situations?

First, let's establish a few facts:

1. This is a dynamic game. It may not be as dynamic as some would like with massive invasions and huge volcanic eruptions and entire NPC governments taking action that affect players' lives, but it DOES change. We add to the game, we change the game, and some times we take away. (Actually, it makes me worry for when the technology is such that we can do all of that stuff -- how will people respond... 'That volcano created a mountain where my favorite hunting grounds were and blocked the entrance to my favorite dungeon and now I have to walk farther to the new entrance at the top of the crater! You nerfed me!').

We cannot have stagnant games, yet when the status quo changes, SOMEBODY always get's upset, especially if the change is not universally positive (and what change is?).

2. We're not perfect! We will make mistakes. We will continue to make mistakes as long as we continue to add content, and things will be out of balance in the future.

3. We learn and get better. For example, lots of universal class non-specific loot like Rubicite is bad.

So, what options do we have:

1. We could just leave mistakes and aspects of the game that didn't turn out as planned. I think that leads to stagnation. I also think it leaves areas of the game untouched... empty zones, ignored quests, etc.

2. We could upgrade the VP items that are weaker to what they should be. This doesn't address the other items that are too powerful relative to their difficulty to obtain, but it would address one side of the problem. The problem is that they'd be comparable then most likely to the other broken items. Many players would then simply try to obtain the broken items since they are as good as the upgraded VP items but easier to get. And we'd still have broken items.

3. We could upgrade the VP items and make them better than the non-VP broken items (and not adjust encounter difficulty). Here's where the MUDflation comes in. Now, what have we done to all the other items in the game... they're all indirectly weaker now. And, we've set a new (lower) standard of risk vs. reward. The game becomes less challenging, etc.

4. We could upgrade the VP items and make them better than the non-VP broken items (and then re-adjust NPC encounter difficulty throughout the game as well as other treasure tables everywhere, raising the entire risk/reward ratio but keeping it balanced). This, by the way, is what the infamous mud wimping article would have us do -- either that, or nothing. Basically, it's a ton of work and would probably introduce a whole slew of new problems and potential for mistakes. Bluntly, we'd rather allocate those resources elsewhere.

So, our solution is to identify the non-VP items that are out there that are too powerful and probably move them to VP. And then take some underpowered VP items and move them elsewhere AND/OR make some new items to replace the over-powered ones.

I also want to emphasize that there really aren't that many items. Also, just to let you know, MORE of this is going to occur when EQ Live starts revamping old world zones (Permafrost, Najena, etc.) We will be adding new content AND moving existing content around.

I know this isn't compatible with some players' feelings that how and where things are in the game the day they started playing MUST also be the same 3, 6 or 12 months later. But, with all due respect, that's simply NOT how MMORPGs work. As mentioned earlier, if anything, MMORPGs will become MORE dynamic and less predictable/dependable in the future.

In closing, I'll quote from one of the EQ Live game designers emails to me regarding some of the items that very well might change -- I want to emphasize again that there aren't that many we're talking about:

"Broken Weapons include: Blade of the Black Dragon Eye, Warding Star, Runed Fighters Staff (Somewhat easy to acquire, godly proc ratio), Moss Covered Twig (we all know this one), etc.

Those are a few examples of broken items which have been acquired by players and severely hamper our attempts to create new and interesting loot. The players should be made happy soon however. We just went through and nerfed the drops of those broken weapons and "added new content" -- replacement new items. Near copies of some of the broken weapons are being placed into VP." -EQ Live Game Designer

-Brad

p.s. I know this might encourage people to camp more short-term, but I wanted to be up front and detailed with all of you nonetheless. Heh, everything's a trade-off.

Dupe Bug: Reward Program
[ add comment ] Submited by Porticemaster

 Hello all,

For those who are new to online games, a “dupe” is a bug that allows a player to “duplicate” or “copy” items, creating two items where once there was one. We've heard some reports that there may be a dupe bug somewhere in game. While we're confident that we do not have any current issues of this type, we'd like to provide an incentive to everyone to report such things should they become aware of them.

The FIRST person to submit reproduction steps for any dupe bug will receive the following: a) A free EverQuest account. We'll mark your current account as free, or provide you with a new, free account, and b) Subject to approval by us, the title of your choice will be applied to one of the characters on your account.

To qualify, you must include all of the information below. Furthermore, THE REPRODUCTION INSTRUCTIONS MUST BE CLEAR, CONCISE, AND PRESENT LOGICAL STEPS ALLOWING OUR TEST DEPARTMENT TO REPRODUCE THE BUG. Send an email with a subject line of "Dupe Bug Report" to eqmail@verant.com containing the following:

1. Station Name
2. Real Name
3. Phone Number (Day and Evening)
4. Primary Play Server
5. Primary Character Name
6. Complete reproduction instructions.

“Dupe Bug Reports” will only be considered if they contain all necessary elements of information including complete instructions for reproducing the bug. While we appreciate notification of all potential problems, only concrete first-hand experiences will be considered for the award.

Discussion Thread of the Week
[ add comment ] Submited by Porticemaster

 Let’s talk about “fun”.

Last week I queried everyone’s thoughts regarding a “Discussion of the Week”. Though the number of different responses was almost as great as the number of posts, many of them dealt with fun, or with concepts related to fun.

I think that it can generally be agreed that EQ’s customer base is very diverse, and with that diversity comes many different types of players. EQ strives to provide as many different avenues for “fun” as possible, leaving it up to the player to make decisions regarding what avenues they wish to pursue. Some find the social aspect of the game to be the most fun, while others have a good time by advancing in levels. Some measure it in items, while others prefer character development along the lines of mastering trade skills or languages. Still others want to personally witness as much as possible within game.

Our goal with EverQuest is to entertain you for the greatest amount of time possible, and to do this we must provide activities that the players consider “fun” in nature. I’ve spoken many times about the concepts of risk and reward, but those concepts can be broken down to the perception of fun. Setting pure socialization aside, as it is one way of having “fun” that does not require a challenge, the goal is that “rewards” provide excitement, entertainment, etc. (cf. Fun), to the players that overcome challenges.

Last week we discussed camping, how it impacts the game, and what the driving forces behind it are. I think that from the responses to that thread, we can agree that camping relates to the concept of “fun” in one way or another. Players either enjoy camping itself, or enjoy something that camping will provide, if not the process itself. The social gamer might enjoy camping because of the opportunities for socialization provided in the downtime between kills. The leveling gamer might enjoy the prospect of raising in level. The item gamer looks forward to the items. More indirectly, perhaps the exploring gamer doesn’t enjoy camping for experience or loot, but gets fun out of exploring new situations, for which they need the levels and items to do, without explicitly caring for those levels or items.

The questions I’d like to pose are:

What is it about EverQuest that entertains you? If you had to label yourself as a particular type of gamer, what would that type be? Though I’m sure that everyone has particular issues that they feel could use improvement, everyone either has fun in game, or foresees the prospect of having fun sometime in the future. For instance, there are players that feel that once they reach their particular goal (whether it is one of items or levels), they *then* can go have fun.

If you are of the type that does not feel they are having fun now, and foresees fun once reaching a specific goal, what is that goal? What is it about that goal that makes you feel you can only have fun once you reach it? Internally, we refer to it as a “comfort-level”. What defines that comfort-level to you? What do you foresee will be “fun” once you’ve reached that comfort-level?

When Kunark came out, we raised the level cap. If you define yourself as one who must achieve a comfort level prior to having “fun”, did the raising of the level limit also “raise the bar” of your comfort level, effectively putting you far below it? Or did you enjoy the level-cap increase? I ask because before the level cap was raised there were thousands of people who were out having “fun” in game. Once the cap was raised, the focus for these people quickly shifted back to the “must-level-fast” philosophy.

Assuming that you have “fun” outside the tangible effects of levels and items, do you feel that “fun” is a just and suitable reward for a risk? Do you have fun outside of the scope of items and levels? Will you give some examples of where you’ve had fun without a tangible reward? While I understand that players who value only items and levels typically do not have fun outside of the acquisition of those rewards, I would imagine that most fall somewhere in between one player-type an another rather than directly in the middle of one of the ones I’ve defined.

If you could make any change to the game, feasible or otherwise, to increase the amount of entertainment you get or fun you have in the game given your particular gaming style, what would that change be? Other thoughts? I’m looking forward to seeing discussions between the players on their various perceptions of fun.

This should make for a pretty good discussion. Often here on the boards, we fail to get a good impression of the different types of gamers out there. It’s not unusual to see two posts on the opposite side of an issue related to “fun” claim to both represent the majority. I really doubt there is a “majority” for most issues in EverQuest, because as in life, very few things are black and white.

This is an opportunity to jump in and be heard.

-Gordon
