I had a long argument regarding networking ethics on IRC, and this issue came up about a month ago. Because of the privacy laws, it would be extremely illegal for verant to send any unsolicited information from your computer back to their network, it's just not legal without express permission of the user, and I personally do not see anything pertaining to process lists or non everquest files being sent back to verant included in the ULA. The only method they could use to stop WinShowEQ is simply to have the application detect it and arbitrarily shut down with some sort of error code, at which point the error code could be sent back to Verant, and be flagged as a violation. But there is no legal way without express permissions that they can send .. especially.. cleartext data of your system back over the net. I may be wrong in regards to some legal privacy issues, but as far as I know, your application can function any way you want, ie. errors, lack of running or shut down after detection of unwanted apps, but when you get to the net you cannot send any unsolicited information one way or the other.

Personally, I don't want anyone viewing or editing anything on my pc.
It was mentioned that if an individual was doing this to another individual or a company, they would have legal charges pressed against them, and I believe the term "hacker", though it has become watered down over the years, would still be pinned on the individual.
If a company or group distibuting software were to be caught doing this same thing, I believe the penalty SHOULD (not sure if it is, but it should) be more severe due to the fact that they we able to achieve thier goal by using thier position to gain confidence of the end user.
By any means, it is a flagrant violation of a person's right to privacy.
To write a program that searches for a particular application so it can run properly depending on that particular application's existance or not is one thing, but to search for ANY/ALL applications active and then send that information out to a server for personal/corporate use is a breach of the EULA by lulling the user into a false belief that they are not being violated.
I would closely assosiate actions of that nature to tresspassing or "hacking".
The user has just had personal information sent to an unknown place to be viewed by persons they did not give consent to. The laws on computers are ever changing and still new enough to be misconstrued, but that doesn't give anyone the right to, and I'll use Verant's favorite term here, EXPLOIT the ability to take information forcibly without permission then claim protection under EULA policy.
