The Dwarven Paladin (back)

This document is intended to help those dwarves who choose to play a paladin in Everquest. It will focus on both the role-play and game play aspects of this race-class combination. What it will not do is tell you how to gain levels as fast as possible, where the best camping sites are, and how the paladin compares in terms of overall power with other classes. If that's the kind of information you're looking for, perhaps Clan Axepeak is not the guild for you. What I will try and do is help you to have fun, develop a believable character, and stay alive during the critical early levels.

Dwarven Paladins as Characters

Dwarven Paladins?

Many people, accustomed to the old AD&D rules in which only humans and half elves could be paladins, think that a dwarven paladin is a strange concept. Nothing could be further from the truth. Paladins are the epitome of holy warriors, and if there are two things a dwarf takes seriously, they're battle and religion. Dwarves were made to be paladins.

What I will try and do is help new players understand how dwarves are generally raised to think, how paladins are trained to think, and how the two can be melded into a single character. Keep in mind that these are only guidelines, and there are as many varieties of dwarven paladins are there are of any other race/class combination. The important thing is to understand the norm, which will help you create a believable character even if he or she deviates from that norm.

My Honor Is My Life

Trite, but true. Paladins cherish honor above all else. They will not lie, cheat, or steal. They will not take advantage of the weak. They will sacrifice their life so that their comrades may live. If this does not sound like the kind of character you want to play; perhaps you should consider a warrior, or even a rogue.

Fortunately, this all sythesizes well with the dwarven outlook. Dwarves are courageous, and live for the thrill of battle. Any true dwarven warrior would die before deserting his kin in battle, and a dwarven paladin would be even more loath to do so.

Honor is the primary reason that paladins may not use bows or spears - those are the weapons of peasants and cowards. A true paladin faces his foes toe to toe, not from a distance. For this reason, paladins will generally not attack a foe from behind, which is considered dishonorable and cowardly. One exception is when fighting the undead, which are not alive and thus honor plays no part in battling them. The goal there is to simply rid the world of as many of them as possible, as quickly as possible. If that means approaching them from behind to get that crucial first blow in, have at it.

Similarly, paladins will generally not attack those creatures that are significantly weaker than they (i.e. those that con green to you). There is no honor in defeating those that cannot hurt you. Some exceptions may be made for both the undead (which a paladin will generally attack on sight, regardless of strength) and for those races that are enemies of the dwarven people - orcs, goblins, and ogres.

As a final note on this section, do not think that a paladin must blindly rush into a battle that he knows is lost. To die in vain would be a waste of your own life, which would be an insult to Brell Serellis, who gave you life. There is no glory in throwing yourself at that mummy, knowing that it will surely kill you. However, if doing so would delay the mummy, allowing another to flee to safety, that would indeed be an act worthy of a paladin - and of song.

Dwarven Paladins and Other Dwarven Classes

How do paladins get on with clerics, rogues and warriors? I'm glad you asked!

Paladins and clerics are, naturally, very friendly toward one another. This can be seen clearly in the design of Kaladim, the dwarven city. Underfoot Hall and Underfoot Cathedral are located right next to one another, so that the paladins and priests may keep in close touch with each other. Paladins have a deep respect for the bond that clerics have with Brell Serellis, while clerics value the strength paladins contribute to the war against evil and the undead.

Paladins are also generally on good terms with warriors, though they don't give them the same respect they give clerics. While they admire a warrior's superior battle skills, they generally feel that warriors lack the discipline that is required of paladins. This isn't quite as obvious in dwarves as it is in humans, or especially high elves, as all dwarves share a certain battle lust that goes beyond the boundaries of discipline. However, dwarven paladins often feel that their warrior brethren aren't quite serious enough about battling evil. They may think that warriors engage in the war against evil purely for the thrill of battle, not for the goals that paladins have dedicated themselves to.

Paladins do not like rogues. Rogues are sneaky, dishonest, and dishonorable. They lie, they cheat, and they steal. They are the antithesis of all that paladins stand for. A paladin may tolerate a rogue in his party, but he will not speak to him unless he must, and when he must don't expect him to be polite. Most rogues would be better off not letting on what guild they belong to when in the company of a paladin.

Game Play Tips

Getting Started

Your dwarven paladin will start his career in Kaladim, the dwarven city. The good news is, Kaladim is not hard at all to navigate once you get to know it. The better news is that dwarves start out with better than normal Sense Heading skill, so I can easily give you directions using North, South, East and West.

The first thing you want to do is turn in your guild note. Find the stone archway to the north that leads to a tunnel and follow it. You'll zone to North Kaladim, where the first structure you'll see is the mine cap (with some interesting mines underneath) and the bank. Continue on to the northern tunnel, which will lead you to Underfoot Cathedral. Behind the cathedral is Underfoot Hall. Atop Underfoot Hall, on a balcony, you will find your guild master. Give him your note, and in exchange you'll get a tiny bit of experience and a tunic. Congratulations! You are now a full-fledged paladin!

The Butcherblock Mountains

Dwarves are blessed with an absolutely wonderful beginning zone - The Butcherblock Mountains. Right outside the gates you can find snakes, bats, dwarven skeletons, and goblin whelps - all wonderful prey for new paladins. The whelps sometimes carry sacks of various sorts, and the skeletons often have weapons, so these are particularly choice prey.

Once you feel a bit bolder and stronger, you can take the road to the right up to the docks. Near the farthest guardhouse you'll find more snakes and bats, plus worker scarabs, rock spiderlings, goblin grunts, and krag chicks. The krags are not hostile, but if you kill enough of them they will be. This can be a problem, since krags tend to appear in groups and will aid each other in fights. They're also stronger than anything you fought so far, by a significant margin. No matter what you're fighting, don't be afraid to run to the guards for help if you need it. If you get down to half of your health and think you might lose, make a run for it. If you wait much longer, you're sure to die if you run.

Once these hunting grounds get too easy, the road leading away to the left from Kaladim will take you to the crossroads. All sorts of fun can be had in that general vicinity, but I'll leave those discoveries for you. Have fun!

Paladin Powers

A word of warning - the early life of a paladin is not an easy one. They are not quite as capable in battle as their warrior kin (though they are certainly no weaklings, and can "tank" very effectively). They have slightly lower hit points, do not learn many of the key warrior skills, and have slightly more restricted weapon choices.

The only early benefit paladins get early on is the Lay Hands skill. This skill will allow you to completely heal yourself or another, once per game day. (Note: this means a game day of actual play time. Your character must be active for 24 Norrathian hours to be able to lay hands again. There are 4 Norrathian days in a real world day.) The moral is, keep that Lay Hands skill in reserve until you really need it. Use it when the only other alternative is death - yours or a companion’s.

At level 9 you get your first set of spells. I'll tell you right not these are not going to turn your paladin into a magic wielding scourge of evil. A paladin's magic is useful, but not nearly as useful as true cleric's. Here's a brief run down of your first set of spells, and what they are good for.

Light Healing - This will heal about 10 points of damage per use, and at level 9 you should be able to cast it about 4 times if you don't fizzle. It is not much use in combat (you're better off swinging your weapon instead of casting this), but it can speed up the down time between battles very nicely, both by healing damage on yourself and others, and taking some of the mana load off of the party cleric.

Courage - Keep this spell up constantly. It adds to both your hit points and your armor class, which is always nice. It can be cast on others.

Yaulp - While this spell only lasts for 18 seconds, it's cheap and is a quick cast. It adds to both your armor class, strength, and attack rating which is useful in tough battles.

Spook The Dead - This spell, if successful, will make undead flee in terror for a brief period. It's mostly useful for evening up the odds if you have more than one skeleton on top of you. It's also just plain fun sometimes.

Flash of Light - This will blind your enemy for a short time. While it's not clear if it has any effect in combat, if successfully cast on an unsuspecting NPC they will run blindly in a random direction, which makes it useful for getting past a tough opponent. If it fails, however, they'll attack you immediately. They'll also attack you once it wears off, so make sure you have a safe place you can reach before they catch you.

Cure Poison - I've yet to find any poison that really does much damage to you, so this spell does not seem particularly useful.

Sense Magic - Rumor has it that some magic items look like perfectly ordinary items until this spell is cast on them. It's cheap, so there's no reason not to pick it up, just in case.

You don't get your next set of spells until level 15. Patience is a key trait for those who wish to play a paladin.

Paladin Skills

When you first start out, my recommendation is to not assign any of your skill points, with the exception of placing one point in Taunt. Your combat skills will go up with use, and it's not difficult to keep them maxxed after you gain a couple of levels.

Once you reach level 9, it's helpful to place anywhere from 3 to 5 points into Channel, Alteration and Abjuration. If you plan to use Flash of Light, drop some into Divination as well. After this initial "seeding", you'll find that simply using these spells should be enough to keep your skills in these areas high. Cast Minor Healing and Courage on yourself and others frequently. Divination may be a bit more difficult, but it's not a crucial skill and I wouldn't worry too much about it.

Another recommendation is to choose two weapon skills that you want to focus on, and start working on them early. You start with a short sword, so your one hand slashing skill will be the first to rise. One hand slashing offers a very nice variety of weapons, including some traditionally dwarven weapons - the axe, battle-axe, and short sword. The only traditional dwarven piercing weapon is the spear, and paladins are not allowed to use spears of any sort. This list does not include the ratings for rusty, tarnished or enhanced weapons (such as combine or fine steel). Your initial weapon is the equivalent to a rusty weapon of the same type in terms of damage and attack speed. This list should make it clear that even if you choose to "limit" yourself to traditional dwarven weapons for the sake of role play, there are certainly enough choices for your paladin to make him effective in combat.

A good method of choosing which weapon skills to focus on is to choose one one-handed skill and one two-handed skill. Decide which of the two will be your "primary" skill and work that skill until it is "maxxed" for your level. The maximum skill level for a paladin's weapon skill is your levelx5+5. So, if your paladin is 5th level, his maximum skill in any weapon category would be 30. Once you max your primary weapon skill for your current level, switch to working your secondary skill. Be aware that due to their slow attack speeds and high damage, two handed skills advance more slowly than one handed skills.You simply don't swing them as many times in a given battle. You may find it necessary to drop a few practice points into your two handed skill now and again if you find it lagging too far behind your one handed skill.

Rumor has it that the majority of the Holy Weapons, which are very potent weapons only available to paladins, use the hand slashing skill, with most of the remaining ones using two-hand slashing. Taking the rarity of these weapons into account (as of this writing, there are no reports of any players finding one), this may or may not be a factor when considering which weapon skills to focus on.

Conclusion

This is only a very brief overview of the life of a dwarven paladin in Norrath. I hope I have helped you to understand the role of a dwarven paladin in the dwarven culture, and given some useful advice on how to make the most of your paladin’s abilities.
