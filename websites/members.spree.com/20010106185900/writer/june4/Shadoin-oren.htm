 THE DISTINGUISHED GENTLEMAN, SENATOR OREN OGLESBY

© Phillip D. Shadoin

The folks in Rock Creek proper and the surrounding communities have little use for politicians. This makes them a lot like most folks in this respect. Oren Oglesby isn't from Rock Creek, or anywhere near Rock Creek, but he got himself elected to the US Senate and that pretty much qualified him, in his own august opinion, to know the business of Rock Creek better than the citizens themselves.

Rock Creek is a sleepy little town. Although it's the county seat and a good place to raise kids and get tractor parts, it doesn't really rate as a metropolis. It hardly registers even as a small town, though it is a small town through-and-through. After all, the mayor and the fire chief sleep together, being husband and wife and all. Everyone pulls together, except during the Ecumenical Picnic the Sunday before Easter when the Catholics and Methodists pull against each other in the All-Church's Tug-of-war. Folks in Rock Creek mind their elders and their manners, help without being asked, attend church and children's pageants and try to live as good neighbors.

The town is run by a Mayor and City Council, which is always the same five folks who take turns running for mayor. They handle the paperwork that goes along with being a twentieth century small town, but the community is run by a group known collectively as the Locals. The Locals are a loosely organized cross-section of the entire town. They are farmers and teachers, truckers and welders, doctors and businessmen. The Locals meet every morning at Daisy's Highway 66 Cafe over biscuits and gravy and every afternoon across the highway in Clem's Feed Store to share sips from the bottle under Clem's counter. The usual topics of discussion are fly-fishing, high school sports, trout fishing, understanding women (discussed in short bursts only), fishing Rock Creek, and Daisy's biscuits and gravy.

John the Liar might be the de facto leader of the Locals. He's a retired farmer who loves to fish more than farm and loves his family nearly as much. Until the Perfesser walked in one summer day, John the Liar was most apt to be cause behind the mischief. Clem Warden, who own the Feed Store (and lumber yard) is the man who tends to say the least and wield the most influence. The Perfesser, who teaches history at the small private college up the road, is the biggest trouble maker of the group. The Perfesser often is the mastermind when shenanigans are played, but is never caught if the plan goes awry. Rockhead, Big Mike, Okie Bob, JoeHoehn, Spud James, Bug June, Geezer Hoak and the rest of the Locals are good guys to have on your side when trouble starts. Nobody wants to be on the opposite side of Big Mike when trouble starts.

When Senator Oren Oglesby decided, without consulting the locals or the Locals, that a dam on the Flat River would help control the flooding down near the state capitol, it wasn't until Clem and John the Liar read it in the Capitol City News that any hackles at all were raised in Rock Creek. The proposed dam site would be a little less than a mile down stream from the confluence of the Rock Creek and Flat River, which meant there would only be about fifteen feet of variance between the dam site and the creek's mouth. A thirty-five foot dam and dike system would mean that the lower two and a half miles of the Rock Creek would be back-flooded during heavy rains, effectively silting-in the rapids and ruining the trout fishing from the Lundquist place south, some of the finest fishing in the state.

Of course this brought a high level of anxiety to Clem's Feed Store. The Locals gathered to talk strategy and discuss dynamiting the dam and other violent acts requiring the involvement of a certain Senator, though probably not as a willing participant. Whiskey and indignation will do that to a man. The Perfesser arrived in due course and being far less intoxicated than the Locals, suggested an alternative course of action. Instead of shooting him, he suggested they invite the Distinguished Gentleman to breakfast.

It didn't seem like such a great plan to the Locals, who voiced their opinions to the contrary. But they deferred to the intelligence and cunning of the Perfesser, who has always managed to be successful in matters of intelligence and cunning. What the Perfesser knows and that the Locals do not, is that Senators love free food and any chance to meet the people and be the center of attention. They behave as the benevolent lord who loves to wade amongst his serfs, basking in his own splendor.

The Distinguished Gentleman regarded the Perfesser's earnest invitation as an opportunity to accept the accolades of his people for his great leadership and keen intelligence. So it was with great flourish that Senator Oglesby agreed to attend a breakfast held in his honor at Daisy's Hwy. 66 Cafe, the following Tuesday.

On that very next Tuesday, all of the Locals were crowded around their usual tables, soaking up Daisy's finest gravy with her freshly baked buttermilk biscuits and downing huge quantities of strong black fisherman's coffee. Senator Oglesby rolls in, fashionably late, with a pretty young aide-de-camp and a serious looking State Trooper/bodyguard/driver. He works the fringes of the room with a practiced smile and an easy grace, moving from table to table, shaking hands and touching shoulders. He is in his element and his is alive with the energy of his constituency.

Some of Daisy's regulars have no idea who this fellow is, but recognize his face somewhat. They are in Daisy's only because she makes the finest biscuits and gravy for five hundred miles in any direction. The aide follows the senator with buttons and bumper stickers. His campaign for reelection, though not officially announced, seems to already be in full swing.

The Perfesser rises to greet the Senator and introduce him around to the Locals. Daisy, being briefed on the Senator's dietary requirements, brings out fresh-baked bran muffins, orange juice laced with Metamucil, and a bowl of freshly cut fruit. Incidentally, these items will make a permanent appearance on her menu after the elder Locals decide that though the biscuits and gravy are superb, those fresh-baked bran muffins might be just the ticket for their own dietary fiber needs.

Oren Oglesby makes himself right at home, calling everyone by first name, as if he has known them forever. As any good politician is good at recalling names, this Distinguished Gentleman excels. Pretty soon what had been a reluctant mob has become a family affair. The Locals are calling the Senator "Oren" and each is regaling the other with stories and family histories. The Distinguished Gentleman listens attentively and asks insightful questions or offers sincere comments in commiseration. As breakfast settles, the Perfesser asks the Senator if he has ever fly-fished.

"Why no, I have never had the opportunity present itself."

"Would you like to give it a go sometime?"

"Of course, that sounds splendid."

"How about now?"

The Senator consults his aide, who consults her leather organizer and nods in the affirmative. The Senator has all of the morning open before he must leave for a party fund raiser at the state capitol. The Distinguished Gentleman has enjoyed the hospitality of Rock Creek immensely and is in no hurry to cut short his stay. Thus encouraged, the Perfesser ushers the Senator out to his waiting convertible and within minutes the Senator and the Perfesser are fully dressed in similar outfits and making false casts in the gravel parking lot across the highway at the feed store. The Perfesser is a good teacher and the Senator is an apt pupil. They fold themselves into the Perfesser's little foreign sports car and roar off down the county road, to a special place on the Rock Creek. The State Trooper and Aide-de-camp are following in the Senator's Lincoln Towncar, and the Locals form a caravan of pickup trucks behind.

The Perfesser pulls into a field of wild flowers, bursting in reds, yellows, lilacs, and blues. Butterflies rise in waves as they make their way down to the Rock Creek. The Senator is taken aback by the beauty. They reach a section of the Rock Creek simply called the Long Stretch by the Locals. It is simply a long stretch of rapids punctuated by small pools and surrounded by great natural beauty. The Locals tend to eschew the area because the pools, though full of trout, fail to yield any trout of remarkable quality.

Within minutes of the first cast, it is apparent to the Locals that the Senator is well out of his element. He struggles to get the presentation right, often snagging his back-cast, misses his targets, and ties legendary wind knots. But he doesn't quit. Oren Oglesby, who in his own mind must get this right in order to win the hearts of his constituents, gets better. By noon, his casts are not yet expert, but they are accurate. He ties on his new leaders with aplomb. He catches a nice limit of fair-sized trout, not knowing that they are the finest this stretch has to offer. Oglesby even gets his pretty young aide to try her hand, managing to pass on enough of his own newly-gained knowledge to get a trout to rise to her presentation. She is all grin.

The entourage retires to Daisy's for a hot beef sandwich luncheon. The Distinguished Gentleman abandons his regular diet to down three heaping servings of finely shredded roast beef and thick brown gravy. His eyelids lower to half-staff. The early morning travel, the hard fishing, and the big lunch have put him in a mellow and sleepy mood. The Perfesser senses that the time is right.

"You have certainly caught on to fly fishing Senator."

"Thank you. I had a wonderfully patient teacher."

"Tonight the bite will be better up by the Trestle Pool...."

"I am afraid I am going to miss it. I have a fund-raiser at the state capitol I must attend."

"We hope you will come back soon, and fish with us again."

"I would like to do that very thing, but I am afraid it won't be soon."

"That's too bad, Senator. If you wait too long you won't get another chance. It seems that there is a dam proposed to block the Flat River just a couple of miles from that very stretch we were on today. It will ruin all the trout fishing along that section of Rock Creek."

"My dear Professor, what ever gave you an idea like that? It would border on the criminal to destroy such a beautiful stretch of nature. It will NEVER happen while I am in office."

"I had no idea you were such a naturalist, Oren."

"Dear friend, I never even knew I was a fly-fisherman."

The pair's shared laughter sent a ripple of approval through the Locals. Clem chuckled and shook his head and smiled. He rose and paid his bill, and the Senator's and the Perfesser's. It was a small price to pay to keep his beloved Rock Creek untouched. Later he would reward the Perfesser properly, as would all of the Locals, when they gathered over at Clem's Feed Store for whiskey and attaboys.

When the hubbub died down from the Perfesser's triumphant entrance to his feed store, Clem asked the Perfesser how he had come upon his plan.

"It was quite simple really. Oren Oglesby is a city boy, born-and-raised. A little taste of the outdoors is usually all it takes to get them hooked. I could tell by his eyes when I was helping him into his waders that this was far more fun for him than anything else he has done lately. The wild flowers and the fishing were just the clincher. He will consider the Long Stretch to be his home stretch for the rest of his days. You can be he'll be back through here to fish again."

"What if you were wrong and your plan didn't work?"

"My friend, then the Senator might have slipped on a rock and probably drowned when his waders filled with water and he couldn't get out of them."

Clem thought on the implications of that statement. He was sure that the Perfesser was pulling his leg. Maybe.
