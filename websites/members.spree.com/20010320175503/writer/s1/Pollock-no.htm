 No More Boundaries
© Michael Pollock

This past week brought tragedy as the Navy destroyer USS Cole fell victim to an apparent terrorist bombing during a refueling stop in Yemen, on the southern tip of the Arabian peninsula. Eyewitnesses state that two individuals stood at attention as their small craft, loaded with explosives rammed into the side of the warship, creating a 40 foot x 20 foot hole in the ships hull, killing seventeen sailors and injuring an additional 39 crew members. My sympathy goes out to the families, friends and shipmates of the lost and injured sailors.

A whirlwind of speculation surrounds the question of responsibility for this seeming irrational deed. Military and civilian terrorist experts from around the world are searching for answers and reassessing national security policies and boundaries in response to the crime. I would suggest this might also be a suitable time to reassess some of our own personal boundaries and beliefs.

We will probably never understand or sympathize with the reasons these two individuals had for taking the lives of so many other human beings, as well as themselves. One thing is fairly certain though. The suicide bombers performed this deadly act out of their unwavering belief that they were somehow different than those upon whom their attack was targeted. They created a mental boundary and decided that anyone outside that boundary was their enemy. In their own mind, they created an "us versus them" reality that ultimately led to death, destruction and possibly war. It's an extreme example of the power of beliefs and the firm boundaries created from them.

The creation of boundaries is nothing new. We all do it every day and there are as many boundaries as there are people who create them. There is the gender boundary between men and women. There are any number of racial boundaries. We have socio-economic boundaries, age boundaries and vocation boundaries. There are boundaries based upon body weight, hair color, athletic ability, intellectual capacity, religious preference, dexterity, the type of car you drive, the books you read, the music you listen to, your education level and even the foods you eat. Frankly, boundaries abound.

You're probably wondering what's wrong with boundaries. It's true that boundaries can be useful at best. I love to sit in the "no smoking" section of a restaurant. And I'm sure that most women would rather not share the shower room at the gym with their male counterparts. But, at worst, boundaries result in division, disunity, prejudice and even death.

We are all human beings, no matter which side of a mental boundary we fall. We all have the same Creator, no matter what name you give that Creator or the belief you wrap around It. And when one suffers, we all suffer, even if only in a small imperceptible way. The USS Cole disaster is proof of that. The tragic deaths of those relatively few souls caused a ripple effect that continues to rock their families, friends, our nation and even the entire world. And all because of a thought, which grew to a belief, which grew to a boundary. That is what's wrong with boundaries.

Spend this week reflecting on your own boundaries. In what ways have you separated yourself from others? How does that separation feel? What do you get out of that separation? Have any of your boundaries ever been the cause of another's suffering? Has anyone else's boundary ever been the cause of your suffering? Imagine how liberating it might feel to transcend your boundaries and be open and caring toward everyone, regardless of outward characteristics. Imagine a world with no boundaries - a world in which all human beings view each other with as much respect, compassion and reverence as they do themselves. Write your thoughts on paper or in a journal.

Let us look to ourselves first when we ask how a tragedy such as the USS Cole bombing may be prevented in the future. We are all human beings and children of the same Creator. Consider that thought as you go into this week. Send me an email and tell me about the boundaries you're going to tear down. I'd love to hear from you.
