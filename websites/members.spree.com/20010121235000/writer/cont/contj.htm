Larry Jaffe

Larry Jaffe began writing at the age of 11 when he realized the air was made of letters, which he could breathe in and breathe out words. Says Jaffe, "Life is a poem and I write it." He is committed to returning poetry to the people and speaks strongly about the role of poetry and poets in today's society.

From the sensually romantic to humor to social commentary, Jaffe impacts audiences with a rich emotional range, masterfully crafted. His poetry appears in numerous anthologies, magazines, and on the internet where he has pioneered web sites that feature poetry of others as well as his own. His recent books include Unprotected Poetry, Hate's Not Natural, Winter Rose, and Jewish Soul Food.

At the Moondog Cafe in Hollywood, Larry hosts PoeticLicense, rated one of the hottest, high-energy venues in Southern California Cal. He strives to provide a safe haven for poets to bring their works alive. He has also created PoeticLicenseNEWS, an online newsletter, featuring an international poet's forum, poetry news bulletin board, an open mike, and local poetic news for So. Cal. Jaffe has conducted featured readings throughout the country, including Las Vegas, and three Austin tours, where he has read during the Austin International Poetry Festival, and at such venues as Waterloo Ice House, and Heritage House. In his SoCal home base, Larry has featured at the Comedy Store, Borders Books, Celebrity Centre, the Onyx, Beyond Baroque, Magic Johnson's Starbucks and many others. He has just completed a five state tour with features at such venues as Jus Gumbo in Fairhope, Ala, Myth.com in Washington, D.C., the Iota Poetry Series in Arlington Va., 13 Bar Lounge in NYC, Cantab Lounge in Boston and Prodigal Son Coffeehouse in Hyanis.



Annie James

Annie James lives on the Somerset levels in the West of England in a converted dairy barn surrounded by fields and bound on the South by the River Sheppey and to the north by a road that, in living memory, was only a cart track.
Before being diagnosed with narcolepsy she ran her own successful managment consultancy company and in the last year that she worked trained nearly 500 engineers in change management and leadership. Little did she know how much she would have to put those "change management theories" into practice herself.
Since then her life has been chaotic and sometimes catastophic as a consequence of confusing diagnoses and various hard-hitting neurological treatments, none of which have worked.
Unwilling to accept a life of helpless disability she bought a computer and covered it with a tablecloth for about a year. Then she struggled with getting to grips with it for about another year - outraged that the system she had invested in and had yet to use was completely out of date by then - and began writing.
"Barking at the Apple in the River" is her first work. The title came to her one day when, sat with a demon focus writing on her on the computer, she was finding it increasingly difficult to ignore the incessant barking of her otherwise beloved dog Bridget. She did not want to break her concentration to see what was wrong but in the end she snapped.
The black border-collie/labrador cross was stood at the end of the garden besides herself with anger and frustration -"barking at the apple in the river." It was caught, enticingly in a reed bed. She could see what she wanted and she wanted it more than anything else. She had whipped herself into a frenzy of frustration and it was not making one jot of difference. There was no way that she could reach it. The bank was too steep, the water was too high, the distance to it was too far...
When her owner saw the situation she collapsed with laughter, forgetting completely her own frusration. The dog neither appreciated this nor did she think it funny.
How many times, she thought to herself, had she been in that situation? How many times?

JeanPaul Jenack

has held a variety of jobs, including school teacher, college professor, TV writer/director, choreographer, street juggler, circus performer and freelance writer. For the past 15 years he has served as Executive Director of the National Circus Project, a non-profit arts organization based in New York. His poetry and other writings have appeared in 250 books, magazines and journals, and he has received grants from the National Endowment for the Arts and the NY State Council on the Arts for his work as an artist-in-schools. In 1990, he received a congressional commendation for "helping to further the cause of international understanding" through his work in the arts (U.S. Congressional Record, 5/23/90). He has poetry recently appearing or forthcoming in print in Anthology, Black Spring Review, Common Threads, Dream International Quarterly, Laurels, Modern Haiku, Neovictorian/Cochlea, Nuthouse, Phoenix, Piedmont Literary Review, The Poet's Pen, Tucumcari Literary Review and The Wicked; and online in The Animist, Ascent Magazine, The Astrophysicist's Tango Partner Speaks, Aught, Big Bridge, Brew-Net, The Bridge, Calliope, Conspire, Dream Forge, Eclectica, Grape (Grepoetry), Haiku Light, Ink Magazine, Pogonip, The Signpost, Tintern Abbey, Word Salad and Ygdrasil.

Luther Jett

W. Luther Jett has spent the past twenty-odd years in search of an answer to the question: "Why?" or more properly: "Who?". He has had variations on this question published in Salon, Tomorrow Magazine, Liberty, Syncopated City, The Robin's Nest, Sublime Odyssey, ABRAXAS, Articulata, The Burning Cloud Review, Main Street Rag, Bulldog Breath, and Synaesthetic. His work has also appeared in Poetry Ink, an electronic literary magazine, as well as in Afternoon, Pen & Sword, and Corduroy, journals published on the World Wide Web. Alan Kaufman

Alan Kaufman's forthcoming book "The Outlaw Bible of American Poetry", is an anthology of fifty years of 'Outsider' Poetry and will appear this year from Thunder's Mouth Press. His most recent book is Who Are We?, a collection of poems. His poetry appears in Aloud:Voices From The Nuyorican Poets Cafe (Henry Holt), Coffeehouse Poetry Anthology, Stovepiper Anthology and has appeared in Long Shot, Tikkun, Beet, Witness, Excursis and other magazines. On-line he appears in Salon, BullHead, Zuzu Petals, The Cafe Review, Subverse and Black Elvis. His poetry is translated and published widely in Germany and Sweden and he has performed in England, Austria, Holland, Germany and Scotland at the invitation of those countries.

Scott Kelly

I'm a middle-aged Information Systems Consultant currently on a short-term gig in Roanoke, Virginia...Y2K and all that, ya know. My home base is Nashville, Tennessee. I've been writing in a semi-serious mode (as time permits)for about 5 years. I haven't chosen any particular style or genre, but I enjoy writing humor, even though it's difficult to be funny without immediate feedback. The story, Southern Hospitality, is based on a true incident and the places described do, indeeed, exist. The characters exist, although I've changed their names to protect the...uh...innocent(?). I've been working on a few companion pieces with titles taken from the lyrics of the theme song of the TV show The Beverly Hillbillies. The next one is: Y'all Come Back Now, Hear?
