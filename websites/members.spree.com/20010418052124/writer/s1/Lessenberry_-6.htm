 6 Rules of Article Structuring -- Break A Rule and You Can Write a Really Great Article
James Lessenberry

Newsletter articles have rules of basic structure which every beginner must learn to use. Unfortunately, the difference in a so-so article and a good article depends upon knowing when (or whether) to break a rule, not in following every rule every time.

In fact, The Two Most Important Rules have nothing to do with structure of the article or even with how well you write. The Two Rules are based on the timing used to present information. They are: 1) the article must "flow" from beginning to end, 2) if you haven't gotten to the point by the third paragraph, your readers will start dropping out like ducks in a hailstorm.

Since this is the third paragraph, I'll use the old article-salvaging trick of presenting the article's idea "at a glance". This article presents the structure of an article and gives suggestions on when to break some of the rules concerning:
Headlines
The first paragraph
The second and/or third paragraph
The body of the article
A Narrative on the Narrative
A Closing Note on the Closing Note

HEADLINES-- The headline of an ezine article must impress only one person -- the editor of the electronic newsletter. If you use a headline that "benefits the reader", it may never be published. This results in the savagely silly "6 Ways to Write Business Articles", "6 Ways to Write Marketing Articles", "6 Ways to Write Sales Articles" (Get the idea?)

FIRST PARAGRAPH-- The main reason for a first paragraph is so you can have the more important second and/or third paragraph. Sometimes it takes the headline and the first two paragraphs to tell readers what the article is about. Attempts to have a strong first paragraph often result in a jumbled, reader-losing first paragraph.

2nd/3rd PARAGRAPH-- Forget your English teacher's command, "The second paragraph must justify your reason for writing the article". Give your readers beneficial information, not a scholarly tome.

ARTICLE BODY-- The main idea is to make sure the article flows from beginning to end. As stated above, your main idea doesn't have to start until the second or third paragraph. From that point on, try to relay a benefit in every paragraph.

NARRATIVE-- A narrative (specifically the 1st person singular - "I") is the best way to present almost any subject or situation on the Net. People love the feeling of interaction conveyed in the form of personal experience. Entire newsletters are written by the famous author --"I".

CLOSING PARAGRAPH-- The only UNBREAKABLE RULE in all of journalism is -- do not bring up or interject any new information into your last paragraph. You can summarize information already presented in the article. You can even close with a clever pun.

But old, scarred, veteran journalists like me have another rule. When you have said everything you need to say, simply stop writing.
