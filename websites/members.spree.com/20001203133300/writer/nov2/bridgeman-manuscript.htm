 Elements of Proper Manuscript Format
© Gregg Bridgeman


The necessity for a proper manuscript format came about in an age when most submissions were either handwritten or typed on a manual typewriter. The accepted standard that is called the Proper Manuscript Format in the industry today evolved because of the need for typesetters to have a consistent source with which to work.

Today, no company in the world manufactures typewriters. There are several questions in the industry about the modern day proper manuscript format. In recent years, many publishing houses and editors accept manu- scripts in electronic form either via e-mail or as a binary word processor file. (Lotus Word Pro, WordPerfect, ASCII text, Rich Text, or even Microsoft Word formats despite the inherent bugs associated with Word Document Format) The standards for proper manuscript format will most likely continue to evolve until a new standard is set.

Still, it is a good idea to make your manuscript as presentable as possible and conform, in at least the most convenient way, to the standard proper manuscript format. Consider the fact that many agents, editors, and publishers in the present day marketplace have worked in the industry for decades and are still very strict about the proper manuscript format. There remain the curmudgeonly dinosaurs, editors who pull a ruler out of their desk and measure the margins of the first few pages before reading a single word. This is one tried and true "measuring stick," to see whether the manuscript that they are about to read came from a professional or not. Having your manuscript in proper manuscript format may just save it from ending up on the bottom of the reading list.

So, without further discussion, here is the proper manuscript format.

Paper - should be a good 20 pound white stock. No neon, ivory, or other odd colored paper should ever be used. Using colored paper will make your work stand out and will also ensure that your work is rejected. Never use erasable bond paper, even if you still use a typewriter. Erasable bond smudges and editors hate to get ink on their fingers. Erasable bond paper also prohibits editors from making corrections or margin notes.

Only use one side of the page.

Use clean clear ribbon or high quality printer cartridges. The words on the printed page should appear sharp and very dark, easy to read.

Never submit anything that shows penciled corrections. Correction fluids or tapes can be used if you submit a clean photocopy of the page upon which the correction fluid or taped portion is invisible. In the long run, it is probably worth retyping or reprinting a fair copy.

Slug lines - Better known as Headers and Footers, this information should be consistent across the manuscript. A running header will contain the name of the work (or a shorthand book title), the chapter (optional), and the author's name in the upper left-hand corner. The page number will appear in the upper right hand corner. A running footer is optional. If you choose to implement it, the footer slug will contain the name of the work and the author's name in the left-hand lower corner, and the page number in the lower right hand corner. The exception to the slug lines are the front matter and the first page of the manuscript. Slug lines are always single-spaced.

Margins - 1 to1 ½ or more inches around the text on every page. Wider margins enhance the appearance and provide sufficient space for editorial notes and comments. However, the wider the margins, the more pages you will ultimately generate. If you have a very long manuscript, it might pay off to set 1-inch margins all around as this will conserve paper and ink.

Justification - all text pages of the manuscript should be left justified with the exception of Chapter numbers or Chapter headings as they appear within the manuscript and the page numbers in the Header and Footer slug.

Widows and Orphans - Widows appear at the bottom of a page as the result of finishing that sentence on the following page. Orphans appear at the top of a page as the result of finishing a sentence from the previous page. Many word processing programs automatically manage this and consolidate paragraphs and sentences on either one page or another. This is called Widow and Orphan control. The problem is that this alters the bottom margin of every page. Proper manuscript format says that there should be no Widow and Orphan control. Rather, the margins are more important. Consult the help for your word processing program to determine how to turn off Widow and Orphan control.

Spacing - all text should be set to 1 ½ or double-spaced. Indent the first line of each paragraph by 5 spaces. The exception to the spacing is if the publisher specifically requests triple or single spacing as they sometimes will for specific business report layouts.

Other exceptions:

Single-space: Use single-space such that there are no empty lines between the text


but double-space between each item in the following:
Quotations
Bibliographies
Notes

(Some) tables - specific to the publisher's guidelines.

Triple-space:Triple-space before and after a blocked, indented quotation that interrupts the text.

Quadruple-space:Quadruple-space (three blank lines where text begins on fourth line) between the title and text of the:
Biography
Notes
Reference Notes
Acknowledgments
Bibliography
Appendix

Chapter Headings:Skip three lines after Chapter number

Long Slugs:Slug lines, headers and footers, are always single spaced

Space between words: Skip only one space between all words, after commas, semicolons, colons within a sentence. Skip two spaces after all other conclusive punctuation.

Begin a new page:Begin a new page on the first page of every major division of a book, such as a Chapter. Count sixteen to eighteen single spaces down from the very top of the page. Center each heading. Capitalize each heading. Page number is optional on these pages.

Like wider margins, if the manuscript is double-spaced, this will make your final work about a third longer than if it is 1 ½ spaced.

NOTE: Spacing, Margins, Widows and Orphans: In the days of typewriters, the proper guideline called for 1 ½ inch margins all around with slug lines appearing four to six lines down and then the text appearing four to six lines beneath that. Meanwhile, Widow and Orphan control presents a nice appearance in most venues while messing up the margins and wasting paper. With modern word processors, there is absolutely no need for this waste of paper and ink. Let me give an example. If a story is 1500 words in length, using double space with Widow and Orphan control turned on with margins set to 1 ½ inches all around, the final product would be five pages long. The same story using no Widow and Orphan control with 1 ½ space with margins set to 1 inch all around, the final product comes out only three pages long.

Numbering Front Material pages, Supporting Material pages, and the first manuscript page are not numbered (See Next Section, Do Not Number). Other than that, numbering is fairly easy. From the page that contains the words Chapter 1 to the page that contains the words The End, simply number pages 2, 3, 4, and so on beginning on page 2 of the manuscript. The page number must appear in the upper right hand corner of the Header (slug). It may also optionally appear in the lower right hand corner of the footer.

Do Not Number The first page of the manuscript should have a completely different header slug line than any other page. This header slug will have your full name, street address, and telephone number, and possibly even an e-mail address, in the upper left-hand corner as a minimum. It will have the copyright date of the manuscript, the final word count, and possibly your social security number in the upper right hand corner. Below this slug six to eight lines, the title of the manuscript will appear followed by a space and then a "byline," with your name or pseudonym. Four spaces below that, the words Chapter One will appear, followed three spaces later by the opening text. This page will not be numbered.

The Title Page, A Table of Contents, an Index, a Foreword, a Brief Author Biography (also called a Curriculum Vitae), an Acknowledgments page, a Dedication page, a Prologue, a Preface, an Introduction, a List of Tables, a List of Illustrations, an Epilogue, and a Bibliography, an Appendix -- all of this is collectively called Front Material and should be included separately, each on an independent page or pages. It is up to your editor to place the Front Material into the final layout as they see fit.

Tables, Charts, Diagrams, Drawings, Photographs, and Illustrations are also not technically part of the manuscript and they are generally referred to as Supporting Material. These should all appear on their own separate page and the items themselves should be numbered in sequence if they appear in groups. In this way, certain Supporting Material should be numbered sequentially, such as Table 1, Table 2, Fig. 1, Fig 2, and so on. If the Supporting Material is broken down by Chapter, it can be numbered accordingly such that Table 1-1 and Table 1-2 would appear in Chapter 1 while Table 2-1, Table 2-2 would appear in Chapter 2 for example. Where Supporting Material is to be inserted in the text inside the manuscript, as in most cases it is, you must make a reference, called an Indicator, to the insertion that should appear in the body of the text itself. EXAMPLE: (Table 1-1 - RAPID GROWTH About Here)

Tables: Margins for pages containing Tables: 1 ½ inch top margin.

Spacing: Double-spaced between lines (rows) of a table and allow a minimum of three spaces between columns of a table.

Line up: By Decimal

Dollar Signs and Percentages: Type $ or % symbol at very top and very bottom row of a column where symbols are used

Now that you are totally confused, try not to worry. If you have specific questions about the exact format that your manuscript should be presented in, there are other guides and other sources. Often, the best source is your agent or editor. If you are uncomfortable with that, one of the least expensive, though a bit difficult to come by and somewhat dated, is the Writer's Digest GUIDE TO MANUSCRIPT FORMATS in hardback by Buchman & Groves, Writer's Digest Books. Also, you can consult the CMOS, or Chicago Press Manual of Style or The Associated Press' Stylebook and Libel Manual, either of which should be available in your local library. The Elements of Style by William Strunk, Jr. and E.B. White is another excellent source, although it contains much more specific material about editing techniques than proper manuscript format.
