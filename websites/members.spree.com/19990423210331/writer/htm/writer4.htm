Common Pitfalls In Writing Fiction



We must remember that fiction is fiction. It is not autobiography, nor non-fiction. Too many times beginners will write fiction which is a poorly disguised autobiography. The fiction they are attempting to write is bogged down by the restrictions of real life. The author never really starts the story because she takes too much time and space in trying to accurately describe and detail every point, whether relevant to the story line or not.

A great story demands more than the truth. Our real lives take place over a long period of time, there is no sense of urgency in the day to day living we are trying to elaborate upon in our fiction. Real life has far too many paths, twists and turns that will detour the beginning writer far afield from her story.

Sometimes we must reinvent truth, altering it intensely in order to create fiction. If your narrator sounds and looks like you, if she has all the same tastes and makes all the same choices as the author - then the work if heading for a big pitfall - a never ending black hole of oblivion. The author should never be the narrator. The narrator should be a character.

One way to avoid this trap is to make your narrator as much unlike yourself as you can. If you have short blonde hair, give them long brown hair. If you drive a jeep have them drive a BMW. This will allow you to separate yourself from the narrator - freeing you to be more open-minded and liberal in your story telling, allowing you to sway from the boring and time consuming minute details of real life. In fiction it is said that we must not be faithful - but we must cheat on the truth. That is how great stories are written.




COMMON PITFALL # 2

Most of my students, and some of my clients have the wild idea that the ending of their stories must hold a huge surprise, or interesting twist, an unexpected and implausible ending. These work in some genres of course, such as mystery or thrillers. But in today’s fiction, contemporary fiction, the “trick” ending is a signpost which alerts the reader that the author is a beginner. I.E. The protagonist jumps from a 16 story building , the pool man turns out to be the employer's father from another lifetime, the narrator is an alien. These types of last minute surprise endings, which fail miserably, cross my desk more often than not.

So what then must be done to provide a sound ending, an ending with integrity? The determination must come from within the character or from an outside element within the body of the story. If a character changes her mind, she must do so as a result of her own experiences and actions. She should not be rescued at the last second by a new character, or be made to realize a monumental fact as a result of a last minute occurrence. These surprise endings are lazy work for the author, and most times they are also fruitless. The endings must be believable and interesting. It is far more interesting and intriguing to have a story draw to conclusion based upon the character’s own motivation, their own behaviors and thoughts. These stories may be much more difficult to write, but they are also much more satisfying to the editor, reader, and author alike.
