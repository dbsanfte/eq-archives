 Next Time I'm Angry, I'll Just Get Naked
© Mike McMeniman

It is really hard to be angry when you're naked.

Have you ever noticed that? It is also true that our desire for self-protection, can create tremendous conflict - both internal & external. I would like to share with you how two people, "Bill" and my son John, helped me discover that it's more fun to be naked, than angry.

I was between speaking engagements taking place in an office complex with a common conference room. Since the conference room was without a phone, I asked a stranger in the corridor where a phone might be, she pointed in the direction of the nearest office. I entered, noticed the phone on the counter and, using my calling card, began to make my calls.

As my secretary was obtaining a number for me, "Bill" walked up and said "Excuse me, can I help you?" As I was writing down the number, I replied, "Just a moment." "Bill" reached over and pushed down the receiver button, terminating the call. I turned and looked at him in complete awe, the anger welling up within me in microseconds. I will spare you the exchange that followed; it was brief, rude and most unpleasant.

Born on July 25, 1997, John is normally most pleasant. Even as an infant, he taught me. However, when John got angry, you could see it in his blood red face and flailing limbs. My wife and I had been frustrated by these tantrums, until one day, while changing his diaper, I got him naked while he was angry. He immediately beamed and brightened up. John had achieved self-discovery; 'being naked was more fun than being angry.' Slowly I began to realize that within that simple statement was contained a profound human interaction axiom; it was more fun to be 'naked' than angry.

Getting 'physically naked' has a dramatic effect on John, but would get you & I arrested! What I am suggesting is that when we become stressed, either through confrontation or 'lack of control' internal issues, instead of reacting to the situation and getting angry, we should become 'emotionally naked'.

Each of us wears an emotional mask for self-protection. This mask provides a figurative separation from emotional harm when others attack. We laugh at the 'all-in-fun' put-downs, participate in 'razzing' people different from us and are secretly grateful that their misfortune is not our own. This behavior is not really us, it's our mask.

What would happen in confrontational situations if we got 'naked' instead of opposing the aggressor? What if we stopped to help that individual discover why they were angry instead of instantly reacting and escalating our own intensity level? When we calmly identify the cause of a conflict, before we get angry, we can then deal effectively with whatever issue is causing the conflict.

Getting 'emotionally naked' is that solution. This bold step requires that we must be willing to remove our own mask first. It is easy to get angry, it takes emotional maturity to get naked.

So how do we get naked?

First, we must be willing to try. Many of us have spent years designing our social, workplace masks; removing them requires courage. Vulnerability is not a commodity our culture values, therefore we must ask ourselves if we dare risk openness with others.

Second, when confronted, calmly ask yourself "Why is this person upset? What have I done, or failed to do, that has contributed to their anger?" This pause allows you to separate their anger from yours and identify the source of their conflict. This pause also demonstrates maturity.

Lastly, never assume anything. Clarify your interpretation of their barrage with an honest, understanding and sincere conversational tone. Whatever they do, remain calm and composed. Seek a resolution that is agreeable to both of you.

Getting 'naked' takes courage. I lost with Bill not because he argued better, but because I got angry. Feeling that anger, I lost my reasoning ability and demonstrated my lack of emotional maturity. The next time you face a 'potentially angry' situation, remind yourself, "it is more fun to be naked than angry.
