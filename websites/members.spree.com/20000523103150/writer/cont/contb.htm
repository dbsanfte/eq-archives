Stephanie J. Baldock Stephanie Baldock (aka "SJ Baldock," or "EsBee") has been accepted for publication by Cigar Lifestyles and The Writers and Poets Treasure Chest. She has been published in International Toastmistress, Emotions Literary Journal, Scribe and Quill, and SEEDS newsletters and magazines, as well as the e-zines A Writer's Choice and WritersBlock. She is currently involved a collaborative effort to publish a book of poems entitled "Highways to the Heart."

Barbara Bales I was born and raised in Southern California, where I still live with my 7-year-old son. I also have two other sons, a daughter and two grandsons. I began writing poetry spontaneously at the age of 13 (thirty years ago) and haven't been able to stop yet.


Sean Bamman I was born and raised outside of Washington, DC in Arlington, VA. My grandmother (who happens to be a very wonderful, kind lady if you're ever in DC) raised me due to my mother's lack of interest in any of her children's lives. I have never met my father. I have written on a regular basis since childhood.

I am currently a high school teacher in the Tidewater area of Virginia. I teach three literature, two world history and one art history class. I graduated from the University of Virginia in 1996. I am currently learning Russian at night with plans to some day get a PhD in Russian Literature. My wife and I were married during a 21 hour trip to Las Vegas. We just celebrated our two year anniversary.

Ryan Barber I believe I have always been a poet, it just took a long time for me to proclaim it to myself. I am a seeker, tying the truths of the world so that nothing can be excluded. I imagine this is a longer than lifetime pursuit. Poetry is my way of expressing life, revelation, and passion with some semblance of clarity. For now, I manage and buy for an outfitting store, own and operate a climbing shoe resoling business, and a myriad of other odd jobs to keep the loose ends tied. I live when I am in the mountains, church if you will, and pay for my stay in society by working in Albuquerque.


Don Barbera A man for all seasons, Don has been a journalist, editor, news announcer, disk jockey, photographer, photojournalist, feature writer, pants presser for Lee Jeans, delivery car driver, television personality and systems consultant. At times he has been sinner and saint, rich and poor, lost and found and always involved in what is going on. For redemption, Don teaches part-time as an English professor at a local Dallas university. Don has been a professional musician for more than 20 years. His loves are painting, drawing and writing.

He is educated. He admits to degrees in journalism, business, English and other areas but sees little value in them other than he managed to persevere to get his sheepskin (fake) -- but his mother loved it.

Don is irreverent, testy, salty, holier than thou, opinionated, soft, hard as nails, inquisitive and totally undeterred by establishment procedures. Writing: The majority of Don's work has been in the corporate designing training curriculum, marketing collaterals, corporate presentations, speeches and public speaking. However, before joining the corporate world Don wrote and edited for a small newspaper The Pittsburg Morning Sun in Pittsburg, Kansas. He later worked as a reported, editor and photographer with the Tulsa Tribune. During that time, he was also a stringer for the Wichita Eagle and the Kansas City Star. Don's other work includes numerous appearances in small art and poetry magazines and various opinion columns.

Michael Barnett considers himself to be a "defrocked" astrophysicist, having graduated with degrees in physics and astronomy. In order to live, he took a job as a software engineer and has been at it since 1979. He used to write to help himself out during times of depression. Interestingly enough, his life has apparently been good for the last fifteen years. Of course, having a family with two small children does make it slightly difficult to keep up with any literary pursuits. So perhaps some encouragement from some fans would spur his creative juices. Michael had originally published "Toll Road" in the "The Cat's Eye", a defunct ezine. He has recently published some of his science fiction endeavors in the Planet Magazine ;, which currently contains a story "That Takes The Cake". His Web site has more of his writing, including juvenile science fiction, astronomical non-fiction, and religious humor pieces

Mickey Bellman is a forester in western Oregon with one wife, two golden retrievers, two non-descript cats and 3000 Christmas trees. His short stories have appeared in OREGON FISH & WILDLIFE JOURNAL, THE BUGLE, AMERICAN HUNTER and CHEVY OUTDOORS. He is also a regular columnist in LOGGERS WORLD and several local newspapers. At fifty-one years of age, he figures "to be a writer when I grow up." His favorite authors include Jack London, Charlie Russell and John Steinbeck. Stamp collecting is a hobby but wolves are his passion.

Melissa Beynon Melissa is a 23-year-old college student in northeastern Ohio working part time on campus as an Office Assistant. When she graduates with a BA in English in December 1999 she hopes to secure a job with a publishing house to use her skills in editing and public relations. She has a sincere love for all genres of fiction and is rarely seen without a book in her coat pocket or purse. This love has taken two other forms, that of a personal website, Beynon Tales , and a monthly e-zine called Short Story Writers Showcase . She considers herself to be a short romantic fiction writer who enjoys experimenting with point of view and conflict. Her stories have been published all over the Internet in the past three years, and she is hoping for magazine publication in the near future. She believes that everyone is a storyteller, merely telling their stories in different ways.

John Birkbeck My main literary interest is in poetry, and I'm concerned with the great range of what is being written in the present. In my own experience of reading, and in writing for other web-zines, world-wide, I've come to see that there is a lot of poetry being written in in these times. And a lot of it is simply fantastic!
What is incomprehensible to me is that, although the market for poetry seems to be a hard sell, and that there seems to be a shrinking venue for poetry, in the paper world, there are at the same time, more people than ever reading and writing it.
The glory of the Internet is that it's possible for publishers to provide venues for poetry at a fraction of the cost of putting out a publicaton on paper. Thus, these publications can afford to take risks on publishing the new and the unknown and the untried voices out there, that a paper journal, with rising costs of materials and overhead, are sadly, unable to do.
