How To Get Story Ideas

How many times have you read an article or a story and said to yourself “The same thing happened to me !!” ? Then continued on to criticize yourself with thoughts such as, “I was stupid not to realize that I could have written about that”
or “I never could of told it as well” ?

How often have you read a story by another person and felt immediate recognition with their thoughts and feelings? Thinking all along, “That’s what I have always believed” or “ I feel exactly the same way”.

How many times has a personal experience of another person helped you to solve a problem, or learn how to accomplish something you had felt was beyond you? Have you ever learned coping skills, or how to overcome crisis by reading someone else’s story?

You should ask yourself some questions about your idea before you start the
writing process :
A. Is there a market for this type of story?

B. Can I show my feelings and emotions without it becoming a rant, or a poor pitiful me story?

C. Is there anyone else who could relate to this story?

D. Could this possibly benefit someone else? Could it provide a warning, or insight, could it teach, or instruct ? Could it possibly help someone else?

If you answer yes to any of the above questions, here are some tips to help you get started.



There are three basic types of personal stories that make good articles.

A. How to
Which is basically sharing information and experience to help someone else achieve the same goal. These can range from How To Stop Drinking, to How To Build Your Own Personal Web Page, or How To Get Along With Your Mother.

B. Personal accounts
An experience you’ve had, or someone close to you has had, that would benefit others through sharing. These can range from : How I Felt and Handled Breast Cancer, to What Sexual Harassment Actually Feels Like.

C. Real Life Dramas
An event that has traumatized or added adventure to you life, or someone else’s life. These can range from: Trapped On Highway 45 In An Overturned Miata, to The Parachute Jump That Went Awry.



Other rules for writing about personal experiences are the same as the rules for writing a good article of any kind.
A. Get the hook !
You have to open with a grabbing line that will interest, as well as inform your readers. You need to let them know in a powerful and interesting way what this article is going to be about. I.E. I was 30 years old, imagine my shock when the doctor told me, “ You have a spot on your chest x-ray”.

B. Follow the leader !
Once you have the hook, you must immediately follow it up with some information about what lies ahead. This is the crucial point to determine whether you’ve landed your reader, or whether they are going to wiggle off of that carefully placed hook.

C. Organization is essential.
Try and relive whatever experience you are writing about. Draw out a timeline and be certain that you have the events down in the order in which they occurred. You lose credibility if you falter in this area.

D. Interest
The story has to hold the reader's interest. They want both fact and emotion. They are seeking knowledge, but also seeking communion. The fastest way to loose reader interest is to be like Joe Friday - “Just the facts ma’am” - will not work here, you have to tie the facts together in a bundle of emotion. Tell your feelings of joy or sorrow, apprehension or anticipation, tension or stress, or your carefree, ever-loving attitude. The reader wants to see a part of herself here, not just the details.

E. The beginning
You should always end your work by referring back to the starting point, the idea that drew the reader into the story in the first place. I. E. My battle with my foreman slowed me down for a bit in the workplace. Now I look back and realize that I would have never had the opportunities I now have if I was still working with that jerk.
