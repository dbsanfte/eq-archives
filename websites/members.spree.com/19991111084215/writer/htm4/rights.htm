Publishing And Your Rights


Leslie Blanchard
Copyright Leslie Blanchard 1996


Copyright Information For Print Publications

There seems to be a lot of misconception regarding copyright. Some writers mistakenly believe that their work is not legally copyrighted until they receive notification from the Copyright Office. This simply isn't true. Anything that you write is automatically copyrighted the minute you place it on paper, or on a floppy disc.

Registration will, however, provide you with some added protection. It will allow you to collect punitive damages if you are plagiarized. It also serves as legal proof of the date of your copyright.

To register, all you need to do is request the proper form from the Register of Copyrights, Library of Congress, Washington,D.C. 20559. Fill it out, enclose a copy of the work you're registering, and send them a check for $20.

You can always cut the cost of registration by including several different pieces of work under one form and one title.

It's always my advice to ask that your notice of copyright be added to ANY published version of your work.


Other Rights
Your work could be used in many different ways. As the creator of the written work you have full control over how it is used. By agreeing to have your work published, you are giving the publisher the right to use your work in one or more ways.

One general rule is - the more rights you sign away, the less control you have over your work,and the more money you should be paid for the license. Writers and editors sometimes define rights in different ways. For clarification - see below.

There are cases where editors don't take the time to define the rights they are buying.

The Copyright Law of 1978 says that usually writers are selling one time rights to their work, unless they and the publisher agree differently in writing.


FIRST SERIAL RIGHTS
Basically, this means that you, the writer, offer a magazine or a newspaper the rights to publish your article, poem, or story, for the first time in any periodical. All the other rights remain with you. If you want to add a geographical limitation to the license you can add a qualifier such as " North American."

ONE TIME RIGHTS
These are also known as Simultaneous Rights. This means that the publisher buys the nonexclusive rights to publish the work once, allowing you to sell the piece to other publications at the same time. These rights are usually used in conjunction with periodicals that do not have overlapping audiences.

SECOND SERIAL (REPRINT) RIGHTS
Gives a periodical an opportunity to publish a piece after it has appeared elsewhere. These are non-exclusive rights and can be licensed to more than one market at a time.

ALL RIGHTS
If you sell all rights to your work, you give up the right to EVER use it again. If you feel that you might try and resell the work at a later date, then avoid submitting to such markets, or you can refuse payment and withdraw your work. Sometimes editors will reassign rights to a writer after a certain time period. It never hurts to ask before totally losing the sale.

ELECTRONIC RIGHTS
Your copyright on your original work includes electronic rights. Sometimes an editor will have both print and on-line ventures, and they tend to feel that electronic rights should be extended to them as a matter of course when they buy any rights. Most writers don't agree. Electronic rights are another part of your bargaining power and should not be taken lightly.
