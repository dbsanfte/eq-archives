How To Quote Others

Copyright Leslie Blanchard



The art of quoting is tricky business.

While plagiarism suits are rare, they always make headlines, and strike fear in the hearts of writers. Most writers are extremely careful and accurate in reporting the sources they borrow from. But there are also a lot of questions regarding circumstances and rights when quoting. Even experienced authors don’t always know all the answers. Below are some specific points that will help protect authors.



WHAT IS FAIR USE?

Fair use is detailed in the Copyright Act - although anytime we are dealing with “Intellectual property” rules and guidelines are very esoteric. The Copyright Act says that 4 things need to be considered in determining what is and is not fair use.
a. The amount of the work you use from another source in relation to the entire length of the original material from the source.
b. The nature, purpose, and character of the use.
c. The nature of the original copyrighted work.
d. The effect of the use on the value and marketability of the original work.
Interpretation of these guidelines is left up to the individual court which tries the case, and as such there are no more well defined judgments to these guidelines. Although, most editors and publishing houses have their own set of rules when it comes to fair use. They have the money to afford the best intellectual property attorneys in the business, and will generally prevent an author from stepping over the lines. A very general rule is that if you’re using less than 75 - 100 words from the original source then you’re safe.



DIRECT VS. INDIRECT QUOTES

If you paraphrase the original material, i.e. shorten it, rearrange it, lengthen it, rewrite it to make your point, etc. you are using an indirect quote. The term refers more to the substance of the material than to the exact words. If you use indirect quotes you have to be sure that you don’t alter the meaning or intent of the original work.
A direct quote is exactly what it says it is. If you quote the words of another, verbatim, no changes, then it is a direct quote.



WHAT IF YOU CAN’T GET PERMISSION FOR USE ?

Let’s say you want to write a book regarding alternative medicine, exposing the “quacks” in the field. Trying to get permission to quote from their books is going to be virtually impossible - unless they are as crazy as their methods may seem. How do authors get around this seeming limitation? How are all these expose books on movie stars written without the author ending up in court? Since the Copyright Act only protects the exact wording of a passage, you may paraphrase the remarks made by the writer without express permission. In order to do this you will need to add a footnote to indicate your source.



LETTERS

As with other written material, words, thoughts and feelings that are conveyed in personal letters are the intellectual property of their creator. In order to use these you must have permission from the person who wrote or sent the letter - not the recipient. In case of an interview conducted via mail, you’ll need to include a permission form, authorizing you to use the material from your subject.



CHANGING THE NAME TO PROTECT THE INNOCENT

In this case I am advising you to change the names, descriptions, and location of characters to protect yourself as the writer. If you are writing a work which contains both fiction and fact, woven loosely, or even directly around the life of a real person, or town, - and you can not get the names of all the sources from which you quote, then this is the safest way to go. Copyright may not be the issue here, but slander and liable could very easily be indicated in such cases.



ELLIPSIS

If you edit a direct quote, by omitting some of the original words, you need to indicate this editing by use of the ellipsis. What is an “ellipsis” ? It is the series of three dots that you see inside a quote. “I never intended ... well that was not my intention,” said the ex Vice President.



GETTING RELEASES

Some writers believe that once their manuscript is accepted, the publishers will seek releases for any copyrighted material contained in the article. While this is true in some cases, it is not the standard practice. Most publishers feel it is up to the writer. If you are a beginner, or have never been through this experience, it can cause you to panic, not knowing where or how to begin the process.

The most common way to obtain permission is to contact the author of the work through her publisher. Send a letter requesting reprint rights to the publisher. At that time the publisher will either handle it, acting for the author, or forward your request to the author. You will need to make copies of all documents involved in this exchange and forward a set of them to your publisher. If there are fees required for granting permission it is advisable not to pay them until your work is actually published. The reason for this is that after publication you will have more money available to you , and in the event the editor has cut some of your original material, you won’t be paying for permission to use something which never appeared in the final version of your work.



ANOTHER POINT ABOUT PERMISSION

Remember that when seeking permission to quote from a copyrighted source, most publishers and/or authors will want to know the exact content and context in which their work is appearing. Usually sending them the page preceding and the page following their quote, along with the page the quotation appears on is sufficient.



WHO PAYS FOR REPRINT PERMISSION ?

This should be answered in your contract with your publisher. The general manner in which this handled is that the costs will be shared with the author up to an amount agreed upon by the publisher, or most often, charged to the author entirely and deducted from the author’s royalties. Sometimes, when exercising fair use for example - there are no fees required. In other cases, the cost of fees varies considerably. Remember to discuss this issue with your publisher before you sign the contract.



WHAT ABOUT COOKBOOKS AND RECIPES?

The general rule is that a list of ingredients can not be copyrighted, but the instructions as to what to do with those ingredients can be. Most recipes from a food company pamphlet or label are not copyrighted, and generally material in newspapers can not be copyrighted. The common way of authoring a cookbook is to change or slightly alter the list of ingredients, and to completely rewrite the instructions.



QUOTES FROM THE BIBLE

The King James Version of the Bible is in the public domain, making it free game for your use. Most of the modern translations however, are copyrighted and permission is needed to quote from these.



GOVERNMENT MATERIAL

Most material published by the Government Printing Office is in public domain. In a case where it is copyrighted material, or some copyrighted material is included in a public domain document - this is usually noted with a copyright notice. You should always cite the source that you are quoting from - whether or not it is public domain.



UNCOPYRIGHTED MAGAZINES

As a common courtesy to the original author, I would suggest asking permission before quoting from an uncopyrighted magazine. Legally, this work is in public domain, and permission is not required by law - unless the author has a copyright notice on the particular piece you are quoting from.



QUOTING YOURSELF

If you quote briefly from a work you previously published, then you’re safe. If, however, you intend to quote a complete passage, or several hundred words, from your previous work you will need to get permission from the publisher.
