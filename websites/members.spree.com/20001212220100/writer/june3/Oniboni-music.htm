 Is classical music going to die ?

© Louis Oniboni

How many music lovers are we worldwide to ask this question ? Thousands ? Millions ? I couldn't say but surely a great lot of people so sorry to learn that the music we love is so far behind the « pop » music.

Sure the record industry is sick and, maybe, going to die in a few years but the discrepancy between those two kinds of music is so evident that we may wonder if the classical music lovers are not nowadays a separate race.

The most eloquent proof of this is the ranking of classical music in terms of record selling. Let's take the example of France which we know well. The percentage of classical music CDs sold last year was under 6% and even less for jazz. Amazing, isn't it ?

When you read that the best seller was Riccardo Muti's New Year concert of January 1st 2000( probably the worst concert of the kind of the last ten years ), that the second position was occupied by the never dying three tenors and that the third best seller was the violinist André Rieu who is to be compared with Menuhin or Vengerov as much as music by the Rondo Veneziano is to be compared with Vivaldi's , your opinion will be done.

Music has become a matter of money just as sport to quote an example of wide public interest. They keep selling quality but they mainly bet on rentability. Who can we pretend to be to reproach this kind of attitude in a world where the master word is MONEY?

I read a few weeks ago a phrase written or pronounced by Rick Jones, critic at the Evening Standard. Those were the exact words : « Michael Nyman is the greatest living composer in the sense that Richard Clayderman is the greatest living pianist ».

Apart from that hateful money-making spirit which, in my sense, is detrimental to classical music interests, what makes that so few people listen to it ? This subject would need the intervention of a specialist in the study of human brain since people love that music and at the same time refuse it as a companion.

Yet,everybody, your neighbour, your grocer, your butcher, your mecanic, is a potential classical music lover. You too, reading this article, who maybe have never spent a full hour in your life listening to Bach or Mozart or Wagner, are an unconscious classical music lover.

It is an admitted fact that we are afraid of the unknown and classical music is also a victim of this form of racism which we know exists in the field of human relationships.

Suppose you tell a friend to go with you to a classical music concert. What would his first reaction be ? He would start smiling thinking that you are making fun of him. And yet, that same friend probably shivered looking at Tom Hanks in the film « Philadelphia » when the latter, aware that he has aids and that he is due to die soon, listens to the air of « La Mamma morta » in Giordano's opera « Andrea Chenier" ».

Do you think this friend remained indifferent when, as Tim Robbins, alias Andy Dufresne, locked in a small room, plays a piece of classical music, all the prisoners taking their break in the prison yard raise their heads to the loud speakers like turned to stone statues. Why « Farinelli », « Amadeus », «Tous les matins du monde » have been such successes ? Thanks to the actors, to the beauty of the costumes, to the stories themselves ? Of course not. What made the worldwide repitation of « Apocalypse now » if not the air attack by helicopters with the « Walkyries' ride » as musical background ? What do people best remember of « Out of Africa » if not the moment of the film during which the second movement of Mozart's clarinet concerto is played ? What would have been Visconti's« Death in Venice » without Mahler's fifth symphony adagietto or his « Senso » without Bruckner's music ? Why is classical music always used to accompany masterpieces ? Wouldn't it be because it is the best music , the music that cannot die ?

F.Nietzsche wrote in his « Twilight of Idols », « Without music, life would be a mistake ». How many people live daily without even realizing that mistake ? They may say it is pure demagogy to pretend that it is every music lover's duty to help one's friends, relatives, colleages and neighbours to correct that mistake. I personally know that it is not since I have been doing it for years for the sole satisfaction of bringing others to beauty and pleasure. I wish we were millions to do the same and, as the Beatles would sing, the world would be better.



Louis Oniboni is the webmaster of "The Music-Lovers' Corner", a site devoted to classical music which can be found under the URL http://www.coriolan.com.This site proposes a monthly quiz to win CDs and anecdotes on composers and musicians as well as links to some of the best inernet musical sites.
