 BUG JUNE, THE STRONGEST MAN IN CHEROKEE COUNTY
© Phillip Shadoin

Every town and neighborhood has one. Everybody politely listens to what they say, shake their head to themselves, and try to change the subject. Every town has that one little fellow that has had a chip on his shoulder for being too short for basketball and too light for football. Yet he acts as if sports was his entire life. Rock Creek has one of those fellows too. His given name Robert, but his daddy called him Bobby and his friends all called him Bug. It was a play on his size as well as his last name. Instead of June Bug he was Bug June. Bug was on all the Rock Creek High athletic teams of his day. But he rarely played. He was too short, to slow, and really, the Locals will remind you, he wasn't very good. His single redeeming quality was his strength. If strong men have the strength of ten men, Bug had the strength of fifteen. He was short and squat and looked more like a fire hydrant than a fire hydrant. And he was taken with demonstrations of strength to anyone who dared to question his boasts.

Bug started life like all the Rock Creek boys, hunting and fishing and getting into the kinds of trouble boys get into. But as he grew older, he got into different kinds of trouble. Nothing serious mind you, just more devious than most. While the other boys went cow-tipping, only Bug never caught on that you can't tip a cow over. His not catching on led him to believe that the reason he had so little success was he was not trying hard enough. So Bug snuck out every night until at last he was successful. Bug was twelve at the time. When word spread of his singular ability, the Locals would gather in an unsuspecting victim and get them to wager that this boy couldn't knock a cow down, asleep or awake. And Bug would. Over and over again. By the time bug was fifteen he could squat down and lift the back of pickup up high enough to keep the wheels off the ground, which came in handy if you had a flat tire or wanted to keep somebody from leaving. By the time he was sixteen, you couldn't put enough bodies in the back of that same pickup to stop him from lifting it up. Bug once lifted the back of Clem's flatbed grain truck completely off the ground by squatting down until his chin rested on the bed and shoving upward with his massive shoulders and legs. By the time he was seventeen, Bug was so widely known for his strength, that the Locals couldn't get even out-of-towners to take a bet. He looked too imposing.

So Bug's lack of a stellar athletic career hardly deterred him from bragging it up, especially when he had been frost-proofing. And as he aged, his strength waned. Sure he was still built like a fire plug, but he was a shadow of his former self. Quite strong sure, but not nearly as prodigious as he was. When Clem's clutch on his flatbed blew out and left him stranded on the Star Creek Road on the way back from a delivery, it was Bug who knew just how to fix it. Since it was a relatively easy repair for the Locals, they decided to fix it where it was instead of towing it back into town. Clem picked up the parts and Geezer Hoak, John the Liar and Big Mike loaded up Clem's pickup and up the road they went with Bug following close behind with Rockhead and Spud James, who since he began to speak a while back, took to hanging around the feed store whenever he was back in town for the weekend.

When the entourage of men and equipment rolled to a stop behind the stricken truck, Big Mike looked into the bed of Clem's truck and came to the realization that they had everything they needed except a hydraulic floor jack to hold the transmission up when the bell housing was removed from the front of the clutch.

"Sonovagun. We gotta go back to town and get it." Big Mike has a way with words.

"No problem," says Bug. "I can hold it up."

"I don't think so. It must weigh close to eight hunnerd pounds. You ain't near as strong as you was."

Now Bug pulls himself to his tallest five foot two and affirms, "Yeah, I can. You get under there and loosen 'er up, I'll hold the shifter and keep it up until you get it blocked up and change the clutch plates."

No one, and I mean no one, thought Bug was serious. Even in his prime, it would have been a feat of such profound impossibility that even the Locals would have take the bet against him. Now they saw an opportunity to put Bug in his place, and perhaps get some peace and quiet for a change.

"OK," said Clem, always the one to sense the perfect moment, "I'll tell you when it's ready."

Clem climbed under the truck and removed seven of the eight bolts holding the two parts together. Bug prepared himself with several cans of frost-proofing. When Clem calls the time, Bug straddles the shifter, placing one foot on each side of the dashboard and grabs the shaft right above the floor. All the trials of youth, all the feats of strength, all of what makes up the essence of this man has boiled down to this. He is past his prime and does not allow himself to admit it. He knows he was never a great athlete, braggin' aside. He was never popular with the women, though he married a nice enough woman who bore him three girls. He only had a brief and shining moment as the strongest man around. This is his moment of revival. His chance at redemption. He tightens his grip and sets his feet. He presses against the seat and nods his head.

Clem removes the last bolt.

What happened next can hardly be told. Clem was able to duck out from under the falling transmission only because Bug was able to suspend it for a second. It went crashing to the ground with a ringing thud. Clem looked up at the hole the transmission left in the underside of the truck. Instead of seeing grease and dust, he sees a pair of hands and plaid shirtsleeves. Then he and everyone in the county heard the scream.

"Pick it up, pick it up, PICK IT UP!" Bug held that transmission up by the gear shaft as long as he could, then gravity, weight, and age defeated him. His success was brief. To the untrained eye, it was too brief to be noted. But it lasted long enough to save Clem's life. Now someone was needed to save Bug's.

Bug was caught in the oldest trap of all: ego. That and the hole the shift linkage used to occupy in the floor of the flatbed. And it was a trap no man could escape. There was Bug with his legs pressed against the dashboard, his head sucked down between his knees and his arms gone from the middle of his forearms down. His still had a grip on the shifter only because he didn't have enough room to let go. He was putting up a scary amount of noise. But the men gathered around could hardly hear him over the laughter. Big Mike nearly swallowed a portion of his beard. Even Spud James was laughing outright at the absurd sight of Bug with feet up and his head down, trying to chase his arms down that little hole.

Finally they assemblage heard the pleas of Bug and took sufficient pity to help him out. With three men pushing from below and two more tugging from above, and a little beer onto his arms and into his gullet, Bug was finally set free. Angry welts swelled on both forearms, giving him the Popeye-after-spinach look. Clem changed the parts in the clutch while Bug sat on the bed of the truck and rubbed cold cans on his arms. The Locals left him alone. They would only look over now and again and turn away quickly so Bug Jones wouldn't see the grins.

The truck was repaired and Clem drove it back to town, Bug and his cooler as his lone cargo. An important lesson was learned that day. It took Bug until the bruises healed before he was bragging about the Herculean effort it took to save Clem's life. Of course he never said a word around the Locals. It wouldn't have done any good. Nobody likes to have to holler over laughter.
