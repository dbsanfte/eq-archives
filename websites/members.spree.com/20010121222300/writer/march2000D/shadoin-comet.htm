 CLEM'S COMET
© Phillip Shadoin

Few events occurring in Rock Creek caused as little stir in Rock Creek as Clem's Comet. Perhaps because it was developed and built in secret and that all the participants were under the age of 13 that the Rock Creek Chronicle failed to give it the full front page coverage it deserved. Perhaps because if any of the participants' parents had discovered what had happened, school would have been called off for the entire month of January while various backsides slowly stopped throbbing and the swelling went down. Instead, a clandestine gathering put on one of the greatest feats of human achievement since the original Olympic Games.

It all started because Tom Warden wouldn't buy a sled for Clem. Tom claimed that Clem would break his fool neck and he was in no mood to discuss with his mother all the stupidity his father demonstrated by strapping her only son on a steel and wood contraption meant for all out speed with no brakes, if you don't count snowdrifts and the occasional tree. So, Clem decided instead to demonstrate some of that ingenuity his father was so sure every boy should demonstrate and build his own.

Owning a feed store and lumber yard has its advantages. There's plenty of stuff to build other stuff out of. For rails, an old hay bale elevator would provide pre-bent material. There were plenty of pallets to knock apart for the planking and if a fellow didn't mind the occasional splinter, they didn't sit too bad either. For steering, Clem added a pair of hand brakes which were really only more pallet boards that pivoted on a single nail hammered into the side of the seat planks. For added comfort, and because there were still boards left, Clem decided to add a roll bar/push bar combination that doubled as a seat back. The entire contraption was a little less than eleven feet long and weighed about two hundred and forty pounds. Only an eighth of an ounce of the entire weight were nails and bolts holding the contraption together. An engineering oversight that thirteen year old boys were apt to make at least once.

It took Clem and four others to pull the sled to the top of the Widow Maker Hill. Since the sled could hold all five, no one felt too out of place sweating in the sub freezing weather, pulling 'Clem's Comet' to the apex of the only hill suitable for an event of this magnitude. During the summer months, the exposed rock slopes of the Widow Maker were unassailable without special equipment. During the winter, exceptionally brave high school and college students would mount the lesser slopes with traditional sleds and innertubes and go careening out of control down the slope, ending always in a pile at the bottom. But Clem, Rockhead, Big Mike, Okie Bob and Geezer Hoak were extraordinary youth on an impossible mission. They felt confident that from that day forward, the Widow Maker would be known as Comet Hill.

It took most of the morning to drag the Comet to the top of the best slope on Widow Maker. The racing committee discussed using a towing device for future runs, already envisioning selling tickets to what was certain to be known statewide as the most exciting thing since the roller coaster. There was little fanfare and no speechifying at the top of the hill. Geezer was placed out front because he was smallest and the quickest to get out of trouble, should trouble develop. Okie Bob was second, his growing bulk would be needed for ballast as the front of the sled gathered speed. Clem was third, manning the two hand brakes that were going to save the lives of the Comet riders if trouble occurred. Big Mike and Rockhead were going to push off and then leap aboard.

With everyone situated, Big Mike and Rockhead gave a less than mighty shove before gravity took over and sucked the Clem Comet down the face of the Widow Maker. Only a super human leap by Big Mike allowed him to get aboard. Rockhead was fortunate that his mittens caught on a large sliver and was drug down hill long enough for Big Mike to reach back and haul him aboard. When Big Mike looked forward again, all he could see was white as the snow the front of the Comet kicked up blinded him. Clem immediately sensed trouble and quickly removed the brakes by pulling on them, so they wouldn't be damaged he claimed later. The Comet approached the speed of light in about forty five feet and the swelling had hardly started in Rockhead's hand when he was slammed backward against the backrest by a panic stricken Big Mike. Nobody had ever traveled that fast before, nor would they have believed that half of a hay bale elevator could achieve that speed. What should have been eight hundred feet of careening fun became eight seconds of the wildest ride any bull could give.

At the end of the first hundred feet, Okie Bob realized that his seat was sliding to the right and off of the sled. Some of the boards hadn't been securely fastened, and a few of those were carrying his ample derriere to the right, exposing them to fly rocks and snow kicked up by the Comet. Only fear saved his life, as well as amazing catlike reflexes for a youngster of his bulk. Soon the boards were abandoned leaving Okie Bob supporting himself with his hands and adrenaline. At two hundred feet Geezer decided that the front of the Comet was going to meet with an untimely end and decided to exchange places with Okie Bob. Okie Bob would have none of that, even if it meant getting his butt back on semisolid wood. So Geezer decided to exchange places with Clem or Big Mike and a small bump put him atop Rockhead's shoulders instead with a death grip on Big Mike's forehead.

The stress of the sudden shift in the center of gravity caused nearly all the remaining boards to disconnect from the hay elevator frame. Only Clem's quick thinking saved the two boards that were to serve as brakes. It became apparent at three hundred feet that he was going to have to deploy the brakes real soon. So he did. The sudden deployment of the emergency braking system caused them to separate from the vehicle without any noticeable effect. Clem made a note of this for Clem's Comet II, for at four hundred feet, it was apparent Clem's Comet, The Original, would only be making a maiden voyage. At five hundred feet the force of air coming from the mouths of the five Comet Riders in the form of Death Screams was beginning to have a slowing effect on the sled. They actually slowed down to under a hundred miles an hour before the ground fell away again and they had to inhale so they could continue screaming.

Everyone had a firm grip on the sled with the exception of Geezer, who still had a firm grip on Big Mike. Big Mike was a little glad that Geezer had a hold of his forehead because Geezer's right arm was blocking the snow that had been pelting him in the face. The drawback was that he couldn't see at all and would later have to have the trip described to him because he had seen so little.

A passerby would have described the scene as viewed from below. "It was an avalanche that occasionally spewed out a piece of rough-cut one by four and sounded for all the world like it contained a full complement of dying banshees."

From the Comet it was a terrifying ride inside an avalanche that sounded for all the world like it contained a full complement of dying banshees. Only Big Mike wasn't screaming. He was laughing. Big Mike is extremely ticklish and the death grip or Rockheads legs around his waist was causing him a fit of giggles.

At six hundred feet Clem was already trying to determine how to dismount at the bottom of the hill with dignity. At seven hundred feet, Clem was divvying up his property amongst his surviving friends. At seven hundred and fifty feet, the Comet reached the bottom of the steepest portion of the hill and began to level out. At eight hundred feet several fillings were loosened with a bone-jarring slam as the Comet changed directions with the ground slope. Now they were hurtling along at under a hundred miles per hour and headed directly for Rock Creek.

At this part of Rock Creek, banks are about fifteen feet above the water and a hundred feet apart. The Comet left the near bank and immediately separated from its human cargo.

Geezer traveled the farthest across the field on the far bank. And to make it look more difficult, he did it upside down while gouging a trench in the snow with only his head. They later found most of Geezer lying in a hump of snow and found the rest of him nearby with only a minimum of digging. Okie Bob rolled across the field like a good fat kid should. The snow stuck to his flannel shirt and when he finally stood up, could have passed for a tire spokesperson. Big Mike and Rockhead landed flat on their behinds and skidded in the gouge made by Geezer's head until they were stopped by the snowball that was Okie Bob. Clem stayed with the Comet until the front of the sled took purchase in the far bank causing the back-end to catapult forward. Since the behind side of Clem Warden was in the way, he became airborne for the second time in two seconds and from his overhead position he marked the resting places of each of his friends, in case the search party was going to need him to help uncover the bodies. Clem landed mostly upright just short of Geezer. He simply bent over and lifted Geezer up and the two of them walked back to help Rockhead and Big Mike to their feet, stepping none to daintily on a prone ball of snow that was Okie Bob.

They discovered their mistake when they finally could count high enough and remember enough to realize that one of their number was missing. The muffled cries from inside the snow mound brought them to Okie Bob's aid. It took a bit of doing to kick the snow from Okie Bob, since none of the Comet's passengers had use of their fingers or hands yet. A quick survey of the results of their experiment brought them to the realization they were on the wrong side of Rock Creek. They decided it would be easier to walk to Hoak's to warm up and catch a ride back to town than to walk the long way around to the bridge and back into town. They only spent a few minutes examining the scar on Comet Hill. Then they trudged off to the Hoaks for hot chocolate and oatmeal cookies.

When they got back to town, word quickly spread about the triumph of the Comet Five. Doubters need only to trek out to Comet Hill to see the gouge and the scattered boards on the slopes if to become believers.

The Comet Five never built Clem's Comet II. Tom needed to little convincing to keep Clem on smaller slopes. In fact, Clem gave up hill sliding altogether. Instead, with the hood of an old Packard, the Comet Five created the sport of tractor skiing that is still being practiced around Rock Creek today.
