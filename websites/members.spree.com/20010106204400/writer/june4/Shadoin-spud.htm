 SPUD JAMES BUILDS A FLYROD

© Phillip D. Shadoin

Spud James was the youngest of Daisy James' four boys. You probably know Daisy real well. Now she runs Daisy's Hwy. 66 Cafe in Rock Creek, across the road from Clem's Feed Store. The Locals find their way into Daisy's every morning for the best biscuits and gravy for hundreds of miles around. Each of Daisy's boys grew up with fine features, straight blond hair, cut across the brow, long even noses, strong shoulders and upright postures.

Daisy's first husband Bud drove a logging truck that prowled the hills above Rock Creek bringing down the timber. It was the winter of 1965 when Bud stopped his truck to help an elderly couple from Canada who were just passing through. They had pulled to the side of the road and had gotten stuck in the snow. As he was pushing them out, a fellow logger at the wheel of a heavy load, couldn't slow down in time. His truck slid out of control and crushed Bud badly. Bud James died three days later in the hospital at the state capitol.

Spud James had been just learning to talk when the accident occurred. Within days of his father's death he quit trying to learn. He regressed into himself and never spoke another word. It was easy to see his father's sudden departure made little sense to the child.

Now, ten years later, Spud is a bright and personable sixth grader. He smiles, plays with his friends, is kind to strangers, and he is still silent. Sure he claps and whistles and makes other noises, but he refuses to speak. Back when he was six, Daisy took him to the same hospital where his daddy died. The specialist there assured her that there was nothing physically wrong with her fourth boy. He could probably speak as well as anyone, but chose not to. The doctor thought he might be able to help if he could keep Spud a while, but Daisy would have none of that.

Daisy tried everything from whippings to bribery to get that boy to speak. The boy would simply look at her with his beautiful clear blue eyes pleading for his mother to understand, and finally she did. She gave up trying to change him, and embraced him for who he was. Spud blossomed into a fine little fellow. The folks in Rock Creek grew so used to not hearing Spud that they hardly took note of his silence.

His teachers, especially the young and eager ones, would take turns trying to get him to speak or sing or hum, but he would look at them too with those expressive blue eyes and all they could say even in silence. If the new teacher didn't begin to understand, Spud's old teachers would take the well meaning new ones aside and counsel and console them. Everyone just took to his silence as naturally as he did. It fit him like his blond hair.

Clem Warden took a strong interest in Daisy James and her four boys. Rock Creek has a band of fishing and hunting buddies that go by The Locals. Clem is the son of an original Local and his Feed Store is where the Locals continue to meet every afternoon to make plans, swap lies and taste a bit of what Clem keeps under the counter. Since the Locals met every morning in Daisy's cafe, they naturally watched with interest as Daisy's sons grew towards manhood.

Those folks from Canada Bud James had rescued owned a bit of stock in railroads and such. They felt such a powerful sorrow for Daisy and the James boys that they sold some of their stock to start a college fund on the boys' behalf. They had tried to give Daisy some money to tide her over until she could get back on her feet. She refused. The Canadian couple gave the money to Clem instead, with the express instructions that somehow he get the money into Daisy's hands. Then the couple quietly dropped out of sight only to call Clem four times a year to get the latest reports on how the boys are doing, ready at the drop of the hat to rush to any of their aid, much as any guardian angel would.

Daisy of course knew about the scholarship money, but she knew nothing of the continued involvement of the old couple. Clem knew enough about Daisy to keep his mouth shut too. The money left in Clem's care, and a little more contributed from the feed store, the hardware store, the barber, the plumber, and just about everyone else in town, was pooled together. It made quite a sum.

When Daisy finally spent the last of the money that the sale Bud's undamaged log truck had brought, she finally went to the bank to see about borrowing a little money until she could get a job and get herself and her boys straightened around properly. To her utter surprise, the bank was only too happy to loan her the money she requested, and quite a bit more besides. They were only too glad to loan her her own money.

Every man and woman in Rock Creek knew Daisy to be a good mother and damn fine cook. Her fried chicken and buttermilk biscuits were always the first items gone from the potlucks held during the community's Fourth of July picnic. The banker told her that she ought to consider opening a cafe. There was one for sale, across the highway from Clem's Feed Store, and it even had a small apartment attached in the back where the boys could come and go after school. She would be able to work from home, so to speak.

So that's what Daisy did. She kept her home of course, but she raised her boys from the cafe mostly. And all of the folks in town frequented the cafe. Some even took turns helping out when she got too busy. In it's first few months, if the apple pie was a little dry, no one said a word. They ate their dry pie, drank their overcooked coffee, smiled a big smile and paid the check, leaving a big tip. Daisy's confidence grew, and so did her kitchen skills.

If you ask her now about those early days, she'll point at some of her regulars and tell whoever will listen that your friend aren't really your friends if they lie to you about how bad your pie really is. Then she'll get a big smile and go back to pouring the best coffee for two hundred miles of two-lane highway.

Ten years after Bud's accident, Daisy's oldest son was going to college at a really fine school back east. He studied mechanical engineering. Her second son was set to graduate high school that Spring and already had been accepted to a prestigious college and hoped to study medicine. Her third son was doing pretty well in junior high. That brings us back to Spud.

Spud at twelve was just the same as any other boy his age, except he never told a lie. He wrote a couple down, but he never told any. He started sixth grade without great fanfare. As October rolled around, the baseball games gave way to basketball and football, both sports never particularly interested Spud. He found himself idling about the feed store, always careful to stay out of the way of the Locals when they crowded in. Clem enjoyed having him around. He would talk to the boy, not as a father to a son, but as an uncle to a favored nephew. They could carry on great conversations, Clem doing all the talking and Spud answering in eye movements and hand expressions. They didn't pretend to be friends, they were friends.

Clem had a stock of fine bamboo sticks, each split from a marvelous length of bamboo. As his father had shown him how to use the tools for shaping the sticks into eight identical lengths, he now showed Spud. Together they were hand crafting a split bamboo fly-rod. Hours each day were spent shaping and shaving, gluing and wrapping, lacquering and rubbing, until a superb nine foot rod was ready for a reel. Brass seats and sockets, single wrap eyes, and a hand turned reel set this rod apart. It was more functional art than fly rod.

After the rod was built, it was time to tie the flies. Clem installed a second vice opposite his own. Each worked at his own pace, creating gossamer and hackle flies sure to tempt the most finicky trout. Before too long, it was April and the two made plans for an expedition up to Rock Creek, above the Railroad Trestle and beyond.

Even though the morning dawns frosted and chilled, neither fisherman is discouraged. The younger is far more eager than the elder, but the elder relishes this first fishing trip with a sanguine nostalgia, remembering the first time his own father took him fishing. Clem feels a sadness in his heart that it is he, and not the boy's own father, taking him on this first trip. Still, he is proud and glad to have the honor, such is his genuine affection for the silent lad. With abundant lunches carefully packed by Daisy herself, they two are trundled out of the cafe with a "Be Careful! Catch lots of fish!" and a "Mind you listen to Clem now."

An hour hike later, the fishermen are casting across frosty rocks and anticipating the first strike. The brilliant morning sun has lit up the hoar frosted country like millions of diamonds, making Clem and Spud squint from the intensity. Spud works his way upstream farther and farther from Clem, questing for untried waters. He rounds the bend, but Clem can still keep an eye on him. The boy's rod and line betray his location over the top of the meadow that separates them.

A scream pierces the silence. Clem wheels in alarm. He searches for the boy. At first he can't see the rod. Then he sees it bent over and straining against a nice fish. Clem rushes up the bank and cuts across the field to where he can watch the boy excitedly fighting a sixteen inch rainbow. Clem watches in silence. At this point, the boy must learn to do for himself. Spud brings the fish closer to the side.

When the moment is right, he drops down into a squat, holding his rod high, and bellies the beauty. He lifts it proudly from the water, letting out an Indian yell that any Indian around would have been proud of.

"Nice fish there."

The boy spins to show off his prize, one hand still filled with rod, the other with fish.

"Wadja catch him on?"

"Redwing nymph," was the reply.

The impact of the words uttered by Spud were lost in momentary admiration for the fish and the fisherman. Then Clem realized he had actually heard what Spud said. He shook his head to clear his eyes.

"What was that again?"

"Redwing nymph."

Now Spud too realizes that he had in fact replied.

"Is that so? One of yours or one of mine?"

"Mine. I made it with an extra long pheasant hackle."

"Some fish. You going to keep it?"

"Naw, it's too nice to eat."

"Wanna keep fishing, or head on home?"

"I guess we better get home. Mom will probably want to know I reckon."

"Yeah, I reckon." Clem grinned at Spud who was split from ear to ear with a grin of his own.

The two friends made their way back to the highway and walked along it into town. They have their heads down, not out of shame, but out of conspiracy. When they arrive at the cafe, Daisy expresses concern at their early return.

"I hope he acted all right for you Clem. I didn't expect to see you back so soon."

"He was perfectly fine Daisy, it's just a little too cold yet to fish."

"Did you catch any?"

"I didn't, but Spud caught one nice one."

Daisy turns to her son to see his hands. "How big was it Spud?"

"About fourteen inches Mom." Those are his first words in ten years for his mother.

"More like sixteen Daisy," interjects Clem, always the fisherman and ready to stretch the truth. He has forgotten that Daisy has been ten years since hearing her son speak with his own voice.

"Is that so?" says Daisy, sensing a fish story.

She too is temporarily oblivious to this historic moment, her 'fishing lie' detector going off. Then it hits her like a bolt. She grabs ahold of her youngest boy with a bear hug that nearly squeezes the stuffing out of him. Then she does the same to Clem. Then back to Spud. And back to Clem. She hugs them both at the same time, laughing and crying and squeezing them both. It gets so bad that the boys are fidgeting from embarrassment.

Clem quietly slips away when mother and son begin their first conversation. He heads back to his feed store about the time the first Locals gather from their morning's own fishing. Each compares fish stories and Clem admits he got skunked, to the great delight of John the Liar Clem does affirm that Spud's hand made rod with its handmade fly landed a sixteen inch rainbow. It's not in Clem's character for him to mention that Spud is now talking. The Locals don't ask. Why would they?

Spud bursts into the store a short while later.

"When can we go again Clem? And my mom says lunch is on her for the rest of the month."

"We'll go again real soon. And tell you mama I said thanks, but it's not necessary."

Spud is gone before any of the Locals take note of who was just there and talking.

"Did that boy just come in here and say something to you Clem? Was that Spud James? I thought that boy was mute. What the heck is going on here Clem?" John the Liar asks what every man is thinking.

Clem just smiles and shrugs his shoulders. He is finally persuaded to tell the whole story, but he places far too much emphasis on the fish than the speaking. He refuses to acknowledge he had more than a small influence on the whole matter. He asserts that the boy made the rod, tied the fly, and chose to answer the question. Clem just happened to be there. Even now, Clem downplays the importance of giving that boy all of his free time that winter.

Clem was on hand when Spud was his high school class Valedictorian. He only wiped his eyes a few times when Spud pointed him out and thanked him right there in front of everyone in town. Spud used his college money to get a teaching degree. He teaches difficult children and those with learning disabilities. He also teaches them how to fish. Clem helps out too. And the two friends still fish together now, though not as often as each would like.

Clem left money for lunch every day that month. Daisy put it in a box under the register. At the end of the month, she put it all in an envelope and pressed into Clem's hand.

"God Bless you, Clem Warden. Thank you so very much."

Clem just walked out of the cafe, his hand was wiping his eyes at the road side. He wanted to make sure the coast was clear before he went back to work.
