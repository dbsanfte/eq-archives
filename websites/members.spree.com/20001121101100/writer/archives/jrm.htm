 James Ray Musgrave

DISTANCES

December 31, 1984 . . . I am writing in this journal to keep from going mad as my father did. We met on New Year's Eve during the Winter Olympic Games. I was standing near the punch bowl next to Tanja, my best friend in gymnasium. He stood no more than fifteen meters from us. It was the last dance of the night, and I could see him pushing his mother and father together in the middle of the party. His father was laughing, holding his huge middle as if he wanted to suck it in and become a young man again. His mother, on the other hand, was shyly smiling her Serb smile-a brave and understanding smile that I was later to see under much more trying circumstances.

Once he had gotten them to dance, he sauntered over to us, his dark good looks winning me over immediately. We were 16 then-mere children. The only closeness we had was our bodies. Our bodies pulled toward each other in the dim, dance floor light like magnets-like moths toward the flame of passion; each pulling back-then pushing forward-until, at last, he was next to me and staring into my eyes. I can see his deep brown pools in my dreams at night when the snipers begin their foray out in the streets. His confident, strong gaze can encompass my being and momentarily drown out the ravaging bullets ricocheting off our apartment building on Koshevo Hill. It was this way that evening in 1984-when all was peaceful-when we all believed in multiethnic solidarity. Why else would the Olympic Committee have chosen us? We were Sarajevo, the soul of Yugoslavia, where Serb, Muslim, Catholic and Jew lived together in harmony, hosting the world with pride in our chests and welcome in our voices.

He asked to kiss me when the New Year was rung in. I should have known then that the bullets we heard being fired outside in celebration were ominous precursors to later events. As I told my mother just the other day, "Only bullets will stop our love." A New Year's kiss and bullets began our romance. I, Admira Ismic, a Bosnian Muslim, and he, Bosko Brkic, a Bosnian Serb, came together and formed something that united the universe-at least it was that way for us. Love, you see, can travel across distances that war and cruelty can never travel. With the beating of two young hearts, we came together for all the world. And my Bosko-my beloved Bosko--and I were to see just how far our love would take us.

June, 1986 . . . After we graduated, Bosko's father died suddenly of a heart attack. He was only 45, and my Bosko took his father's death very hard. He told me he was going to join the army and get his compulsory service over with.

On a warm night in July, I drove with him out to the train station, where he would travel to the Serbian Officer's Candidate School of the Yugoslav Federal Army. We had never been apart. When I kissed him at the station, I felt alone in the middle of the crowds of people. Love can do that to you. You are drained of emotion when your beloved must go. The hands of the clock are ripped from the dial, and you stand with your heart broken, holding the arrows up to the sun.

I waited every day that summer for a letter. He wrote to me each free moment, and I almost tackled the postman when he arrived with the mail. Sitting in the corner of our house in Sarajevo reading his letters, I remembered the laughter we shared as we watched my cat, Tito, chase a ball of yarn around the room. "My dearest Admira: Every night when I go to bed, I cannot sleep because I'm thinking of you. My love, you are the only happiness I have. Now all my life points to the day when I'll finish my military service and see you again."

I would also read Naka Gruber's letters. She was living in Srebenica. As my grandmother, she was the only one in the family who seemed to sense the disaster that was about to befall us. She remembered the days when the Serbs were nationalists and racists, and she believed these days would return again after the fall of Communism. "Get out of Sarajevo," she wrote to my mother. "Take the kids and go. The old hatreds will return like vultures to the carcass. Get out before it's too late!"

Father, who had been in Tito's Pioneers after the Second World War, did not believe what Naka Gruber's letters advised. He believed we were going to remain an example of a successful and modern multiethnic state that would remain independent, even though we had lost the militant protective cloak of Communism.

Bosko and I believed father and a majority of the others in Sarajevo who were, for the most part, cosmopolitans; we had television, we had radio, we had rock-and-roll, American Levis, and computers . . . our lifestyles were not that unlike any of the Western teenagers we had met during the Olympic year of 1984. But we did not count on the distance. We are distant from America and from Europe, even though we can see them over our television sets and hear their songs over our radios.

Bosko and I sang together in the disco when he arrived eleven months later. We sang an American song popular in Sarajevo at the time: "We Are Family," by Sly and the Family Stone. We received American records much later than Europeans did. Bosko gazed into my eyes that night of his return and said, "We will make family! Wait for me, Admira. Will you wait?"

Today I am waiting for him to appear. We have permission to soon leave Sarajevo. Celo has negotiated with the Serbs, and we are to finally cross over into Serbian-held territory in Grbavica, beyond the "no-man's-land," over Vrbana Bridge, what we called the Brotherhood and Unity Bridge before the war. But, once again, we did not count on the distance. Men have a difficult time staying out of "no-man's-land," and this was no exception. Bosnians were on one side, Serbs and Chetniks were on the other. And we, Bosko and I, the symbols of multiethnic pride, we were in the middle of their guns.

April, 1989 . . . The Oslobodenje Daily reported a speech by Serbian leader, Slobodan Milosevic. He was speaking at the anniversary of an ancient battleground called "the Field of the Blackbirds," where over a half-million Serbs gathered to honor their 14th century war hero, Prince Lazar. This "Prince" had fought the Muslim Turks at the battle of Kosovo in 1389 and his defeat had not been forgotten. Now, a modern militant politician was using this loss to stoke the fires of war with a call to nationalism to re-claim their ancient territory in Bosnia: "It is a coincidence of life and history that Serbia in 1989 is regaining its state and dignity. So in this way, we are celebrating a historic anniversary from the ancient past that has symbolic importance for the future of Serbia. The Muslim menace is raging all over the world once more. Terrorists bomb free airplanes and machine-gun innocent passengers in airports. These are the invaders from within that we must conquer-or they will conquer us!"

This Serbian threat led to a declaration of independence in Croatia in 1991. When war broke out, Naka Gruber's home was gutted and burned to the ground. Both the Serbs and Croats turned their greedy eyes toward Bosnia and, of course, toward us. But my Bosko was a special kind of Serb-he was to marry me, the ancient enemy, the Muslim invader, or what the Serb Nationalist Chetniks liked to call us: the blackasses.

April 5, 1992 . . . Many times during the week I would travel the five miles between our houses, a distance that I traveled quickly on my Triumph motorcycle. Bosko and his mother lived in a large apartment on Koshevo Hill. I knew the way like I knew the face of my beloved. Bosko was always telling me I was too wild for a woman. I liked to speed on my bike, work on cars, and I can even shoot a rifle. Bosko, on the other hand, is a cool character, often reflective and philosophical. He balances me well.

On this day, I was traveling on my cycle to Bosko's apartment, but the streets were unusually active. I saw hundreds of my fellow citizens: Serbs, Croats, Muslims, and they were all demonstrating their support for a new, independent, and multi-ethnic state. Oslobodenje had written long editorials in support of racial and political harmony, and the people were responding by carrying banners and newly constructed Bosnian flags. But they were also reacting to the gathering clouds of violence in Serbia and Croatia that were forming all around us.

As I was walking my bike down Marshall Tito Avenue I heard the shots. They sounded like firecrackers, but when people began running and screaming, I knew something horrible had taken place. People ran past me, pulling their children, covering their heads, yelling and screaming curses into the morning air. I stopped one old woman and asked what the commotion was. "Those bastard Chetniks-they're shooting into the crowd!" she screamed.

The distance between me and my lover became much longer in the coming weeks. For the siege on Sarajevo had begun.

July 4, 1992 . . . The shelling had almost leveled the city. Each day, dozens of men left us to join the Federal Army and attack us. We could not understand how men could forget the 500 years of peaceful relations to become terrorists in the hills, shooting down mortars and artillery shells to massacre their brothers and sisters.

Bosko was more reflective about the problem. His parents were college educated and so was he. I loved him more at such times. He gave me the wisdom to get through the days of terror, when at any moment your life could be snuffed out like a candle flame. "Violence is easier to many because it is hard work to earn your way in this world. Bullets and bombs are fast, direct and immediate. With violence, one does not have to argue and get disagreements out into the open to be fairly resolved. When you kill the opposition, no one is left to argue with you. Albert Camus knew this when he wrote his famous essay 'Neither Victims or Executioners.' I have known many men whose only answer to problems in life was to take a violent course of action. I do not believe violence resolves the root of the problem." Bosko spoke like this whenever he become philosophical.

Misa Cuk was living with Bosko and his mother. The older Serb neighbor had already sent his Muslim wife and children out of Sarajevo, and he had joined the Bosnian defense forces. Each day, he would argue with Bosko and try to get him to join. "Take this gun, he would say," handing an AK-47 to my Bosko, "because if somebody comes for you and it's time to die, at least you can protect yourself." But Bosko refused the gun. He said he picked it up with the tips of his fingers, as one picks up a handkerchief. "Misa, my friend. I am in the middle. I cannot go into the Muslim army to shoot Serbs. They are my people. And I cannot join the Serbs to shoot down on Sarajevo. My Admira and her folks are here. You have sent your loved ones away so you can be free to kill. I do not have that fortunate circumstance." Many people said there was a bounty being given by the Germans of 500 marks for every Sarajevan killed. Some said the Germans were trying to practice their old ways of ethnic cleansing, so they were supporting Milosevic and his Serb nationalists as they began their extermination campaign. Needless to say, it was dangerous to go out into the streets. But we had no electricity in much of the city, and water had become scarce, not to mention the shortages of food and clothing.

One day, my mother came into the apartment with a bag of something. Each day was an adventure out in the streets because snipers were always out there ready to silently kill you as you stand in line for water or for bread. Mother poured the contents onto the kitchen table and stood back to admire them. Right there on our dining table were about a dozen old bras! That's right, my mother had finally gone totally delirious, and this was her complete mental breakdown. "Mother, have you lost your senses? What the hell are these?"

"Celo got these from the Serbs," she said, "German women are getting coupons worth 50 marks if they donate their used bras for women in Sarajevo. Isn't that kind of them? They understand we must maintain our modesty in these trying days."

"Nera," (I call my mother by her first name only at times of extreme distance between our generations) "we cannot wear these putrid garments!" I picked one up and held it as Bosko will hold a gun. "We have lost six of our relatives in this war, and you want to accept used bras from the very power that supports the extermination of your family? Where is the food? Where are the medicines? Where is the United Nations? All we get are bras. If we had oil like Kuwait, I guess we could get the whole American army! Wait, let me try and call those Americans." I picked up one of the huge, German bras and carried it over to the window as if I were carrying a dead cat.

"Admira! What are you doing?" my mother admonished me.

I began to wave the bra around as if it were a flag of surrender. "Hey, big boys! Come and get it!" And I sang an old American song from the 1950s, "Come onna my house, come on, come on! I'm gonna give you ever-y-thing!"

Bosko and I laughed until we had tears, picturing the look on my mother's face as I waved that old German bra. In a strange way, it was our flag of surrender.

Today, May 19, 1993 . . . Before we surrendered, we were married. Misa Cuk drove us to the wedding in central Sarajevo. I had moved into Bosko's apartment with him and his mother, Rada. The distance to the Hall of Workers in downtown Sarajevo is about six miles. It was going to be relatively safe because Celo Akim was also going to be wed.

Celo is a friend who had earlier taken Bosko under his dark wing. When Celo was 17, the tough Muslim was arrested for armed robbery and rape and sent to a maximum security prison. While in prison, Celo made an important connection when he protected a frail political prisoner by the name of Alia Izetbegovic, who would later become the president of Bosnia. After six years in prison, Celo returned to the streets a hardened criminal and gang leader. His group, the "Ministers of Justice," controlled much of Sarajevo's drug and protection rackets. But Rada Brkic, Bosko's mother, would always find time to take Celo in and nurse his wounds, feed him, and attempt to get him to go straight.

But when the Serbian attacks began in 1992, Celo formed the first armed resistance to defend his city. He used his criminal connections and sly manner to protect the less devious citizens like his childhood friend, Bosko Brkic. Celo's protection and his connections to the exchanges of civilians and prisoners of war were invaluable to us. In a civil war, the distance between hero and criminal becomes much closer together.

Once again, as we kissed, there were bullets surrounding us. This time, however, we were getting married in the center of our beloved city, and all of us were expecting a mortar round or artillery shell to come crashing down on our celebration. It was quite a lavish affair. Bosnian military generals, cabinet ministers and even Celo's prison friend, President Izetbegovic, were all there to stand in the center of the armed guards and machine-gun nests, and toast champagne to the newlyweds. Celo called us "the good and the bad couples," because he would often get drunk and tell Bosko that he, Bosko, was the one with all the courage. "This war makes heroes of men like me because we have nothing to lose," Celo would say, draping his dark arm around Bosko's shoulders. "You, however, are an educated man. You show us the way of peace, and yet we still fight on. I must protect you and the president because of your wisdom. I must die, if need be, but I will protect you!"

Bosko's mother came to Celo in secret one night in August, 1992, after her son was nearly killed by an artillery shell that landed in their apartment's kitchen. Misa, Bosko's older friend, had fled Sarajevo with his gun, a two-way radio and secret military codes from his unit in the Bosnian army. Misa had deserted the military and was now with his wife and children in Serbia. Many of the local toughs were making it hard on Bosko because of his friend's actions. Celo had to intercede to physically protect him on many occasions.

For a momentary diversion, Bosko and a young friend were watching a tennis match on television in Bosko's apartment on Koshevo Hill, and they had just left the kitchen when the shell struck. Shrapnel was everywhere, and they would have surely been killed. But when Rada saw the damage, she decided to go to Celo.

Bosko's older brother, Bane, was now living in Serbia, and he had grown up with Celo. Rada went to Celo's field headquarters on August 23, 1992 and begged him to help her. Celo agreed. He promised to get an exchange for her and two other Serbs for three Muslim prisoners of war. He also promised to protect Bosko and me and to send us later on. The next evening, Rada made it across "no-man's-land" successfully. It was during some of the heaviest shelling of the war, and we considered it a miracle.

However, the Serb program of "ethnic cleansing" had begun that summer all over Bosnia. My fellow Muslims were rounded up and placed in Nazi-like concentration camps where they were executed. The newspaper, Oslobodenje, which was now working inside the bombed-out basement of the once-noble Tito Building, reported that over 200,000 Muslims-mostly civilians--had been slaughtered.

The distance between the killers and ourselves was getting shorter. We moved into the shell of an apartment in early May, 1993. We would sit up at night and watch my cat, Tito, as he would play in the rubble of our room. I held Bosko so tightly then. I was very afraid. He whispered to me, "It won't be long, love. Celo will get us out of here. It is only a matter of days."

Those days were clouded with violence. Once, as I was going downtown to get water, a mortar shell hit a group of people standing in a bread line near Kosevo Hospital. The blood from their mangled bodies was sprayed all over me. Until that moment, I had actually believed I was indestructible. But the screams and the vision of staggering people moaning and holding their intestines inside their bodies were experiences that made the distance between you and death instantly closer. I held Bosko all that night, but I secretly cried for my own life.

The pressure increased in those days in May 1993, leading us to today. In early May, Bosko received a summons ordering him to appear for questioning by the Muslim police. Misa Cuk had been known as the "Commander of Koshevo Hill," and Bosko believed they wanted to kill him, Bosko, as they had once killed his grandfather during World War II.

We had to leave. This morning, as I stood in the kitchen with my mother, she gave one last appeal for me to stay. I told her, "Mum, do you know what you are asking me? I can't do anything else but go with Bosko. Go back to the past, about 30 years ago. Would you have gone with Father?" She finally admitted that she, too, would have left to be with her husband, no matter what the dangers were, just as she was staying with him now, despite his mental peculiarities.

My father, the mechanic Zijo, did not say anything to me. He refused to see what was happening all around him. He was living in the past. He refused to see any distance between his Communist paradise and his living horror. He ranted and raved all around the apartment, constructing candles out of old wax and holding them up to us as though he had invented the electric light bulb. "Marshall Tito is coming!" he shouted. "Just wait, you'll see. He will not let us die!" Father was another kind of victim of our war.

This morning, I wrote a note and left it for my mother to read. It says: "My dear Mum: It seems we are finally leaving tonight and whatever happens is God's will. I'll call you as soon as we are safely on the other side. I most worry about you and father and my little cat. Please talk to him and sleep with him at least once a week. And don't take any more bags of bras, please! Bosko and I have been talking about when the war is over, and we will come back to Sarajevo. Everything will be fine, just like this war never happened. Do not worry about me. Think about yourself. It will be much easier for me this way. I love you so much. Your Admira."

Bosko stands against the side of the headquarters' wall pulling at the front of his Adidas tee-shirt. He looks nervous. His dark eyes pan the room and finally focus upon his childhood friend, Celo, the Bosnian hero and criminal. "Celo, are you certain they have accepted the terms? We will be able to pass in peace?"

Celo turns from the radio and grins his best dark, Muslim grin. "Sure, pal, I get hundreds across every month. Didn't your mother and your brother get across without a scratch?"

Bosko looks worried. "Yes, but I have a bad reputation. They think I was working with a deserter, Misa Cuk. Did you tell them I am not a traitor?"

Celo looks down at his polished shoes. He is wearing new clothes these days. The civil war is doing more for him than his peacetime gang activities had done. He suddenly looks up and his face turns serious. "Look. Things are getting out of hand. There are five different factions out there, and they all want blood. Shatzo's police, special forces from the Chetniks, HVO, shit man, I ain't Jesus Christ you know! You religious types! You're always wanting a guarantee-if you die there must be heaven-if you live there must be peace. Well, little man, in war there are no guarantees. War means you have to face reality. And the reality of war is life or death."

I decide to speak up for my husband even though Muslim women are expected to stay out of men's affairs. "But your war means life has little meaning. It means lowlifes like you get to be in charge because they can kill the fastest and with the least care for the victims. Bosko does not fight because he cares for both sides. Don't you understand? It is the killing itself that he hates. To him, there is no objective distancing of himself so that he can profit from the death all around him. It is only types like you who make out. You can separate yourself from everyone because you think you are superior. Bosko does not think this. He thinks we are all valuable."

Celo looks over at me and spits on the floor. "Yeah, well, I have protected his white ass many times. Sure, he's got an education. I know he'll be on top again once this thing's over. But now I'm in charge! If you want to live, then you have to listen to me. You will leave here at five PM just like they all do. They are no different. I make the deals-do you hear me? Without me, nobody would get out of this hell hole alive! So, when the time comes, just get your asses out there and walk to freedom! But remember. I will still be here to take all the shit!"

It is now five minutes before five. I am holding Bosko's hand. We carry our Adidas sports bags. I did not bring any cosmetics or even clean underwear. Bosko tells me his mother and brother will give us all new things when we reach Serbia. A grim calm surrounds us both as we step out onto the soil of Sarajevo. We can feel the crunch of earth as we step, and Bosko tightens his grip on my hand. There is no distance between us because we are the symbols of a new Sarajevo, the union of Muslim and Serb without the terror, without the constant fear. As we walk along the Miljacka River in no-man's-land, visible to both sides, I feel like I am a living memorial to something they can never kill. A life with no distance, a life with no boundaries between humans, a life where we can all live in peace. I pull at Bosko's hand. I want him to skip with me and sing. After all, it is springtime, and the flowers along the banks of the river leading to the Brotherhood and Unity Bridge are in full bloom.

I stop writing in this journal, and I will toss it aside. I do not need to keep a record now. We are free. Time and the memory of time does not exist for those who are in love. We will sing and skip together, and our voices, I expect, will carry to them as they hold tightly onto their weapons and look down on us out here in no-man's-land. "We are family!"

Epilogue . . . They walked about 1,000 yards before the sniper bullets hit them. Admira is wearing a dark skirt covering the soft curves of her young body, and beneath her blouse she is wearing a German bra. Bosko is in jeans-what else?--and they both wear sneakers. Admira is embracing the body of her young husband because he died first and she had been wounded, living just long enough to crawl over to his lifeless body that was bleeding into the spring grass. They were born in the late 1960s when the world was painfully trying to come together. They died in the early 1990s, when the world ignored a city in Yugoslavia called Sarajevo, the soul of the republic. Their bodies lay in no-man's-land for eight days because each side was arguing over who would retrieve them. The Serbs say they ran out and gathered the two youths' bodies and carried them back to the bunker. Muslim prisoners of the Serbs say they were tethered by ropes and dispatched to fetch the decomposing corpses. Nera visited the site where her daughter died, accompanied by Italian U. N. soldiers. "It was difficult," she said. "The soldiers had to help me lay some flowers as there were still mines all around. But they knew all about the story once I explained. They said my daughter and her husband were being called the Romeo and Juliet of Sarajevo."
