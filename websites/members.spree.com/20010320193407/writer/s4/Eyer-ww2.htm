 A WWII Veteran can open your eyes about electronics, computers and the Internet
© Kim Eyer

So many people know so little about WWII and what impact it has had on our everyday lives today. Perhaps the most important thing, which we may know, is WWII made the world a much safer place. But, we don't realize how much this great global war gave us in our everyday lives.

I had the privilege of gaining this awareness when we recently participated in a Small Business Expo in the Pinehurst Area of North Carolina. Other than website development, our business also offers computer related business services and training, private computer tutoring, and owns retail websites. Both individuals and business owners are clientele to us. We had a wide variety of both types of visitors in our booth.

But, this short story is really about a very interesting man I met that day – and how much I learned by meeting him - about WWII and the many electronic conveniences, including the Internet, that we now enjoy today.

A man approached me in our booth at this Expo and asked me if I would consider reviewing a book he had written, and perhaps consider helping him market the book online. His name is George H. Sweet. My initial reaction was how sharp, and what a direct and honest business approach this man had. He was also very comfortable and easy to talk with. Intrigued, I started asking him questions about the book, its contents, and what audience were potential readers of the book.

As it turned out, Mr. Sweet is a retired Naval Veteran, who had participated in World War II as the Captain of the USS LST 358 - all at the young age of 24. The title of the book is, "Lightning Strikes". Mr. Sweet wrote and published this book for many reasons, one of which is his desire for young people to know more about World War II.

Of course, we were busy in the Expo booth that day, but I did agree to consider Mr. Sweet's requests. He went to his car and brought back a copy of the book to give me. The following day, after the Expo was over and we were settling down in the office again, I took some time to read through the book.

Now, being "thirty-something+", I'm not that young, and do have some knowledge of World War II. But what impressed me about this book was how much I learned about "the Amphibs", a new arm of the US Navy that was implemented at the time. It is also loaded with on site period photography, which as a collector and lover of "old things" was a great feature to me.

The efforts of World War II were the basis for most of the conveniences we enjoy today. Almost everything we consider everyday necessities were born during or as a result of that war. Television, cell phones, stereo discs, improved radio and radar – practically everything and anything electronically – computers probably being one of the most common – had their beginning in that war. World War II truly spawned an industrial revolution greater than anything the world had ever known. Without this war and the industrial revolution it generated, we would not have our precious computers, the Internet, and the open communication of today!

If you want to learn more about the birth of today's electronics, or are a history buff, or a collector of war memorabilia or books, or perhaps you are a student or researcher in need of WWII material - then "Lightning Strikes" is indeed a great find.
