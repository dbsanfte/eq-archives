 CATASTROPHIC
© Steve Franks

An exploding star may have caused the world's worst catastrophe 250 million years ago, it was claimed today.

The Permian Extinction wiped out 90% of all the species on Earth, and was far more devastating than the asteroid impact thought to have killed off the dinosaurs 65 million years ago.

Yet the cause of the cataclysm has always remained a mystery to baffled scientists

Years of research have failed to turn up any solid evidence for an asteroid impact at this time. Other theories such as volcanoes and depletion of ocean oxygen have not been taken seriously.

Now a Hungarian scientist claims to have found evidence of debris from a supernova, an exploding star, which could have destroyed almost all life on Earth.

Csaba Detre (correct), of the Geological Institute of Hungary, has unearthed metal-rich globules, three to 20 micrometres in diameter, in Permian rocks in Japan, China, India, Armenia, Iran and Hungary.

The abundance of elements such as aluminium in the globules suggests they came from outside the Solar System, New Scientist magazine reported.

Astrophysicists have calculated that every few hundred million years a star explodes within a few tens of light years from the Earth.

At that distance, high energy particles and radiation from the blast could strip away the Earth's ozone layer for two hundred years, with catastrophic results.

Without the ozone layer ultraviolet rays both from the Sun and the supernova would pierce the atmosphere, changing its chemistry and killing surface plants and animals.

Disruption to the Earth's magnetic field may also allow cosmic radiation to reach the ground, causing more damage.
