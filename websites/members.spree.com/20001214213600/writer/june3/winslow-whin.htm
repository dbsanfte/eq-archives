 Dealing with conflict and issues in a positive way - No Whining Allowed

© T.W. Winslow

In every relationship there are times when each partner has very different needs, wants, desires or possibly there has been an issue of infidelity, etc. It is at these times when the relationship and the commitment of the partners are truly put to a test.

Someone recently wrote to me telling of how their wife wanted to quit her rather high paying job to be a stay at home mom. Like many American families today, they were basically spending what they made each month. Therefore, in order for the wife to quit her job, they had to make significant sacrifices - selling their home and cars to buy much less expensive ones, reorganizing dept, doing without the extras such as going out to dinner so often, and other such financial considerations.

The husband in this case was somewhat torn. He understood the value of having his wife home with the children, but was frustrated by having to make such drastic changes to their finances and way of life. Ultimately, he conceded to his wife's wishes, but was having a difficult time letting go of the resentment he felt towards her for forcing these changes upon him and the family.

It's important in these times to remember that marriage is a partnership. When an issue arises, it should be thoroughly discussed and the options carefully weighed. Working together to find a solution is crucial. However, once a decision or plan of action is arrived at, the work is not done. Often times, this is the deciding point as to whether or not the ultimate outcome will be a success.

Whenever there has been a compromise on any issue in a relationship, there are always going to be feelings of resentment, jealousy, hostility, etc. - at least to some degree. To deny this is to deny our being human. The key is for both partners to recognize this fact and to deal with it. It's extremely important for the partner who conceded to understand that by doing so, he or she gives up the right to what I like to refer to as the ever-popular and cynical "Yeah, but."

"... yeah, but if you hadn't quit your job we could afford a vacation this year." "... yeah, I'm glad you are happier staying at home with the kids, but I sure miss the boat we used to own."

We're all guilty of doing this, and I'm sure as you're reading this, you're recalling your own tirade of infamous "Yeah, buts."

When we work together in resolving issues in the best interest of the relationship, there is no room for the "yeah, but." Understanding and accepting the consequences of our decisions is mandatory, and means we don't get the option of complaining about it later or bringing it up each time a new problem or issue presents itself. In basic terms, this means NO WHINING ALLOWED.

Presumably each compromise in a relationship is made out of the desire to ultimately make the relationship better and stronger. This simply can't happen if we are unable to take responsibility for our decisions and accept the consequences thereof. Therefore, hard as it may be, we all need to say goodbye to our old friend the "yeah, but."
