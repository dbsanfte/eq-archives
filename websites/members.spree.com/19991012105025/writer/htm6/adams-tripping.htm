Tripping
Wm. F. Adams, Jr
copyright Wm. F. Adams, Jr

He sat in place as
darkness lapped against his legs,
and then he saw the choice: else
he was mad or not alone, but
shared - somehow - his head.

So he was mad.
"It's not so bad", he thought,
"I'm functional, and I don't
foam, or roam at night annoying
backyard dogs. There is no harm.
I'm closet-mad."

He listened to the
darkened building, listened
for a telling sound. He was
alone. It spoke anew: "What
are these crazy thoughts - they're
other's thoughts, but other who?"

He tried to feel another
presence, some intruder
in his head, so he could
fight to win his mind back;
called "Who's in there?"; heard,
"It's me, it's only me".

He couldn't let it be.
As night grayed into day, he
struggled still, and still it
seemed the only sane conclusion
was that he was simply mad.

He sought refuge in reverie,
as if the past awaited him
with answers more than bones.
He'd lived his life in solitude,
behind great minding walls
reserved for such as he, and fervently

he craved for touch, to live direct
unproxied life - and like Pinnochio -
to be a real live boy. His friends
were close and few; ideas ruled his
world, and he would play with
them as favorite toys. As he did still.

"I love ideas", it whispered close
behind his twitching ear. He sighed,
"I guess you're really here." And,
startled, "Who are you?", it cried.

Wm. F. Adams, Jr. (Bill) is
an aging father of five, who
lives in the hills of
NorthWest New Jersey and
writes in an attempt to
reconcile what he knows
with what he sees:

"it is maddest of all to accept
the world as it is, and not
as it should be."
