 CLEM AND THE MAKING OF A LEGEND
© Phillip Shadoin

Long before the Perfesser made his historic debut in Rock Creek, there was the Legend of the King. And before there was event that created the Legend of the King, there was Clem. And immediately prior to Clem, there was Clem's father, Tom Warden. Tom was a great bear of a man, who after World War two, built a fine business in Feed and Seed on the strength of his hard work and the dependability of his word.

Tom spent his war years in the Philippines driving heavy equipment through jungle islands to carve out airstrips for the United States Navy. He had been shot twice while operating his bulldozer. The first time the bullet grazed the back of his right hand and put a hole in the toe of his left boot. He didn't even know he had been shot, and had the darndest time trying to figure out what scratched the back of his hand. It wasn't until later that he discovered the part of his boot missing that he finally put it together. When he reported it to his CO, the Marines were called upon to ride shotgun for all the operators to keep this near tragedy from recurring.

The second time he was shot, the Marine riding shotgun was the trigger man. The jungle is rough terrain and the Marine was a little antsy. So much so that he kept his finger too close to the trigger. The bullet from the M-1 entered Tom's shoulder behind the bone and left in nearly the same place on the other side. For Tom the War to End All Wars was over. He recuperated in Subic Bay and Honolulu and then San Francisco. From San Francisco he was sent up into Oregon to train new recruits on the proper handling of heavy equipment. The most important thing he reminded them, is to make sure your shotgun man is riding with his weapon pointed outward.

Tom Warden and Judy Guerber were married on June 3, 1947. A year later, Clem was born and with him, perhaps the most important fishing legacy of Rock Creek. By 1951, Clem and Tom were regular fixtures mornings and evenings along much of the Rock Creek. Clem was all of three years old and already he was casting a four foot split ash fly rod fashioned by Tom's loving hands. Whatever Tom did, Clem emulated. Though Tom was not thrilled by killing, he taught his growing son all manner of woodcraft, hunting and trapping. Clem was versed in conservation and respect long before it was cause celebre. The two Wardens would often disappear for the night, carrying only a canteen and canned goods. They had fashioned themselves a lean-to of sorts and there they slept in Army Surplus sleeping bags and ate trout and beans and chased it with fried potatoes and Rock Creek water. Tom might bring along a radio and the two would listen to a ball game carried to them from as far away as Chicago or Cleveland by the vagaries of the upper atmosphere. Life was simple, punctuated by the changing of the seasons and unfettered by the routine of school. By the time Clem was in kindergarten, there were few fishermen that could catch more fish than Clem Warden, but Tom Warden was one.

Clem's school years were a blur of sports, fishing, studies, friends and work. The Warden's were like all families in Rock Creek: hard working, God fearing, dependable and solid. As Clem grew, his status as the Creek's finest angler was largely decided by word of mouth and deed. No one in town brags upon themselves or their kin, but they will grin and look down while you do it for them. So it was upon Clem's friends and the friends of his father to put out the word that if you were serious about angling, you'd better go fishing with young Clem Warden. Since Clem fished everyday, most days mornings and evenings, he often found himself fishing alone. Everybody wanted to see for themselves if Clem was for real, yet few wanted to fish more than once or twice with him. Clem was pretty convincing in his first demonstration. So he wandered up and down the creek working each riffle and rapid, testing each drop and cut. There was only one pool that eluded his skill. It was below the old mill that had thrived only briefly in the thirties before being washed out and shuttered for good. The only way to reach this pool was to walk in the stream itself, generally mucking things up for all the down streamers. So Clem decided to fish it only on days when he knew other anglers wouldn't be out. That meant only on days when it was terrible weather for fishing.

It was one day such as this that Clem discovered the King. Clem was working a late season nymph and having some success with a half sidearm cast beneath the overgrowth. This pool was deep and clear and dark. So dark that even in good light, you couldn't make out the details clearly. But on this day, there was a heavy mist and a deep fog and the visibility was quite poor.

Clem felt the strike before he saw it. The initial indication was that this was a Trout of Notable Quality. This kind of fish comes along only once in an angler's life, and only then if he's lucky and persistent. Clem fought this great beast for more than an hour, with the trout neither gaining ground nor surrendering it. Finally, with a furious twist of his large head, the fish was gone. After a brief rest, Clem tied another fly on and went back to work, never to get this big bugger to rise again.

A change came over Clem. He fished this one pool only, and not only on bad days. Finally Tom Warden was made aware of Clem's single-mindedness by some upset anglers who couldn't fish any lower pools because Clem's boots were cascading mud down the stream. Tom and Clem had words, not harsh ones, but words that made it clear to Clem that his pursuit of this fish was beginning to have an effect on others and it was his responsibility to treat the waters with respect in regards to other fishermen.

Clem altered his routine. Finally out of frustration, Clem grabbed the porcelain fixture his dad had just removed from the feed store lavatory and hurled it deep into the pool, as deep as he could heave it.

"You act like you are the King of All Trout! Here's a &@##@^ throne!"

And with that epithet, the Legend of the King was born. Clem put his rod away and for several months never ventured back to the Rock Creek. Sure he fished, just not there.

Clem was busy at the time being a teenager, playing sports and discovering the fairer sex. Clem married his sweetheart Karen Gustafson and built her a house with his own sweat and toil. When Tom Warden retired, it was Clem's feed store to run. Clem and Karen Warden had three children of their own. His oldest son, Tom, became a Peace Corp volunteer out of college and died of malaria in Micronesia. His second child, a daughter, married a man named Gates and moved to Washington State, where he is doing pretty well. His youngest son is in medical school, having done his undergraduate work at the state university down in the state capitol.

Clem might have had only had three children of his own, but he like his father before him, was dedicated to all the children of Rock Creek. His was a good life and a successful one, though it was one marked by tragedy and triumph.

Until the Perfesser came to town some years later, Clem never again ventured down to the water that had become known as the Throne Pool. Other anglers did and they all came back with tales of woe and defeat. And it was little consolation to Clem when the King was finally caught by the Perfesser and his true royalty was revealed. But that's another story.
