 I Should Have Listened to the Little Voice
© James Lessenberry

"I should have listened to the little voice inside my head. It told me that something was wrong. When I don't listen to the little voice, something always goes wrong."

We've all said that at one time or another, usually 100s of times during our lives. Believe it or not, the little voice is part of a normal thinking process. Unfortunately, we can't summons the little voice anytime we want to. But there is a way to double-check the little voice to see what it's telling us.

Please be advised that I didn't hear a little voice telling me to write this article. I learned this thought process while participating in a study of Southern creative writers. (A study of the thought process of writers, not their writings.)

First, let's assume that you already have a website and you are considering new distributorship programs. You have decided that none of them is exactly right. But you need extra money, so you pick one that your associate, or spouse, thinks might work.

But then, the little voice, the uneasy feeling, or .... something tells you not to do it.

First, get all the information together that you have on the dealership, especially ads. Go to the website. Start a list of "product features" set forth in this information.

You will see things like: "We're positioned to be the world's leading cyber hamburger cooker". "Our marketing plan calls for running tiny little ads in magazines." "We're in 85 countries". Write everything down.

Make no judgments now. You are not looking for an excuse not to join the program. You are looking for: "What gave me the idea not to do this".

Review the product list. Cross out everything that's irrelevant. "We're in 85 countries"; unless you want to set up an additional foreign language website and answer emails in a foreign language, cross it out.

Now start over again. While reading the list, visualize yourself doing each step necessary to operate the new distributorship. Visualize paying for running the tiny ads supplied by the company. (After you crossed out the irrelevant hype, do you have any of the ad left?) Who would you visualize as a potential customer?

Then turn everything around. Start a separate list. Let's call it the "benefits list". You've already visualized the potential customer so think like they do. Does the program offer any real benefit to the customer?

Many times that's it: there simply was no real need for this company to go into business. Where else can I buy this product? Would I quit my present supplier to do business with this distributorship?

After you have written the "benefits" list, you can put both lists away and start on something else. If you happen to think of something on one of the lists, reread the other list. The answer you are looking for is a combination of something from list "A" and list "B".

The answer, what the little voice was trying to tell you, will probably pop into your head sometime in the next day or two. A new distributorship, even if it's free, costs time and money to promote. (You did consider promotional costs didn't you?) So it's an important decision.

It's easy to see why this is an important thought process to use: What's the first thing the boss or spouse is going to ask? "What gave you the idea not to do it?"
