 I Would Prefer If You Didn't Touch Me
© Mike McMeniman

It was early labor, you know the easy part (compared to what comes later) and Kay was experiencing first hand what her 'trying to be helpful friends' had warned her about, the pain of your first childbirth. Her 'trying to be helpful' husband was massaging her ankles and feet when suddenly he hears, delivered with a low, but not unpleasant growl, "I would prefer if you didn't touch me!"

Kay has been my wife for over twelve years. Through those years we have developed a close and intimate relationship. We can even know what each other is thinking...sometimes. In new situations, this 'telepathy' tends to breakdown, since each of us is trying to digest the plethora (I love that word) of new information bombarding our senses. Our first child being born was definitely one of those new situations.

Has today's business environment put you and your co-workers in new situations? Has the chemistry that you seemed to have developed over the years recently broken down? Do you now feel as if you don't know what anyone wants anymore, even yourself? This is a normal reaction to the changing world around us. Do not fear, you are normal.

In my communication seminars, we define communication not as an act of one person conversing with another, but rather, base the definition on the result of the exchange. Simply put, we define communication as mutual understanding. Without this critical connection between the 'sender' and the 'receiver', what has been accomplished? In my opinion, very little.

Think back to the last time you thought the person you spoke to understood what you meant. With this assumption of understanding, you probably walked away with a warm and fuzzy feeling that all was well. Only to discover, minutes, hours or even days later that what you had desired was not only never done, but worse, the 'receiver' had done exactly what you had warned them not to do! You did not have 'mutual understanding.'

Our normal reaction at this point is to think about how stupid, ignorant or inept the 'receiver' was to not have understood such simple directions. Is this true? Have you ever felt frustration towards another person when a request was not fulfilled? Have you ever felt like you could hit them over the head?

My message is a simple one; whose fault is it if a receiver of a message that you sent does not understand it? How much control does the 'receiver' have over how the message is phrased, enunciated and emphasized? The answer is none! The task of the 'receiver' is to listen to the best of their ability to any delivered message. Then, they must interpret it through the use of their internal thought processes, language studies and experience and try to determine what you meant. Quite a daunting task.

My challenge is to those 'senders' of messages out there who are frustrated by poor listeners. I suggest you pause before you speak and think about whom you are sending a message to and ask yourself, 'How can I phrase my message so it is most easily understood by this person?' If you pause consciously before each time you speak for the next several weeks, this will develop into a subconscious habit. This habit will do more to enhance the relationship with your friends, co-workers and even your boss than anything I can suggest. Why? Because any message you send will have a greater chance of being mutually understood.

By the way, Kay's message to me was mutually understood. I didn't touch her again until she asked. As a result, she delivered John Franklin McMeniman, 6 pounds, 10 ounces and 21" long five hours later. Now that was mutual understanding.
