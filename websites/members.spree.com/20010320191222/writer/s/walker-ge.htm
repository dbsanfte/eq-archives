 The Next General Election Starts Here!

© Phillip Walker

Across the Western World, over the next eighteen months we will experience very important series of national elections to government.

A number of significant factors will make these elections different to others. The first factor is the demographics of the electorate themselves. The second is the global village factor in that what happens in America has an effect on Europe; what happens in the UK has a distinct effect on Africa. Third factor must be the Internet. People are so well informed today and can always become more so. That it is possible for Joe Bloggs to discover world news almost before his head of government can, empowers him to be highly accurate in his choices.

It is not really possible for governments to pull the wool over one's eyes anymore. Everyone now knows the issues; if you like the only issue now is interpretation.

What is already happening is that polarisation is bringing the various opposition parties of the western world closer together and 'issue' politics is becoming the only difference. The 'issue' this year might be health, next year food, the year after the dollar, the pound, the yen and God help it, the Euro.

It is difficult to find constants anymore and it is likely that the next batch of elections will root them out. One of the few constants that is growing and is likely to continue to grow over the next twenty years is our demographics of living.

By 2002 a half of the electorate in the west will be over the age of 45. Seniors, Thirdagers, Golden Oldies or silly old sods, whatever we are labelled as, we will have much more power, grey power if you like, as to how our governments will behave.

It is almost like an era in waiting and what is more, the current standing governments are waking up to it. It's 'let's look after the oldies time'. For the first time ever 'they' are taking older people issues much more seriously than ever before. The opposition parties are fighting with one another to capitalise on the 'Grey Vote'. However the older generation are not as easily bought as they used to be.

We are staying 'young' longer; we are fitter, wealthier and much more opportunistic than our forebears. Dare it be said that at 65 many of us are more ambitious than we have ever been in our lives.

Far more over 45's than under have taken up the opportunities thrown up by the Internet. We are empowered. Older people, in their wisdom, understand the values that the World Wide Web gives them. They realise too that it is becoming a smaller world every day. We holiday in every single part of the world. Our families no longer live in the same village; this is a global village and half the family lives half a world away. Add to this the modern miracles of communication. From the UK I can send an email to Japan and get a reply in an hour. The older generation is the only generation, because of a lifetime's experience, to understand the full value of these benefits and we will demand that our future governments recognise this. They are beginning to get the message. We must make sure that the clarion call is deafening in their ears.

The next election starts here !
