 a cube of sugar
© Gary Paul Lehmann

Eduard was playing the piano as he usually did after dinner, when Cook-san ran into the sitting room. Of course, I didn't care if she came in on the family, but she had very strict rules. Except to serve us, the servants stayed in their part of the house, which left us the big luxurious side of the house to ourselves. The flip side of that was that we weren't to venture into their territory. So, when Cook-san arrived in our sitting room, we were all surprised and even mildly alarmed.

"Turn on radio, Mama-san. Emperor speaks. War over."

Of course we all knew that it couldn't go on much longer. Food supplies had long since given out. The American bombers came over the hilltop each night and dropped endless loads of bombs on Kobe. We had heard of even worse destruction in Nagasaki and Hiroshima, but we didn't actually learn the horrible extent of it for months. To those Japanese who knew first hand what happened, it was too horrible to express in words. The government was not anxious to spread fear, and the rumor mill was strangely quiet.

Ever since the German's surrendered in Europe, the position of all the German consular and trade personnel had become very tenuous. We always knew we were being spied upon by our servants, and the merchants and garbage collectors who came to the house each week, but we had nothing to hide, so it didn't matter much.

Still now, there were rumors that there was a train waiting in the station for us to board and that the Japanese government planned to take us up to the frozen northland and drop us off on some remote island, without food or shelter to die of exposure. I don't know if this was true. Strange things happen in war times.

I ran to the radio and switched it on. I glanced over at Eduard, still sitting behind the piano. His eyes registered his concern that they might be announcing our imminent departure by train, or worse. His consular status had protected us during the war, but now the Japanese had no love for Germany any more. Anything might happen.

A thin wispy voice emerged as the radio warmed up. It seemed just about as substantial as the flickering light that came up behind the opaque dial with all its national and international radio numbers. This high pitched sing-song voice floated out of the tiny speakers into our living room like a trail of thin smoke or a single strand of cob web waving listlessly in the air.

"The emperor, his voice," Cook-san said folding her hands and bowing excessively as she backed out of our presence. No one had ever had the privilege of hearing the Emperor's voice outside of his massive Palace. Only those in the Japanese nobility were allowed to associate with the Emperor. Even many of them had never heard him speak. Hearing his voice for the very first time, common people felt a natural sense of awe and respect, but they did not know how to act in his radio presence. Evidently bowing was the only thing they could think that might be appropriate. They all did it, I found out later from my friends.

We listened very carefully to his voice. Our lives might hang on what he was saying. He spoke in an ancient form of Japanese only used inside the Palace grounds. It was unintelligible to us. My tin ear only picked up a certain amount of Japanese at the best of times, but even Eduard was puzzled by this antique language.

"Cook-san," I called out to her to her shadow just as it disappeared around the corner into the kitchen. "Tell us what he is saying. Translate for us, please. We do not understand." Cook-san returned still bowing and still with her hands folded in front of her chest. She cocked her head as he spoke. She squinted slightly. She was trying very hard.

"I cannot tell, Mama-san," answered Cook-san. "His words are sacred, and so it is natural that we not understand them." I looked at Eduard. He shrugged his shoulders. We both looked at Cook-san. To judge by her expression this was a completely adequate explanation for his speech. She smiled her usual smile and hurried out into the kitchen where she could huddle with the other servants, and not miss a word.

Cook-san's parting glance at me told me that my demand for meaning in the Emperor's speech seemed to her crass and unworthy of the moment. She was glad to be able to retreat. I tried to listen with her ears, and I had to agree with her to some extent. It was therapeutic to hear the Emperor's steady, confident voice at this time of crisis. It had a calming effect.

There was great dignity in it. How did he manage to put so much pride into simple words? Yet a clear sense of honor and moment emerged from behind his small, wispy stream of words. Listening to his voice, it did not matter who won or who lost the war. It only seemed to matter that we lived in a nation with an Emperor such as this one who could speak and lead with such aplomb. How could anything important go wrong?

No Japanese Emperor had ever announced the defeat of his nation to his people before. There were no protocols to follow. I suppose if such a thing has to be done, it is best done with dignity and serenity and if no one can understand the words, yet the message is conveyed, while avoiding any petty fighting over terminology.

After it was all over, Eduard got on his hat and coat, the same hat and coat he wore on the boat we boarded in San Francisco just before the war was declared. Ours had been a hot, fast romance. He was an exchange student and I his English tutor. My mother warned me about the dangers of going out with a German, but there's no explaining, or avoiding, love.

"I need to go downtown for a while," he said. "I'll be back as soon as I can find out what is happening." When we first arrived in Japan, we were welcomed into the trade delegation with open arms. Those were the days when we still pretended to have a country club and a tennis club, though they had no food and less and less liquor as the war deepened. After a while there weren't even any valuables left in the club to bribe the band into playing and soon the colored water in the liquor bottles began to mold over and the party was over. The croquet balls and mallets were neatly packed away. The nets were taken down and the clubs were closed forever, as it turned out.

Eduard had a car and a driver, back then, but the car was lost when our first house was bombed. The driver had to be let go. I was very sorry to do it. We were his only source of food, for himself and his family, but there was no point in having to feed a driver with the car out of commission.

The second house we lived in was much nicer, but the servants were not sure if they should be serving us since they never actually received instructions to that effect from the Lutheran minister who had taken his family back home at the outbreak of war. They went across Russia in a locked train, headed for Germany, and we got back very bad reports about the possibility of their making it all the way. Still, the servants were loyal to their master's word, even if their master was in some Siberian labor camp, or worse yet dead.



Eduard hit upon the idea of calling the bishop. He understood immediately and came right over that very evening. People stuck together back then more than now. He brought his own head servant, and, while the bishop chatted with us in the sitting room, the head man talked with the servants to arrange for our safety and comfort. While the real business was going on in the kitchen, we pretended to make polite conversation.

"Bishop, I'd like to introduce my children," I said formally. "Here is Robin. He's 6 and here is our darling Susanna, 4."

"Nickta suss," I instructed her. Susanna rolled her eyes as she thought about each step. I could see her reviewing my instructions word for word. "First grasp the edges of your dress on each side. Then place the right toe behind the left heel and bent your knees ever so slightly." She almost lost her balance, but caught herself at the last minute. When she arose, she almost applauded herself for having executed her moves without flaw.

Meanwhile, Robin, unbid, bowed very formally from the waist, one arm bent across his waistline at the back and one arm bent across his pudgy front. He wore an enigmatic smile which with any luck the childless bishop would not be able to translate. His leather short pants creaked as he bent over.

"And I understand that you are carrying yet a third," he said. I was only in my third month, and I thought I didn't show as yet, but gossip is more telling than anything else in these matters, and a bishop is sure to know everything. Gossip is all we really had to concentrate on in those days. There was no trade. We weren't involved in the war effort. All we had time to do was scrounge for food and gossip. The one we did just adequately, but the other we became quite expert in.

"Yes," I replied. "It should be in late June or early July I think. I have not been able to get to the hospital more than a couple of times. It is quite a long train ride and a long walk, as you know. It frightens me to be pregnant among so many desperately ill."

The bishop nodded, and shifted in his place. At last the bishop's head man appeared at the kitchen door, his hands clasped respectfully behind his back. The bishop rose up from the sofa, where he sat with his hat in his lap the entire time, and dutifully shook hands with each of us before he left.

"That was a very fine curtsy," young lady," he said in parting to Susanna, who blushed and ran off toward her room.

"Good day, bishop," I said to his back. "Thank you very much."

That house was pretty well crushed one night when a bomber dropped its load prematurely and hit the water reservoir over us. The water mixed with the dry dust of the hillside and by the time it hit the house, it was a rolling pile of mud and stones. Luckily Cook-san was still out for the evening after her day off, and we were all tucked in bed at the other end of the house. Still, we had to move. The whole house was leaning precariously. It collapsed entirely some days later.

Eduard had a strange premonition about that house. On the night of the misguided bombing run, he got into bed and said altogether casually that he felt certain that this would be the last night we would have to sleep in this house.

"Why," I asked him. He didn't have this sort of intuitive burst very often, hardly ever.

"I don't know how I know," he replied. "I just feel like our time is up here."

"Go to sleep," I said. "You're imagination is getting the better of you," but somehow he was right.

Eduard returned late with news. The war was over. The Emperor had given his speech, the text of which was published in the newspaper in normal Japanese, not that it mattered much. Basically, he said that he would always be their emperor, which wasn't true, and they would always be proud and free, which was very soon not true either. True to Cook-san's report, the importance of the Emperor's speech had nothing whatever to do with what was said.

No one knew what was about to happen next. Would the Americans drop more bombs to neutralize any resistance, or would they come in with an invading army, guns blazing? It was a time of great uncertainty. We were safely ensconced in our third and last house for the war and rid, at last, of that one batch of troublesome servants. They hung around the leaning corpse of the old house until it collapsed and then they dispersed. I never saw any of them again.

The last day, as we left, they gathered outside the remains to wish us farewell. Most of them spoke to us in English, though they had always before maintained they did not understand the language. The linen girls and washey girls giggled and poked each other as we passed down the line shaking their hands and leaving them each with a small token of our affection. We didn't have many tokens left by the end of the war. We desperately went through our new house looking for things that looked like they might have been ours to give them. They would not have liked it if they knew we were stealing, but what was the choice? Everything was quite confused. Every one had to make do as best he could.

Having servants might sound grand from a post-war perspective, but it was a lot of work feeding all of those mouths. They gladly provided us with every conceivable service. They cooked and cleaned, washed and polished. They would stand for hours waiting on table or attending to the children with utter patience. It didn't matter how many hours it took to make dinner or how many hands were needed.

If I signaled Cook-san in the morning that we were ready for a number 1 dinner, she lit up like a candle. She gathered all the servants in the kitchen, and set everyone to chopping and washing things. All day long, you could hear the happy sound of chatter throughout the house.

But if I held up three fingers, as I so frequently had to do at the end of the war when food was so terribly scarce, Cook-san drew a very long face and did the Japanese servant equivalent of stomping off into the kitchen. Today I see many good restaurants where that is the best level of service available, but to Cook-san a good, number 1, meal meant that we and the servants and all their families had a fine dinner to look forward to, but if I signaled a number 3 meal we all ate sparsely.

Their energy was unbounded, but there was one thing they would not do, find food. If I gave Cook-san coins she would take a basket and go to the Black Market where she knew she could negotiate a good deal for a few yen, but she would not grow or harvest anything. That was out of the question. Some things were set in stone with the servants and that was the A-Number-One-Thing. Masters provide the food.

What that really meant is that Eduard and I had to scrounge and scramble to find enough food. Since Eduard worked at the embassy most of the time, that left it up to me. Sometimes Eduard would take things in his briefcase, like jewelry or brass candlesticks, and trade these goods in Kobe for a few coins which we could hand off one at a time to Cook-san for barter. Most times, however, I would have to spend my afternoons out in the garden hoeing and raking and watering our little garden. I had to chase away the deer and rabbits and bring in whatever harvest we managed to eke out of this dry unlikely land.

The servants were very polite about it. They gladly scrubbed the stains from my garden dresses for hours but they would not help in any way. That was my work, and I had to see to it myself.

If my crop yielded more than we could consume before it rotted, I took out a basket and made the rounds of each house in the district. Maybe I would have a pile of carrots or some spinach. At each house, I would offer my goods implicitly by settling them down by my feet as I talked over the news of the day, i.e. the newest gossip. My hostess might offer me some tea if she had some or just water if not. We might talk for hours, but eventually the subject of the carrots and spinach would come up and soon enough a deal would be struck to every one's agreement.

If my neighbor had no ready-to-eat food to trade she might have cigarettes which were the only universal currency. God only knows how many hands these packs that made it all the way through the war had passed through. As long as the cellophane seal covered the pack, it was legal tender for all debts public and private. I never smoked. It was a habit none of us could afford, but we all had cigarettes hidden away in little secret places where we could find them when someone came calling with something fresh to eat in a basket.

I don't know how the Japanese civilians found food. I suppose in much the same way. Sometimes a young Japanese girl would show up on my doorstep with a basket of fresh food. She was not begging. This is how we all had to live. I would give her a pack of cigarettes, divide the produce into one family units, and begin trading. Though the end of the war signaled terrible shortages, we always managed to find some way to get by. People were very kind to one another during the war. I never had as much trouble being an American during the war in Japan as I did after I returned to America and tried to live in Albany with family ties to Germany.

One day Eduard and Robin climbed up the hillside behind our house to watch the City of Kobe explode and burn as the bombs destroyed it block by block. I stayed down below in the garden. As I stood there listening as the bombs went off in the distance, I heard a cry of delight or alarm, I couldn't tell which it was from the behind me.

Cook-san stood in the clearing at the far end of the garden, near the root -cellar we used when the bombs came too near, and there I saw her with one hand over her eyebrows. No Japanese planes had been in the air for weeks. I ran up to the clearing behind her and saw in the sky what she saw, a shining shaft of polished aluminum reflecting in the sun. It shone like a piece of sculpture as it swooped down on the town. It seemed to hover on a whisper.

"It's so fine," Cook-san said. I never got used to the Japanese sense of beauty. We did not know if this plane was delivering bombs, troops, or food, but to her it did not matter. She had no control over these life-and-death matters. There was no sense in having the beauty of the moment disturbed by any anxiety for what the future might bring.

Often in the newspaper a report of a battle was headed with a picture of a Japanese officer smelling peach blossoms from astride his horse. Sometimes, later, we discovered that the officer had been dead for months or even years. Sometimes these same men returned. It did not matter to the affect the newspaper was trying to create.

"Kirei des ne," Cook-san shouted pointing into the sky. "It is beautiful, isn't it?" she repeated, holding out her hand to it as if biding this silver bird to light on her outstretched finger. It turned out that the planes that came that day and for many days to come were loaded with troops and supplies. Soon American servicemen in Jeeps were seen everywhere. They wore sidearms and some came with rifles in the jump seat. When they learned that an American woman was living up in the hills, they came to visit. They came to speak English, play with the children, and learn about the local customs.

It was hard to know what terrible circumstances these men endured before driving up into our barren yard in their Jeeps. Iwo Jima, the Salomon Islands, and the battles for the other Pacific islands had all been theirs to fight, and then survive. They all wore brave faces and did not speak of their inner suffering. Many of them showed me the pictures of their own children at home. They played catch with Robin and bounced Susanna on their knees. For a few moments they seemed happy, and perhaps a little sad at the same time.

One day a lieutenant, who came home with Eduard for dinner, gave Susanna a single cube of sugar after dinner.

"Ohhhhh!" she exclaimed. "Mama look!" She cried out in utter delight holding the cube above her head where I could see it plainly. "Isn't it wonderful!" The soldier was amused. He couldn't be expected to know that we had not seen actual sugar for several years. Even the beet substitutes gave out months ago. Here it was, a perfect square of crystalline hope, a hallmark of our pasts, and a reminder of what we hoped would soon be ours again.

I looked in wonderment at this single artifact of a world we only just remembered. It was like a message that washes up in a bottle on a foreign shore. It reminded us of how far we were away from home. Like castaways holding up a sacred text, peering at its mystic inscription, we were too flabbergasted to know just what to say. It was Christmas time, and Susanna, who hadn't received hardly any presents, but desperately wanted some, ran to her room and returned some minutes later with the cube all wrapped in rice paper and tied with a tiny string. The bow was impossibly small and dainty. We all laughed. She put the tiny present in the middle of the table, and we all laughed a great deal more.

The next day, Susanna accompanied me on my usual rounds to barter for food from my neighbors. Everything was harder than ever with the coming of the end of the war. Susanna naturally brought along her diminutive present, and showed it to each house frau as we went along. When we reached the home of her best friend, Greta, Susanna retrieved the tiny package from her pocket and gave it reverently to her friend.

When Greta opened it, she couldn't believe her eyes. "Oh Susanna," Gerta cried, "you shouldn't give this marvelous thing to me. It's the most wonderful thing you own." Susanna, just looked at her friend with bemusement.

"I know," she replied simply.

Greta was ecstatic to think that she owned such a wonder. In the mid-day sunlight, it was as glistening and crystalline as the jet airplane Cook-san had pointed out days before. It seemed like pure magic to us, the narrow end of the wedge.

That Christmas there were very few presents and a great deal more gossip than usual, so everyone made the rounds to pass Season's Greetings upon all of their acquaintances. Eduard's equal knowledge of English and Japanese came in very handy at this time. He worked closely with the United States Air Force and translated documents and memos from civil and ex-military authorities. He began using his engineering skills to help set up regional refrigeration units to store perishable supplies both for the occupation army and for the indigenous population that was very nearly on the brink of starvation.

Several days after we had visited Greta, the same officer who visited us, made a stop at her house. This time he bore chocolate candy bars. These were a treasure almost beyond imagination. The children held them in their hot hands without knowing what to do with them.

"Go ahead," the officer urged. "You can eat them. I have a whole box of them here in the Jeep. Instantly, the children tore into these magical gifts, caressing the delicious chocolate with their avid little tongues, while all the adult eyes focused on the box in the Jeep. Obviously the officer had no idea that he held a small fortune in his easy grasp. On the Black Market, a family could live for several months on the remaining contents of that box, but how was the officer to be expected to understand that? He came to us from a different world.

Greta was so amazed at his generosity that she ran into her house, pulled a dining room chair up to the mantle where her mother had stored the sugar cube, and retrieved it. Eagerly, she ran back out into the yard, and got the soldier's attention. Kneeling down, he saw what she proffered him, the most valuable thing she owned, a cube of sugar. He smiled and took it from her gratefully. She smiled and gave him a big chocolatety kiss on the cheek.

It's hard to say how soldiers feel about things. They are trained to hold everything within themselves. He seemed somewhat choked up. He walked directly to his Jeep and made a hurried departure. I don't know what happened to the cube of sugar. Did he pop it in his mouth? Maybe he threw it into the woods. It probably was a bit soiled by now. I suppose it doesn't really matter.

After a few months, we got word that the Air Force had arranged with the Navy to take us back to San Francisco aboard an empty cargo freighter. It was all painted military gray and looked as dismal on the outside as it was austere on the inside, but we were very happy to go aboard.

We had long farewell dinners with each of our friends. "Stay in touch," they cried out as we departed. "We'll never forget you," we cried back.
