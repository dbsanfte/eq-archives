 HOMECOMING
© Phillip Shadoin

1969 was a trying time for almost everyone in the United States. It was no different in Rock Creek. No, there wasn't any antiwar demonstrations or kids burning draft cards or bras, but there was a community unrest that was hard to ignore. The unrest stemmed from the missing young men from the fields and homes in and around Rock Creek. As the mothers of Rock Creek had always done when asked, they surrendered their share of boys to the war in Vietnam.

Some of those boys came home in passenger cars. Some came home in box cars. But all of them came home. All that is, except one.

Paul McAuley was the son of Louise and Louis McAuley, Lu and Louie Mac, if you saw them playing pitch down at the VFW Club. Paul never came home. The United States Marine Corp had no record of what happened to Paul. His patrol was in a Huey shot down over some dank jungle and were never heard from again. Officially, Paul is listed as missing in action. But no one who survived from Paul's unit held any hope that they were alive after the first month. They weren't released as POW's and the Red Cross had no record of ever serving them in any Vietnamese prison.

About eighteen months after Paul was shot down, a Marine Major appeared at the door of the McAuley's. Lu and Louie offered him iced tea and a seat on the divan. The major had been the captain commanding the unit that sent Paul's patrol out. He told the McAuley's how dreadfully sorry he was he could bring no news, but rest assured, if he heard anything, they would be the first to know. Louie walked him to the door, thanked him for coming, and watched the green sedan leave long after it had disappeared through the wheat fields. Louie didn't turn to look at Lu. She wouldn't be there. She would be upstairs, laying on Paul's bed, crying and praying that Paul be all right somehow. And somehow he would defy the odds and make his way home.

Life for the Maculae's became a simple one of work, church, and home. They were quiet and kept to themselves, unable even to stay at the VFW where their friends were. Too embarrassed to show their face, ashamed of their false hopes and self delusions, they kept themselves and the secret prayer that soon they would be reunited with the son they loved so much.

Like all children of Rock Creek, Paul was at home outdoors. He hunted and fished and knew how to find his way home on a moonless night. There were no bogeymen in the lives of Rock Creek youths. He was better equipped than most Marines at the art of woodcraft and survival. There was some hope. It was slim hope. But there was some hope anyway.

In 1989, a battered old van was seen parked under the trestle bridge. A hairy man in his late thirties, early forties began camping there. He gathered up fallen limbs and dried grasses, started a cooking campfire, and set about catching fish with an old buggy whip fly rod and poorly tied flies.

The Locals caught wind of the squatter by mid afternoon. Rockhead and John the Liar were headed to the Trestle Pool to try out some new flies they had tied when they ran across this hermit, living in his van/cave. The stranger saw them coming and quickly gathered his fishing gear and retreated to the safety of his fire. Rockhead and John did what they came to do, nodded at the unwashed man, and headed to Clem's Feed Store to report what they had seen.

The Locals had seen hobos, hitchhikers, and runaways all take up residence under the trestle bridge. None ever stayed long. They never went out of their way to harass or welcome the strangers. They respected them as lone individuals and let live. But this time it seemed different. This fellow seemed like he meant to stay. No transients had ever arrived equipped to fly fish before.

Clem worked the stretch of water below the trestle pool early the next morning. Partly he was there to fish, partly to make the acquaintance of the van's hermit. While he watched, the stranger emerged from the van. The stranger greeted the morning with some enthusiasm. He grabbed a bucket and headed for the water. There he dipped a bucket full of cool clear Rock Creek water and carried it back to the van. Part he poured into his coffeepot, part he set on the fire to boil. While the bucket of water boiled, he took a pair of scissors from a small ditty bag and adjusted the van's mirror so that he could give himself a haircut. He either didn't see Clem, or didn't pay him any heed. The vagrant trimmed his hair as best he could, and then his beard. After the beard was trimmed, he got the hot bucket of water from the fire and gave himself a first class sponge bath, completing the toilette with a straight-razor shave. The now clean vagrant reached into his van and pulled out a pair of new blue jeans and a brand new T-shirt. Clem watched the man carefully comb his hair. Where a dirty beggar had stood less than an hour before, Clem saw a road-worn but somewhat handsome man. Clem decided to introduce himself, after all, he had been just staring for quite a while.

"Good morning! I'm sorry to be so forward, but I reckon I owe you an apology for staring at you."

"Think nothing of it. I am used to people staring. I let myself go there a little. Don't worry, I'll be moving on soon. I only need to find enough work to get me by for a while on the road. Do you know anyone around here that's hiring? I am pretty good with my hands and I give an honest day's work."

"I know the fellow at the feed store on the highway is looking for someone to lump grain sacks. Interested?" Clem was being coy.

"That would be great! I need to work some of this sag off me anyway." the stranger replied, patting a nonexistent gut.

"Come on then. You can ride with me. Name's Clem."

"Nice to meet you Clem, my name's Al." The two men shook hands.

Clem treated his new hired man to a big breakfast at Daisy's Highway 66 Cafe. Then he walked him over to the feed store and put him to work loading bags of feed on his straight truck for deliveries. He also helped the walk in customer's load their own purchases.

The Locals gathered in the feedstore, paying no mind to Clem's new man. They had gossip to share about the squatter down at the trestle pool. Neither John the Liar or Rockhead could find any sign of the stranger. His camp looked lived in, but he was no where. Al heard all this of course and made an effort to introduce himself but he was quickly cut short by Clem, who wanted to have a little fun at John the Liar's expense.

For more than an hour the Locals debated who the stranger was and what had become of him. Al kept himself busy, but always in the background and always within earshot. Every so often Clem would have to give him a sign to keep his secret, Al wanted to let the men know he was no serial killer, rapist or escaped convict. But Clem was enjoying this far too much to let it end. If the conversation would seem to die down, Clem would fan the flame a little. It kept the debate heated.

Finally John the Liar asked Clem right out of the blue who his new man was. Clem introduced Al around. The Locals sized him up as another fellow who occasionally worked for Clem, a little worn around the edges, but a good fellow nonetheless.

"What brings you to Rock Creek, Al?" was John's first question.

"I was in Viet Nam and promised a friend that if I was ever in the area I would look him up. His folks have a farm near hear. Maybe you know them? The McAuley's."

"You know Paul McAuley?"

"We were in the same unit. I told him after the war I would look him up."

"Paul never came home from the war. MIA. His folks never recovered. But I'll bet you they'll be tickled pink to meet someone who knew their son," John suggested hopefully.

"I wouldn't want to intrude." With that, Al returned to his work, not caring to speak anymore on the subject.

At Daisy's the next morning, John the Liar corned Clem at the Local's table.

"You sneaky bugger. Why didn't you tell us all Al was the van guy? Imagine my surprise this morning when I was fishing down at the Trestle Pool and Al came out of the van." Clem just laughed in reply.

Al arrived at work a little early and went right to work. An older couple drove their Ford pickup in to load it with horse feed. Clem took their order and hollered at Al in the back to help them load. Al came out with a bag of pellets slung over his shoulder and carried it right out to the truck. He heaved it into the back and turned around to find himself face to face with his mother, who he hadn't laid eyes on in nearly two decades. Lu looked him right in the eye and fainted dead away, right into his arms. Louie came running to his wife's side, fearing the worst. Louie never looked up from his wife to take a good look at the man whose strong yet gentle arms cradled the woman who had given his heart more than forty years before. Clem brought water and soon Lu was coming around, but strangely she wasn't looking into Louie's eyes. She was staring into her son's. Tears of joy were streaming down her face, but Louie took them for tears of pain. He searched her desperately for the injury that was causing them.

"Paul. Is that really you?" the question that had been hers to ask for so many years.

"Yes Mama. It's me. I'm home."

Louie looked into the stranger's eyes for the first time and realized his wife was not suffering from delusions. His son was really here, kneeling on the sidewalk in front of Clem's Feed Store, holding his mother.

"Son? What is...where have....how did you? God damn it's good to see you!" And tears were streaming down the older McAuley's face too.

"Louis! Watch you language!" scolded Lu.

It took the three McAuley's twenty minutes of hugging and crying before they realized a crowd had gathered. By then, Paul explained that he was trying to figure out how to approach his parent's after twenty years of being an anonymous vagrant, with a messed up life and screwed up head. He HAD been a POW for quite awhile, but only after being on his own in the jungle for more than two months. He had set up a little camp in an out of the way location, and he with the other three survivors from the helicopter crash decided that the war had passed them by, and rather than go looking for it, they would wait for it to come back. Distant thunder each day had told them all they needed to know about the war's continuing existence.

The four years in the prison camp had taken quite a toll on Paul. Cooped up like an animal had caused him to lose a little of his mind. It had taken him eight years in the VA to get his head right and another eight years on the road to work up the nerve to come home. Each day away had made it harder to return. Finally, he found himself stranded in the very place he had meant to avoid, not wanting to find out that his parent's had died or had changed their hearts about him.

Now he felt such a fool. But he was home.

Fittingly, Paul Allen McAuley was given a hero's welcome. In 1995 President Bill Clinton awarded him a Congressional Medal of Honor in the Rose Garden of the White House for the efforts he gave to save the lives of the rest of his squad and to keep them alive with his hunting and trapping skills. Major General Isaac Washington was on hand to apologize for not letting the McAuley's know their son was still alive. He had been the Major who had visited so many years before.

Clem paid off Paul McAuley before he left the store that day. Paul had wanted a little money so that he could go home with something, not destitute and filthy, which is not a fitting way for a mother to see her son. Clem gave Paul a bit of a cuffing for not being honest with him, but he allowed that he understood.

Paul is still farming the McAuley place, married to a widowed woman with three small children. Grandma Lu and Grandpa Louie love those stepchildren like their own. The elders live in the old house, Louie built the newlyweds a new place on the farm. The McAuley grandkids ride the bus to school every day, and the new Mrs. McAuley, Susan, prays every night that her husband was the last McAuley son to ever leave Rock Creek to fight on foreign shores. Louie and Lu are regulars again at the VFW. Having a son come home with such journey behind him and have him become a Medal of Honor winner too is all they need to hold their heads high again.

Paul takes his daughter and two sons fishing with him every chance he gets. The boys are learning to cast and tie flies, and to them their new dad is just a super guy. Paul's homecoming is complete.
