Regular Army Man


The regular army soldiers, to paraphrase F. Scott Fitzgerald, are different from you and me. They have a separate roster of serial numbers, a payroll all their own, and they are eligible for admittance to the Old Soldiers Home in Washington, D.C. when they can no longer cut it militarily. They pay a pittance each month toward the upkeep of the home.

Staff Sergeant Martin was a regular army soldier. His back was straight, his salute a snapped, shining example, and the way he held his head attested to pride in what he was doing. The sergeant was in the twenty-fourth year of his goal of being a thirty year career man. He had spent years on the all-army rifle team and his footlocker teemed with medals his expertise had brought him. On marksmanship training on the range, he was a quiet man, a thorough man, who helped his charges do their best without having to resort to the bark and bite manner of the other non-coms.

When we went on a five mile march, the sergeant walked twice as far as the rest of us, moving up and down the line and checking on the condition of each of the men entrusted to him. Back in camp from sloshing through rain or snow or mud in hand to hand combat training, Martin, once he had checked on us, would set about maintaining his equipment before he finally hit the sack.

In short, Staff Sergeant Allen Martin was good at what he did because he loved every moment of doing it. Ellie Mae, the wife of Staff Sergeant Allen Martin was good at what she did. And she loved every moment of doing it.

The good sergeant constantly got every "far from the post assignment and pulled the most remote guard duty. He reasoned that this was proper, because they had complete confidence in his ability.

Once, out on a cossack post, one of the men was injured enough to require help from a medical corpsman. The sergeant loaded him into a jeep and took him to the base hospital. While waiting for the man's release back to duty, Martin stopped by his non-com billets home to change his shirt which had been stained by the blood of the injured man.. He opened the door in time to see a soldier trying to put on his pants and run at the same time. This seldom works. Ellie Mae, realizing that nothing would help the situation now, just pulled up the covers and prayed.

Martin caught the fleeing man just a few feet out the back door, and would have dispatched him with his bare hands had he not been held back by two other sergeants who had been lurking in the area in case Ellie May found an opening in her busy schedule.

"Why pick on me," the unfortunate fugitive yelled. "Everybody's doing it." The incident was too much for the ordered mind of Sergeant Martin. It snapped, and he was sent to a psychiatric care unit and section eighted out of the army. We missed having him as an instructor and role model, and everything would have been better if we could have continued to miss him. But he came into our khaki colored lives again.

The guard at the front gate recognized him when he returned, and waved him through. Then, questioning his own actions, the sentry called the sergeant of the guard nd reported it. Two Mps took off after Martin's car. Ex Sgt. Martin showed up at the Company B orderly room and was greeted by the charge of quarters. In return for the greeting, he put a .45 to the head of the CQ, handcuffed him, and took his keys. He then opened the gun rack and took one of each type weapon secured there. He opened the filing cabinet and took out a handful of service records. In his twisted mind he had visions of using these to start his own military unit.

Finishing that, he set forth on his prime mission. He had forgiven the men for what they did to him, but had transferred the guilt to the company officers who failed to control the men. To set things straight, he meant to kill them all.

As he entered the quarters where the officers were sleeping, the first sergeant showed up on a mission to drag the C.O. out for a little late R and R. He came through the door as Martin was leveling the .45. If he rushed his former friend, it would be over before he could wrest the piece from him. He took a different tack.

"That son-of-a bitch is mine", he said, "You can have the rest of them." He reached out his hand, and Martin, with years of reacting to commands, handed him the gun, probably glad to have help in his effort.

The first sergeant laid the butte of the weapon against his old buddy's head and the two MPs, coming through the door, caught him as he fell.

Ellie Mae, evicted from army living quarters, moved into an establishment in the little town just outside the post enclosure. She found that the commodity she had distributed on a friend or friend of a friend basis had real financial potential.

This whole incident scared hell of the rest of us. If this could happen to the perfect example of a U.S. Regular Army soldier, what could we Kilroy come lately, ragged ass yardbirds expect from army life.

My Life of Crime


It was Billy Crow that got us into it. We weren't even full grown yet when he started talking about how easy it was to make a living from a life of crime.

"All we need is a couple of six-guns and the money will start rolling in. Then we can steal some horses and really be in business. I bet that railroad train comes through here twice a day is loaded with gold and payrolls and such."

It didn't take much to convince us. After all, we were approaching that age when we needed loving and living and both cost money unless you were luckier and handsomer than we were.

The guns came easy. Billy stole them from John Lundy who was so old he didn't realize he had them anymore.

The horses were a different matter. We ended up taking them from the hitching rack outside the saloon in the next town. We figured by the time their owners sobered up enough to miss them, we could make enough money in our new business to buy our own.

We were waiting by the water tank outside town when the train came to a stop to fill up.

"Jest follow me and do like I do," Billy told us. He had taken a silent vote and elected himself head honcho. He grabbed the handle by the door and swung himself up the steps, then tripped and fell on his face at the entrance to the car.

Dillard Ennis surveyed the situation, then obediently fell on his.

To hell with these kid games, I thought, so I waited for them to get over their foolishment.

Billy made it to his feet and pulled his gun from his britches.

"Give me all your money and jewelry," Billy told the first passenger he came to.

"Why should I?" the executive type man with the big cigar asked.

"I don't know, but if you're still holding on to it when I think of a good reason I'll blow your fool head off," Billy advised him. It didn't occur to him that the big gun he held in his hand was reason enough.

One by one the passengers divested themselves of their belongings and we train-robbers had quite a stash of loot. We were so busy raking in the filthy lucre that we didn't notice the train had started and was rapidly rolling down the rails, leaving our horses far behind.

When the engine finally slowed almost to a stop going up White Oak Mountain we jumped off and looked around. Not a building, not a tree, not a bit of shade could we see.

"Who's the damn fool didn't think of how we were going to git back?" Billy wanted to know. "I figger out who it is I'll kick his sorry ass all the way to Albuquerque."

"Why we going to Albuquerque?" Jim Shane wanted to know.

Billy gave him a sharp swipe with the butte of his gun and Jim didn't insist on an answer.

Cowboy boots ain't made for walking, and felt hats just keep the sun's rays out and the sweat in. Half hour of hiking made us a sorry lot of bad men.

"Right now I'd give my share of the take for a cold glass of water and a good piece of ass," Buffalo Bart opined.

"Way you been sweating bullets ain't enough juice left in you for a lay," Billy said.

"Maybe not, but I could sure dry-hump myself to death and die happy."

The conversation stopped when a band of half a dozen horsemen came over the rise. The distinctive sombreros and gaudy clothes identified them as Mexicans. Their general appearance made them out as banditos.

"You got any water?" Billy Crow wanted to know.

"All the water you want two miles over the rise. You want to swap for some fresh horses?"

"What'll you do without them?"

"Remount station not too far away. We walk there and pick up new mounts."

"How much you want for 'em?"

"We don' need money. Got plenty. What we need is guns to protect what we got. Give you our three horses for your three guns."

Three guns were all we had among us.

"Sounds good to me. Here's my piece. You sure there's water two miles that way ?"

"Why would I lie to you? You'd just find out I was lying."

The other two handed over their weapons.

"Muchas gracias, Amigos." The bandits pointed the hand-guns at bank robbing Bill Crow. "Now hand over them bags you're carrying. Pronto."

Billy looked at them in disbelief.

"You're robbing us?" he wailed, "We're train-robbers. We're your kind of people."

"Bet yore left tit we're robbing you. Anybody wouldn't rob a damn fool bunch like yourn oughta be singing in a church choir. Oughta have their train-robbing license took away."

They rode off without bothering to check the contents of the bags.

"Only a empty headed loon would fall for an old trick like that. I ought horse-whip you two for going along with it." Billy growled.

Neither man answered. Buffalo Bart was thinking about a glass of cold water. Dillard was trying to figure out how he ever joined up with this complete idiot, and I was remembering that Mama told me to stay from this bunch.

After we had walked three hours in the desiccating sun we found themselves at a set of railroad tracks.

"We're in luck. Here's how we git out of here. When the train comes along we hitch a ride to where it's going and then try to borrow enough dinero to get back where we come from. Except for losing our horses and our guns we ain't no worse off than when we started. The loot wasn't ours 'til we robbed the train."

In the distance they heard a whistle. The smoke from the engine darkened the sky. The train slowed down as it approached and we four exhausted not-quite-men climbed aboard. In the first car was a familiar looking big man with a cigar.

This time he held a carbine across his lap.

"Good to see you gentlemen again. This trip back it's my turn." He motioned to the Conductor while holding the gun on us. "Lock these buzzards up until we get back to civilized territory. I think they're going to have a story to tell the Marshal."

That's when I decided a life of crime wasn't for me. The judge looked me over and decided I didn't fit in with the others. I got two years probation, providing I got a job and stayed out of trouble. That's why I'm shoveling away here in the livery stable. It was the only job in town and I'm sure not going to get on that train to go to one some place else. No telling who you'll find sitting in a railroad car or what he'll have across his knees.

Darling Ellie


The cell was about eight feet by ten. It was one of six in what had been the Sheriff’s Office before a new County facility had been built. The guard pushed one of the levers at the end of the row and the cell door was unlocked. He turned the handle and opened the door for the visitor to go in. The prisoner had been remanded to this sub-standard facility because they couldn’t protect him from the inmates at the regular prison.

A single-faucet sink was on the cement block back wall. An enamel slop-jar, the only toilet utility, was beside it. A mesh-covered single light bulb was in the center of the stucco ceiling.

The prisoner sat on a steel cot, his head in his hands. Stubble covered what had been a handsome face and his hair looked like it hadn’t been combed in a week. His voice was harsh when he spoke and sounded as if he had been crying. The blue prison garb hung loose on him, as if it might once have fitted him before he wasted away.

"God knows I didn’t do it," he said. "I loved Ellie too much to take her life. It pained me to know how they found her, shot dead and lying on the cold ground. She was the only good thing ever to come into my miserable life. I changed for her. I stopped drinking and gambling and tried to find a job to support her and my child. Her Pa told her if she married me he’d turn her picture to the wall and never look on her face again. He didn’t know she was going to have a baby, but it wouldn’t have changed his mind."

"If you didn’t do it," the visitor asked, "Why did you run? Why didn’t you stay and help find the one who did it?"

"I wouldn’t have had a chance. Her Pa and her kin took up their Winchesters and came hunting me soon as she was found. I ran away to stay alive until I could think of some way to help. I was glad when the Mount Airy Sheriff caught me and put me in jail. I thought I would be safe there. All six weeks I was there I prayed they’d find the killer and I could come home to cry over the grave of my love and my unborn child."

"Folks say you killed her because you didn’t want to be tied down to a wife and child. They say you’ve been a wastrel so long you wouldn’t know how to live a good life."

"A good woman can change a man. And Ellie was as good as they come. Before I quit drinking she’d walk two miles in the snow to get me liquor when I needed it the morning after a drunken night. Many times she got up at two o’clock in the morning to make a fire in the wood stove to fix hot biscuits and fry chicken for me when I hadn’t eaten all day. The girl was a saint. She was too good for me or for anyone else in this cold, heartless town."

"You swapped a cord of wood for a pistol not long before she was killed. They claim that’s the weapon she was killed with."

"I got the gun to kill myself if Ellie decided to go along with her Pa and not marry me. I didn’t get it to harm her. I threw it away a few days after I bought it because it looked like everything was going to be all right." He lifted his head for the first time and looked at the man in black standing before him. "I don’t recognize you. What church you from? Of course I wouldn’t know you if you was from the one a few doors down from my place, I ain’t been in one in so long. God and drinking and gambling never mixed too well. But I’m sure glad you came. I want to make my peace with God before they take my life away. I want to be able to see the one I love in the hereafter, and maybe make up for the things I did wrong here on earth."

The visitor placed his hand on the young man’s greying hair. Two months ago it had been coal black.

"Rest in peace, my Son, and find help in the fact that you will sin no more. Things are going to be pretty bad for you in the coming days. When you feel they are too much to bear, take these pills. They will help you bear the pain." He handed a small vial to the sobbing prisoner.

"Guard, I’m ready," the dark suited visitor called. The guard let him out and he went his way.

It was three days later when the guard brought in a breakfast plate and found the prisoner dead in the cell. During the night he had heard moaning and groaning, but that was usually the case in the small prison.

"The coroner said he took poison," The Sheriff told the newspaper reporter. "He must have had it stashed away where we couldn’t find it. Doc said he must have spent the night in great agony. Wonder why he didn’t get something that would let him die easy? I thought he made his peace when that preacher was here, and would be all right for the new trial. Guess he just snapped."

"Who was that Preacher anyway," the reporter asked. "I’ve talked to every Man of God in the county and no one ever heard of Reverend Jimson. Must have been some Journeyman Preacher. Holy Roller or Pentacostal or something."

"Jimson," the Sheriff mused. "Wasn’t that Ellie"s mother’s maiden name?"
