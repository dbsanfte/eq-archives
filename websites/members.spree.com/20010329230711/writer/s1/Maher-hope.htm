 The Dreaded Triad: Parkinson's, Alzheimer's and Dementia: New Hopes
© Dr. John H. Maher, A.B.A.A.H.P.

Parkinson's affects more than 500,000 Americans, 3% of the population over 50, including actor Michael J. Fox and perhaps still the most famous athlete in the world, Muhammad Ali.

According to the Alzheimer's Association, about 4 million Americans have Alzheimer's disease and 14 million will have the disease by the middle of this century unless a cure or prevention is found!

And approximately 25% of persons over 65 already show signs of senile dementia!

While we do not yet fully understand what causes these age-related diseases, research from around the world keeps gathering more pieces of the puzzle.

Benefits of B Vitamins for Depression and Dementia

Researchers from the Sticht Center on Aging in North Carolina say deficient amounts of the B Vitamins may lead to depression and dementia in the elderly. Their research is presented in a recent issue of the American Journal of Psychiatry.

Researchers found disabled women over the age of 65 were twice as likely to suffer from depression if they had a vitamin B12 deficiency than women who had a sufficient amount of the vitamin. Nearly 30 percent of the severely depressed women in the study were vitamin B12 deficient. Only 15 percent of the women who were not depressed had the vitamin deficiency. Vitamin B-12 (Cyanocobalamin) is linked to the production of the vital brain messenger chemical (neurotransmitter) acetylcholine. It helps prevent nerve damage and promotes normal nerve growth and development. B-12 is of particular importance for the maintenance and protection of the fatty sheaths (myelin) that cover and protect nerve endings. Marked B-12 deficiency leads to pernicious (deadly) anemia.

Folic acid, another B Vitamin (B-9), is actually considered a "brain food". Yet, it is the most commonly deficient vitamin in most modern diets, most especially in the elderly! Deficiency in pregnancy is now much publicized as causing birth defects (neural tube defects). However, less well recognized is that folate deficiencies can also cause depression, probably because B-9 is needed in the maintenance of normal serotonin levels, another important "good mood" brain messenger chemical. (Young, Canadian Journal of Physiological Pharmacology, July , 1991.) Indeed, the most popular prescription anti-depressants are designed to optimize serotonin levels. As folic acid is also needed for energy production and red blood cell formation, deficiencies can also cause anemia, fatigue, insomnia, memory problems and even labored breathing.

These two vitamins should always be taken together. Indeed a deficiency of any of the B vitamins can cause mental symptoms!

Mental Abilities Enhanced by Vitamins C and E

Another study done at the University of Hawaii shows vitamins C and E may also play an important role in mental health. Their research is presented in a recent issue of Nature. Researchers found those men taking vitamins C and E appeared to have superior mental abilities to the men in the study who were not taking vitamins. They say antioxidants, like vitamins C and E, can protect the body from free radicals.

Kamal Masaki, M.D., from the University of Hawaii, says, "Antioxidants like vitamins E and C may protect against vascular dementia (brain damage from compromised blood supply) by limiting the amount of brain damage that persists after a stroke."

Vitamin E helps prevent the learning and memory deficits caused by the senile plaques (amyloid beta-peptide) in the brains of Alzheimer sufferers. Vitamin C levels are depressed in Alzheimer's disease, though this is not caused by diet apparently. At least one study shows that mega-doses of Vitamin C in the elderly is protective against Alzheimer's.(Morris, Alzheimer's and Associated Disorders, Vol. 112, No 3,pp121-126)

On the other side of the coin, according to the Dec. 19, 1998 Lancet, smoking, which increases free radical levels and oxidative stress (a pro-oxidant)and reduces antioxidant levels, may be doubling your chances for developing dementia and seriously boosting your chances of developing Alzheimer's!

Exposure to Solvents Is Linked to Parkinson's Risk

Parkinson's disease is a slowly progressive neuro-degenerative disease caused by the death of a small group of brain cells (the substantia nigra) that are intricately involved in the metabolism of the brain messenger chemical dopamine and control body movement. Symptoms include tremors in arms and legs, rigid "stiff" muscles, slowness of movement, weakness and impaired balance.

Jobs involving the use of common solvents hydrocarbon solvents, found in common petroleum-based products such as paints and glues, put people at risk of developing more severe symptoms of Parkinson's disease earlier in life. So reported a new study published in Neurology, the scientific journal of the American Academy of Neurology.

The severity of the symptoms was directly related to the amount of hydrocarbon exposure experienced. Occupations that accounted for more than 90% of the hydrocarbon solvent exposure included petroleum, plastic and rubber workers, painters, engine mechanics and lithographers.

It is likely that hydrocarbons cause the release of "free radicals" into the blood. Free radicals are unstable molecules that are an important factor in many health conditions, including the aging process, and probably Parkinson's. The resultant "oxidative stress" literally burns out the part of the brain that makes the brain messenger chemical (neurotransmitter)dopamine. Antioxidants protect us from excess free radicals and oxidative stress. Therefore, just like in Alzheimer's, antioxidant supplementation has some role in the prevention and early treatment of Parkinson's.

One such antioxidant, Co Q10, has been reported to be of use in early Parkinson's by Shultz here in Southern California at UCSD, LaJolla. Neurology researchers in Israel found that a special family of antioxidants, the thiols alpha lipoic acid and glutathione, were highly effective in rescuing brain cells from dopamine-induced death and may prove very beneficial for individuals with Parkinson's.
(Offen, Experimental Neurology, Sept 1996).

Interestingly, a new study, reported in this months issue of the Nutritional Science News, says, caffeinated coffee seems to protect against Parkinson's. It appears that caffeine increases the level of dopamine. It is the pre-mature aging of the part of the brain that makes dopamine (the substantia nigra)that results in Parkinson's. Don't forget green tea contains both caffeine and poly-phenol antioxidants and is likely protective as well, especially if several cups are consumed daily.

Green tea is to be even further considered because, unfortunately, coffee consumption might increase risk of Alzheimer's. You see, coffee increases blood levels of homocysteine, (an amino acid metabolite) the levels of which are thought to be directly related to risk of Alzheimer's, heart disease and perhaps even cancer. Eating foods high in folate (Vitamin B9 or folic acid), like green vegetables and oranges, lowers homocysteine. This may be why folic acid blood levels are directly related to dementia, heart disease and perhaps even cancer!

Estrogen Protects Older Women Against Cognitive Decline

According to the latest research supported by grants from the National Institute on Aging, older women with high estrogen levels are less likely to suffer cognitive decline and eventual senile dementia.

Women in the study who had the highest free estrogen levels, had a 70 percent lower risk of cognitive decline compared with the women who had the lowest estrogen levels.

Scientist suspect that estrogen in females, and perhaps testosterone in males, promotes new nerve cell formation (neurogenesis)

According to Dr. Yaffe, the head researcher from the University of California, if women with naturally low estrogen levels can be identified by testing, it is possible that they could take estrogen to help ward off cognitive decline.

The researchers explained that the new study measured "free estrogen" that was free from other proteins, which is the form of the hormone most likely to affect the brain.(This testing for "free estradiol" is via saliva, and is now available OTC and on the internet.)

However, before deciding about hormone replacement therapy women should consider other factors, such as evidence that estrogen can reduce the risk of osteoporosis and heart disease, but can increase the risk of breast cancer.

It is further theorized that phyto-estrogens from herbs like Black Cohosh, and foods like beans and legumes, especially soy, and flaxseed may have this nerve stimulatory effect as well, though likely less pronounced.

By the way, this new study supports the theory that taking estrogen after menopause may help some women avoid Alzheimer's disease. Which leads us to our next interesting development.

New Natural Weapons Against Alzheimer's

Alzheimer's is a neurological disorder characterized by loss of memory and progressive decline of cognition. Though its cause is not yet well understood, it appears that again oxidative stress from free radicals is strongly associated with its development. Therefore, there is much well grounded hope that antioxidants may be useful in both treatment and prevention. Such a strategy of maximal and multiple antioxidant supplementation seems especially warranted in those of us who have a genetic history or marker in both parents. Some hopeful antioxidants include Vitamin C, E, Alpha Lipoic Acid, and Co Q- 10.

Antioxidants are probably useful because the brain is so rich in fatty acids. Indeed our brains are structurally 60% fat. (Yes, we are all fat heads!) And 25% of that fat is in the form of DHA! Fatty acids are particularly prone to oxidative destruction. Prasad from the University of Kentucky demonstrated significantly decreased brain levels of membrane DHA, and other fatty acids in Alzheimer patients compared to controls. Therefore a rationale preventive approach is a diet rich in DHA, which is mostly found in cold water fish, and abundant in antioxidants. These can be further optimized by supplementation.

As Alzheimer's is characterized by deficiency of the brain messenger chemical most involved in memory, the neurotransmitter acetylcholine, those supplements which optimize acetylcholine levels and metabolism are often suggested to at least slow the progression of this most frightening disorder. These substances include such esoteric names as Huperzine A, N-acetyl-l-carnitine, DMAE, pantothenic acid (Vitamin B5) Alpha-GPC, phosphatidylserine, phosphatidylcholine, and CDP-choline. (See Vol. 3, lesson 2 of the FREE Anti-Aging Course to learn more).

A new treatment for Alzheimer's disease, using a drug extracted from daffodils, is in the final stages of clinical trials.

The promising results mean that a new drug might soon be available to sufferers of the debilitating brain disease, which destroys the minds of millions of people worldwide.

The National Botanic Garden is apparently so excited by research as the above that it has commissioned a study into the use of native plants in medicine. Officials there are urging anybody with old herbal remedies to get in touch so they can investigate whether other flowers could hold secret cures!

Many of the most effective modern drugs are derived from plants, and some of our most traditional medicines such as aspirin (derived from a chemical found in willow) have herbal origins.

The much more well known herb, ginkgo biloba, can aid concentration and improve memory, probably by boosting blood flow, oxygen, and glucose to the brain. University of North Umbra study showed that ginkgo may help to improve attentiveness and reaction times. According to a 1997 article in JAMA, gingko may help to fight the early stages of Alzheimer's. This may be due in part because Gingko also increases the sensitivity of our brain's memory message sites (acetylcholine receptors).

High and Low Tech Early Warning Diagnostic Test for Alzheimer's: Brain Scans and Scratch and Sniff!

Researchers at the University of California at Los Angeles studied 30 test subjects between the ages of 47 and 82 who had normal scores on standard memory tests. They tested each subject for a genetic variant known to be associated with Alzheimer's

The subjects were then given a battery of tests that required them to memorize and recall unrelated pairs of words while their brains were scanned using magnetic resonance imaging (MRI). The tests were repeated on the same subjects two years later.

The study showed that those who possessed the Alzheimer's genetic variant had greater blood flow to parts of the brain near the temple and forehead, indicating a greater effort was being made to recall the information. Researchers suggest the subjects with the Alzheimer's variant literally had to work harder to retrieve the information.

Two years later, the subjects with the variant showed a measurable decline in memory, though none was diagnosed with Alzheimer's.

The study suggests that MRI has the potential to be a diagnostic tool, allowing for early intervention to delay the onset of the disease.

Details of the study are available at The New England Journal of Medicine.

A simple scratch-and-sniff test might improve the diagnosis of Alzheimer's disease among elderly people at risk for the mind-crippling illness!

Columbia University psychiatry professor D.P. Devanand says people who can't name common smells, but who remain unaware of their inability, appear to be most likely to develop the disease.

"The test was meant to be simple," Devanand said. "Most people score 35 to 40 with no trouble at all."

But of the 19 who had difficulty but weren't aware of their problem, 16 went on to develop the illness.

"People who did poorly and said they had no problem smelling were particularly prone to develop Alzheimer's," Devanand said. The study is reported in (Monday's) edition of the American Journal of Psychiatry.

"This is the first time that olfactory deficits, accompanied by lack of awareness, were shown in a group of people at high risk of developing Alzheimer's disease," said E. Kloss, assistant director of the Alzheimer's program at the National Institute for Aging.

It has long been known that people with Alzheimer's have an impaired sense of smell, she said.

Alzheimer's attacks a region of the brain (known as the hippocampus)which plays a key role in memory. The so-called olfactory bulb, which is responsible for smell, is nearby, and may be damaged by the same processes.
