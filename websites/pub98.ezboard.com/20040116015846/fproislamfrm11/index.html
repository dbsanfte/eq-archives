• 12/30/02: View the entire article: Moral Intervention and the War on Terror


    • 9/11 introduced a new Age to America. As we looked into the ashes that was once a symbol of our wealth, a fever overtook us; a need to do something, anything, to make sure that this would never happen again, and the the terrorists that did this would live to regret it. On September 20, 2001, George W. Bush stated that his administration would not rest “until every terrorist group of global reach has been found, stopped and defeated,” pursuant to the new War on Terror campaign. An admirable pursuit, no doubt, as long as one fails to consider that the US has, in the last 50 years, established and/or protected more terrorists than those that were culpable for this act.

    For a moment, let us consider the real hypocrisy of US terrorism policy. Let us give up the platitudes and tired clichés that really belong in a fairy tale, and peer into the truth behind US foreign policy over the last three decades. Let us disregard, for the moment, remarks about how we belong to a country that values freedom above all other rights. Let us forget that we have been told that the US promotes peace and democracy, and exports a culture of tolerance and mutual respect. Let us disregard comments we have been fed about how the US is a bastion of democracy, and is the living embodiment of freedom and justice. Let us, instead, talk about Iran.

    In 1953, the CIA engineered the collapse of the Mossadegh regime in Iran. Mossadegh made a foolish mistake in the high-stakes game of the oil business; his administration, with the support of Parliament, decided to nationalize the oil industry. This was simply too much for the United States to tolerate; after all, Iran had always been a nuisance, failing to take sides in the cold war. Nationalization had irritated the British, and had a communist-like flavor to it. In 1953, the CIA toppled the democratically elected leader, and installed the young Mohammad Reza Shah Pahlavi. This new totalitarian administration accomplished much in the next twenty years; thousands were slaughtered in the name of fighting communism. Poverty, despair, and torture defined the lives of Iran’s millions of citizens. Political dissidence was annihilated through the use of a secret police called SAVAK, the brainchild of the CIA and Israel. The CIA freely admits that it played an integral role in the development of torture techniques that were used by SAVAK, and Amnesty International was forced to conclude that Iran had “the highest rate of death penalties in the world, no valid system of civilian courts and a history of torture which is beyond belief. No country in the world has a worse record in human rights than Iran."

    Of course, it can be argued that the United States in 1953 was a much different country than the United States after glasnost. We are far older, far wiser, and much more of a moral society then we were back then. In the 1950s, the United States was gripped by Cold War paranoia, and Iranians should understand that we didn’t mean to kill and humiliate them. But since they don’t, we’ll call them a part of the Axis of Evil.

    So we tell the world: forget how we established the bloodthirsty dictator Saddam Hussein in the 1970s. Forget how we installed Ferdinand Marcos in the Philippines. Don’t think about how the Nobel-prize winning Jimmy Carter orchestrated the Soviet-Afghan proxy war from the day the Soviets crossed into Afghanistan, determined to give the Soviets their very own “Vietnam.” Don’t think about how Ronald Reagan first started our good friend Osama bin Laden by supplying him with money, weaponry, training, and access to the most sophisticated equipment on the planet at that time, militarizing the entire civilian Northwest Frontier of Pakistan. Please do not consider how we killed over a million Vietnamese in our illegal war in that country. Please excuse our support for the Khmer Rouge in Cambodia. Pass off our “police action” against North Korea; at the time, we thought it was moral intervention. And Noriega was an oversight, nothing more. After all, this was before.
