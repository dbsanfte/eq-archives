Magnet Magazine #45

The Terrifying Experience
"Magnetic Breakthrough" [Mental Telemetry Records]

The Terrifying Experience is rather the opposite of their name, providing a varied view of straight up rock music. Fronted by former Guided by Voices guitarist, Mitch Mitchell, you can pretty much guess the direction this is heading. And while it does skirt the edges of the former frontman's outfit, it's brief, yet enjoyable when it does happen ("Universal Sheep Coma", "Elizabeth and Mercury"). Primarily, the Terrifying Experience is a rock outfit that houses punk energies that almost seems to careen at breakneck speeds from time to time ("Woodshed Fist Fight", "The Road to Hell"). I guess to sum up the sound of the Terrifying Experience, try and imagine what would happen if Guided By Voices got rough, dirty and edgy. Personally, as a nominal Guided By Voices fan, the nods to the former outfit made this album pretty cool in my book. The times when The Terrifying Experience picked up the pace and started to rock at mach speeds were also rad and got my foot tapping, but sometimes bordered on silly (the lyrics to "The Road to Hell" are rather childish). Overall, however, this is a quality rock album.
kurt - actionattackhelicopter Jan/Feb 2001

The Terrifying Experience
Anvil Jumper (7")
Sample 30 seconds of "Anvil Jumper"

This double dose of The Terrifying Experience catches the band's dirty guitar-led pop melodies in fine form. Less abrasive then previous releases, the two songs on this 7" put a classic 70s rock feel into the year 2000, producing an up-to-date combination of rockin' riffage and familiar pop melodies that can attract a diverse crowd. The vocals are turned up on both songs, placing the chorus-heavy melodies at the forefront as the band drives home the message that loud rock can indeed be cool in the indie arenas of the world. Supposedly from the band's upcoming CD, these two songs are a good indication that another daunting Terrifying Experience will be well received. -- am

Impact Weekly Dec 12-20, 2000
Rock evolution
Terrifying Experience releases second full-length CD
By Sara Farr 

Few people embody rock ‘n’ roll like Mitch Mitchell does. Currently the vocalist/guitarist for Terrifying Experience, Mitchell has had a long and fruitful career in several of Dayton’s highest-arcing rock bands, including Guided by Voices (and Workhorse)…. 

Now that Mitchell is doing his own thing…his voice and his uncomplicated, clear songwriting have developed into a veritable powerhouse.  Terrifying Experience is the sort of band that picks you up by the scruff of your neck and makes you rock – no excuses.  Mitchell’s songwriting cuts to the chase…. 

“Mitch has an ability to write very straightforward, easy songs,” Workhorse guitarist Todd Sonnanstine said.  “His songs really grab you – he knows how to cut away through the flash and the icing and get to the meat and potatoes.  It’s just a gift he has…Mitch’s minimalistic approach created great songs.” 

Nowhere is that more true than on Terrifying Experience’s latest release, Magnetic Breakthrough, on which Mitchell uses a go-to-hell crunch of guitar, drum and bass to create a fusion of the best elements of rock experimentation.  The rock anthems are there, but what makes Magnetic Breakthrough so interesting is its diversity and Mitchell’s evolution as the lead songwriter of a band. 

The CD took a full year to record, according to Mitchell, mainly because of the distance between band members (guitarist Duane Hart, bassist Geoff Ortlip and drummer Mark Deane all live in Columbus), but also because he wanted the album to sound as though it captured different feelings and time frames.  “When you break it down, you can get different sounds and tones,” Mitchell explained…. 

The attention to detail shows.  Songs such as “Tearful Champion” and “Anvil Jumper,” originally released as a single, have been reworked and sound incredible; what makes “Tearful Champion” work so well are the intertwined guitar and bass lines.  The guitar work is polished, yet still raw, and Mitchell’s cigarette-stained vocals are clear and confident.  It’s also a fairly experimental album.  Once again, Mitchell has structured the sequence of the album as though it were a vinyl recording, complete with a Side A and Side B, split by…(an 80-second) electronic interlude titled “Underground Pressure,” concocted by Mitchell’s friend DJ Ali Obvious.  It’s a wild, totally unexpected addition to a TE album, a cut that shows Mitchell’s penchant for experimentation. 

“I like to use weird things to show the separation between the two sides,” Mitchell said.   “I like to work with different people and see if it will work.  I’ll try anything.  If it doesn’t work, who cares? And if it does, it’s captured.” 

Perhaps the best song on the album, “Universal Sheep Coma,” reveals Mitchell’s evolution as a player most clearly – effects-laden guitar lines snake through the song, creating an earthy, instinctual groove.  “That’s a real dynamic song,” Mitchell said.  “The way it’s set up is that it’s quiet, then just bursts into this full rock thing, then it’s quiet again.  I just like that song because of the guitar work.” 

That Mitchell still evaluates his guitar ability, or songwriting ability, is one of the things that have helped make him the godfather of punk to many in Dayton.  His DIY ethic and devotion to the craft have been with him since he first picked up the bass when he was 9 years old. 

“I feel I still have to prove myself,” he said.  “If I’m worthy of praise then great, and if the (songs) are bad, then I’m worthy of that, too.  I guess I’m an eternal optimist.  I want to earn what I get because that’s the only way it’ll mean anything.” 

…“I like this album a lot,” he said.  “I’m really proud of it.  We’re able to do what we want, since we’re on an independent label, and I’m thankful for that.   I do music for the sheer love of doing it….I have never done it for the money and I never will.”
