Better living through dissonance

An interview with Kansas City’s behind-the-scenes independent label god, Ian Thomas

By Seann McAnally
 (See Seann McAnally's bio)

Ian Thomas may be the most criminally ignored force in the Kansas City music scene. Hit the Hurricane in Westport, find the nearest disco-addicted weekend lesbian and ask, "who the fuck is Ian Thomas?" and you won't find out. Hit Davey's Uptown, find the first black-tee-shirt-clad hessian making devil-horns to Cretin 66 or Parlay and ask, "who the fuck is Ian Thomas?" You still won't learn anything.

That's because Thomas is the Quasimodo of the Kansas City music scene. Others go for the flash, the glory, the noise, the chicks and the big bucks. Thomas has put his independent record label to work out of love. And he really doesn't care if you know his name.

Although he has retired from performing his own music, Thomas provided tracks for two tribute albums devoted to members of Guided By Voices. As The Vulture Club, Thomas contributed a psychedelic four-track version of GBV's "Are You Faster" to the My Big Day Jimmy Pollard tribute album. It is currently available in MP3 format at: http://go.to/jimmyp.

Thomas also contributed an acoustic version of "Scissors" to the Tobin Sprout tribute album I, Tobot, originally released on CD-R from Economy Island Records and now available in MP3 format at http://come.to/tobot.

Funded with overtime hours and the sale of precious musical equipment, Thomas' label, Mental Telemetry, is a case study in do-it-yourself, grass-roots independent rock. The label recently released a single by Guided By Voices off-shoot The Terrifying Experience. The single has sold out through Mental Telemetry's distributors, and more releases are in the works.

I've known Thomas peripherally for several years but I've never bothered to dig around inside his head until now. What I learned was that Thomas is a far cry from what I thought an aspiring record company mogul was: he's conscientious, articulate, and he epitomizes an ethic I thought was dead and gone.

This interview was conducted over several e-mails and is reprinted here verbatim. 

What's Mental Telemetry's focus...more or less so-called indie-rock?

There's "independent rock" in the context that relates to Mental Telemetry, and then there's the genre of independent rock, or indie-rock. Mental Telemetry is an independent record label by design...We're operating without help or influence from larger entities, that this entire operation is completely self-contained and motivated. On the other hand, my interests lie very far from the actual genre of indie-rock. I consider Mental Telemetry more of an underground or "outsiders" label with my focus being not so much on specific genres, but on releasing recordings that are unconventional and interesting. Therefore, I would definitely release a good spoken word album or say, a psychedelic country album, if something like that was to catch my attention, and I dig what is going on there.

There are thousands of independent record labels. Why start up another one?

There are tons of interesting small labels putting stuff out these days and I've asked myself the question "why start one up?" many times. Starting a record label was just something that I've always wanted to do, something that I've felt compelled to do for a long time. A lot of inspiration came from some of my favorite punk and underground labels such as Crass, Alternative Tentacles, Touch 'N Go, Datapanik, Siltbreeze, Xpressway, Dischord, and so on. Labels like these both inspired me and infected me with their independent spirits. In a way, I guess I'm sort of paying homage to that entire aesthetic -- the beliefs/systems that these labels helped create and prolong. Aside from that, Mental Telemetry gives me an opportunity to work with artists that I profoundly respect and admire, which is really great. Setting the "business" of things aside, I'm still a music fan and it's just nice to be able to reach people this way.

When did you launch the label?

Mental Telemetry has been active since about January of last year and our first release was issued in February of last year. I tried a number of times in the early ‘90s to get a record label started. But most of those attempts were thwarted because there was either little money or little interest. After my first attempts to start a label failed, I basically holed up and began studying all of the processes involved in manufacturing records and running a label. I also tried to realize a clearly defined goal for the label that I hoped to create. Which brings us to where I am right now with Mental Telemetry.

So how did you hook up with the Terrifying Experience?

I hooked up with the Terrifying Experience pretty conventionally, but how I got them to do the record will always be a favorite story of mine. I'd been a fan of the Terrifying Experience ever since Mitch Mitchell parted ways with Guided by Voices. I really wanted to do something for Mitch and the Terrifying Experience, and I also thought it would be great to launch the label with them. I just wrote Mitch up one day and asked if he would be interested. I sent him a postcard, which pictures of Sonny Bono sporting a tennis outfit, pointing at the recipient. Printed in big bold letters over Sonny's head was the phrase "I WANT YOU!" I think it was a few weeks later that I got the E-mail from Mitch saying they wanted to do the single! So to make a long story short, I sent Mitch and the guys a big bottle of the best bourbon I could find and the rest is history; they released the "Anvil Jumper" single with me. I always like to think we closed the deal with that bottle of bourbon.

Are you making any money or is this a labor of love?

Unfortunately, the label is not paying for itself at this point; it is a serious labor of love for me. Not only have I sold nearly everything I owned to start Mental Telemetry, but I've also worked insane hours of overtime at my day job to keep the cash flowing. I hope to keep the release schedule going -- and maybe eventually, the label will begin to break even financially. I would really like to break even so I could release records without worrying about where the money is coming from.

Do you see this going somewhere money-wise, as in "fuck yes I get to quit my job now!" or do you plan on keeping this as more of a hobby-type project?

I'd be lying if I told you I wouldn't mind quitting my day job and running the label full time. It's great fun, and I'd love to wake up to it every morning, but I don't see that happening soon. Living off of Mental Telemetry is really not a priority for me, and I believe a choice like that might ultimately hurt the label rather than help it. After all, my day job has been keeping everything going thus far. I'd love to eventually have the freedom to run M-Tel full time, and I'm optimistic about being able to do that someday. Things would have to be going very well before I took such a big risk.

What's the biggest challenge you face? Money?

There are many challenges facing a label of this size. However, the biggest challenge is definitely financial -- everything else seems to tie into finances or they pale in comparison. For example, I can't sell records unless I advertise and I can't advertise unless I sell records; small distributors may not carry our catalog because we are unknown and larger distributors won't carry us because we're small or because we are working with lesser-known artists. However, I do feel that a lot of these challenges are essential to Mental Telemetry's growth and that many of them will be eliminated with a little perseverance, ingenuity and time.

So there's no financial reward...what is rewarding about running Mental Telemetry?

Being able to release great recordings and work with some of my favorite musicians are definitely the most rewarding aspects of running Mental Telemetry. I suppose if I was more concerned with turning a profit I might not feel fulfilled at this point, but that's entirely not the case. Just doing this is reward enough. Plus, seeing all of my hard work and effort turn into something after all of these years is a nice bonus too.

How do you promote a small record label?

Promotion and advertising for Mental Telemetry have been pretty low-key at this point. I like to keep attention focused on the music, not so much on the label itself -- and that's where I've spent much of my energy lately. It's an interesting balancing act. Most of my promotional efforts to date could be described as grass roots or guerilla style. I don't have a lot of money to funnel into advertising, so I've relied on friends of the label who help promote releases within their home regions. And then there's always word of mouth. I've done things like photocopy small handbills with label/release information, then slip those inside free weekly papers. I've also done typical things like sending loads of promotional records and letters out to small press and college/pirate radio. But again, it's been a very grass roots effort and only time will tell how well it all works out. For future promotions I've considered hijacking a city billboard, hiring a brigade of bicycling megaphonists and having a Mental Telemetry Day parade…The mayor still hasn't returned my phone calls for this one.

What's the feedback been like so far?

All of the feedback I've received has been very encouraging, and that means the world to me. I get a lot of support from fans, family, friends, and the artists themselves, which makes those days of coming back from the post office empty handed still seem worthwhile. Ordering has been a bit slow through mail order, which probably has everything to do with the fact that we're not equipped for online ordering. Stepping through into the E-commerce realm is just not financially possible at this point in time, so we sell mostly through distributors who are equipped for online sales. Our first release, the Terrifying Experience single, has completely sold out through our distributors.  I hope it's only a matter of time before people feel inspired to order directly from the label. I definitely prefer a more one on one interaction with people as opposed to selling through distributors and record shops, because all I see in that situation are numbers and not names.

Any press attention?

Mental Telemetry itself hasn't received any press that I know of. However, our debut release, a single by Dayton, Ohio's Terrifying Experience, has received some press in alternative music papers and online magazines. As far as more accessible press, this single was reviewed in Magnet Magazine recently, which is pretty big for us and I've heard we might see a review in CMJ as well. I'm truly hoping the small press will get interested soon because there's a lot of support for smaller labels there. As far as seeking press for the label is concerned, I don't like to create a lot of hype around myself. I owe it to the artists to spread the word about their releases and I fully intend to do that, but I'd rather achieve notoriety through subtlety and not flamboyance.

What labels do you admire? Who do you look to for inspiration?

I admire and draw inspiration from so many labels that it's hard narrowing it down to a few, but I'll try to list a handful of my favorite contemporary labels and discuss them a bit...I'd encourage anyone reading to seek out releases from these labels.

Siltbreeze is a damn fine label out of Philadelphia, their output has been top-notch since day one. Over the yeas, Siltbreeze has released recordings from artists such as Angus Maclise (original drummer for the Velvet Underground), Jim Shepard (late Ohio artist/poet/musician, founder of Vertical Slit and V3), The Dead C (beautiful discord by way of New Zealand) and a long list of many other brilliant artists.

Datapanik is now a defunct punk label from Ohio, who I've always admired for their "instant" (read: cheap) approach to releasing records. Datapanik albums usually came in photocopied or screen-printed sleeves and occasionally they had handwritten labels, all of their stuff had a real personal feel to it.

Xpressway was an underground label from New Zealand run by Bruce Russell. Xpressway has been out of operation for quite a while now. But in my opinion, it's one of the most important underground labels to ever exist. X/Way was incredibly prolific throughout its run and released many great recordings from New Zealand, too many to properly give them justice here. Most of X/Way's releases were issued on cassette and done in relatively small, handmade editions - all of them beautifully done. Bruce eventually shut down X/Way because it had met its original intention of exposing New Zealand artists to an international audience. Bruce now runs Corpus Hermeticum, an excellent CD label that follows in the Xpressway tradition.

Mental Telemetry distributes another label, Minutewax...how does Minutewax differ from M-Tel?

I treat the Minutewax label as a separate entity from Mental Telemetry, although it still functions under the same basic principles and ideals. Minutewax is more of an "instant" label. What I mean by that is I don't have to spend a lot of time and money to make releases happen. All of the recordings that are coming out on Minutewax will be done in small limited editions (anywhere from 50 to 100 copies per release) and issued on the CD-R format only. The basic premise here is identical to many underground and punk cassette labels that have flourished over the years, I'm just utilizing a newer technology, CD-R. There's more freedom involved by doing things this way as well. If a release doesn't sell many copies, it will not ultimately affect the label's output. Honestly the initial drive behind Minutewax was to keep me busy during the interim between Mental Telemetry releases. But it's beginning to take on a life of it's own, which is very exciting. I don't know where the future will take Minutewax, but ultimately, I'd like to showcase some of the more unusual blues roots music that's going on here in Kansas City.

You're an extremely talented musician...but it's my understanding that you're more or less retired from the performing side. What led to your decision to step behind the scenes, so to speak?

My decision to stop writing, recording and playing my own music was a very difficult one, but it just seemed like the right time to give it up. After nearly a decade of doing my own home recordings and experimenting with rather loud instruments, I've developed a nagging case of tinnitus in my left ear. The tinnitus was one reason I thought it better to stop now, while I still have my hearing intact. I then decided to sell all of my instruments and recording equipment to help raise money for Mental Telemetry. Of course, I don't want it to sound like I quit only because of this ringing in my ear or to start up my own record label. I had also grown quite disillusioned with much of today's independent music and figured the easiest way to change anything would be to change it from the inside. I also did not want to continue on my own musical paths, because they were becoming less interesting to me, less challenging to me and too conformed for my own tastes. One can only take so many lonely malcontents, bashing away at various instruments in their basements and recording it all to a four-track, before it becomes completely redundant and boring. There are many talented people out there who are much better at doing that type of shit than I am.

How long has music been a part of your life? Is there was one big incident that set you on the path or has it just always been there?

Music has been one of the only constants in my life for as long as I can remember. Some of my very first childhood memories are of my father playing music while I sat in my crib. At the time I was too young to know what it was, but on a daily basis he would play stuff like the Beatles, Led Zeppelin, the Doors, Thin Lizzy and the Velvet Underground. So I literally grew up listening to all of this stuff and I know it has made a deep impression on who I am today; the music was always there. My dad was also a pretty big record collector himself and I think I've picked up much of my rock and roll "romanticism" because of that as well. At a very early age I can remember sitting down with my dad's vinyl records, studying the covers, reading the liner notes, looking at the pictures and wondering how it was all done. I didn't begin dreaming about running my own label until I discovered punk music and got caught up in seeking out these strange and unknown bands on obscure record labels. At the time, I felt a lot of those punk and underground records were being made just for me, like they were speaking just to me, which is what initially sparked my interest in putting out records. I longed to be a part of that, because those records made me feel like I belonged somewhere.

I can't wrap it up any better than that, kids. Mental Telemetry is on the web. Check it out:
