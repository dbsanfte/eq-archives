THE TERRIFYING EXPERIENCE
MAGNETIC BREAKTHROUGH
BY DAVE HEATON
ERASING CLOUDS #5 (April 2001)

Forget Guided By Voices for a second, would you? Yes, Mitch Mitchell played guitar in the "classic lineup" of GBV, but with his band The Terrifying Experience he's been following a wholly different path. More in line with the Stooges-influenced dirty punk of Ohioan bands like Prisonshake and Cobra Verde than GBV's eccentric arena rock, The Terrifying Experience blast through 15 tracks of loud-as-hell, straightforward rock and roll on their latest CD Magnetic Breakthrough. While the most striking part of their music is the guitar sound (big, crisp and raging), here and there they'll surprise you with an almost-pop melody or with a switch in genre, as with the bluesy "The Road to Hell" or the lazy, Meat Puppets-ish dreamer "From Chariots to Garbage Cans." And while the vocals are relatively low in the mix, Mitch Mitchell has a quite sensitive singing voice, one that balances nicely with the rolling thunder of rock that the band delivers. I hope this CD can break out of the "GBV-related" category and reach a wider swath of rock fans, because at this point the GBV comparison is irrelevant and will just lead people to false conclusions and misguided expectations. All in all, what's here is plain and simple, little analysis needed: it's rock and roll, delivered straight to you, without any pretense, posing or slick production.

THE TERRIFYING EXPERIENCE
MAGNETIC BREAKTHROUGH
BY CRAIG REGALA
LOLLIPOP MAGAZINE

Old school Ohio punk-rooted rock. Old as in splitting the difference between the Dead Boys, the Pagans and one of those Raspberries-influenced bands that got lumped in with the punks until someone started using the term New Wave and its Who-derived buddy, power pop. So you get mid-tempo to fast stuff – but not hardcore fast – with thick slabs of Gibson-through-Marshall-stack roar leavened with sturdy melodies riding the riffs or thrown up over them via voice. The guy who put the band together, Mitch Mitchell, has done time in Guided by Voices but it’s the difference between, say, King’s X and Pantera, or The Dickies and the Ramones, or No Use For A Name and New Bomb Turks... Or a huge box of assorted, multi-layer truffles and a life-size bust of Lemmy made from 30 lbs. of Hershey’s chocolate, with a quart of whiskey to wash it down. This is a good CD. I’d pay money for it, and the last new CD I paid money for was Judas Priest’s Hell Bent For Leather. I did get The Gathering’s How to Measure A Planet? used though. Thanks for askin’.

>> Lollipop Magazine

THE TERRIFYING EXPERIENCE
MAGNETIC BREAKTHROUGH
BY KURT
ACTIONATTACKHELICOPTER (Jan/Feb 2001)

The Terrifying Experience is rather the opposite of their name, providing a varied view of straight up rock music. Fronted by former Guided by Voices guitarist, Mitch Mitchell, you can pretty much guess the direction this is heading. And while it does skirt the edges of the former frontman's outfit, it's brief, yet enjoyable when it does happen ("Universal Sheep Coma", "Elizabeth and Mercury"). Primarily, the Terrifying Experience is a rock outfit that houses punk energies that almost seems to careen at breakneck speeds from time to time ("Woodshed Fist Fight", "The Road to Hell"). I guess to sum up the sound of the Terrifying Experience, try and imagine what would happen if Guided By Voices got rough, dirty and edgy. Personally, as a nominal Guided By Voices fan, the nods to the former outfit made this album pretty cool in my book. The times when The Terrifying Experience picked up the pace and started to rock at mach speeds were also rad and got my foot tapping, but sometimes bordered on silly (the lyrics to "The Road to Hell" are rather childish). Overall, however, this is a quality rock album.

>> actionattackhelicopter

THE TERRIFYING EXPERIENCE
MAGNETIC BREAKTHROUGH
BY ANDREW MAGILOW
SPLENDID EZINE (2/26/01)

Mental Telemetry's last Terrifying Experience release revealed a more subdued side to the band. As I listened to that 7", I wondered if the Experience had decided to go soft on me, throwing in their abrasive licks for a more refined pop approach. As the feedback on Magnetic Breakthrough's opening track squeals uncontrollably, it's quite clear that the Experience has plenty of muscle left in their Marshall stacks. As a matter of fact, the band's latest release falls somewhere in between feral psychedelic punk and overdriven rock, crossing back and forth between each genre regularly. Several tunes remind me of Cleveland's The Pagans, without that legendary band's over-the-top vocals, as gritty guitar licks brusquely drive home the point that distortion is still king. Piano, acoustic guitar and even a drum machine-inspired techno number follow, giving you a brief break from the band's deafening sound. Overall, though, The Terrifying Experience has dispensed another round of raucous vocals, amps cranked up to 10 and staggering riffs that'll make eardrums bleed and have you smashing up your parents' furniture in a fit of rock-fueled rage.

>> Splendid E-zine 2/26/01

MAGNETIC BREAKTHROUGH
IMPACT WEEKLY (March 5, 2001)

This is pure unadulterated rock 'n' roll. Former Guided By Voices guitarist Mitch Mitchell and company churn out high-octane tunes with the "less is more" philosophy: they find a cool riff and ride it for the entire song. Distorted guitars and singing -- not screaming -- on some tracks recall the glory days of Husker Du. " Universal Sheep Coma" shows these guys can strip away the distortion and still pull it off.  TE also includes an electroncia instrumental midway through the CD to keep you guessing.

THE TERRIFYING EXPERIENCE
ANVIL JUMPER 7"
BY ANDREW MAGILOW
SPLENDID EZINE (7/31/00)

This double dose of The Terrifying Experience catches the band's dirty guitar-led pop melodies in fine form. Less abrasive then previous releases, the two songs on this 7" put a classic 70s rock feel into the year 2000, producing an up-to-date combination of rockin' riffage and familiar pop melodies that can attract a diverse crowd. The vocals are turned up on both songs, placing the chorus-heavy melodies at the forefront as the band drives home the message that loud rock can indeed be cool in the indie arenas of the world. Supposedly from the band's upcoming CD, these two songs are a good indication that another daunting Terrifying Experience will be well received.

THE TERRIFYING EXPERIENCE
MAGNETIC BREAKTHROUGH
THE BIG TAKEOVER #48

Perhaps the most overlooked (former) member of Guided By Voices still making music, Mitch Mitchell has released three singles, an EP, and two full-lengths that have garnered only a fraction of the attention afforded the solo records for Tobin Sprout and Robert Pollard.  The second of those LP's, Magnetic Breakthrough, is the first to feature drummer Mark Dean, formerly of Columbus legends Pica Huss.  Dean has sparked some renewed spitfire in the band, and Magnetic is an inspired album of diverse allure.   "Anvil Jumper" is a potent three minutes of cataclysm, while "Leprechaun" is propelled by a driving bassline and riff.  GBV fans will most likely find "Elizabeth and Mercury," which resembles Bee Thousand's quieter moments, most appealing; however more than anything Magnetic Breakthrough makes it plain that not all the songwriting talent of that band resided with Pollard and Sprout.

>> The Big Takeover

THE TERRIFYING EXPERIENCE
MAGNETIC BREAKTHROUGH
RAVE MAGAZINE (Australia)

Back in 1996, guitarist Mitch Mitchell left Ohio's cult rockers Guided By Voices and stayed in his Dayton garage to front The Terrifying Experience.  Though they're not as scarifying and abrasive ad the name suggests, Tex (as they sometimes call themselves) have an unadorned, hardened guitar sound that blends the gravity of Detroit rock with the pace of American style punk, but with variable melodic edges to it.  At least, that's where this second full length CD heads, with occasional piano, acoustic guitar and even some half serious techno effects.  It's a bit Radio Birdman (Woodshed Fist Fight), a bit Hüsker Dü (The Tearful Champion), and even a bit Guided By Voices (Universal Sheep Coma).

But, with even more splendid titles like "From Chariots to Garbage Cans," "Stress is the Best Reliever," and "Absolute Bloody Shocker," these guys find their own space in that list of connections.  They shift gears from the high-octane "Road to Hell" to lo-fi grit in "Leprechaun" to a more seething mood in "Static Arrival", even to starkly pretty in "Elizabeth and Mercury".  And that's not the sonic stretch it first seems.  Mitch Mitchell and his band don't terrify at all, but they know how to impress.

ANVIL JUMPER 7"
BY MATT HICKEY
MAGNET MAGAZINE #45

Presented in "new terrorphonic" high fidelity, these two tracks from ex-GBV guitarist Mitch Mitchell and friends are simple slices of crunching, sloppy garage rock.  "Anvil Jumper" succeeds thanks to a strong riff, though the weak, repetitive chorus is unfortunately structured so it sounds like the record is skipping.  Mitchell's vocal style is, to be charitable, still evolving, but he's better on the flip which kicks almost as hard as the a-side but is sweeter and catchier.  Fun, if disposable.

>> Magnet Magazine
