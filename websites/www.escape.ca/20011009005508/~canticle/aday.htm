ANOTHER DAY

by Jeff Franzmann

illustration by John Zubrovka

It’s dark, not the kind where you’re tripping over the pile of unwashed laundry, or where you fumble desperately for a light switch. Or a bra strap, I guess it depends where you’re sitting. It’s more that dark where the light from the television is adequate to the tasks at hand.

Why do I play the host? Some odd sense of responsibility, I expect. I pour a rum and coke, and three vodkas. You would think there would be some variation, but I suppose that the familiar is something comforting.

Into the living room, vodka, rum and coke, vodka, and one for me. I think for a moment about turning down the television, but what would be the point? Shattered glass and crimson streaks of grime might be better than another round of Martha Stewart, but anything gets tired after repeated viewing.

Beautiful missile shield there Dubya.

So they don’t want the drinks. Fucking ingrates.

Not much less than I expected, though. No one’s been here for what seems forever and a half. It just is not what it used to be. It never will be again, I expect.

Who will carry me?

I keep looking for some sign, some indication that there is more to this than what lies before my eyes. High minded intellectuals spent decades, centuries, even more, praising the infinite capacity of man to outlive his own frail flesh.

Wouldn’t they be disappointed to know that the sum total of human knowledge was spread out over the stratosphere, or at least what was left up there. It’s hotter than it was this time last…month? Year?

When everything was the way it was before, I could barely remember the it was in the middle of 2 weeks paid vacation. Permanent leave hasn’t exactly honed my timekeeping skills to any great degree.

It’s definitely hotter. That much I can be sure of. Maybe it’s summer, or maybe it’s just that much less of something this place needs. Ozone slowly baking off into space.

Well, if no one else wants them, I am not about to let a few perfectly good drinks go to waste.

A scratch, something in the dust.

I spin on my heels. Nothing has moved without the bidding of the wind in entirely too long.

Nothing.

Imagination is truly a dangerous thing.

 

***

 

Now, standing here where I am, it seems so long ago and far away. It did not seem so far away, when I was young, climbing through a window as the moon grew ever more bright in the night sky. The scent of the lawn, freshly cut, the stains on my knees as I rose to my feet, looking up and catching the faint call of rain in the air. Every sound amplified. Surrounded by the sighing of the wind in the leaves, and my passage through the grass.

When I finally reached my destination, I would lie back and stare as the thoughts ran through my mind, the prototype of perfection. Sounds of a name.

The breeze picks up, scatters dust over the makeshift boots covering my feet, and I look across the surface of the river. It was here, so long ago it seems, that I found that single moment in time I so longed to share. If she were here, now, would she even remember?

Fetid waters fill the pond, a stinking mire where once I saw reflected the hopes and dreams that carried me through so very many nights. To hold in my arms one more time those hopes and dreams, and know that they would be more than what they are.

There is no coming back.

“Remember me to the one who lives there, she once was a true love of mine…”

There are those who spend moments of their lives as I spend the entirety of my own, staring down at the monuments of those who have passed before and wondering what it is they can do to remember them.   

The living will envy the dead.

 

***

 

The taste of chalk in my mouth, the swirling dust hypnotic, intoxicating. I pull the scarf over my nose, hoping to keep the metallic tang at bay. Standing on the porch, one hand on the rail, another inside the trench coat, the comforting leather on leather whisper reminds me the holster is still there.

Isn’t it always?

I close my eyes, and remember it as it was, the days before the shroud of the sky was blown off. Looking up from this very spot, pinpricks in the velvet sheet, twinkling ever on and off, moon full and bathing the world in faerie light. It was a long time ago, and only a memory exists.

Now, toxins scud across an angry red sky, chasing sickly green whispers. The stars are just a memory, like so much else.

I pull the goggles over my eyes, and step down to the street. This never gets any easier. Not when the wind wails through the dead trees, kicking up the waste and litter. Never easier, still required.

The playground is as empty as it ever is, the same scene I’ve seen what seems a thousand times before. The roundabout spins lazily in the breeze, the little skeleton still draped over its bars, overalls in tatters. I should really do something about that, but I can’t really figure out what. Every day I step out, Winnie the Pooh smiles at me, waving through a film of red dust and grime, and empty sockets ask me why it had to be her.

I never have an answer.

Boots sink into the scorched wood chips and dust, kicking up a haze around my feet. Through the goggles, peripheral vision has that eerie fishbowl effect, and the shadows seem to dance at the edge of my eyes.

I’m too scared to close my eyes.

Cars wait for owners that aren’t coming back. I amuse myself drawing a happy face in a Sunfire’s rear window. I wonder if it will still be there when the rest has rusted away. I wonder if I care.

I step over the cracked glass and twisted metal, empty aisles and shattered bottles on the floor a testament to panic and the end of time. What else are you going to do when you’re told to hold tight and take your last breath because the missiles are on the way? Pull out the lawn chair and toast the apocalypse with the Captain, of course.

I look up and smile. I’m not sure why, I’ve been here a dozen times before, it never changes. I pull a bottle free from the sticky residue, and feel it through the leather gloves. Back home, a lawn chair awaits.

Across the street, everything seems as it was, waiting the flick of a switch. The coffee shop proclaims itself open, and that it is, the glass fronting blown out, inviting me inside.

Why not?

I marvel at times at what was spared.

I sweep shattered porcelain off one of the shelves, and staring me at me is a faerie in relief. Everything else blown to kingdom come, and this one coffee cup dares me to take it.

Who am I to argue with good fortune?

I pull a chair out front, and right one of the tables. The wind is dying down, the dust is settling, and the sun will soon set. It always sets. There are some things you can count on.

I look towards the bridge, and wonder where everything went wrong.

Does it matter?

I put my feet up on the table and watch the sun go down.

Tomorrow’s another day.

 

***

 

There's a low hum in the back of my mind, that insistent electrical droning you feel when you've spent entirely too much time watching TV, or sitting on the computer. The silence always amplifies it, emphasizes the artificial nature of the company you keep.

Only this time, no one's left the TV set on, or forgotten to power down their system. I stand before the empty pews, wiping a film of rust red dust from the pulpit and wonder exactly what it is that I can say. I wonder if I'm the only one who ever gave a eulogy for everything that ever lived, and the only answer that makes sense is no. Everyone who ever stood here before me had the same reason for doing so, and for so very many of them it must have seemed the end of the world.

It is the end of the world.

That vacuum, that gnawing, aching reminder that something is no longer where it should be. And still, nothing adequate comes to mind. Those who came before me, their loss is something I will never understand, just as mine is something they would not understand. How can you possible explain something that defies explanation?

The hand of God made manifest?

Yes Lisa, there is a God. And he is a wrathful, vengeful one.

Maybe the combined sins of Middle America's trailer parks proved just a little too much for the big guy. Maybe it just happened because it needed to happen. I could spend the rest of my life, whatever that might be worth, trying to fathom the reasons for what took place, and the only thing I will ever really understand is that I will never understand.

So here I am. What do I say?

That I should have spent less time watching Springer and more time appreciating what was around me? That I should have known what I had, and spent less time wishing for what I hadn't? That I should have worn more sunscreen for the apocalypse?

A person like me could go mad contemplating everything that wasn't. I could probably spend the rest of my life wondering why I forgot to watch the last episode of the Simpsons that anyone would ever have the chance to see. I could easily spend as much time wondering why I never said hello to the woman who sat next to me on the bus who wore the faint scent of lilac and so very much black.

I look over my shoulder, and Jesus is crying. Rot runs under his eyes, twin little rivers. I run my finger beneath the lids.

It's not that red.

So here I am, I stand before the empty pews, the son of God's tears between my thumb and forefinger. You would think I would have a captive audience, but when the missles landed, there were few who sought the solace of the Church. Maybe it was the Rapture, and I was simply left behind. You would think if that were the case, I would have a little more company. Or maybe it is a hell on earth. Maybe the rest of the world is waiting for me to join them.

Maybe I am just not supposed to know.

What do I say, now that I am here? Why did I come here to begin with?

Perhaps I simply do not want to forget. Perhaps I do not wish to get over it. And perhaps I just wish to remind the empty world of what exactly it has lost.

I was twelve when I saw Jacob’s Ladder. Since that time I have climbed every step of the way, and wondered when the child would arrive to take me into the light. While I am lying on my back, staring at the ceiling, watching a broken bulb dangling from a dead string, I find myself asking if I will ever wake up.

 

***

 

Point me to the sky above.

I pull on my boots, and for the first time in as long a time as I can remember, the lace snaps. They used to do that all the time. I would pick up a package of four from the Dollar Store, just because they were there, and I would go through them in a week. Sometimes it was longer, if I were playing the part of a shut in for the week. I guess at some point I finally got tired of dropping a dollar for black laces, and I picked up a few that would last just a little bit longer. Or maybe I just happened upon that lucky package that contained laces that were not assembled in some South East Asian labour colony.

I can’t get there on my own.

No laces. At least there are still a few rolls of electrical tape. Not like it’s useful for anything else at this point. Round and round it goes. That dull, droning hum has returned. I thought I would finally be rid of it, but I expect it will follow me around until everything finally ends, the last living legacy of the species mixed with that ever present bleeding dust, choking winds carrying the memories six ways from Sunday. Will this be the last time?

Walk me to the graveyard.

I suppose it was inevitable. No one, not a one, can endure as I have. What am I doing? I used to believe it was because there had to be others. How could I possibly be the only one? Evidently, I am the answer to my own question, that elusive riddle I have been chasing since that single day when I raised a bottle to the sky and said I was sorry into a hail of fire.

I guess apologies just do not mean all that much in the grand scheme of things when you’re really not sure what it is you are sorry for.

I never did walk up to the casket. I sat in the back, looked down at my boots, and wondered if my memories of her face would be enough to remind me that for a brief period in time, she had been the most important thing in the world to me. That she saw the world, even if only for a second through eyes not her own and had shared a moment that was exclusive and unique.

Dig up her bones.

The air is thick with dust, thicker than it has been before. I expect it will be time, before long. Will I be ready when it is? Will I ever be ready? When I have the answer, I will be sure to the let the world know. Nothing ever ends, so long as I am here to remember.

One day blurs into the next, an uninterrupted constant broken only by those periods where I dream of all that is around me, shattered again on my waking soaked in salt and dust.

It was a long time past that I stopped wondering what day it was, or the time of year. As a child, I used to turn my face into the rain and taste it on my tongue, the scent that came with the fall of the drops filling my senses.

These days, if I dare to play the part of that long ago child, it is as if I have placed my tongue to the tip of a battery, and I recoil at what remains.

I step outside once more, wondering what it that carries me through each day and the next.

I can't remember the last time I saw it snow. Flakes drifting down from the grey skies above, touching the earth, melting in its warmth, followed close behind by another, and still another. I pulled the goggles down around my neck, looked up at the sky, and marvelled at how grey it really was. It wasn't orange, or green, or the technicolour chemical stew which I had become accustomed to, but a grey slate waiting for me to write patterns on it with my fingers, flakes falling and melting on my face.

I looked across the street, a smile playing at the edges of my lips as the snow collected on the surface of rusted hulks sighing against the shattered curbs. I remember, now, the last time I saw snow. Walking through the park, hand in hand, flakes settling on the still surface of the pond. There was no ice, not yet, and against the sheets of white covering the trees it seemed a sight to live for all at once.

I was the last man on earth.

And I was no longer alone.

"If you are frightened of dying, and you find yourself holding on, you'll see devils tearing your life away. But if you've made your peace, then the devils are really angels, freeing you from the earth..."

I can never remember exactly what it was called, thinking back on those times so far in my past, but it amused me nonetheless. It was hardly the greatest movie I had ever seen, I wouldn't even call it anything close to a good movie. It was the idea behind it that was fascinating.

Living the same day, over and over again, and only your own frame of reference remains the same. Perhaps one day you crawl out of bed on the left side rather than the right, you step on a tack, and the entire course of your day is altered by the consistent, constant, metronome pain shooting through your foot. You bed down for the night, and awake to the same song, the same sun, the very same day, and this time, this time you set foot on the right side of the bed. You walk that much faster across the street, and you make the bus you missed. You sit down where you hadn't been able to before, and the scent of lavendar drifts across the aisle. By the end of the day, you had a phone number you didn't the day before, but you can't bring yourself to call it. And as the day begins all over again, you make that decision to call, or perhaps you dial the number fresh in your memory before it has even been given to you.

An endless series of possibilities in an ongoing cycle, without resolution.

If only it were such a simple thing. One night in my past, from a pitch sky, the rain began to fall. It seemed rain at first, and the scent was clean and fresh. There was a winters chill in the air, and the rain mixed with heavy wet flakes of snow fell to the earth, a damp slush forming on the concrete, the paving stones, the streets. There was an audible crack in the silence as a branch laden with the wet snow gave beneath the weight. I lay on my bed, staring out the window, and I closed my eyes. I dreamt of someone I had not thought of in so very long a time, and for the first time I can remember she did not seem burdened. She stood beneath a dented stop sign, waiting for me to cross the street. I sat with my back against a building, red brick harsh against my back, looking up at a sky which promised rain and slush.

She walked over to me, nervous smile on her face, and put her finger to my chin.

"Do you remember her?"

"It couldn't be. She has another..."

"Why?

"Not every story has a happy ending."

There was the sharp stereo crackle, a hiss, a pop, and her form broke into fuzzy static lines.

"Maybe,
you'll sit and sigh
wishing that I were near
Then, maybe,
you'll ask me to come back again.
And Maybe, I'll say, maybe."

Shattered glass and a thousand yard stare.

There's a film of grime and dust over the lenses of the goggles, and I absently bring up my hand to wipe it away. The wind has picked up again, ending the eerie days on end of silence. The same day, playing over and over again. Left side, right side, only the buses just don't run anymore. There is still no resolution.

I reach inside the pocket of my coat and pull out the snapshot. Well worn, the edges ragged after a thousand and one touches, tear stained edge blurring the background. A lopsided smile, and those eyes. It was always her eyes. And I smile. Despite everything, I smile. For so very long, I asked myself why I continued going on, why I held so close to the anodyne of routine, why I made myself the legacy of the world around me.

Maybe I've found my reason.

And maybe, I'll say maybe.
