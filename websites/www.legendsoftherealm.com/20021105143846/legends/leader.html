Thoughts on Leadership and running a guild
By Rosha Cristi

These are my thoughts and opinions on how to lead and run a guild. I have been involved with leading guilds since before Ultima Online. I first started out on MPlayer with a MechWarrior 2 "Clan" and League. I helped establish the league and was involved with the running of a MW2 Clan. I started playing UO sometime in 1997 and was first involved with helping to run a guild and then later, the actual leader of a guild on another server (the original Legends of the Realm). I established LotR in EQ and lead the guild from it's creation in May of 1999 until October of 2001. These are just my reflections and thoughts on the matter, this is not a "How-to" or a "Must follow" guide. What worked for me may not work for others.
  • A Guild is NOT a democracy
    That was always my number one thought. A guild cannot be run by the masses of the guild. There needs to be one and only one person with ultimate authority and responsibility. This does not mean a leader ignores the inputs and suggestions from the guild. It simply means when there are choices and decisions to be made, you know who is the final authority. If a conflict with another guild arises, you have one person that is ultimately responsible.
  • A Leader surrounds himself with competent people
    I never claimed to know every aspect of the game and I never conducted a large-scale raid. I would rather concentrate on other areas and maintain a view on the entire guild than get bogged down with the nuances of running a raid. I carried the same thought when it came to loot decisions. My feelings in this area were to remain as impartial and objective as possible by allowing the officers to develop the loot lists and loot order. That way if a question ever arose, I was not already partial to a list because I had developed the list. I was able to make a decision based on all the relevant factors.
  • A Leader listens
    By that I refer to a couple of things. I would just listen (read) to discussions or arguments between the officers and/or members. Allowing them to discuss the issue freely without any chance of my opinion adding a bias helped me to understand all the sides to an issue before making a decision. It is very important to try to get to the heart of an issue without letting any emotion about the issue, or people involved, cloud a decision. However, a leader needs to know when to intervene. If the discussion turns from a productive one to a flame war, then it is time to step in and take action.
  • A Leader communicates with the guild
         The guild needs to know the leader is out there and that he/she actually listens to what the members are saying. This is probably one of the most difficult tasks. Listening to the guild doesn’t mean the leader does everything an individual member asks. There are many times when it is near impossible to implement every suggestion offered by a member. However, the leader needs to communicate with the member and guild to let them know they were heard and perhaps explain why that idea is not being acted upon immediately. Providing feedback is not easy, but ignoring the guild will cause bigger problems later on.
  • A Leader establishes direction
    This one is one of the most visible things a leader can do. People like to have a purpose and they like to know why they are asked to do things. Providing goals, objectives, and direction is a means of communicating the purpose for being in the guild. This is an on-going process. As the guild evolves so must the guild’s direction. As goals are met, new ones need to be set and communicated to the membership. This gives people a sense of accomplishment as you meet goals and set new ones. They have a purpose for playing and for being a productive member of the guild. The guild goals or direction should be communicated to the guild on a regular basis. You can deviate from the plan for a while, but if you stay off the plan for too long the focus of the guild begins to blur. It is ok to change the goals and direction of the guild. Change is good and shows that people are in charge and paying attention to what is going on around them. However, don’t make a change simply for the sake of change. There needs to be a purpose behind the change otherwise it will fall flat.
  • A Leader is compassionate
         We are all human. That means we all have feelings and our own goals within the game. The concept of a guild is to merge individual goals with a group, thereby accomplishing bigger and better personal goals. A Leader must never forget that his/her guild is a conglomeration of individuals. Try not to make decisions when emotional. If you are angry or upset with a member, it is best to wait a while before taking any action or making any decisions regarding that member. Take the time to understand their position.
  • A Leader moderates he does not instigate
         A Leader should not do things to add to heated arguments or flame wars between members. A Leader should moderate such things while maintaining a neutral stance if possible. If the leader must choose a side, he needs to do so objectively and without attacking the opposing side. A good rule of thumb is to attack the idea, not the person.
  • A Leader is firm and decisive
         When a decision is made, the leader needs to communicate it unequivocally. The guild needs to know the leader's position and that the decision has been made.
    •  
  • A Leader supports
    A leader should support his guild and the members of his guild. This is not to say the leader will support a very bad action of a member, but if the member was in the right or doing nothing wrong, the leader should support and defend that member.
