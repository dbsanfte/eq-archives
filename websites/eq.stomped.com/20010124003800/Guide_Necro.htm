    Aaahhhh, the Necromancer
     

    The Complete Necromancer Guide Levels 1-40

    By Eerok (a.k.a. Ctuchik the Necromancer, Errollisi Marr)

    For questions, comments, clarifications, and additions email me at ebenton@umacs.net

    Last Updated 08/20/99



    Aaahhhh, the Necromancer. Evil. Powerful. Fun. These words sum up this class rather well. They are an evil class, reviled by most citizens of Norrath. Their power is drawn from the undead and the life forces of the living, and such power it is! The are very fun to play, as they have a wide range of spells and abilities, and are rich in role-playing opportunities.

    Why a Necromancer guide? I asked myself this same question some time ago when I wrote up my Druid guide, and the answer remains the same: because the current guides and information available SUCK and in many cases is just plain wrong. Even though I haven't updated my druid guide in a long time I STILL get email every day telling me it’s the best guide they have ever seen (ahem…its available at Allakhazam's site at: http://everquest.gamestats.com/). So with that type of feedback I take on the project of a Necromancer guide, mainly because that’s the type of character I chose to play after I stopped playing my Druid.

    As with the Druid guide I will base this guide on my actual playing experience, with an emphasis on the spell descriptions and how I use them (and my opinions of them). I also tend to liberally interject comments on a wide range of things that I have encountered in the game. As with the Druid guide I will add some Necro Thoughts at the end of the document, and add to the guide as long as I continue playing this character.

    Creating your character.

    You can choose from Human, Dark Elf, Erudite, and Gnome as your starting race. I try to stay away from recommending the "best" because it's pretty subjective, but clearly from a straight "best stats" perspective, the Dark Elf and Gnome are the leading contenders. Erudites have the super high Intelligence, but their Agility (affects Armor class) and Dexterity (affects how often you can recover casting a spell after getting hit) are much lower than the DE or Gnome. I chose a Dark Elf and this guide will be skewed towards that race. A clear advantage the Dark Elf has over the other choices is being welcomed with open arms in their starting city. The other choices have the advantage of not ALWAYS being KOS (Killed On Sight) in "good" zones. They are KOS by some NPC's, but just not as frequently as Dark Elves. Stat-wise I pumped my INT up to 120 and put the rest in AGILITY and DEX (I think I have 95 in both stats).

    Starting Out.

    As a Dark Elf Necromancer I started in the Nektulos Forest, and I gotta say that this zone really bites. None of the newbie monsters carry good loot other than decaying skeletons and the monster spawn is low. One mistake a lot of newbie Necro's make is summoning their first level pet to help them out. There is NO reason to do so and it will only slow you down in your leveling. I don't even recommend buying your first pet spell, wait till 4th level. Your goal here is to level to 5th as fast as possible and then GET OUT OF THIS ZONE. However before you do that you have to master your 1st rank of spells, and they are as follows:

    1st Rank Spells

    Lifetap: A minor life drain spell. It will start out by draining 4hp of life from an enemy and, if you are not maxed on health, it will add 4hp to your life. It improves to 5hp as your skill improves. At 9 mana cost (uses Alteration skill), it is very mana-inefficient if you view it as a burn spell. Get used to this. Necromancers do not get a true burn spell until 24th level, and there will be many times you wish you had one. The Lifetap line of spells is your only method of applying a significant amount of damage instantaneously (to non-undead), but due to the large mana consumption you want to avoid doing this if at all possible. A smart Necromancer only uses this line of spells when he is truly in need of healing.

    Disease Cloud: Inflicts 5pts of damage on an enemy and diseases them, preventing them from regaining health by natural regeneration. It cost 10 mana to cast and uses Conjuration skill. The disease effect lasts a good long time, so you should only need to cast one during a typical combat. This spell is very useful for the first 12 levels or so, as it enhances the effectiveness of your DOT spells by counteracting the monsters regeneration while the DOT slowly kills them. After level 12 you have enough DOT spells to stack on a monster that kills them so quickly that casting this spell is not necessary.

    Cavorting Bones: Your first pet spell. It summons a 1st level skeleton to fight at your side. It cost 15 mana and a bone chip to cast and uses Conjuration skill. I do not recommend this spell for beginning necromancers, as using your pet will slow your leveling (your pet shares your XP). You are powerful enough solo to kill moss snakes and decaying skeletons at first and second level, then hit the spiderlings and fire beetles till you hit 4th level, at which time you can summon a REAL pet.

    Cold Light: A minor light spell. 15 mana to cast, uses Conjuration skill. Can't comment on it because as a Dark Elf I have Infravision and therefore never bothered to purchase it. You have plenty of Conjuration spells to get your skill up without using this spell.

    Invisibility to Undead: Self-explanatory. Cost 40 mana to cast and uses Divination skill. Many undead can see through normal invisibility (Gather Shadows), but Invisibility to Undead makes you undetectable by most (not all) undead. There will be situations (like in Lower Guk) when it is necessary to have both Gather Shadows and Invisibility to Undead in order to go completely undetected (note you cannot accomplish this in your own, as casting one dispels the other) Not useful to raise Divination skill because of the high mana cost, rather you should use…

    Locate Corpse: An extremely useful utility spell. Allows you to locate the corpse of a targeted person, or if you don't have a target it will find the nearest corpse. Necromancers do not have to sweat dying and being unable to find their corpse, thanks to this spell. Cost 5 mana and uses Divination skill, so a very good method of keeping Divination maxed out.

    Minor Shielding: Your starting self-buff spell. It adds to your AC and health (6 AC and 10 health approximately). Keep this spell active all the time. It cost 10 mana and uses Abjuration skill. Let me warn you young Necromancers to ALWAYS max the Abjuration skill out at every level by casting this spell. I ignored it and am now paying the price when I cast my higher level shield spells that are VERY difficult to cast and will fizzle repeatedly, draining your entire mana bar in the process.

    Reclaim Energy: This goes down into the list of "junk" spells in my book. This spell is used to kill your pet and recover some of the mana cost of casting. Good in theory but in its current form the insignificant amount of mana recovered isn't worth the effort of memorizing the spell. Cost 5 mana to cast and uses Conjuration skill.

    Sense the Dead: A very useful spell. It points you in the direction of the nearest undead. The downside of this spell is it WILL point you towards other Necro pets (but not your own), so its pretty useless in Nektulos. But in other zones without a bunch of Necromancers running around, its very useful tracking down skeletons when you are on a bone chip hunt. Cost 5 mana to cast and uses Divination skill.

    Siphon Strength: A spell of small usefulness. It has the effect of draining 9 pts of strength from a monster and adding it to your own. Repeated castings do not give continued benefits. Doesn't really impact the damage dished out by monsters, but it is a useful spell if you are encumbered and don't want to drop any of your loot. Cost 5 mana to cast and uses the Alteration skill.

    Once these spells are mastered and used effectively, gaining 4th level should be done in a matter of a couple of hours. At this time I highly recommend any Dark Elf or Human Necromancer (based in Freeport) make the journey to Innothule Swamp to fight the Froglocks there. Doing this will give you a huge jump in your ability to gain XP AND loot. The main trouble with Nektulos, East Commons, and North Ro is the paucity of the loot gained. Sure you can get experience, but what good is it to gain your next rank of spell casting ability and not be able to buy any spells? For this reason I suggest the journey to the Swamp. There you will be able to fight Froglock varieties until 8th level, at which time you can go into Guk dungeon and get Woven armor or you can go to Ogguk and fight at the tough skeleton camps to get bone chips and loot. Of course this tip doesn't help Erudites and Gnomes much, but hey, I admitted this guide would be skewed towards Dark Elves =)

    Once in the Swamp you should begin slaying numerous Froglocks with your newly acquired 2nd rank spells. They are as follows:

    2nd Rank Spells

    Clinging Darkness: Ah, your first DOT (Damage Over Time) spell! Be excited! Be very excited!! DOT spells are the foundation for of your Necromantic power. No other class has the range and potency of DOT spells (and reliance upon) that the Necromancer has. Clinging Darkness is the first in your line of Darkness spells. The effect of this spell is to slow down your opponent for a period of time (like Snare) and at the same time cause damage. The Darkness line of spells will almost always be your "openers" against opponents. If things go bad but you got a Darkness spell to stick, you should be able to outrun your opponent and escape to safety. They are also perfect for preventing cowardly opponents from fleeing your wrath. The Clinging Darkness spell at one time was bugged and had NO end to its duration. Of course I made this discovery by casting the spell on myself to test its effects and was shocked as I watched it slowly kill me, despite my efforts at healing and having a bard friend play his healing song. However, that bug has since been fixed and the current effect of this spell lasts about 30-60 seconds and does about 20pts of damage to an opponent. It costs 20 mana to cast and uses the Conjuration skill. All DOT spells are very efficient in Mana-to-Damage ratios.

    LifeSpike: The next in line of your life drain spells. It costs 18 mana and drains 10hp of life, adding to your own if you have been injured. It improves to 11hp as your skill increases.

    Endure Cold: Increases your resistance to cold. Cost 20 mana and uses Abjuration skill.

    Fear: An excellent spell. When successful it causes an NPC to flee for a period of 10 to 30 seconds. This is an excellent spell to use if you have successfully cast your DOT's on an enemy and do not want your pet to take too much of a beating. However only use it if you are not concerned about the monster bringing back friends. The monster will run away, allowing your pet to get free hits on its back, and your DOT will be damaging it the whole time. Very nice. Also a nice "get away from me" spell when a monster is kicking your ass. Unlike most Necro spells, it has a fast casting time, so if things are looking grim (what? Your pet died? Uh oh…) you might try this spell to get a mob off of you long enough to gate out. Cost 40 mana and uses the Alteration skill.

    Gate: Your all-purpose take me home spell. I can't verify this, but it seems to me that the Necro version casts slower than the Druid version. Anyway, it takes you to your bound point, same as all the other Gate spells. Cost 70 mana and uses Alteration skill.

    Grim Aura: Almost qualifies for the "junk" pile, but not quite. It adds to your attack value, making it easier to hit monsters in melee. Problem is, you really shouldn't be in melee Mr. Necro. There are times when you may want to trade some hits so your pet doesn't get pounded, but your purpose then is as a mini-damage sink, the damage you're able to do is pathetic. Therefore after about eighth level I no longer bothered casting this spell. Some sites claim this spell also enhances your AC, but don't believe it. Cost 25 mana and uses Alteration.

    Leering Corpse: Your first pet spell worth casting. It will summon a 3-5th level pet, and dramatically enhances your killing power =) If you got a rusty weapon, go ahead and give it to him. If you got a scythe, DEFINITELY give it to him, cause it looks REALLY cool =) Cost 40 mana and a bone chip to cast.

    Numb the Dead: An undead specific Lull spell. Lets you turn undead to non-aggressive. Its main purpose is to allow you to Numb several members of a large camp of undead, thereby allowing you to pull and fight only the ones you want, not the whole mob. Cost 20 mana and uses Abjuration skill.

    Poison Bolt: An excellent spell. Does an immediate 8hp of damage, and poisons the opponent. Total damage for the spell is about 30hp. It stacks nicely with Clinging Darkness. At low levels you can easily take out tough (white, yellow) monsters by opening with Clinging Darkness, telling you pet to attack, and hitting with Poison. Spell cost 30 mana and uses the Conjuration skill.

    Sense Magic: Useless.

    The battle of the Froglocks should easily take you to eighth level without ever having to leave the Swamp. You should have started noticing a phenomenon that will be with you your entire Necromantic career. That is, the first two levels after gaining a new spell ranking go by fairly smoothly because your pet is very powerful in relation to the monsters you need to fight to get experience. The last two levels are much harder as your pet is weaker in comparison to the monsters you need to fight to get experience.

    To this point I haven't talked much about grouping. Although the Necromancer is a highly soloable character, much like the Druid, I would recommend grouping every chance you get to build up a list of friends. Although it is very possible to solo to very high levels, all of the good magic items are dropped by NPC's that require groups to defeat. If you ever hope to get good equipment and truly experience the game to its fullest, get used to grouping at low levels. If you get to 20th level solo and have never made any friends, don't expect that people are going to let you join their group just cause you enter a zone.

    So once you hit eighth level of course you run out and get your 3rd rank spells. Now what? I spent my time diving into Guk dungeon and hitting the skeleton camps in Ogguk. This prevented too much monotony and boredom. Of course, with these tougher monsters its essential that you use your new spells effectively, so here they are:

    3rd Rank Spells

    Bone Walk: Your next pet spell. Summons a 6th to 8th level pet. Essential to your survival. Costs 80 mana and 2 bone chips.

    Dark Pact: This is a great spell. It slowly drains your life and adds to your mana. If you are doing a good job and staying out of harms way, this spell will dramatically decrease your down time. I cast it every time I need to meditate 2 or more bubbles of mana and I have full health. Combined with meditation it about doubles the rate at which you recover mana. Can also be cast when you are beginning a long journey and don't fear being ambushed. By journey's end you will be low on health but have your mana restored =). Its double effective because once you have full mana you can use your life drain spells to restore your health. It’s a very sick circle of Necromantic power =) Cost 5 mana to cast and uses the Alteration skill. Also, once you hit 20th level you can heal almost as fast as this spell drains your life, so casting it has very little impact on your health. I still prefer this spell over Allure of Death in many situations

    Dark Empathy: The Necromantic way of healing your allies. Drains your life and transfers it to your target. Recipients of my benevolence have told me it heals about 40hp a casting (damaging you 20pts in the process). Mana cost 20 and uses the Alteration skill.

    Ward Undead: The first in your line of undead specific direct damage spells. It is an excellent addition to your repertoire. It only cost 30 mana to cast yet does 41 pts of damage, so it has very good damage-to-mana ratio for a direct damage spell. It is also your first spell to use Evocation, so you will likely fizzle and be resisted a lot until you get your Evocation skill up to speed.

    Lesser Shielding: The second in your line of self-buffing Shield Spells. It adds approximately 30 health, 12 AC and 8 MR. Nothing great, but certainly good enough to keep active all the time. It cost 25 mana to cast.

    Deadeye: The necromantic version of See Invisible and Infravision. An excellent spell for those situations where your party needs to travel invisibly. Unlike most other necromantic buff spells, this one can be cast on others. It creates a cool little sparkle effect out of the eyes of the recipient and it lasts a long time. Cost 35 mana to cast and uses the Divination skill.

    Gather Shadows: The necromantic version of Invisibility. Makes you invisible to most creatures, but it has a very erratic duration. This is a self-only spell. Cost 35 mana and uses the Divination skill.

    Impart Strength: Haven't used yet.

    Mend Bones: A pet healing spell. Although your pet regenerates very quickly, if you are full mana it is more efficient to cast a couple of Mend Bones on the pet and then meditate while your pet heals the rest of the way, than it is to sit around doing nothing while your pet heals all the way. From 8th to 12th levels you can also use this spell to help keep your pet alive in a tough combat, but after 12th this spell doesn't heal enough damage to offset the amount of punishment your pet is receiving. You are better off casting on the creature beating your pet to kill it faster. It costs 25 mana to cast and its difficult to judge how much it actually heals, since you don't know how many HP your pet has, but it appears to be pretty mana efficient. It uses the Alteration skill.

    Shadow Step: An escape spell. It teleports you a short distance in a random direction. It’s a spell to use when you recognize that if you don’t take some desperate measure you're going to die. Certainly it is not a sure fire escape. It can easily transport you to a worse location, especially if you are inside a dungeon. In fact I make sure I DON'T memorize this spell in a dungeon, because it can potentially really complicate corpse recovery. In large outdoor zones however it can be very useful. The best use I have found is to cast it two or three times in quick succession then cast Gate. It cost 10 mana to cast and uses the Alteration skill.

    Vampiric Embrace: A short-term self-buffing spell. If you like to enter melee combat frequently then this is an excellent spell, as it will randomly fire a LifeTap spell when you strike an opponent. The spell starts by draining 29pts from an enemy, and increases as your skill goes up. It does NOT fire off very often however, which is disappointing. It does usually give you at least one drain per casting, which essentially "pays" for itself, as it only cost 30 mana to cast. Sometimes it will fire twice before the duration expires, which is nice, but I've never had it go off three times during a single casting yet. Contrary to popular belief, you CAN use a weapon while this spell is active, and you increase your chances of the spell working if you use a faster striking weapon (logically, because you are giving it more attempts to work). Its not much of a spell for Necromancers to get excited about, but I bet it ROCKS when a 22nd level Shadow Knight gets to use it =) It uses the Alteration skill.

    Mastery of these spells takes us to 12th level. Congratulations! 12th level is HUGE for a necromancer, as you get several very significant spells. By this time you probably have a mostly full set of Woven armor from killing the Froglocks in Guk, a good supply of bone chips, and a desire to spread your evil to other parts of the world. Although you should still hunt Guk due to the excellent experience and loot you can get, there is nothing wrong with trying new places. About this level many characters start hunting crocs in Oasis of Marr. I laugh at those mindless drones. How BORING!! Sure you can power level to 20th in Oasis fairly easily, but you'll be bored out of your skull and broke as well. I sneer at people that tell me they are bored with Everquest because its so monotonous, and all they have done is hunt crocs in Oasis to level as fast as they could. Surprise! There are other places to hunt that give excellent experience AND good loot, plus a lot more excitement than killing crocs. I spent a lot of time in South Ro hunting Dervishes, zombies, mummies, spiders (wait till you catch a glimpse of Terrorantula) and madmen and NOT ONCE was I killed by a sand giant. There are many places like this if you are willing to seek them out.

    However your first order of business is to travel to Neriak and buy your:

    4th Rank Spells

    Bind Affinity: Used to reset your home point. Cost 100 mana and uses Alteration skill.

    Convoke Shadow: Your next pet spell. Summons a 10th-12th level pet. This is significant because a 12th level pet is able to hit magic-only creatures. So if you want to go wisp or ghoul hunting you need to keep summoning until you get a 12th level pet. Also at this level its probably best to stop giving your pet crappy rusty weapons, as you will limit the speed and damage of their attack. The spell cost 120 mana and 2 bone chips to cast.

    Endure Disease: Never used.

    Engulfing Darkness: An excellent upgrade to your Clinging Darkness spell. Same slowing effect as Clinging Darkness, but the damage-over-time aspect is considerably improved. It does about 100pts of damage over a 50-60 second span of time. At 12th level its expensive and you'll want to keep Clinging Darkness memorized for those times when all you want is a snare effect to prevent monsters from running. It cost 60 mana to cast (as opposed to 20 for Clinging).

    Heat Blood: A new DOT line of spells for you! This is the first in the Blood series of spells and it is an EXCELLENT addition to your arsenal. It does about 120 pts of damage over a 50-60 second span, and it only costs 72 mana. Better yet, it is stackable with the Darkness line of spells. Combat now will consist of you opening a combat with Engulfing Darkness, sending your pet to attack (assuming you are solo), and then hitting the enemy with Heat Blood. Then you sit down and meditate while the pet finishes it off! If your careful to watch your pets health you can often be close to full mana by the time the combat is over =) I caution you tho to not cast Heat Blood too soon. Let your pet beat on the enemy for awhile. If you immediately double DOT the monster with Darkness and Blood, it will almost certainly ignore your pet and try to take you out. However if you let the pet fight awhile before Blood, you should be ok. Just keep an eye on the pet and heal him if needed.

    Lifedraw: Next in line in our life-draining spells. It’s a good one too. It starts at draining 40pts of life and adding it to your own and slowly increases as your skill goes up to a max of 43. At 63 mana a casting don't think to use it as a burn spell unless the situation is grim, as you will very quickly be OOM (Out Of Mana).

    Sight Graft: Never used.

    Spook The Dead: An excellent addition to our undead-only spell list. It causes an undead monster to turn and run for 10 to 30 seconds. Strategic use of this spell will allow you to solo very tough undead creatures such as Drybone skeletons. My strategy is to pull the undead monster with Darkness, have the pet intercept before it gets to me, cast Heat Blood on the monster and then Spook it. This causes the monster to flee (slowly, because Darkness is still in effect), letting the DOTs work and the pet beat on its back. This method will let you solo yellow and red Drybones (assuming they are barely reds). No other class can solo Drybones as soon as a Necromancer, not even a Cleric. Always be wary when attacking red monsters however. One or two resists will mean an end to your plans and you better start fleeing while you pet is still alive to distract it. Yellow or Whites you can usually handle one or two resists, but not red. This spell costs 10 mana to cast and uses the Alteration skill.

    Wave of Enfeeblement: A pathetic excuse for a spell. Supposedly reduces the STR of monsters in an area effect. However I have never noticed any difference in the frequency of hits or damage monsters do after this spell is cast. Worse, it does NOT give you any additional STR. I can't think of a time when you would not be better served by casting more DoTs or life drain spells rather than this one. Cost 40 mana and uses the Alteration skill.

    That brings us to 16th level, where once again a new rank of spells becomes available. Now it starts to be more important to group on a regular basis. Although soloing is still very viable you will find that its more difficult to find solo-able monsters that give good loot and treasure. You will find that just "average" loot of a few silver to a few gold off a solo kill will not be enough to raise the funds to purchase your next rank of spells. Certainly killing crocs won't do it. So now you need a group to go hunting for those monsters that drop the items that can be sold for REAL money, like Runed Totem Staves and Bronze armor and weapons and other various magic items. If you haven't built up your list of friends by grouping before this then you might find it difficult to "break the ice" with adventurers that already have their list of friends established. Hopefully by the time you reach 16th you have enough money to buy your (as you will see by the number of spells I haven't used, I didn't have the money to purchase them all yet):

    5th Rank Spells

    Banshee Aura: Never used.

    Cancel Magic: Theoretically this spell is excellent. My worst nightmare in combat is to be rooted. Cancel Magic is supposed to be the answer to root. In practice it doesn't work that way and I never memorize this spell unless someone specifically asks for me to cast Cancel Magic on them. The problem with root is that almost certainly you have monsters beating on you if they bothered to root you in the first place. It is a difficult spell to get off in combat, and to complicate matters it doesn't always work. Therefore I have found that if I am rooted and in serious danger I devote all of my energy to either killing the threat or Gating out. This spell IS useful to get rid of those nasty DOT spells that remain from combat with a spellcaster. Cost 30 mana to cast and uses Abjuraton skill.

    Cure Disease: Self-explanatory. A must if you plan on fighting mummies. Cost 20 mana to cast and uses Alteration skill.

    Feign Death: Although I have heard reports of people successfully using this spell, I have placed it on my "useless" list. It has a high mana cost (60) and it frequently just doesn't work. It makes you fall to the ground and you are completely immobile until you hit the "stand" button. Trouble is, even though you ALWAYS fall to the ground, the monsters are not always fooled, and will continue beating on you as you lie there. Dungeons are the only exception, as I hate using Shadow Step in a dungeon. Truth is I rarely have any escape spell mem'd other than gate. Uses the Abjuration skill.

    Heart Flutter: A new line of DOT spells! This is a good one, although not as good as Engulfing or Blood due to the fact that it is resisted quite a bit. It does about 100pts of damage over time and reduces the enemy's Strength and Armor Class by a good amount. This makes life easier on your pet, so I usually cast Flutter BEFORE I cast Blood now. There are few monsters (in your level range) that can stand up to you putting Darkness, Blood, and Flutter on it while your pet beats it to death. At 80 mana its expensive, but if you are depending on your pet I highly recommend it.

    Hungry Earth: Never Used.

    Infectious Cloud: Never Used.

    Restless Bones: The latest and greatest pet spell to date. Summons a 13th-16th level mean fighting machine to protect you. Highly recommended for the sophisticated Necromancer. Cost a whopping 160 mana and 2 bone chips.

    Shielding: Next in line in your personal shield spells. Adds about 45 health and 15AC to your total. You still can't tank, but I suppose every little bit helps. Cost 50 mana to cast.

    Shieldskin: A new shield spell for you. It absorbs about 100 hp of damage before disappearing! It is an excellent spell with a big problem in that it requires a Cat's Eye Agate every casting. Those things cost 2 and a half gold each, so you don't really want to buff with this spell unless you know your gonna get beat on. 100hp protection sounds like a lot, but at this level you are basically talking about two, maybe three rounds of protection. Good enough to let you get off one or two spells while a monster is smacking you, but that’s it. Still, it is often the difference between life and death in those nasty melee's. BEWARE that if a monster casts a magical attack on you it instantly dispels your shield. Not for sure why, but its true so don't waste your mana or Agate if you expect to get blasted by magic. Mana cost is 41 and it uses the Abjuration skill.

    Spirit Armor: 16th level must be the Shield level, because you get yet another Shield spell. This one is different in that it only increases your AC (about 20 pts) and you can cast it on others. However it is very difficult to cast and unless you have followed my advice and maxed out your Abjuration skill, you will frequently burn your entire mana supply just trying to successfully cast this spell. Its EXTREMELY irritating. Mana cost is 75.

    Voice Graft: Never used.
     
     

    Using these spells wisely will quickly get you to 20th level and access to your:

    6th Rank Spells

    Allure of Dead: This is an upgrade to the Dark Pact spell. Instead of stealing 4hp and restoring 4 mana per round, this spell does 8 and 8. It also lasts longer than Dark Pact, so be careful! An absent minded Necromancer can easily kill himself with this spell. I like Allure and it’s a good addition to our arsenal. In most normal meditating circumstances I still prefer Dark Pact, as Allure will leave you severely damaged most of the time. However, for those times when the mobs are coming one right after the other and you are doing constant life draining spells, Allure is awesome and you will have very little down time. Cost 5 mana to cast and requires the Alteration skill.

    Animate Dead: Our next pet spell. Summons an 18th to 20th level pet. Enjoy it, as this is the last time you will ever summon a white pet. Cost 200 mana and requires 2 bone chips.

    Expulse Undead: This is the next Undead only direct damage spell for the Necromancer. It cost 60 mana to cast and does 95 pts of damage, which makes it a little more mana efficient than Ward Undead, but beware that unless you have your Evocation skill maximized this spell will fizzle often.

    Harmshield: This is a research only spell that requires you to find the Word of Refuge and Word of Absorption before you can create it. Make it a high priority to do so however, because this is an excellent spell. Although it takes FOREVER to memorize, it casts almost instantly, by far faster than any other spell we have (including Feign Death or Shadow Step). It casts so fast it would be the worst form of bad luck for you to actually get interrupted during the casting. Once cast you are provided with about 15 seconds of complete invulnerability. I see the natural use for the spell as a way to allow yourself to get out of trouble. Simply fire off the very fast Harmshield then run for the zone. If you have faith in your companions, you can use it to give you a few more seconds of life while they kill the monster attacking you, but this is a risky gambit as once the spell wears off you cannot cast it again for a couple minutes. Also beware that while this spell is active you cannot cast any other spell. It cost 85 mana to cast, so make sure you save enough mana. This spell uses the Abjuration skill to cast.

    Identify: Never used.

    Siphon Life: Our next life draining spell. Cost 72 mana to cast and initially drains 65 or so health, but steadily improves up to about 75 health per casting. An excellent spell.

    Word of Shadow: Our first damaging Area of Effect spell. It is centered on the caster, so you have to throw yourself into a group of monsters to get maximum effect (which can be a BAAAD thing). However in certain circumstances this is an excellent spell to use. Probably want to make sure you have Shieldskin or Leatherskin active before you cast it however, as you will certainly attract the attention of at least some of the monster. It does 58pts of damage to any monsters within a small area of effect around your body. Since it costs 85 mana to cast, its not really mana efficient unless you can get 2 or more monsters at once. I usually keep this spell active in dungeons, but not so much outdoors, as people tend to spread the monsters out more. Uses the Evocation skill.

    Gameplay at this point hasn't changed much from 12th through 23rd level. Whether soloing or in group combat your most effective spells are the DoTs with your pet as support. However, now we have reached 24th level and things change rather dramatically. I would argue that 24th can be considered the "peak" of the Necromancer's power. It is at this level that we finally get a Direct Damage spell, Shock of Poison. When I say "peak" I mean that at no other time is your relative power in relationship to the monsters so high. It is a fun time, and the change in gameplay comes along just as you may have been getting a little bored by the routine of things. So without further ado, here are the

    7th Rank Spells

    Shock of Poison: Celebrate Necromancer, as you know have your first (and ONLY until 44th level) DD spell, and a good one at that. Costing only 100 mana to cast, it does an impressive 150hp of damage improves as you rise in levels till it maxxes at 160). As an added bonus it uses the Conjuration skill (and not the more difficult to keep maxxed Evocation skill). Its mana-to-damage ratio is so good that when grouped you will find little reason to use your DoT spells any more (although they are still very necessary for solo play). This should be the first spell you obtain of the 7th rank, as it completely changes how you play. Until you hit 29th level and gain much better DoT spells, gameplay will be transformed into that of a Magician style of play (pet with burn spells). Even though the Necromancer powers are primarily DoT based, the added flexibility of a good DD spell is definitely a welcome addition.

    Breath of the Dead: Underwater breathing spell. Cost 45 mana and 2 fish scales. Uses Alteration skill. No need to buy until you plan on doing so serious underwater adventuring.

    Screaming Terror: This is our replacement spell for the removed Charm spell. Its effect is to both Fear and Memory Wipe a mob. It is easily resistible, but when it works it is very nice. The mob will run away, and if you don't do anything to gain its attention back it will just wander back to its "home" spot when the fear wears off, completely forgetting about you. It is ONLY useable in dungeons. Unfortunately it is possible to Fear the monster and have it resist the Memory Wipe, resulting in nasty trains when the monster returns. It cost 90 mana and uses the Alteration skill.

    Haunting Corpse: Next in line pet spell. This pet is much weaker to your level than any previous pet. You can summon a 21st through 23rd level pet with this spell. Cost a whopping 240 mana (I hate fizzling this one) and 2 bone chips to cast.

    Intensify Death: A research only spell that I haven't gotten yet.

    Leatherskin: An upgrade to your Shieldskin spell. I estimate that this spell absorbs about 150 to 200 hp worth of damage before being dispelled. Has the same problem as Shieldskin as any magic attack will instantly dispel it. Uses Bloodstone as a reagent (at 6 and a half gold each) but at this level you should have enough money to keep it active pretty much all the time. Makes a big difference in how well you survive a chaotic group combat situation. Cost 85 mana and a bloodstone to cast.

    Major Shielding: Next in line in our Shielding spells. Adds about 70hp plus 28 AC and 14 Magic resistance. Cost 80 mana and unless you have Abjuration maxxed you can look forward to fizzling a LOT.

    Resist Cold: Never cast

    With these new spells at your command, your power is now such that you can start hunting the good magical items in the game. With a good party you can successfully go for such highly desired items as the Flowing Black Robe and Journeyman Boots in Najena, or the Shimmering Orb and Gatorskin Armor in Guk, or Marnek's Dagger from Befallen. The addition of a good DD spell makes a necromancer more useful to a normal party during these levels, but at the same time our solo-ability is weaker than it has ever been. Our DoT spells are by now fairly old and under powered for the level of monsters we need to be fighting, and our pets are very weak tanks. Soloing your way to 29th level will be difficult and boring, because even though it IS possible, it is only possible by killing the SAME monsters over and over, as there is very little variety of things for you to solo kill. Grouping is definitely the way to go at this point, and before you know it you will hit 29th level and have access to your:

    8th Rank Spells

    Boil Blood: Finally an upgrade to our Heat Blood DOT, and a GOOD one at that. For about 150 mana this spell does over 400hp of damage (at 29th level, I've heard it improves as you go up) in a 60-70 second duration. That’s close to a 3-to-1 damage to mana ratio! Although not as important for groups, this spell dramatically enhances our ability to solo, especially when stacked with…

    Dooming Darkness: Another DOT upgrade, this time to our Darkness line of spells. At 120 mana it is twice as expensive as Engulfing, but it does 250hp of damage and slows the creature for about 70 seconds. Unfortunately I discovered that it shares the problem with the other darkness spells, in that it is resisted more than our other DOTS, so if you just want to slow something down, keep Clinging Darkness memorized, since you can cast it 6 times for every casting of Dooming. However if it sticks and you combine it with Boil Blood you can do almost 700 hp of damage to a monster for only 270 mana. Again, because these are DOTS its not that useful for group combat, but it’s a major improvement when you need to solo.

    Summon Dead: Our next Pet spell. Its hard to tell exactly what level of pet it is because after 24th level you no longer can distinguish the different power of blue conning creatures…they all appear as "formidable". It is significantly tougher than our previous pet, never fear. At a whopping 290 mana, it SHOULD be.

    Spirit Tap: Our next life draining spell, and to be honest its somewhat of a disappointment. It drains 127 hp from your victim but costs a whopping 144 mana to cast, so it is LESS mana efficient than our previous life drain spell, Siphon Life. Update: at 34th level it drains 140 hp. Still not 1-to-1 efficient yet.

    Dismiss Undead: Finally had an opportunity to use it, and the results are good, but not spectacular. Cost 90 mana to cast and does 154 pts of damage, although I'm sure that figure will increase as your skill goes up.

    Panic the Dead: Got it, but haven't had the opportunity to cast it yet. I'll update this spell later.

    Renew Bones: Don't have yet

    Vampiric Curse: This is a new DoT spell we got to replace Vampiric Wave. It costs 150 or so mana to cast and does about 250 damage over 60 seconds, while at the same time restoring 250hp to yourself. An excellent spell, especially when used in conjunction with Call of Bones.
     
     

    The most important spells out of the Eight rank are the upgraded DoTs and the new Vampiric Curse. Unfortunately after 29th you hit what is the first of the "Hell" levels, as you reach level 30. A Hell level is a milestone of upper level characters that has the characteristic of taking 2-3 times as much experience to get through as a normal level. This can be frustrating and boring if you are a soloist, but if you are having fun with a group going deep into dungeons then it will soon be over and you will reach 34th level and have access to your:

    9th Rank Spells

    Invoke Shadow: Conjuration. Mana cost 340. This is our 34th level pet spell. It summons a pet that is in the high 20's/low 30's in level
    range. Pets at this level can Dual Wield and Double Attack. A highly recommended practice at this stage is to start saving all your Fine Steel
    daggers and give them to your pet. The effects can be rather amazing as your pet attacks incredibly fast and hits very hard.

    Beguile Undead: Alteration. Mana cost 170. A high level Charm Undead spell. As with our other undead-specific spells, this one is incredibly useful under the right circumstances. In dungeons like Lower Guk, where you don't need to have 5 DoTs memorized, you should have a spare memorization slot for this spell. There have been more than one occasion in Lower Guk when we were swarmed by several undead froglocks and my pet died. Normally this would be a dire situation, but my ability to turn one of the undead froglocks to our side would turn the tide. I also use this spell in Oasis when hunting Spectres (which all the croc hunters REALLY hate). Since its difficult to pull just one spectre, it is frequently necessary to charm one and turn it against the others to whittle them down. You can spook a lot of lowbies by walking down the Oasis beach with your pet spectre in tow =)

    Venom of Snake: Conjuration. Mana cost 160. A new DoT spell. Or maybe its an upgrade to the 5th level spell Poison Bolt, I dunno, but either way it's a heck of a spell. Due to the Necromancer being pretty useless in groups due to our slow-acting DoTs, Verant made this spell VERY quick acting. You can almost think of it as a DD with a real slow recovery time. It hits for an initial 40pts of damage and does an additional 500 or so points over the next 30 seconds. This is a wonderful spell and allows us to be useful to groups again. We share this spell with the Shaman class

    Steelskin: Abjuration. Mana cost 149. Requires Jasper (1.2pp a pop or so). An upgrade to the Leatherskin line of damage absorption shields. This spell effectively gives us an additional 200 or so HP. It is also very quick casting so if I consider the odds of me being mauled in melee to be very good, I will keep this spell memorized so I can recast it during combat.

    Greater Shielding: Abjuration. Mana cost 120. The next in line in our Shielding series of spells. Adds to HP and AC. I forget the exact amounts, but its fairly standard. Nothing exciting. As usual its better to have the spell active than not.

    Root: Alteration. Mana cost 30. Here we are at level 34 and we finally get a Root spell! I fully expected this spell to be useless by the time we got it. I have several wizard friends who say that root sucks after 20th level and they upgrade to higher versions of the spell. However I am happy to report that the Necromancer root works just fine on all levels of mobs (providing of course they are lower level than you). For those of you that don't know, Root freezes a mob in place for 1-30 seconds. I don't know if our Root is a beefed up version of the wizard spell or what, but I have been pleased by the effects of the spell. Sure it gets resisted and sure sometimes it wears off very fast, but as far as I know all root type spells suffer from these problems. Of course as necro's we don't have the firepower to pull off a Root-and-Burn routine, but it is extremely useful in dungeons when a mob is beating on you. Very fast casting and almost never interrupted. This is a must have spell for dungeons and a welcome addition to our arsenal.

    Resist Disease: Abjuration. Mana Cost 50. Rejoice!! We get the most awesome spell in the whole game!! A spell that raises our Disease Resistance!! Whoopeeee! Sorry I can't tell you what the exact bonus to your resistance is, because I've never cast it.

    Call of Bones: Alteration. Mana cost 5. This is an upgrade to our Allure of Death spell. It drains life and replenishes mana at a very rapid rate. The cool thing is it also alters your form into that of a dry-bone skeleton. You have to be very careful of your health bar while this spell is active. It can kill you very quickly if you forget about it. Using this spell in conjunction with Vampiric Wave and your life-drain spells allows you to play in a more Wizardly fashion if you so choose, although it is still less mana efficient that DoTs, it can be much more practical in dungeons. Again you have to be careful. Although I always had a blast using this and allowing myself to drain down to 2 bubbles of life or less, and then sucking the life out of monsters in the next combat, it can be annoying to the clerics in your party who feel it is their responsibility to keep people healed. Often it takes some explaining to Clerics and healers in general as to what strategy you are using. Plus its so cool being a skeleton =) It does backfire on occasion tho when you get jumped for some reason and your pet and other party members can't seem to get the mob off of you (like when you pull several by mistake). A low health necromancer doesn't last long in these situations (which is a good reason to keep Harmshield memorized). Now here's a tip as well: make friends with Shamans and Druids. At some point these classes get a little spell called Chloroplast that is an awesome Regeneration spell. If they buff you with this spell and you use Call of Bones you basically are a Burn class with almost unlimited mana. It is an awesome thing to behold.

    Surge of Enfeeblement: Alteration. Mana Cost 100. This is an area of effect spell based around yourself. Its effect is to drain everything within a short radius of yourself of 20 strength points. I tested this spell several times in Solusek on Kobolds. It does make a difference in how often they hit for max damage. You will see them hitting for minor damage much more frequently. Unfortunately this spell does not stack with Siphon Strength and it does NOT transfer any strength to you. However I am sure it would stack with Heart Flutter (which drains 20 strength also). So theoretically the spell is not useless and does have an impact. However in order to make it worth the mana cost you really need to get 2 or 3 mobs with it, because a single mob will usually drop so fast that you are better off casting DD or DoT. Even with getting multiple mobs per casting there are much better ways to use your mana.

    Invoke Fear: Alteration. Mana cost 120 This spell is an upgrade to our Fear spell. In those situations where Fearing a mob is a good idea, Invoke Fear really does a good job. It lasts considerably longer than Fear and seems to be resisted less often. Otherwise it is exactly the same.

    That covers the range of 34th level spells. By far the best spell of the bunch is Venom of Snake, however Root and Call of Bones (and of course your pet spell) are welcome additions. Of course now you are faced with yet another Hell level at 35th, and it is even tougher than 30th was. It will take a long time to get through that level, but once you do the remaining levels come fairly quickly. Before you know it you will be 39th level and have access to your:

    10th Rank Spells

    Augment Death: Alteration. Mana cost 100. An upgrade to our Intensify Death pet buffing spell. It increases the attack, damage, and regeneration capabilities of your pet. One good thing for those Necromancers (like me) that never researched Intensify Death, Augment Death can be purchased from vendors. Although it is hard to tell, the official word from Verant is that the spell lasts for 3 to 6 minutes. The spell takes a long time to memorize and cast.

    Expel Undead: Evocation. Mana cost130. An upgrade to our undead only DD spell series. It hits for 250+ damage, but unless you max out your Evocation skill its gonna fizzle a ton. Make sure you take it with you when you visit Lower Guk.

    Drain Spirit: Alteration. Mana cost 189. This is the spell I was most looking forward to at 39th level. Unlike its predecessor this spell starts with a mana:damage efficiency better than 1:1 and continues to improve. At 40th level it does 211 pts of lifetapping love, which is really a pretty respectable burn spell, even without considering it heals you as well. As with all spells in this line it takes a long time to cast and can be difficult to get off if a monster is beating on you.

    Scourge: Conjuration. Mana cost 170. Yet another powerful DoT to add to our line. This one is a bit more slow acting than most others and takes a full 2-3 minutes to reach its maximum damage. When I am soloing I like to cast this one right after my Darkness DoT to give it maximum time to work. It does a pretty typical 500-600 damage, plus it hits initially for 50pts. We share this spell with the Shaman class.

    Counteract Disease: Alteration. Mana cost 50. A more powerful cure disease spell.

    Malignant Dead: Conjuration. Mana cost 390. The 39th level pet spell. This spell is difficult to get unless you have some very high level necro friends helping you (like I did, thanks Azakar!), as it is research only. It summons a pet that is in the low to mid 30's in level. It is a good upgrade on the 34th level pet as it does more damage, takes more punishment, and from my experience it TAUNTS extremely well. Highly recommended for the sophisticated pet summoner
     
     

    Necromancer Notions

    The following comments & tips are simply my opinions and observations from my life as a Necromancer.

    The Road Wasn't That Tough: 08/20/99. I am now 41st level, and if you read the paragraph below you will see that I was fearing a terrible future without pets. Although that is a legitimate concern, the Necromancer class gets enough interesting spells that it doesn't hurt as bad as you would otherwise expect. Plus I did manage to get my 34th and 39th level pet spells, although several levels after I was actually able to use the spell. After 29th there is definitely a de-emphasis on your pet. I found that when in a group, the pet is a nice addition if you control him properly (don't let him get your party killed…hot key that "back off" command), but even if you don't have your most powerful pet spell your class can be a good contributor to a party. Plus if you are fighting undead, you are a great contributor with your Ward Undead line. If you don't get your 34th level pet spell right away, make good use of the Beguile Undead spell. All in all its going to boil down to how many friends you have made. This is a compelling reason to join a good guild if you have the opportunity. If you don't have lots of friends that want to group with you, then strangers will almost always prefer some class other than a necromancer to join their party.

    It’s A Tough Road: I am officially entering the Dark Levels for Necromancers. Ctuchik is now 31st level (6/21/99) and the future looks bleak. Levels 32-?? Are going to be very difficult. Our pet spells after level 29 are RESEARCH ONLY. Our group of 3 necromancers has been all over looking for the proper words for the 34th level Necromancer pets, and we have yet to find any. I think this is a pretty lousy thing to do to a class that is one of the two PRIMARY PET classes. Our power is our pet, and a Necromancer with a weak pet is a weak character.

    Unrest: Pretty cool place. Not so hot for Dark Elves, because we have no merchants on Feydark to sell stuff and no bank to access, but still the experience and loot is very good. You can go there at 12th level and fight easy stuff in the courtyard, and as you rise in levels you can pull from the mansion. All in all a great place for necromancers and clerics.

    Befallen: A totally lame dungeon. I hate this place. If you go there at like 10th level to fight the easy stuff on the upper level you will die repeatedly from the random trains that spawn. If you go there with a 20+ level group to get the various magic items from the third level it will be a totally boring camping session in which you sit and wait for 30 minutes for everything to spawn. Yuck. If you want the Bonebladed Claymore, or the Dagger of Marnek tho, this is the place to get it.

    Cazic-Thule: The first 10 times I went here I thought it was totally lame. We sat in the courtyard and pulled lizards out of the maze. It was slow and boring. Then we moved back to the temple area however, and things definitely picked up. The danger went up dramatically, but there was less competition and much tougher mobs to fight, resulting in better experience and loot. The temple itself is ver spooky if you go inside and swim down the water column. Haven't managed to find Cazic-Thule himself and I'm not sure I want to =). If your lucky you can pick up Lizardscale Armor and Rubicite Plate in here.

    Upper Guk: Wow, this place is HUGE. Dangerous too, so don't plan on going deep unless you got a full party. Even at 29th level the Gaz Frogloks still Aggro on me, and the Tuks still Aggro on my pet, so soloing this dungeon is IMPOSSIBLE. You will be slaughtered by the wave of greenies. However, a good party can do well and get experience all the way up to about 30th level, although there are definitely better places if you are that high. Its not a cake walk either. Our party of four 28th level characters got wiped out when we pulled a Shin Lord and his henchmen. However this IS the place to be if you want one of those nifty Shimmering Orbs.

    Najena: Najena is a pretty difficult dungeon. There are several nice magic items that can be found by killing the three primary NPC's in here, Rathyl, Drelzna, and Najena. The secret to this dungeon is figuring out the spawn of these three NPC's. Our party has picked up Several Journeyman Boots, Flowing Black Robes, and I even got a Bloodclaw Dagger, a very sweet blade that does DAM 6 Delay 23 and Bloodclaw effect that does an extra 20pts of damage at random intervals. If it wasn't for these awesome items I don't think there would be much use to this dungeon at all.

    Dueling Tactics: While waiting for Najena to spawn recently, I tried dueling several of my party members out of boredom. Here are some things I learned: Pets: pets do not help you duel on non-pvp servers, I have no idea why. It is a severe handicap to the Necromancer, as so much of our power is pet-centric. Dueling a Wizard (both of us 27th level): Unless you get creative, you cannot win this battle. Our lifetap spells do not drain enough to offset the enormous amount of damage the wizard dishes out. It allows you to withstand three castings instead of just two, wonderful! However, it IS possible to win this fight. The key spells here are Fear or Charm. Fear in my opinion works best. Cast it first and then as your opponent runs around helplessly, blast him to pieces with Shock of Poison. If you are in a zone with tough NPC monsters, Charm would work as well. Charm your enemy and send him off to kill a sand giant hehe. I also dueled a troll Shadowknight and a troll Shaman. I didn't have any trouble with either, but both were three or four levels below me so the results are pretty meaningless. Cast Engulfing Darkness, Heat Blood, and Heart Flutter, then Fear and watch them run around till they die. The key here is a high DEX and DODGE skill that makes it difficult for them to interrupt your casting.

    Dexterity Rules: One of the huge advantages the Gnome and Dark Elf have over the other Necro-eligible races is a higher starting dexterity. When I created my character I did NOT dump all of my bonus points into INT. I rounded INT off at 120 and then pumped my DEX and AGI up to 95. I IMMEDIATELY noticed that my Necromancer recovered from interruptions and continued casting a LOT more than my Wood Elf Druid EVER did. As I rose in levels I placed a priority on DEX enhancing magic items, and now my DEX is 108 and the difference is DRAMATIC. My character resumes casting from interruptions MORE OFTEN THAN NOT. It is awesome

    Solo Tactics: Verant has recently made some changes to our DOT spells that make them more resistible. Although this is very annoying and will cost many a necromancer their life, I do not agree with many alarmists that scream that they have been ruined. Necro's are still one of the few classes that can solo yellow and weak red mobs past 20th level. We have the tools, it is up to the caster to use them wisely. The tactics I use are not secret, they just call for using your spells wisely and keeping your pet alive. How do I do it? Listen well, young Necro, and learn. Timing is the key to successful soloing of a yellow mob that is 20th or higher in level. A wise necromancer understands that if he does not rapidly complete these steps, or if the monster successfully resists more than once or twice, then it is time to leave the area while the pet can still delay the beast. First I get at the far range of spell casting distance from the monster if possible, then I tell my faithful pet to attack. After heaving a big sigh of resignation your pet will charge forward to engage. As soon as my pet runs towards the target I start casting Heart Flutter. The pet and the DOT should hit at the same time. Then I immediately begin casting Heat Blood. When HB hits you will see the monster trying to come towards you, but your pet should be successfully taunting it and delaying it. Once HB hits I fire off Engulfing Darkness. I like Engulf to be the LAST of the three DOTS because once the third spell hits the monster will usually be pissed enough to ignore the pet totally and come after you, and I want him slowed for as long as possible, hence Engulf is last. At this point your pet is probably in pretty bad shape and won't live much longer if you don't do something. If the monster is coming straight for you, you need to turn and run in the opposite direction for a few seconds to put enough space in between it and you in order to fire off another spell. If it is still engaging your pet, good, but hurry with the next spell or the pet is going to die. This next spell must be Fear. Fear serves three purposes. First it will save your pet from dying. Second it provides 5 to 15 seconds of additional time for your DoTs to work without retaliation from the monster. Third it gives your pet free hits on the monsters back as it walks (remember it has Engulf on it) away. Now after the fear takes effect I will keep an eye on the monster and often check the health of my pet. After a few seconds of Fear, I start re-applying DoTs, especially Engulf. You do NOT want the monster to be at full speed. Then I will re-apply Heat Blood. I usually do not cast Heart Flutter again, as it is easier to resist and doesn't do as much damage as Heat Blood. Now you should just need to keep your distance between yourself and the monster. If you have enough mana and you are still concerned, you might re-apply Fear. If you are 24th or higher of course you can be using Shock of Poison during this time to quickly take out the monster.

    Groups of Necromancers are extremely powerful. The best time I've had so far playing EQ is when I grouped with three other Necromancers and we went adventuring. The effective use of our pets and careful coordination of our DOTs (each caster is assigned one primary DOT to cast) made for a very powerful group. I shudder to think what a group of six high level Necromancers could do.

    Most Necromancers use the Lifetap line of spells too much like a burn spell. There is no point in wasting all of your mana casting lifetap to take a monster out quickly if your pet walks away from the combat without a scratch on him. Find a balance between how much damage you want your pet to take and how much mana you want to burn. Ideally you will meditate and your pet will heal and you will both get to max at the same time.

    If you like to engage in melee always cast Vampiric Embrace first. It only needs to fire once to pay for itself, and often you get two shots before it wears out. That’s a 2 to 1 damage to mana ratio.
