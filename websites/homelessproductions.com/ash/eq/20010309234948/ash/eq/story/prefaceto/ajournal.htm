Preface to a Journal

Today, for no apparent reason, I decided to begin a journal...a diary if you will. Perhaps it is for my own benefit, so I can reflect on where I have been, or what I have done...perhaps so I can become a better person, a better bard...Or perhaps it is in case I suffer the final death, so that a record of my achievements, and my losses shall be on paper, and the Choir, (whom I suppose will be written about in depth in the coming years), might write a song about me.

I am Ryndal, a bard from Freeport. I am slightly arrogant, or so I have been told, and I do-quite often-break of into rants, and tangents. Why I don't just sit and act like the others around me is obvious. This world is full of adventurers. To be set apart is to become an individual. And an individual is much more likely to become a hero, than your everyday, average, BORING, adventurer. Especially the ones whom I would consider cowards. You know the ones...they never really leave the confines of the cities, and are always looking for a safe place to have their souls bound, in case of death. How difficult is recovering your equipment, anyway? At least the gods have given you a second chance of life. And life is, after all, ruined by the living. Indeed, perhaps Undeath would be a better life than this...but I digress (see, I told you I would go off on tangents).

As I said before, I am Ryndal of Freeport. My father was a merchant, and spent most of his days in the Marketplace in North Freeport. He ran a small shop that sold supplies to adventurer's, and to other stores. He met my mother, who was a ranger, during one of her frequent visits to the fine city. My mother, a wood elf, was fairly tall for her race, and extremely beautiful.

Speaking of beauty, I seem to see it in everything, as most people should. But the beauty of a woman...The subtle curves under leather armor, and the way they smell, even after hacking the legs off a giant spider....Is it not perfection in the works? The gods must have spent an eternity arguing over the way a woman's legs might curve, and their breasts....not unlike the rolling hills near Qeynos! . . . . . . . . . . . . sigh.

My parents were married soon after my mother decided to retire from adventuring, and help my father run the store. He did a great deal of new business, as my mother drew the attention of all her adventuring friends. It was then that I came to be, and these were wondrous times. Such is the greatest beauty: A happy family.

After a few years, just a few, mind you, but I was old enough to know what happened, and I remember it as clear as yesterday, my father decided to expand the business into the Western Commons. He seemed to think that more supplies would be sold on a battlefield, than deep in the city. Perhaps he was right, and so, against the wishes of my mother, he proceeded to open the place.

On his first day at work, a messenger appeared at our little store in the city. "Victors! West Commons branch is open for business, mi lady." Said the messenger to my mother. "It is doing fairly well. Victor misses you, and the little one, and will return by suppertime". With that, the messenger turned and walked out the door.

My mother, always excited about such things, decided to make a large, banquet style feast for dinner. She began preparations immediately after the leaving of the messenger. A friend of hers, who worked for the city of Freeport, allowed us to rent a hall fairly cheaply. I remember the hastily cooked fish, boar, steaks, and other edible, but expensive, delights. The hall was decorated by city staff, and all was finished. She sent out a multitude of messengers to get word to all of our friends in the Freeport area, and placed signs all over town, inviting all to the Hall for the feast to celebrate the success of my father.

When dusk settled over Freeport, we began expecting my father to return. But he did not. The hall, full of eating adventurers and merchants, seemed empty without him. Hours went by, and, although the feast went on without him, I began to suspect that something was wrong. I remember feeling lost, as I wandered the hall, looking for him, or my mother. Both seemed to have disappeared.

Soon after this, a fat merchant halfling from Rivervale, (who made the most wonderful Iron rations around), came to me and said, "Little Ryndal, you will be spending some time with me the next few days! You can stay with me, and help me run my little outlet here!"

When I asked him where my mother was, he just smiled and said, "She had a few errands to run. She'll be back."

I did not believe him, but his tone was soothing, and I had been with him before. I decided not to worry, as perhaps, she really did have a few things to do. But I was still a bit disturbed.

The next few weeks, I spent with this halfling. We didn't do much, other than run his store, and he ignored all my inquiries about my parents. On a gloomy day soon after, my mother returned, and said, "Oh, little Ryndal. Poor, little Ryndal." Her eyes welled up with tears. "You father will not be coming home. My worst fears have come true, and on the first day of business!"

Further questioning revealed little that day, but I came to learn later, much later, that my father had, in fact, been slain by a Griffin. The nasty beast did slay him, and put his soul to the permanate slumber.

We moved from Freeport to Kelethin after that, and I was amazed at the tree city. She opened a bakery there, I suppose she didn't want to continue in the general store business, as it reminded her of my father too much. The days passed there in safety, and I soon got over my loss.

But a man named Gregory Lutesong is who really helped. He was an adventurer, but was a bit different from all the others. He would come into my mothers bakery once a week, and I would always race to see him. He would come in spinning tales of adventures, and singing wondrous songs. Indeed, he was an inspiration to me....and to many others as well. Groups of people would flock to him, and ask about his latest tales of riches (not that he ever seemed to have any...indeed, mother was always giving him food).

His tales, true or not, left nothing to the imagination. Swords flying, and feet kicking, and this bard would sing these beautiful songs, and inspire his battlemates.

One time he came in, I followed him out. I wanted to see where this man went in our fine city. Sneaking several feet behind him, he went to a smallish shack across a long bridge. I read the sign above the door: The Bards Guild. Oh, the wonders that came out of that building! Poetry, song, and many woodelf's would dance the old dances to these songs! I began to get swept up in the old magic's of my (well, half of my) people. I was hooked.

And so it came to be, that I became a bard. I would spend hours with the guild, even though I was much too young to become a member. I met many nice adventurers during these times...and the man I followed to the guildhouse the first time began teaching me the mechanics of a mandolin! What a fine instrument the mandolin is. So many different sounds to be made into music. And music, my friends, is what drives the soul. The secret to battle is not courage, strength, valor...nay, ask any general...the secret to battle is morale. And what better way to raise others spirits than a beautiful song, and joy, and merriment!

This is why I chose the path that I am on, for I want to bring happiness and joy to others around me. I want to see the beauty in everything--from a springtime flower, to a troll. (Aye, the last one is a bit difficult). Fairness to all, is my motto, and has paid off greatly, as I have made some friends, even with dark elves. (Although I can not speak of them to anyone).

I suppose I began this journal more for a point of reference than any other reason. As I graduated the basics course at the guild, my mother passed away. Indeed, she was getting old, but I think more loneliness did the job than age. She gave me a wondrous life, without my father, but never seemed to be the same again. Rest in Peace, mother, I will spread the word of your beauty to all, and, one day, publish a report of your wondrous deeds while you were adventuring.

I closed the bakery last week, and moved to Freeport, so I could be closer to my origins. Kelethin is a beautiful place, but one which holds too many painful memories for me right now. Perhaps in time, I can return, and my jaded eyes can once again become soft with the greenery there. Perhaps.

As for now, I have begun a repor with the guildhouse in Freeport, and I will begin to forge my way in this life very soon. Sooner, in fact, than I would have hoped.
