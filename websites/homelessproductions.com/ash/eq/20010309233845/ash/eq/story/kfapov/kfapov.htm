Kiedatar: From Another Point Of View

"At least its not raining," Kiedatar muttered as he strode down a well worn trail in the Karanas, heading back to Qeynos, his home in exile.

As he came over a small rise in the road, he encountered a slightly built man in a robe. Judging by the man's size and the lack of any visible scars, Kiedatar quickly surmised that this man must be a magic user like his friend Lenhaj.

Not entirely comfortable with magic, Kiedatar decided to press on without speaking. After all, the man might be in league with the bandits that infested the area. Still feeling the effects from being poisoned by a giant spider earlier that day, Kiedatar had no desire to get involved in another fight, especially one that might involve magic.

As he passed the man, he spoke up, "Excuse me, sir, but I seem to have gotten turned around. You see, I saw a giant in the distance, panicked, and got lost until I found this road. Unfortunately, I do not know if this is the road to Qeynos, or which way Qeynos would be."

The man stood up, brushing the dirt and dust from his robe. He seemed harmless enough, Kiedatar supposed. "I'm headed in that direction myself," he said. "Feel free to tag along. If you can't keep up," Kiedatar shrugged as if to imply that he wouldn't wait if the man could not keep up.

"Thank you, good sir, you are a life saver. Karana was most kind in sending you to my rescue."

Kiedatar just shook his head and began a brisk jog down the road toward Qeynos. To his surprise, the stranger managed to keep the pace and began talking with him. Or rather, talking to him, for Kiedatar did not encourage the conversation. At least the man seemed well learned and intelligent and his talk, while idle, was not the inane prattle of many of his countrymen.

About thirty minutes later, the low throaty cough of a lion startled the two as they walked along. Kiedatar froze and held up a hand to silence his companion. He looked around, but could see no trace of the lion. Wait a minute! There in the bushes. Two green eyes set upon a tawny face looked back at him.

By the size of the creature's mane, Kiedatar knew this lion was older than many of the lions that he had seen before. This was not a good thing, for surely with age, the lion had only become more and more dangerous.

"Barbarian," his companion hissed. For a moment, Kiedatar was irritated. These soft southerners did not seem to realize that they were far more barbaric than their northern neighbors. They had no respect for nature, the Great Spirit, or even themselves for that matter.

"What?"

"We've got a problem." The man jerked a thumb behind them and Kiedatar glanced in that direction. The man was right, they had a problem. Three of them as a matter of fact. The lion in the bushes, and the two lionnesses that were sitting calmly on the other side of the road, not much more than 30 feet away.

They were in trouble.

Kiedatar was still feeling nauseous from his battle with the spider earlier and his companion did not look capable of taking out three lions even with magic. For a brief moment, Kiedatar considered making a run for it and hoping that the lions would settle for a meal of wizard. Even as he thought the thought, he recognized that the Great Spirit was testing his moral resolve.

As he glanced around looking for a defensible position, he saw a tall tree. If they could get up there, hopefully, they could the lions off until they grew tired or until help came along. He forced a smile so as not to frighten his companion unduly, "When I say go, we are going to make a run for that tree. You'll have to drop your pack. With luck, they'll stop to eat whatever food we're carrying and leave us alone. Besides, it'll make climbing easier without the packs. You understand? Good." Kiedatar slowly slipped his arms out of his backpack and noticed that his companion was doing the same.

"GO!!!" He shouted, dropping the pack, and began to sprint toward the tree.

Behind him, one of the cats let out a bone chilling screech and he knew instantly that the cats had not gone after the packs. Kiedatar reached the base of the tree and jumped, grabbing hold of one of the lower branches. Using his strong arms, he quickly pulled himself up. Behind him, his companion was struggling to get up the first branch. Bracing himself, Kiedatar reached down, grabbed the man's wrist and yanked him up just as the cats reached the bottom of the tree.

One of the lionnesses tried to jump up into the tree as well, but was met with heel of Kiedatar's boot. The cat dropped back to the ground, snarling. The two travelers worked their way up a few branches. The cats began to circle the tree, but none of them made the attempt to climb the it again. Kiedatar could not resist it. "Hah! You silly cats, you were not meant to lunch on Kiedatar today. The Great Spirit protects me and some day I will eat your hearts and take your courage."

CRACK! The branch under Kiedatar's feet snapped and the big man plummeted to the ground, hitting several other branches on the way down, knocking the breath out of him. He hit the ground hard and it seemed as if the very stars were dancing around his head.

He was dead. He knew it. The Great Spirit was punishing him for his pride and arrogance. The cats would be upon him at any moment, tearing the flesh from his bones. He would never see his beloved north again. The People never give up. Even winded he tried to regain his feet to climb back up the tree. One of the lionnesses clawed at him, tearing deeply into his thigh. The lion looked about to pounce.

KABOOM!!! The world exploded, or so it seemed to Kiedatar. A ball of fire exploded right in front of him, throwing him against the tree and knocking his newly regained breath right out of him. He felt his eyebrows singe, and he cringed when he thought of what that blast might have done to his wolf skin helm. He heard the lions shriek in pain.

"Don't just lie there, you oversized barbarian. I merely scared them with that blast, and I lack the mental energy to cast another spell. Get up here."

Kiedatar grunted, staggered to his feet, and pulled himself slowly into the tree. At last he reached the same height as his companion. The man regarded him with a bemused smile. Kiedatar wanted to punch him.

"Thanks," me muttered irritably.

"It was my pleasure," the man said, nodding his head. "Try to pick a sturdier branch this time."

"I'll keep it in mind," Kiedatar grumbled.

The man stuck out his hand, "My name is Gilgadet. I'm a wizard."

Kiedatar reached out and took the hand, engaging in the strange southern ritual of handshaking.

"I am Kiedatar of the People. I'm tired, hungry, and slightly crisped."

The man smiled and shrugged. "At least it's not raining."

"Aye, at least it's not raining."

It started to rain.
