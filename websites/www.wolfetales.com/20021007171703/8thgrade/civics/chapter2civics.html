Common Heritage

          The colonists shared a common heritage.  History told them about the governments in Greece and Rome.   The governing body in Britain was devised in part based on a combination of  that found in early Greece and Rome

          Greece – Athens had a direct democracy – meaning all the citizens were empowered to decide and vote on laws.

          Rome – Elected representatives

 

The colonists were

                    Subject to rule by Britain

                    Believed in Self-government

                    Christian

 

Thomas Hobbes

Society chose leaders.  Once chosen. Society had no further voice.  The leaders made and enforced the laws.  Society gave up its power to choose.

 

John Locke

Locke took Hobbes’ theory a step further.  Society would vote to choose representatives to make and enforce laws.  In addition, society retained the right to continue to elect its officials.

 

Charles-Louis Montesquieu

Montesquieu agreed with Locke’s theory but added  three functions for government.  He did so because he believed that no single branch of government should have power that would exclude that of the others.  No group should be too strong.

           Montesquieu’s three functions of government were:

                    Make Laws

                    Enforce Laws

                    Interpret Laws

 

The separation into three functions was to assures a system of “Checks and Balances”.

 

Colonial Governments

 

          Royal Colonies

                    Ruled directly by the British government

                    The governor and his council gained their positions by royal appointment.

                    Upper House – British appointment

                    Lower House (assembly) Elected by the colonists.

                  

Veto –The power to refuse a proposal  ( refuse to approve)  Governor could Veto.

 

          Proprietary Colonies 

          Individuals or groups were granted land by the crown. 

They were still subject to rule by Britain. 

But---Proprietors were free to rule as they wanted.

 

Proprietors appointed the upper house.

People elected the lower house.

                  

          Charter Colonies

                    Established by groups of settlers

                    Rights and privileges were given to them by the Crown.  (CHARTER)

                    Colonists elected their own governors and members of both houses.

                    Governor could not veto acts of the legislature.   

 

The Colonists Learned

          The colonists began to form ideas about the kind of governing body they wanted.  They learned based on what they had experienced.  We often learn what we do want by experiencing what we don’t want.  This was true of the colonists as well.

          The Colonists learned that:

                  

The power of the executive branch should be limited

 

A way to check the power of the governor should be in place

          Controlling taxes and funds they believed would be good tools.

 

Legislative branch could be used as a check on the executive.

 

Judicial system – should on certain “NATURAL RIGHTS”

Natural Rights – believed to be the right of all individuals

a.      Right to trial

b.     Right to post bail

c.     Right to face one’s accuser

 

 

Road to Independence

 

          Economics

                    Mercantilism

Nations in Europe, including Great Britain measured their strength (power) by the amount of wealth that its government could accumulate.

                                       Way to gain wealth:  Sell more than one buys

                  

Britain counted on the colonies for profit and believed they existed for the well-being of Britain’s economy.

                             Colonies supplied:

                                       Raw materials for factories  (Cotton)

                                       Colonies bought goods from Britain.

                  

          Britain was involved in the French and Indian War and so for a while it was easy for the colonies to ignore some of the orders Britain tried to enforce.  The was ended in 1763.  Britain was looking for a way to help restore some of its lost wealth, which was due to the war.

                    Britain turns up the heat on the colonies

                             Meaning…

a.      Enforce existing laws

b.     Insist the colonies trade only with Britain

c.     Pay a larger share of the expense for the British soldiers that were there to protect the colonists.

King George III

          Levied new taxes

         

Colonists Cry “foul”

The colonists believed the taxes were unfair because they had no representation in Parliament.  They had no say in whether or not the tax should be enacted.

 

The cry that went up was:

“No Taxation Without Representation”

 

 

 

 

The Colonists Unite

 

Stamp Act of 17 65

 

Special stamps for

Newspapers

Legal Documents

Business Agreements

 

 

Colonists were bitterly opposed.

 

The Stamp Act Congress  (meeting)

 

Delegates from nine colonies went to New York City.

The Stamp Act Congress approved the (Declaration of Rights and Grievances

It said:

 

The Colonists had the same rights as British citizens

Colonial Assemblies had the sole right to tax the colonies – not Britain

Called for a repeal of the Stamp Act

 

Repeal – Do away with (withdraw)

 

To add support to the Declaration of Rights and Grievances, the colonists boycotted (refused to buy) British goods.

 

The Stamp Act was eventually repealed, but the British government kept looking for ways to raise money in the colonies.

 

As a result the colonists continued to resist.

 

Boston Tea Party

Another protest against British domination of trade.

Remember that we said the British did not want the colonies to trade with other countries.  IN protest the colonists dressed up as Indians and dumped an  entire cargo of tea into Boston Harbor.

 

Parliament passed more laws designed to punish the colonists.  As a result the colonial resistance grew.

 

Colonists called the laws the Intolerable Acts.  Their determination to defend their natural rights increased.

 

Remember----a push is always returned with force…And the colonists were being pushed.

 

1774 – First Continental Congress

          The legislature of Virginia and Massachusetts called a meeting to talk about coming up with a response to the Intolerable Acts

 

All the colonies sent representatives accept for Georgia.  The met in Philadelphia.

          Adopeted a Declaration of Resolves which they sent to King George.  It demanded Repeal of the Intolerable Acts

          The Declaration affirmed the colonists rights to

                    Life

                    Liberty

                    Property

 

The Continental Congress also asked the colonists to continue to boycott British goods.

Eventually Georgia (named for King George) agreed as well.

 

 

A New Nation is Born

 

Declaration of Resolves did not improve the situation.  The noble ideas looked good on paper, but the situation just got worse and in April 1775

 

          SHOTS RANG OUT

                    Lexington and Concord in Massachusetts

 

                             3 WEEKS LATER

 

Second Continental Congress met

          This time there were delegates from all thirteen colonies.

 

          Actions of the Second Continental Congress

                    Organized an army

                    Appointed George Washington commander in chief

                    Arranged to issue currency

                             Borrow money

                             Buy  supplies

                    Negotiate with foreign countries

 

Because of all their actions, the Second Continental Congress was America’s first national government.  It remained in control for six years.

 

Declaration of Independence

          Congress most dramatic act occurred July 4 1776 when it voted to ratify the Declaration of Independence.

 

          (RATIFY – approve or confirm)

Thomas Jefferson wrote the Declaration of Independence

          It also set down many of the ideas that Americans valued

                             Equal  - under God

                             Rights – Natural rights (unalienable)

                                       Life

                                       Liberty

                                       Pursuit of happiness

 

It stated further that the purpose of government was to secure those rights.

Governments power comes from the governed.

 

When a form of government becomes destructive and no longer honors those rights

          The people then have the right to alter or abolish it.
