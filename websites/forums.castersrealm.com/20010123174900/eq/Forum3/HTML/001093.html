(Warning, this post became much longer than I expected it to be, and hence is not for those lacking patience. Thank you )

I would tend to agree with several posts above. The concept that a hyrbid "spends more time" learning both the skills of a warrior and the spells of a mage (and hence the extra needed experience) only applies if the hybrid is essentially as skilled as a warrior as well as having decent magic. This, however, is not the case.

Imagine a Paladin and a Warrior training together in an roleplaying scenario. Both get up at the crack of dawn and begin practicing their weapon skills. They spar with one another using various types of weaponry. However, as daylight begins to dwlindle, the Paladin apologizes to his Warrior friend and retreats to inside the nearest temple and begins his study of basic clerical magic for the day. Meanwhile, the Warrior has lit a bonfire and is practicing and mastering that parry that was so effective today, or perhaps experimenting with new maneuvers using a weapon in each hand. 5 hours later, they both decide to go bed (individually, not with each other, this isn't that kind of story ). The next day, they meet again early in the morning to spar as they have always done. This time, the Warrior stuns the Paladin with a well timed shield bash and drops said shield and whips out a mace. In attempt to deflect the Warriors well timed, incoming assault, which the Paladin could never amount an effective defense against, he mutters a few words and gestures at the Warrior, who immediately scowls and staggers about, blinded by an intense light.

This story could continue endlessly, but the point is that the Paladin sacrifices weaponry and combat skills in order to gain another asset, that of basic clerical magic. This is balanced, and it would make sense, translating these characters into a more technical RPG setting, to have these characters "level" at approximately the same time, although, granted, with different levels of skill.

In AD&D, a Paladin of equal experience points (not levels) would be essentially equal to a Fighter, but he would gain levels (and hence bonus abilities) slower, because of this inherent advantage. Therefore, in a battle between a lvl 8 Paladin and a lvl 8 Warrior, the Paladin SHOULD be able to beat the Warrior on a purely statistical basis, given that the Paladin spent more time to get to that level.

Neither of this is the case in Norrath.

Unfortuantely, hybrids get the worst of both worlds, they are essentially equally skilled statistically to equally leveled Warriors, but nevertheless have an experience penalty so it takes more time to get there. It doesn't make sense, but it's the way Verant has decided to do things, and, as Abashi has stated, it isn't going to change.

So, why do I still play a Dark Elf Shadowknight as my primary character? <shrugs> I love the options available to him, despite the penalties. I wouldn't want to be a Warrior, nor would I want to be a Necro, but being a Shadow Knight appeals to me given the balance between the two. It is my opinion that it is these "options" that we are paying the exp penalty for. As an SK, I must earn more exp for the ability to fear kite (albeit rather poorly) with my pet and DOTs and conceivably kill something without liting my blade. Likewise, I could also just run up and start hacking at the thing, and, quite possibly, emerge victorious. Granted, this is all circumstantially based, but those options remain.

Do I agree with Verant's decision? No. Would I have made that decision if I was designing the game? No. Do I still love my character and what he's capable of and will gladly continue to play despite whatever obstacles are placed before me? Yes, I do.

Everquest isn't perfect in many respects. It's basically just a MUD with a GUI (sounds silly, I know, but for those who know the terms, it makes sense ), their attempts at "balance" and consistancy with "The Vision" seem lacking at best. And much of the intricate plot of Norrath seems haphazardly distributed which doesn't really add to the gaming atmosphere. However, it does have an excellent system of risk/reward, and because of that, true exultation, frustration, and fear can be felt within aspects of the game. Remember (or imagine) getting your first amazing piece of equipment, or that spell to end all spells, finally getting your armor color coordinated, gazing awestruck at the massive form of Lady Vox, starting/joining a guild, walking into Lavastorm mountains for the first time and watching drakes fly past the glowing pools of lava, almost dying in a battle with a powerful creature or actually losing when the battle was really close and vowing to return when you had improved your skills.

All i'm saying is, Everquest is what you make of it. Allow yourself to be absorbed into the environment, and allow yourself to relax and have fun and the game will prove to be infinitely more enjoyable. Granted, I understand that some people just can't allow themselves to do that if they feel that their class has been wronged in some fashion, and I pity them and their plight. I will still post on boards when I think improvements can be made, but most of all I think we should enjoy the game for what it is, not what we think it should be. If you really dislike the way EQ is set up and think you could do a much better job, do what I did and become a game designer all you need is the right training and, most importantly, a love for games.

My apologies to those eager to get back to b!tching about Abashi, my diatribe is over, I humbly bid you all adieu.
