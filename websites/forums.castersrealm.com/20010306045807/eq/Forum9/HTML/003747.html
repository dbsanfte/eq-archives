Igbert's Guide to Charm Solo'ing
49th Chanter
Rodcet Nife


I successfully solo'ed my enchanter from level 40 to level 49 in a matter
of 4 weeks.
This was basically one weekend day, and 3 or 4 evenings for a few hours per
week. Not a ton of time really. Some people say enchanters are horrible at
solo'ing (I played a rogue to 50, trust me, enchanters are wonderful
soloers). I would even go so far as to say enchanters MAY be the best
solo'ing class around. My backing is that solo'ing is all about damage
output and (avoiding) damage input. For casters damage output is about mana to damage
efficiency. Damage input is almost irrelevant because a casters job (even
in soloing, is to not get hit). Given the right circumstances (which I'll
discuss), the charm spell is the most mana to damage efficient spell in the
game. A charmed 45th level mob puts a mage pet to shame. Especially considering
such a mob can be weilded by (theoretically) an enchanter of 39th level.

I've also read the laments of enchanters saying that charm is a death trap.
As I said, I leveled 40 through 49 with almost no grouping and just charm
solo'ing. I died 3 times in that entire time. Once because of linkdeath
(which is incredibly dangerous in a charmed scenario) and the other two
times because my pet hit me and I had a bad string of 3 successive stuns.
Three rounds of combat from my pet was enough to drop my rune and kill
me.
There are two thoughts associated with that. One is, don't let your pet
hit you for 3 rounds . And two, despite I'm a pretty wimpy little
enchanter, 3 rounds of combat that can kill me must be doing some very serious
damage.

First I'll list the absolute required ingredients for successful charm
solo'ing for me. I'm sure other recipes work, but this is my success list.
I'll then describe some very specific scenarios.

1. SoW. I hate this requirement and that's why I list it first. Mostly
because enchanters don't have it naturally, but despite my disdain, I
cannot see my way around it. Jboots work fine. Enchanters have little
trouble trading clarity to a solo'ing druid for sow and potions aren't
that
expensive. Sad but true - you must be able to outrun your pet.

2. A wide open zone. Along with SoW, you need to be able to run. And
Outrun. You "can" rely on your color-stun spells and charm indoors (getting that
wonderful xp bonus) but that really is just too dangerous. You are
accepting the fact you'll get hit once in awhile. I've gone killing 30
mobs without getting hit once (even my rune). Outdoor zones are solo'ers dreams.
The zone should also have some dead area where chance of getting jumped is
minimal. Trakanon's teeth is outdoor, but the whole zone is ambush
nightmare.

3. A high-level mob undercon near many normal conning blues. At level 43
I solo'ed BOTH the kromrif guards in eastern wastes that stand in front of
Kael. My 55th wizard friend lamented he barely had enough mana to do one. I
could do both simultaneously. It was a dance but I could do it. They conned
white or yellow at that time. I did it, but it was inefficient. Efficiency
is everything, not dramatics. When those two guards fought each other,
they pretty much beat the snot out of each other. Huge hitpoints and huge damage
output. Once my pet won, I'd blast him to kingdom come. This cost me tons
of mana for 2 kills. I learned quickly that by charming one guard, he could
kill 10-15 dire wolves, walrus, snow griffons, giant scouts, etc before
dying. While he was killing, I was medding. I rarely went below 60 mana
until the time he was almost dead and had to zap my former pet dead. Trust
me, this is the key to efficiency - your pet should be very tough and
should kill many, many low blues. As solice to my wizard friend, I could not (and
still cannot) solo one of those guards.. only two .

4. The mobs you will kill (and charm) are not casters. Fighting casters
is always a pain. They simply put out too much damage too fast and ignore
your runes. Chanters are all about controlling melee fights. Stick to it.


That's really it. That's what you need to get going. Your spells are all
you need after this.
Firstly, I'll describe levels 43-49. I'll describe earlier levels
subsequently, because charming lower lev mobs is easier. It is easier because of one
very important factor. The casting time of Beguile (which works on up to
39th level mobs) is 3.5. The casting time of Cajoling whispers (which works
on up to 45th level mobs) is 4.5. That casting time is incredibly
important.

The absolute, number one, most IMPORTANT rule of charming is:
(pardon the caps, but this really is important)

DON'T EVER BE NEAR YOUR PET. EVER.

(I don't care if he cons amiable, its a lie, he really wants to rip your stinky little
gnomish guts out, eat you, and poop you out tomorrow)

The two times I died occurred when my pet unfortunately broke charm in
the 5 seconds per hour that circumstances had me standing by my pet.

Simple rule: If your pet is fighting - you are standing OUT of charm/root
spell range medding. You have your pet targeted, your mouse cursor on your
root spell, and your fingers on the CTRL-S keys ready to stand up.

If your pet is NOT fighting - you are running with your pet chasing (slowly
losing ground) to your next target.

The enchanters that sit medding with a 45th level Frost Giant pet standing
near them are the ones that say charm never lasts and is suicide.

By the way ? I did this successful charming episode with 170 INT and 115
CHA (buffed). Charms lasted anywhere from 6 seconds to 5-6 mins
(estimated). Any charm over 30 seconds is useful. Charm Breaking is not
really a problem. If you aren't ready for it, you die. If you are, its
just the cost of doing business.

Ok.. Spell line up from 43-49.

1. Tash
2. Direct Damage
3. Root ? (not immoblize, not enstill.. your 8th level Root ? there's a
reason, read on)
4. Fastest Haste
5. Tepid Deeds (not shiftless deeds, tepid)
6. Rune III
7. Cajoling Whispers
8. Memblur


Notice I use root and tepid deeds instead of better spells. Root casts
in 2.5, immobilize (39th) in 1.8. For this reason, you may choose immobilize,
BUT you will rarely, if ever cast direct damage on a rooted mob. You don't
care if root breaks. Generally, you WANT roots to break quickly. If there
was a 20second sure-hold root, it would be perfect. Also, you'll cast alot of
roots and the mana savings adds up.

Tepid slows 50% - shiftless 60%. You will hardly use this spell anyway. You
shouldn't fight too many mobs that pose a challenge to your pet. Tepid is alot
less mana than shiftless. Really not worth it here imho. Generally,
spells 5 and 8 (memblur) are optional. If you wish to put Gate there,
that's a good choice. Another type of root (enstill is longer than root)
for situational use is good too. Also mez if you aren't doing giants.

Buffs: The only buffs I keep up are Shielding, CHA buff, and Rune III (and
Clarity duh). Rune 4 is just too dam expensive (10 Rune 3's per Rune 4
in casting cost). If you are rich, go for 4. Also, keeping up Troll Illusion
isn't a bad idea if you get hit - the regen is nice. Of course, last but
not least - Sow (or jboots). Buy potions, give druids backrubs, whatever.
Its required.
Shade is ok, but I don't really get hit much. And when I do, Runes eat
it up. Resist magic could be very real, but fighting casters isn't a good
idea anyway.

Ok.. Here is my charming scenario levels 43-49.

Zone: Eastern Wastes
Important points: Kromrif guards outside Kael can easily be pulled solo.
Just north of Kael is a "dead" zone" there seem to be no wandering
monsters there. Just east of Kael there are tons of wandering blue mobs.
Kromrif guards conned white to me while 43, 44, 45. They seem to vary these
levels. Also, Cajoling Whispers (according to eq.castersrealm.com) charms
only up to 45th level mobs and I never had a problem charming these guards.

Medded to full, buffs on as indicated above, spell line up as above.

Steps:
1. Walk down ramp, as far away as possible, tash a guard - run up ramp.
Note that tash is uber important. MR is what seems to matter for broken
charms, not CHA. When my tash spell wears off, I expect a charm break soon
after.
2. After traveling past top of ramp awhile, turn, wait one sec (so as to
not be spell interrupted) and cast root.
3. If root is resisted (not often with tash) run north to dead area.
Side slip, run past trees, jump - whatever it takes to gain some ground on the
very upset giant. When you get ample distance, try to reroot (use 3rd
person view camera liberally to check distances).
4. Once pet is rooted, cast charm. Recast until it sticks. (resists are about 20%
for me I'd estimate).
5. If root is still holding (likely), approach pet and cast your haste
spell on him (spell range is much smaller than charm). If root is not
holding, head southeast towards mobs. Worry about hasting him once he
is fighting. Since this will be your pet for the next 10-20 minutes, you can
remove the haste spell from your list and replace it with something else if
you like.
6. If root still holds, go south east and find some prey. Target it and
tash it. Run back to your rooted pet hitting your hotkey "/pet attack"
when near him. Try not to get too close to your rooted pet. Once past pet,
turn and root target near the pet. It is important that during the fight,
you keep the target mob rooted. That way, when charm breaks (not if) you
only have to deal with a free mad pet, not two mad mobs. Also, I did group
some with druids during this. Their snare made recharm a snap but I felt
constrained getting my pets to new locations. However, their tracking is
incredibly useful in finding the next mob. EW is great because there are
LOTS of mobs, a sparser zone would be annoying. Nothing is more frustrating running
a long way with a charmed mob in tow - you know the charm is begging to break.
7. While pet is fighting target mob, head out of root range and med
watching the battle. Keep your PET targeted (F1 twice) and your fingers on
CTRL-S to stand up. Also your mouse on the root casting icon. Some battles,
your pet will not break charm and you simply get the kill. If he does
break, stand up, root, and recharm. If anything is resisted, start running
and try again. Sometimes, you will lose an almost dead mob because of an
unfortunate charm-break and resists but don't worry about this. Start
working on the next mob, such losses are rare. Also, if the target mob
breaks root during the fight, you can reroot him. If he is almost dead,
don't bother. The best way I've found to reroot is be out of root range and
start madly clicking the root casting "spell gem". Once he enters range, it
will start casting. I tried making a hot key "/sit off" then "/cast 3" but
that meant I had to guess I was in root casting range.. too iffy.
8. Eventually, you'll have two mad mobs chasing you. If both are giants
(kromrif ex-pet and say a scout) then they are fast runners and you must simply do
some creative rooting to get things under control. Remember, the northwest
area is your friend (just NW of kael entrance), it's a wide open uninhabited area with plenty of room
to root. If the target mob is a manticore or dire wolf, they are slow.
Run awhile, root your ex-pet, then root your target next to him. Even though,
neither is charmed they will fight! They still have each other on their
hate list. If your uncharmed giant kills the target mob ? it will poof!
Don't let that happen if possible, recharm asap.
9. Once your pet gets hurt enough. Tell him to sit. Then cast invis on
yourself and blast him a few times to get that kill too. There is a nasty
bug that an almost dead mob will warp back to his starting point. This
does happen to these guards and its quite a bother. Blasting my pet is what
really eats mana. A kromrif guard with only a sliver of health still seems
to take a few 44th DD's to bring down.

Other topics:
==========

Memblurring for xp:
Its said that if a pet does over 50% of the damage, it gets 50% of the
kill. So, you can try to outdamage your pet, but that's horribly mana
inefficient (probably impossible once you see those kromrif guards quading
for 130). Also you could call your pet off an almost dead target and
memblur the old target. Wooly Rhino's start out indiff so on a successful
memblur they go back to indiff. I can attest sometimes it has taken me
4 memblurs to make that happen! I can then blast and (theoretically) get
full xp for the kill. Keep in mind however, the entire time I am casting memblur
(which has a long recast time) my pet is standing right next to me. A charm
break then is deadly. To me, this has become not worth it. I typically
don't personally damage my targets at all. Let the pet do the work and
make up for it in volume. If memblur worked consistently, I might consider
changing my tactics.

Note that one kromrif guard has killed up to 14 (fourteen!) xp yielding
mobs for me before being so damaged I needed to retire him. That's in a
span of 10-15 minutes. I then med to full in a few more mins and go get the
other guard!

Poofs:

Poofs suck. That is, you blast a mob for final kill and it disappears!
No corpse, no xp. This happens because some other (non charmed) mob did more
damage than you and is still alive. Scenarios:

1. Your pet and target mob fight until almost both are completely dead.
Then charm breaks. They both wander off because they are both at a sliver
of health. They have both done 99% damage to each other. If you blast
EITHER, it will poof. If you blast both, the first will poof, the second
will not (because you killed the first who had the most credit for the
kill!). The answer? Recharm your pet, blast the second (or let your pet
kill it), then cast invis and blast your pet. You'll get both kills.
2. Both your pet and mob are rooted. A moment before the target mob dies,
your pet breaks charm. Because they are rooted next to each other, they
still fight. If the target mob dies then, it poofs. Recharm immediately if
possible.
3. Your pet fights some mob almost to the death then breaks charm and
chases you. Roots are resisted for awhile. By the time you get your pet
under control, you are halfway across the zone and have no idea where the
original target mob is. One battle later, you blast your own pet and he
poofs. The old target mob (across the zone somewhere) still did more damage
than you and got credit. The only solution here is to memblur your pet
a few times and hope it works.

Eventually, you'll lose a nice kill to a poof. Again, its part of the game,
just say "oh poop" and move on.

General other rules:

Keep Rune up at all times. You are a silly person if you don't.

If you are waiting for someone, need to do nothing, whatever - still don't
let your pet near you. Tell him to "/pet sit down" somewhere and stand away
from him. Or just keep running with him chasing far behind.

Slowing mobs is not too important. You can, but if your undercon charmed
pet is adequate it doesn't matter much. The final goal is have a pet that
has a SLIVER of health left and kills as many mobs as he can before then. I
slow manticores against my kromrif pet because they really do alot of damage.
If you are full mana during mid-battle (often) then feel free. Full mana is wasted mana.

The 35-43 game:
--------------------

This just changes your spells and location. Also your tactics slightly.
First off, change Cajoling Whispers for Beguile. Your DD is weaker but
that isn't too important. You STILL must follow the rule of finding a mean pet
to kill low blues. Iceclad works for this. The two giants standing at the
bridge can kill dire wolves and snow dervish like no tomorrow.

The most interesting change is in recharming. A Frost Giant takes about 4
seconds to get from just inside recharming range to splattering you across
the snow. Beguile takes 3.5 to cast (Cajoling whispers 4.5). If you
sufficiently outrun a mad ex-pet, can just rapidly click the Beguile spell
until it starts casting and get the recharm intact just before he reaches
you. It saves a lot of trouble in re-rooting. The bad news is that it truly
does take you to the edge of do-ability. If the charm resists, you will
likely get hit once or twice.

Possible charming zones:

+Levels 20-30: Griffins in the Karanas are a known undercon. You can charm
a green griffin and do some fine damage with it. Karana's have nice dead
zones hear the south water (don't get too close to guards)
+Levels 35-40: Iceclad giants at bridge are nice pets. Plenty of dead zone
near bridge and by water
+Levels 38-45: Frost Giant fort in EW. Berzerkers are good, so is Frost
Giant Captain. Dead zone south (same as "dead zone north" for kael
entrance)
+Levels 43-49: Kael entrance - kromrif guards. Dead zone north of kael
entrance. This is Mecca. These giants can pulverize so many mobs that live
just east.
+Levels: 44+: Burning wood - no wonderful undercon but wasps work. Wurms
are trouble because of their long necks. Dead zone by skyfire zone
entrance.
+Levels 30ish: Overthere: No definitive undercons, but plenty of room.


Conclusion:
There you go. Charm-soloing is incredibly profitable in xp. Note that I
rarely loot, I fill up too fast. These rules can be applied to druids too.
They haste their pets BETTER than enchanters and their snare spells makes
recharming a non-event. Wooly Rhino's in EW work well for a 40+ druid.

Also, charm soloing is nowhere as near as boring as necro/mage soloing or
root/nuke. It's a dance and you have to get good at it. Don't get attached
to your pet either. He'll want to kill you in a minute or two. Switching
pets midstream is great if the mana is high. Your pet is a walking piece of
experience too.

Lastly, I'd just like to say I played a rogue to 50 (my first primary)
and it was 100 times harder than an enchanter. My enchanter at 43 could kill
10 giants in 10 minutes. My 50 rogue couldn't kill one. Sure, sure,
solo'ing is not a measure of power. Then what is? My rogue has died to a
green that jumped him in the dreadland tunnel. I still see rogues shouting
for groups, post 50, pre 50, etc. This game was built to level a caster and
twink a melee for fun. That's not a horrible thing, I just wish it said
that on the box.

Very nicely written post, Igbert!

I'd like to add my own comment on the whole solo vs. group thread that will inevitably start...

Charm Soloing is FUN!

I started brand new enchanter just so I could charm solo all day with him! It's SUCH a rush, but Drekaar the dark elf enchanter (hey... HIDE kicks ass when charm soloing) is just a stud... almost 20th now, and loving it.

I've got some differences from you still... but I think this is probably the best charm-soloing guide I've seen. Even the stuff you do that I don't, you explained your reasonings to the point that I can't argue them. I can just say I like my way better--but I'll be trying yours now.

Myself, I got a couple things I do different:


  • I *never* haste my pet. The pet needs an "edge" (unless you happen to find that perfect underconning pet) for beating your opponent, but I'm a big fan of attack slows or damage shields (Drekaar doesn't have access to those yet).
  • I'm also not a "runner" on the recharm. I stand there and just take it--it's more dangerous, but allows tight quarters. Granted I'm 19th and using Charm (which Color Flux nicely covers for casting time), but even with Xornn I use Mesmerize (level 4) to cover the casting time on Cajoling Whispers/Allure. Color Shift perfectly covers the casting time on Beguile, which is why I was so disappointed with Color Skew. I felt we should get a color stun that would cover the cast for our current charm... Flux for Charm, Shift for Beguile, Skew for Cajoling, Slant for Allure... but ole Skew just doesn't do the job.
  • While I do the same method of running my pet from mob to mob till time to kill him, there are times when I just want to med up and I like my pet, so I'll med with him healing up. The key is /pet guard here, and getting a long ways away. I'll usually med till charm breaks, recharm, then go about my business again.
  • On the blurring... I've found that Blanket of Forgetfulness almost ALWAYS blurs, and the easy way to till if it works... when the mob starts to flee throw BoF... if your pet doesn't return to you, blur didn't take, so just let it finish. Now... I DON'T blur while charming for two reasons: 1)It's costly to use blurs... mana I don't have to spare since I'm trying to conserve. 2)Since the best idea is to keep the mob rooted, it rarely flees, so it's not worth it. Hmm... add 3)The charmed pet is standing by you. Never thought about that till you said something.

Here's how my charm-solo usually goes for Drekaar:
Buffs: Breeze, Shielding, SoW (Rune just isn't worth it till 34th)
Lineup: Sanity Warp, Charm, Color Flux, Root, Enthrall, Languid Pace, Tashan, Swap (usually Breeze)

Find my pet-to-be and Tashan... run back to my "camp site" and Charm. I keep three hotkeys "/pet attack" "/pet guard here" "/pet follow me" (the other pet commands are hotkeyd on the next bank over). Guard pet and run out to get my mob. Tashan it and run back. If charm breaks at any point during the pull I root my incoming (if it exists) and go back to recharm.

Otherwise as I return to pet I flick Attack and Root the incoming, then slow the incoming and med. I med a long ways from the fight but usually not far enough to charm in time. Stay targetted on pet, toggle to enemy once and awhile... but keep the pet targetted. ReRoot always, because one mob to recharm is nothing. It's two that gets you killed.

Why haste over slow? Countering points really... haste makes the pet win by increasing damage output thereby shortening the fight thereby making the charm "longer" by allowing it to hold through more xp gain. However, the haste makes your pet a greater threat to you (as if it weren't able to maul you anyway). Attack slows cripple the opposing mob, and increase the life expectancy of your pet, thereby allowing him to be used for more fights. Damage shields are nice and cute, but don't increase the life of your pet and only ensure your pet wins... they don't really allow him to OWN a mob, which is the lure of hasting. To each their own I say. (I'll admit... if a mage is soloing nearby... I haste. )

Okay... so charm breaks. Best case, I stand and recharm, pet goes right back to beating ass, or if he's really low I kill him, then kill the enemy mob. To recharm... either fire off the spell if you have room, or else you can Color Flux/Charm (or Color Shift/Beguile... or Mesmerize/Cajoling-Allure), or you can Root/Charm. I like my pet going back in ASAP which is why I like the stuns.

Worst case... enemy root and my charm break at the same time. Run. Land mez on your pet as quickly as possible, then get your enemy rooted again. Run back and Tashan/Charm your pet again. (Anytime you have a spare time to recharm, retash too.) THIS scenario is what drives so many enchanters from the charming racket, including me for 44 levels. It wasn't until I finally bit the bullet and got GOOD at it that I started seeing 1)How rarely it happens, and 2)How a cool head saves you.

After all, we derail 5-10 mob trains all day, and this is only 2?

Again, WONDERFUL guide, very detailed, and even if I do it differently, I have no argument why NOT to do it your way. I recommend everyone wanting to learn to try Igbert's methods out and then adapt them to your feel!

One last addenum. Root casts in 2.0 seconds flat. Enstill is the 2.5 second. Root is just such an amazing spell.

Mal,

See you need a spell slot for your nuke. Drop blur--you HAVE to keep the enemy rooted for consistent results anyway, and the ole pet doesn't back off of a rooted mob well anyway.

I don't mem both stuns because I'm using an attack slow. A mob with a slowed attack rate isn't just crippled for your pet, it's crippled in the event it rushes you. I like the attack slow (good point was raised, it's a large chunk of extra mana) because the worst case scenario has much less impact with attack slows in play. Look at the three most used "edges" (4th place goes to DOTing/Nuking--but don't EVER DOT when solo--you will regret when you can't mez, or accidentally charm the DOTed one).

I take two mobs attacking on a delay of 30, swinging every 3 seconds. Let's assume they are same level, so will roughly take each other down at the same rate. These mobs will likely take each other to nearly nothing. That means you get one fight out of your pet, and unless you try to do some very messy blurring, 1.5 mobs of xp in the end, minimal mana usage. (Without "edge".)

Adding "edge" at level 50 (for really raunchy haste levels) we have:

Swift Like the Wind:
60% haste, changing the pets swing time to 1.875 seconds apart. Now the pet will have 32 combat tics to every 20 tics of the enemy. Very very impressive. When charm breaks (not if) you will have 1.125 LESS seconds between combat tics to deal with the mob. Haste will make the mob MORE deadly to you, and in the "worst case" scenario, you are in deep kaka. SoW/JBoots makes hasting viable, as you just run away, but Haste is suicide without SoW (stay tuned for more on this mention). Haste usually lets your pet nail 2 mobs, then give itself for your xp pool... 2 mobs of xp, minimal mana usage. (Bear in mind still that undercons only live in select places... I'm looking at the run of the mill "make me Jabober" mobs)

Feedback:
11pt damage shield on the mob. As the mobs are going to beating the snot out of each other, the enemy will take an additional 11 damage per hit it scores. Assuming both mobs score even hits (not a stretch) then assume after every 20 tics, with double-attacking mobs, if half the blows land, that's 220 extra damage. Very impressive--at 29th. Seeing as most mobs only double hit without two weapons though... it's still about 220 extra damage at 50th. Not so impressive, and it will help, but not likely your pet kill more than 2 mobs. Worst case scenario, two mobs swinging at you at normal rate. Still full xp for pet, two half xp kills, 2 full xp equivalent.

Shiftless Deeds (my choice):
Slow at 50th: (5% + Level) = 55% slow, so our 3 second delay becomes 6.67 seconds. Now for every 20 combat tics for our pet, the enemy has 9. (By the way, Tepid Deeds at 50th... (20% + Level/2) = 45% slow or 4.62 second combat tic--13 tics per 20.) Anyway, the pet doesn't have an "edge" it's got a rigged fight. Vs a similar mob, it will SLAUGHTER it. Vs a weaker one it will emerge nearly unscathed. Vs a tougher one, it can often drop it, but you might need to drop a nuke in assistance. But just looking at the combat tics, if your pet needs 20 combat tics to drop the mob (1 minute of combat--pretty realistic guess), then it is answered by 9 attack tics. Less than half it's life if they are shelling out about the same damage. Counting it's healing time, you can drop 3 mobs in a row consistently. That's 2.5 mobs of xp per pet charmed. And the question most important to me: Worst case scenario--my normal speed pet, and a mob that swings every 6.7 seconds! I can almost ignore it! THIS is my lure to slows... it trivializes the worst case scenario into something MUCH easier to manage.

Mana cost?
Haste - 250 mana at charm start.
Feedback - 75 mana per fight, 150 usually.
Shiftless Deeds - 200 mana per fight, 600 usually.

Haste DOES use very little mana in the long run... one cast for however many mobs the pet survives. Mana comes back faster than hit points though... keep that in mind.

Feedback is next to nothing still... you gain it back during the fight STANDING with Clarity.

Shiftless Deeds is a monster resource hog, but even Tepid Deeds is crippling, and only 100 mana. But 4.5 seconds vs 6.7 seconds on the attack tic... over TWO extra seconds!? I didn't even have to think about it. You will see more mana usage. But honestly you just med during fights. That's what you do--and 230 mana per fight (Root and Slow) isn't that hard to regain. Your mana drops at charm time, not during the fight.

Xornn's "edge" explained.

All three methods work good... and if you really want the necros/mages to weep, hell yes, find a mob that will dual wield, give it SLtW, and proceed to DESTROY everything. Just remember who buffed the **** out of that thing when it rushes you.
