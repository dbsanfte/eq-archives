    State of the Game: Part 3
    4/6/02, 7:29 PST | Stryker

    The EQ Team has released another State of the Game. This time, it discusses the new interface, and the fabled bazaar. I will post a little of it below, but read the whole message here.

    EverQuest's interface consists of over 5,000 interface elements spanning 140 screens, tools, and windows. Some may not know this, but GMs and Guides have their own administrative screens in the EverQuest game client. In addition, Designers, Artists, and Animators have some of their tools built right in as well. All of these need to be recreated in the new interface.

    Writing a new, solid system and integrating it with that many moving parts is a large and complicated job. It's compounded by the fact that while doing this, we need to make sure that we don't break either of the two existing interfaces. It's definitely an interesting balancing act.

    Suffice it to say we have a number of developers and at least one artist working on this and nothing else.

    One of the more common rumors is that we're holding it back to include it as a feature exclusive to Planes of Power -- That's not going to be the case.

    Once it's nearing completion and is good enough to use for the majority of gameplay, we hope to begin rolling it out to the test server. Barring any unforeseen complications, that stage will begin sometime in the next few months.

    When it's complete, the new interface will be released to all players as a standard part of the EverQuest game client.

    The Bazaar

    The story of the Bazaar follows closely with that of the new interface. As our production cycle neared its end, we realized that we would not have time to complete the features properly before the release of Shadows of Luclin.

    Because this is a complicated new trade system, rushing this project could create item and cash bugs (both duplication and loss). So, as with the interface, we decided that it was best to do it the right way.

    The Bazaar will include several new windows and other such things, so creating the complete Bazaar has to wait until the new interface system is ready. Completing the Bazaar first would mean developing a number of windows for the old interface and then repeating the work for the new one.

    We hope to have the Bazaar available on Test shortly after the new interface is live.

    We're definitely sorry to keep you waiting for this. We're hoping that by the time it's finished, you'll agree that it was worth the wait.

    --The EverQuest Team

    Watch Your Password
    4/5/02, 10:19 PST | Kwill

    Absor had some comments on security on the Developer's Corner:
    But it is certainly worth pointing out again, we will never ask for your password. We don't need to know it. We only need it when you log in and on the guide application. We will never ask for it by email.

    You should never give out your password and you should change it frequently. Don't pick a simple password. Don't make your password the same as your character name or your login ID. Don’t use EQ words (don't use a class or race name for a password).

    I don't mean to sound like I'm being harsh, but protecting your password is important. I spent a few years working in a government office. I can't tell you how many times I was able to locate the users password just by glancing around their cubicle, either in written form (heck, sometimes sticky-noted to the monitor), by noticing that they had an affinity for a certain animal or by learning the name of their kids by reading the signature from their art projects. The same can happen using the Internet. If you choose a password that is easily guessed, someone will guess it. Use your kids name and then spend some time chatting on a message board and you just might mention it.

    The simple solution is to use a non-word for a password. Make it something you can remember, but that isn't a word.

    Check out his thread to see the most common place people "hide" their password on their computer desk.


    No More Player NPCs
    4/5/02, 10:15 PST | Kwill

    Remember Project M, where players could play NPCs? It was a big favorite with the player base, but it isn't going to be part of EQ for now, according to Absor:
    Unfortunately that project is very much on hold right now. Many of the changes and updates we've been making lately have made the old functionality unworkable. There isn't any time in our schedules for fixing it, there are too many priority concerns right now. There were also some concerns from players (and us) that players being monsters is rather disruptive to play on servers.

    Playing an NPC was indeed a blast. But I wouldn't expect it to return in the foreseeable future.

    More RPG History from GameSpy
    4/3/02, 8:50 PST | Kwill

    Today's installment of the five-part series on the role playing genre concerns multi-player games. From the article:
    Multiplayer, or more accurately, "multi-user," games sprung up on mainframes in the 1970s at universities and government facilities -- in the dank dungeons where networked computers lurked. It was there that MUDs were born. MUDs (multi-user dungeons) are worlds that exist without fancy graphics primarily consisting of shared imagination and text. These games were written by a handful of prolific companies and dedicated players, and supported by people who preferred to imagine the marauding trolls rather than have them rendered by high-powered video cards and processors.
    Read more in Computer Role-Playing Games - Part III: Massively Multiplayer Madness.
