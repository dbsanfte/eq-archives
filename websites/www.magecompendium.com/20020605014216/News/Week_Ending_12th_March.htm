Topic :   Gordon on Pet power

 

This came of the official EverQuest message boards.

 

Enchanters are not pet classes, but pet changes do affect them.

 

Magicians are pet classes, DD changes affect them.

 

If Magicians DD was accidentally nerfed, would they get compensated ?

 

Enchanters pets got nerfed as a side effect to a code change, I want to know why we did not get compensation.

 

Whether or not enchanters are a pet class holds no relevance to whether or not we are affected by pet changes, and that is what I am trying to display to you. Assuming our pets were balanced previous to the dagger haste changes, we lost something for no reason, and yes, it did affect us.

 

All I want is an explanation other than you are happy with the way it turned out.

 

I understand your point. My point was that the dual wield issue caused us to have to rebalance all of the pets. When we were in the process of doing this, mage pets got their dual wield and became the top pet we would give anyone based upon their combat performance. Everyone else had to be lower than that. The next was necromancer pets. The change affected them to a pretty large degree, so we added the lifetap proc. Shaman pets weren't affected at all since they never dual wielded. Enchanter pets were affected, but we determined that the pet ended up where it should be in the new scheme of things.

 

Now, with the necromancer, we found that though their pet fit in the proper place, the change had reduced the combined power of the necromancer too much. We therefore added the additional spells to their class.

 

With enchanters, we decided that the total "power" of the class was not reduced far enough to warrant any reimbursement for the change in weapons delay. This is primarily because when then enchanter was balanced, it was not balanced with a pet. The pet was introduced post-production in order to deal with a balance issue at lower levels. This is why they aren't a "pet class".

Given my time here at Verant, I think I can finally articulate this in a more accurate manner. I think "bug" is probably not the correct term to describe why hell levels are there. "Mathematical Consequence" would probably be a better description. Trying to put it in easy terms :

Basically, everyone knows that the experience needed at every level is a curve. Well, it's not so much a curve, as it is a series of straight lines. As you level, the "curve" gets steeper. However, due to the formula used, in the first level after the curve gets steeper, you must "make up" all of the experience as if the curve had been that steep all along. As such, a "Hell Level" is the first level where a new experience curve took affect. Ideally, it would have been nice if the formula spread the additional experience required over all the levels of the next range, rather than requiring it all in one level, but the outcome is the same.

As for 50+, it's probably safe to say that experience required in those levels won't be on the same curve as 45-50, and as such, any time the curve steepens, the "Hell Level Phenomenon" will be present for the first level of the new curve.

Basically, this is just the way it is, and we won't be readjusting the experience curves. They haven't been changed since release, and no one ever said that progression would be linear.

However, I'm still waiting for an explanation why you loose more experience (or appear to loose more experience) upon death in a level after a hell level (31, 36, 41, 46).

You really don't, though you are right that it appears so. What you are losing is a percentage of the next level that is higher than what would seem warranted. What you are losing is a percentage of your total experience. Since you have much more experience than you did before the previous level, it shows up as a bigger portion of the next.

Basically, if you have 30M experience, and you die, you lose the same amount of experience regardless what level you are in.

Oh yeah, we believe that. lets see at 45 we die and lose very little but ONE level later we lose nearly a full bubble. Can you explain why it takes more kills at 35 than it does at 36 to travel the same distance even though you say 36 takes more exp gained ?

I didn't say that.

It looks like you lose more experience in Hell+1 because you have more experience total, and therefore lose a bigger percentage of the bar. For example, suppose level 30 takes 100M experience, and level 31 takes 30M experience to get through. When you die in 30, you'll lose a percentage of your total experience (NOT the experience required to level, but the TOTAL experience). For example, suppose you lose 5M experience. That will appear to be a quarter bar.

Ok, now, suppose you are in level 31. You have more experience total, so when you die you lose 6M experience. 6M is 1/5 of the experience required for 31, so you lose a full bar.

As I mentioned above, the amount of experience you lose (in points) is the SAME regardless what level they are. So, if experience loss were 10% (it's not, this is just an example), and a level 36 Human has 100M experience, he loses 10M. A troll SK might only be level 32 at the time when he has 100M experience. He still loses 10M when he dies.

If this is not clear, please let me know.

Basically Abashi is saying this. You need X amount of EXP to reach 50th level. You need to obtain that number to reach 50 whether you get 98% in level 1 and the other 2% spread out over the other 49 levels or what not. So hell levels suck, but you have an easier time in the other 4 levels in each set because of it. In the end it will equal that X number you need to be 50.

Another example, if you need 5000 exp points to go from the beginning of 30th level to the end of 34th level, you will do 2000 of those points in level 30 and 750 exp points per level afterward for a total of 5000. If the "hell level" phenomenon hadn't occurred you would have to gain 1000 exp points per level making 30th level much easier but making the other 4 levels 25% tougher. Either way you need 5000 exp points to do those 5 levels.

What Abashi is saying is that because this happens in sets of 5 levels that level 51 will be a hell level and that's not going to change. He didn't say anything about levels 52+. Topic :   Rare Spawns and Spawn Rates

 

This came of the official EverQuest message boards.

 

Well actually, NPCs don't have spawn rates. Encounters have spawn rates. NPCs have spawn percentages and are attached to encounters. In other words, if you kill a mammoth in Everfrost, it may respawn as a spider, orc, leopard, etc. Rare spawns also work the same way.

 

A rare NPC might be on an encounter table by itself with a long spawn time. This would make the NPC spawn every so often. Another way to do it is put the rare NPC on an encounter table with something else, with a low spawn percentage. In other words, one type of creature can act as a placeholder for a rare spawn. You could sit alone in a zone, never kill a thing, and never see the rare NPC spawn. However if you kill everything, it could potentially spawn a dozen times a day.

 

Does this answer your question ?

 

- Gordon

Back when EQ was in its primary development cycle, the thought of resolutions higher than 640x480 wasn't expected any time in the near future. Video technology did progress to that point later in EQ's development cycle, but at that point we were too far in to do a major UI redesign.

 

In order for the default UI to support those higher resolutions in the manner requested, it would have to be redesigned for each specific resolution. This would result it a very large download, containing copies of the UI art for each resolution.

 

The redesign of the default UI would also require a good deal of manpower, manpower that we feel is better applied to other aspects of the game. As such, we have no short-term plans to redesign the default UI so that it fills the higher resolution screens.

 

- Gordon

Topic :   Overcrowding

 

This came of the official EverQuest message boards.

 

I was at the EQ Gathering 2 in St. Louis yesterday and Brad McQuaid spent several hours discussing issues with the players there. One of the big issues, obviously, was overcrowding. He knows that overcrowding is a problem and is determined to fix it. He said has a three-step plan in place for resolving overcrowding :

 

1. Adding new servers. They will continue to add new servers and will allow some of the players from the servers with the highest prime time populations to move their characters if they desire.

 

2. Ruins of Kunark. The expansion pack will increase the world by 33% and provide many more zones for players of all levels. They demonstrated ROK and it looks awesome. There are new mid and high level zones that will blow away places like Lower Guk and Sol B and will definitely draw players out of those zones. The zones on ROK are also designed to be able to handle more players without people stepping all over each other.

 

3. Re-tuning zones. They will make changes to currently underutilized zones like Permafrost, Sol A, and Najena in order to make them more enticing to players. They hope this will help spread the population more evenly among the zones.

 

Brad also expects The Hole to open around May. He was hoping to have it open prior to the expansion but it's running late.

 

It was obvious that Brad and Verant are serious about resolving the overcrowding issue and have a plan in place for doing so. It won't happen overnight but I think in a few months overcrowding will be a non-issue.

 

- Xhen

Topic :   Gordon on a Role-Playing Server

 

This came of the official EverQuest message boards.

 

We consider all servers to be RP servers since this is a role-playing game. Role-playing is a personal decision that each player makes for themselves. The joy for role-playing should be role-playing itself, not any tangible benefit handed out by the game. That's why when people say "There's nothing to encourage role-playing", I get very confused, as there is everything to encourage it, except perhaps ones own propensity to become self-conscious.

 

Now, if you are asking about an "Enforced Role-play" server, we'd consider it as soon as someone comes up with a definition for role-play that would allow us to enforce it. We catch a lot of flack for our naming policy, which is much clearer than any enforced role-playing server could be in my humble opinion.

 

Now if we are talking about an un-enforced role-play server, I'd have to say that that would do no better than the servers we have now, which are all basically un-enforced role play servers. Perhaps in name alone, it would support further role-playing, but I'm sure that many people of all play-styles would join it, if for no other reason than it had less players for the first few hours.

 

- Gordon
