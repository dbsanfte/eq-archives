Topic :  Pet Power

 

This came of the official EverQuest message boards.

 

I would have posted this in the other thread but figured that it was different enough to deserve its own thread for discussion:

I was just sitting here thinking over the latest issue with pet procing, and the sword of runes, and I noticed something.

A number of the complaints in this thread cite changing something that's been that way since release, or saying that if we are going to nerf something, why don't we fix something before we nerf something else.

In truth, we did. Mages only got the ability to hand weapons to their pets 10 months ago (January 28th to be precise). The problem with the procs going off on everything and going off so often only became an issue then. Sure, there was the odd necro who had a magician friend who would summon up weapons, but it certainly wasn't wide-spread enough to make a global difference.

Your pets are more powerful now than they were at release, even given the latest change. They dual wield now. Sure, that was a bug workaround, but the pets were bumped up in power several times before the dual wield workaround because they weren't beating necro pets. In many ways, they became overpowered when they suddenly began dual wielding, an imbalance that wasn't addressed until now.

So, as you see, we did address a problem that resulted in increasing the power of the pet prior to "nerfing" a small portion of it. Now I do wish that there had been less time between cause and effect, but it's too late for that at this point. The point is that this is not an ability that has been around forever. While I agree that many people might have come to take it for granted, mage pets are still stronger than they were for nearly a year after release.

- Gordon

Topic :  ?

 

This came of the official EverQuest message boards.

I'd like to let you all in on something that has been a fairly common occurance in regards to how these things happen. It's been noticed before and assumed to be either coincidence or spite, but I assure you that neither is the case. What I'm talking about is the phenomenon whereas a class starts complaining about their class, and subsequently have something "nerfed" a patch or two after the complaints reach critical mass. Well I'll tell you that it's not a coincidence, nor is it done out of spite to "punish" those who complain, but it is those complaints that bring about the "nerf" for a different reason.

What happens is that people begin complaining about an issue. Those complaints get louder and louder until someone sits down and looks into it to once and for all determine what problem, if any, exists. However, the person looking into the issue doesn't take into account that issue alone, but the class as a whole. Classes are more than the sum of their abilities, and any issue with a particular ability or lack thereof must be juxtaposed against all of the other abilities of the whole class.

It is this scrutiny that leads to quantifying the true impact of any particular facet of a class, even when a problem is already known. A good example of this is low-delay weapons and pets. Everyone in house knew that necros handed weapons to their pets. They however did not know the true impact of doing that. They did not know that not only was the pet being sped up, but it was dual wielding only as a result of those weapons. So when compared to mage pets, by the numbers, it appeared that mage pets should have always had the advantage, where instead necro pets would tear through them like a wood chipper through a tree-branch.

Back on topic, whole-class scrutiny in regard to one complaint leading to a "nerf" has happened many times. It happened with necros several times, not the least of which was when lifetaps became subject to level caps. It happened to monks after the first "nerf" of Feign Death. It happened to magicians here: There were a ton of mage complaints on the boards over the past several weeks and one of the programmers sat down and ran every aspect of the class through some comprehensive balance tests.

So what can I tell you? "Balance" is not a perfect science by any means. It's not a perfect point by which any change imbalances a class in one direction or another. Every class has some area where they are probably under-balanced, and every class also some area or areas where they are probably over-balanced. Tendering complaints about the "under-balanced" areas draws attention to the "over-balanced" ones as well, and we won't ignore the latter while exclusively treating the former. Truth be told, we would probably be "ok" if we did neither, but we are trying to be responsive to the concerns as they are raised.

Just some food for thought for those who wanted to know how things like this happen.

- Gordon

Topic :  Mage Pets

 

This came of the official EverQuest message boards.

 

It looks like half the threads on the first page are about mage issues of one kind or another. Let me see if I can clear up a few of the rumors going around, and please try to keep the number of threads down.

Though it might seem that this many threads are necessary to properly express the feelings that some are having, all that will happen is that we'll have to start closing threads.

Call of the Hero: No intentional change was made to the way that this works. However, there were some changes made to the way intra-zone teleports are handled (to actually make them work better), so it is possible that it might have had an unintended effect on Call of the Hero.

Pets: No changes were made to pet stats. The only change that was made in regard to pets is that they now proc all-target procs as if they were a player character. They proc special-target procs half as often as if they were a player. The next patch will make it so that special-target procs are proced just as often as the others, but will only work on the intended target type.

This change does not affect what we call a "contact innate" (e.g. A spell that procs directly off of an NPC such as the necro lifetap procs on 44th and 49th) or "spell innate" (casted spells such as that done by mage pets).

We will most likely be scaling the dexterity of pets with their level so that the higher level ones will proc carried weapons more often, nearly as much so as before. We will also look into creating new summoned special-target proc weapons for mages.

All of the other things that we should have fixed instead: Every time we make a change considered negative to a particular class, the boards are filled with complaints that we should have fixed something else instead of spending time on this. While I appreciate everyone's thoughts, we're not going to exclusively stick to making changes that everyone will like at the expense of the overall determent to the game. To use an analogy (which you all know I like to use), complaining that the officer should be out catching real criminals instead of writing you up for speeding is not going to make him say, "You're right", and stop writing your ticket. There are things that need to be done. Things that upset the balance of the game (typically by making it too easy thus trivializing aspects of it by removing the challenge) will get priority over other things that don't have this type of impact. It's the way it works and the way we need to do things to keep the game interesting.

- Gordon

Topic :  Proc Nerf

 

This came of the official EverQuest message boards.

Alright everyone, here's what happened.

As all weapon-wielding pet users probably knew, up until recently pets would proc weapon effects much more frequently than player characters. We recently changed this so that they now proc effects with just about the same frequency as player characters with the same dexterity. The exception is targeted (meant for only one type of enemy, such as summoned creatures) effects, which will now proc about half as often as they do for player characters, but will still proc on anything (this, as you know, is a bug).

Shortly we will be fixing this bug so that pets with targeted proc items will only proc on the appropriate target. At that time, we will bump up the proc rate to match those of any other weapon proc for the pet.

This only effects weapon procs, not innate procs.

No pet attributes were changed.

All pets currently have the same dexterity. It is possible in the future that we will modify the dexterity of pets based on level and type to make them more flexible for their masters.

I'm sorry that we didn't mention this change earlier.

- Alan

Topic :  Patch

November 21st, 3:00am

Patch Day

Today's patch addresses a few problems noted by customers since last week's big patch. The following changes were made:

- Fixed some problems with sound volume being set above the maximum or below the minimum.

- Fixed a problem that caused sound volume to be reset regardless of the slider setting upon zoning or entering game.

- Fixed a bug that would cause some sounds to be unduly amplified whenever the volume slider was moved.

- Fixed a bug causing pink fringes to show on whiskers in association with mipmapping.

- Fixed a bug with /usercolor causing settings to be saved in the wrong slot.

- Fixed weapon scaling problems with off-hand weapons and those carried by dwarves.

- Fixed a bug that caused the client to display an amount of experience in slight disagreement with the server.

- Fixed the Paladin Epic Quest reward, which was procing a spell that would do around one million points of damage if left to its own devices.

- Fixed a faction problem that made some quests designed to be doable by anyone, regardless of race or class, undoable.

In other news, the Tarew/Povar split server, Xev, will be up following the patch today. Xev is named for Xev Bristlebane, the god of fortune. We would have named the new server under its full name if not for an attempt to avoid confusion between the existing "Bristlebane" server (named for Fizzlethorpe Bristlebane) and Xev.

- The EverQuest Team
