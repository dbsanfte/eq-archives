Drinal

Actually, it's really somewhat of a myth that the animated trees in Kunark and Velious cause a significant framerate hit. It's really just the density of trees and other objects as well as how many polygons were used to create the objects (both of which were significantly higher in Kunark and Velious than they were in the old world). Animation really isn't very costly because most if not all of the scene in a 3D renderer has to be re-rendered every frame anyway.

The best solution is to, as you mentioned, crank down the clip plane, which will give the graphics engine less polygons to draw. Also, with Luclin, we'll be introducing some new technology (Umbra) which more efficiently decides what to draw and what not to draw based on what the player can see from his vantage point. The great news is that Umbra will also work with all of the other zones in the game (old world, Kunark, and Velious), giving players both better framerates and also the ability to push their clipping plane further away.

- Camenwolf

There are many ways to approach implementing content in massively multiplayer games, but I will try to explain why I don't like random content in general and why it's really not compatible with EQ's Vision.

To me, dynamically generating content/zones/adventure areas would risk losing a lot of the hand-crafted and immersive feel you find in the world of Norrath. We are very proud that each and every zone was put together by hand and that, hopefully, they each have a personality. The more random or automated the process is, the less compelling and creative it is, in my opinion. And while our priority is to make a great game, it's also a goal of ours to create a real virtual world, like those you've read of in the great fantasy novels out there. Certainly, for example, I don't think the Mines of Moria in the Lord of the Rings would have been nearly as interesting were they the product of 'randomized dwarf passages' strewn together. Instead, Tolkien made us believe such a place could be real, and intricately described the setting to us in both a fantastic AND believable way. My dream and vision is to eventually make virtual worlds as detailed as those Tolkien and others have described, and quite frankly, I don't see how we can get there by succumbing to the allure of random and dynamically generated content (even if it does make it easier and faster to develop).

As for making zones exclusive to the player or group of players assigned to a quest, I also feel that's incompatible with EQ's vision. Despite some problems with camping and criticisms that EQ 'forces' interdependence upon players, I think a shared world has really helped build community and relationships within the game. Having to deal with annoying or disruptive players can certainly be a problem, but then how many times have you met somebody new because they helped you (or you helped them) out of a bad situation in the depths of a dungeon? Again, I believe it makes the virtual world more immersive and better suspends disbelief if the adventure area you're in is actually attached and part of the world, accessible by all. I really think that taking the 'massively multiplayer' out of the MMOG because having other players around can be problematic does less to fix the game then it does to change the game into another genre of game altogether.

These are just my opinions, however, and why they're not compatible with what we want EverQuest to be. It's one of our strict policies not to comment negatively about our competition and there's plenty of room in this emerging genre for all sorts of approaches. Ultimately it's the game players' opinions that count, and one should choose a game with the style, features, and game mechanics that work best for them personally. Heck, play them all and make your decision accordingly

Hi Brad,

I've been playing EverQuest for a limited amount of time now, and there's one thing which boggled my mind pretty much from the first time my poor necro managed to summon a pet, and then loose it cos he walked across a zone boundary.

Why DO pets vanish at the boundary ? Is it a balance issue, or are/were there technical reasons.

Both technical and balance issues, really. It is more difficult to pass an NPC (which pets are) from one zone to another, but it is possible (for example, boats are really NPCs, and they move zone to zone). But we also wanted character classes with pets to have to summon pets every once in a while instead of them being persistent - it allows for different types of pets to be summoned and with some variation of their attributes, etc. So in that it was difficult to have them zone in the first place, it sort of made sense to use that as one of the mechanisms by which the player would need to summon another pet.

All that said, it looks like we might have 'familiars' in Luclin, which are really persistent pets that would indeed stay with you when you zone.

This, of course, leads to a second question: Why do mobs stop at a zone boundary. Again, is it for technical reasons, or was it intended for the players to be able to run to safety by moving into a neighboring zone ?

Again, both. By making zones more self-contained it was and is easier to balance them. Knowing that NPCs stop at zones allows us to put the world together with a more modular approach. And, as I said earlier, it is more difficult to have NPCs zone, although they can (for example, I heard recently that after a very powerful dragon was unleashed it traveled to several zones, slaying its enemies all over the face of Norrath).

Lastly, are either characteristics likely to change with upcoming patches? I mean, the idea of having a char being chased through a larger area by a group of angry Orcs has a certain appeal to me. (Ok, I know *my* poor necro would die a lot more often if that would happen)

Probably not, except for very special encounters. Making a significant change to how NPCs stay or don't stay within zones would change the game so radically that it probably wouldn't be a very good idea this late into release.

Got a bunch of stuff jumbled all together here, please bear with me.

First off, design questions about the basic game. When you chose a "Class-Based" character development/creation schema over one that was "Skill-Based", what factored into that decision?

Primarily the desire to foster interdependence. Relying on others in order to do things not only builds community it also allows us to make mid and high level combat and adventure very tactical in nature. We really wanted to avoid the 'tank-mage syndrome', where one player could be the 'jack of all trades'. That, and it's easier to balance a class-based system than it is a skill-based system because of its inherent restrictions.

What methods/tactics/theories of (valid) game play have developed that you predicted? That surprised you? I'm not talking about cheating, but "legal" methods of dealing with MoB's and encounters.

Well, we definitely set out to create a game that was easy to get into but became very involved later on. And watching several groups work together to slay a high level mob and all the strategies and tactics involved, I'd say we pulled it off pretty well. But as I've mentioned in past Ask Brads and elsewhere, tactics such as pulling, camping, and kiting were not part of the original plan. So we've definitely been surprised on many occasions by the player base and their resourcefulness (which, all things considered, is a good thing).

Did you anticipate the "Big Three" scenario of Kunark ? (The prevalent theory that you must have a Warrior, Cleric and Enchanter to deal with high-level game play in Kunark)

Honestly, no. We thought that there would be a place for both 'generalists' and 'specialists'. But it turns out that some groups and guilds are so concerned with being as efficient as possible that they sometimes exclude the 'generalists' in the high end of the game. This is indeed unfortunate, and were we starting over we'd probably make more core roles and then have the classes share those roles more equally. That said, I also don't think it's a problem of apocalyptic proportions. Hybrids and other non-specialist classes still get groups at high levels, are very productive guild members, and have a blast playing EQ from 1-60. It's just that sometimes in some situations they can be more frustrated than the core classes when dealing with extreme min/max'ers.

What are some of the more "crazy" ideas that fell by the wayside during the early stages of EQ's development? Kunark ? Velious ?

Heh, well, one would be getting Kunark and Velious done by the first release with as much detail and content as we wanted. Other 'crazy' ideas included Guild Houses and Horses. Honestly, though, there aren't that many - all in all we pretty much made the game we set out to. And with Kunark and Velious there were even less ideas that didn't make it in because we knew so much more about the game, the tools we had, and how long it took to implement features and build content.
