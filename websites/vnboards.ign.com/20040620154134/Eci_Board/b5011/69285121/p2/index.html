<<<Since evolution does not stop, since we supposedly evolved from apes, then where are those apes that are continuing to evolve? It seems to me that we should have some that are just apes and some that are somewhere between men and apes.>>>

This seems to be a common theme from critics of evolutionary theory. However, you need to understand that the phylogeny of evolution is not linear, but divergent. This is most commonly called a universal ancestor approach. A single progenitor organism can branch to a variety of other organisms. Such could be the ape which holds ancestoral bond with us. The split with that organism may have been from a variety of factors...genetic drift, gene flow, mutation, or a genetic recombination in the DNA that prevented interspecies mating.

The reason for no man-apes appears to be somewhat developed in history. Current humans are homo sapiens, progentiors being Cro-magnan. Concurrently living with Cro-magnans were Neanderthals. Though, natural selection promoted Cro-magnan's differential reproductive success (due to their increased intellect, a la cephalization). The inability for Neanderthals to compete with Cro-magnon would be an easy end to them. A few theories also speak of the potential ability for them to interbreed. However, should this be the case, then they were of the same general species initially, and simply aligned together.

Current apes are evolving. Though, not all creatures share a similar pattern. Consideration must be taken to account for predation, environment, and the abiotic factors that afflict the organisms.


One of the more recent theories on evolution comes from Mitochondial Eve. This name is based on religion, though scientifically supported; the mother is the only one who can pass down mitochondrial DNA.

In prokaryotic cells, (i.e. cells lacking membranes, as well as organelles) the main type of energy production is glycolysis. This fermentation reaction breaks down sugar into pyruvatic, then finally lactic acid. This is the simplest, yet most ineffective energy gathering mechanism.

Eukaryotic cells, (i.e. complex cells making mostly everything else) contain either chloroplasts (plants) or mitochondria (animals + plants). Glycolysis still occurs, though the mitochondria take the pyruvate product and refine it into an energy making machine. This is rougly 17 times the energy for the workload vs. glycolysis. The specific factors are known as the Krebs Cycle and Electron Trainsport Chain, should you be so inclined to research.

It appears that an early prokaryote absorbed a free living mitochondria and thus bloomed a mutualistic relationship. With all the extra energy, the early ancestor in this case, could evolve into much more complex things, such as us. The proof is that BOTH mitochondrial and prokaryotic cells have different DNA. Mitochondrial DNA replicates/mutates at 10x the speed of cellular DNA. This leads to the hypothesis of potential evolution and liberation of mitochondria over time. That however, is a totally seperate discussion, yet goes to reinforce this point.

Ever feel your muscles burning from running? Thats glycolysis taking place in your body. When there is not enough oxygen for the mitochondria to energize, glycolysis assists by causing fermentation in the blood. The burn is from lactic acid buildup. Thus a human being still shows its roots in the prokaryotic cell, the great precursor to all higher forms of life.

Hmmm, I guess the Riddle of Epicurus is somewhat tangential to Evolution vs Creationism, but hopefully the other posters will pardon it.

Alikat writes:
> >>"Is he able, but not willing? Then he is
> malevolent."<<

> This is the line where your Epicurian delight breaks
> down.

I assume you mean this "tongue-in-pique" since someone who takes the Riddle seriously isn't necessarily an epicurean.

> There is nothing malevolent about a mother bird
> teaching her young to fly, even if a couple of them
> don't survive the fall.

This seems like the "sometimes a small amount of suffering is necessary for learning or for the greater good" theodicy. The other one I've heard is, "to the child, the pain of an inoculation seems like a great evil, because she doesn't understand the greater good being served."

I think the traditional response is that suffering might be necessary for a being of finite powers to accomplish a greater good, since there are always necessary tradeoffs for such a being. E.g. The parent must choose between subjecting their child to the pain of a polio inoculation or the risk of her experiencing the much greater suffering of polio itself.

But no such tradeoffs would be necessary for an omnipotent being. If such a being wanted us to be wise, he could make us such. If he wanted to prevent us from a greater suffering, he could instantly abolish the greater suffering, without a single moment of lesser suffering. Since he chooses not to do so, this raises the question of whether he is unable or unwilling.

> We're living in the wilderness because our ancestors
> couldn't obey the one rule they were told to obey,
> and then they tried to lie about it.

This suggests the "all suffering is the result of human choices" theodicy. For me, this one gets a little farther, except that it only explains the subset of evil that results from human choices, such as car accidents and lung cancer and serial rape. It doesn't explain "all natural suffering", like mudslides and hurricanes and ebola.

Personally, I think the only tenable defense against the Riddle is to capitulate on the omnipotence of God, but most Christians won't consider it.

Kalanar, you missed the important part of one of those sentences: "We're living in the wilderness."

At least read Genesis. It's a short read. God specifically cursed Adam & Eve and all their descendants, and had them driven into a wilderness of thorns. He didn't do that solely for punishment, either. He did that mainly to prevent humans from eating the fruit of the Tree of Life, and thereby living forever. With our new knowledge of Good & Evil, and our taint of disobedience and dishonesty, we were not fit to receive eternal life. Sin is like a bad smell to God, and He doesn't want to spend eternity with companions who stink. But He still loved us, so He didn't unmake us. So He created a way for us to wash off the stink, by using that marvelous faculty He had given us, free will. All we have to do is choose to accept His sacrifice, and He will accept us. Think of it as a litmus test to find out if we have what it takes to directly worship God and obey Him.

So yes, God is omnipotent, but no, God does not choose to exercise His powers to abate human suffering. That's because human suffering is the direct result of our ancestors' actions, which got the human race kicked out of the Garden, where there is no disease and there are no thorns or hunger, and into the wilderness, where the law of tooth and claw prevails.

What I think is your problem here is that you think that if He exists, God was created by Man to serve Man's needs, make Man comfortable, and that is a bit skewed. We were created by God to serve God's purposes, and that doesn't necessarily translate into our living a stuggle-free life with a permanent orgasm contorting our features. People have argued on this board that we owe our country a couple of years of service (the "Reinstituting the draft-Phase 1...." thread), and currently that type of service means taking risks such as being flown to the Middle East and being shot at, but the being who created this universe and all the creatures in it, including yourself, must be nuts to expect you to endure pestilence, plague, and famine? You must have gotten your notions of what God is from watching Disney films! Who told you that God is a warm fuzzy sweety-pie? Some hippy?

Edit: Yes, I was trying to be Pun-tastick with the "Epicurian delight" [sic] bit, I see nothing particularly sensual about arguing religion on a message board.

Edit2: Oh, and as for why does God not use His omnipotence to remake us and the world to His exact specifications, why would He? After all, He is also omniscient, and He knew exactly what was going to happen before He ever started this project, if "before" can be considered a valid concept in a being who created Time and Space. Sure, He COULD have created us so that we did exactly as He wanted us to, but then we wouldn't have free will, would we?

Think about this one: He created us with free will even though He knew in advance that we would choose wrongly about half the time. It's all part of that "made in His image" thing. It's what keeps us from being boring extensions of His own will. We aren't robots, we're free creatures. Try playing chess with yourself some time, and tell me how exciting that is. Why would He remake us into something less than we are?
