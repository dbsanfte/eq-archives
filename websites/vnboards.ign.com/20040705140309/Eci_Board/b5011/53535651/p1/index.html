Most of the incidental expenses of the "big-name" bands are paid by various producers hoping for a profitable cut afterwards. Video production is a prime example. Only bands with terrible business management produce their own videos, the better-selling bands have to turn down offers to make videos all the time due to time constraints. And the arena managements not only pay the bands to show up, they also handle the insurance expenses for the venue, security, you name it.

Established bands only have to worry about transportation, equipment, and paying their own road crews. Album production costs are covered up front by the producers, including studio time, promotion (air and print), production, and distribution. If the album does well, then it's deducted from the gross, if not, the studio absorbs it.

The main thing wrong right now with the system is that the bulk of the recording industry's decision-makers aren't really "into" the music they produce, they are simply following some business-school programming that says that if more than 65% of the populace likes Miracle Whip, you should only sell Miracle Whip and not bother stocking Mayonnaise. That fails to take into consideration peoples' strong cultural attachment to "their kind of music," just as it fails to take into consideration that perhaps 35% of the populace is literally nauseated by Miracle Whip and they don't want it to be their only choice.

The best of the big music studios are the ones that have a lot of musicians and former musicians in Management. They actually listen to the tunes they are producing with an esthetically-tuned ear. Unfortunately, most of those are mainly producing Rap and Hip-Hop, which are themselves mainly marketing-spawned phenomenae, where literally billions are spent telling young people what's "cool" to be "into." Rap originally came from the street, but it's been so co-opted by the marketing suits now that most of today's Rap and Hip-Hop "artists" are about as spontaneous as "The Monkees."
