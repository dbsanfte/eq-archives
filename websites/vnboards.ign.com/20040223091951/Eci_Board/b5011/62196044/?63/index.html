(Please excuse me if this tabbing is messed up, I wrote this in Wordpad, as I do all of my larger posts.)

Much like the prohibiton on alchohal, the war on drugs has done more harm than good. The following reasons will be outlined in the following paragraphs. This is a huge topic to cover, with many facets, so please bare with me.

The war on drugs has been lost. Billions have been spent to fight to keep the drugs out of this country, but they still freely flow into it. Even worse than that, we have payed with civil liberties. With the accusation of a drug related crime happening on your property, with no other involvement from yourself, your property can be siezed indefinitely. Much like the "War on Terrorism", the War on Drugs was the means to impliment many laws taking away your privacy and your constintional rights. The fourth amendmant gives you the right to be safe and secure in your home, from unjust searches and siezures. Your property can be searched and taken simply due to an accusation by the police that you aquired it through drug profits.

Beyond just the loss of your civil liberties is the loss of money. Billions have been spent to fight a losing war on drugs. For every shipment of drugs, intended to come to this country, that are siezed, many more slip by. The money is spent in vain. This comes directly out of the taxpayers pocket, who generally have no reason to, or straight out do not agree, with the expenditure. Some of this money is spent on ad campaigns about drugs, which completely lie to the public. Sure, you might let the baby you are babysitting drown when you are high on marijuana, because you aren't paying attention. But imagine how many times more likely it is to happen when, say, you are playing videogames. Sure, you might crash your car and kill your little brother when you are high, but it is much more likely that you will do so sober, for any number of reasons (mechanical failure, lack of attention, excessive speed). These ads are ludicrous.

Aside from our losses, there are many other things that make drug prohibition pointless, futile, and outright dangerous. The drug prohibtion raises crime. Just because something is made illegal does not mean it will end, it just means that those that have nothing to lose will take over the trade. Gangs, much like the mafia of the 1920's, make millions on the drug market. These gangs attain and hold territory through violence, violence in which innocent people are killed. This, in turn, makes the streets unsafe, more so than legalized drugs ever could.

Let's first look at the crime rate prior to, during, and after the alchohal prohibition. In the 1920's alchohal was made illegal in the same way drugs are today. When it was when towns went dry due to its outlawing, outlaws jumped to supply the thirst. Organized crime sprung up everywhere to bootleg and smuggle alchohal as well as setting up places to drink (speakeasy's). During todays prohibition, gangs grow/smuggle drugs and supply them, and, though not nearly as widespread (as distribution is much easier now), there are still speakeasy's, such as opium dens or crackhouses. When prohibition began, the homicide rate went up more than 70%. Since the beginning of the War on Drugs, our homicide rate has risen 200%. When the alchohal prohibiton was ended, crime returned to its previous levels, as the booze business was taken from the mafia. The same would happen today if the drug business was taken from the Bloods and Crips.

Crimes are often committed to support the users habit for these artifically inflated drug prices. Due to the drugs being illegal, and the demand being so high for the limited flow (and danger of sustaining this flow), drug prices are insanely high for even the smallest quantities. For a single marijuana cigarette, the price is up to 10 dollars. That's the price of 2 packs of cigarettes (40 cigarettes I believe), which are widely known for being very expensive. Prices for drugs like cocaine, heroin, and methanphetamines are even higher. To support their habit, users turn to crime such as robbery, auto theft and burglaries. It is estimated that 25% of all auto thefts, 40% of robberies and assaults, and 50% of burglaries and larcenies are attributed to drug users, feeding their desire for a price inflated product.

Half of police resources are spent attempting to stop drug trafficing. These are resources that could be devoted to stopping violent crimes, the crimes that actually plague our lives.

The solution? Legalize all drugs. There are many premises under which this could be done, but my personal favorite is government licensed rights to production. Due to the fact that, if the government was solely responsible for the creation and distribution of drugs, they would still be overly expensive and now watered down, licenses should be granted to pharmicutical and farming companies to grow and distribute the drugs. Not only would this keep drugs like exctasy, cocaine, heroin and meth from being badly made/cut, which causes deaths, it would also allow for a battle of prices between the company, which benefits the consumer. Adding sale of drugs, which would likely big as big a business as alchohal and tobacco combined. Another solution is a tax of 5-10 additional percent (on top of sale tax) to each sale of a narcotic.

What will this do? Crime will be GREATLY reduced. The economy will be boosted with the addition of a huge new industry. Tax revenue to the government will allow more spending on civil services, such as schools and police. So little bad will come from so much good that I am amazed it has yet to happen.

Since the federal government claims to regulate all American commerce, those two sentences are semantically identical. They didn't get Al Capone for murder or bootlegging, they got him for income tax evasion. The federal government is not authorized by the Constitution to make laws outlawing any particular herb, so they use the "commerce" clause to regulate that herb so tightly that there is no way to buy or sell it without violating federal tax laws. Yes, Virginia, the federal government treats marijuana dealing as a tax violation.
