Dinlunar writes:
> I'm interested in knowing what you, the public
> feels. What can I do to become a better all around
> guy?

I'm suspicious that this is more trollery and drama-bait, but I'm willing to take it at face value.

It's encouraging to see anyone ask this question, no matter where they are in life. Although every person is the hero of his own story, I often wonder if someone like Hitler (and I intend zero comparison) ever thought about "what he could do to be a better person." Probably not.

Unfortunately, there's really no Atkins Diet for the soul. The kind of experiences that seem to positively affect a person's empathy, humility, and respect for life are mostly things like going to war, torture, prison, terminal illness, loss of loved ones, near-death experiences, and a lifetime of living.

On a lesser scale, there are certainly some books and films that seem to (at least temporarily) affect some people, such as:
o Schindler's List
o American History X
o The Shawshank Redemption
o Requiem for a Dream
o Dancer in the Dark

From what I can tell, most personal development seminars suck. Although if you can ignore the goofy lingo and self-serving sales pitches, I've seen some people come out of the Landmark Forum with some pretty good insights on life:
http://www.landmarkeducation.com/

To provide a more specific critique of someone who's greatest weakness might be over-sensitivity may be over-ambitious, but I will try.

First, let's look at your strengths:

1. You're intelligent and creative.
2. You're articulate.
3. You understand feelings well enough to know when yours have been hurt and what kind of statements will hurt other people's.

And now some weaknesses:

1. Oversensitivity/Defensiveness. I think one of the most important realizations a person can have is that while you have almost zero control over the behavior of others and the circumstances of your life, you can have absolute control over how you react to them and let them affect you. Events by themselves and life in general have NO intrinsic meaning. All the meaning for every event you will ever experience in your life is generated in your mind, by your mind, for your benefit (or detriment). That means when I write, "You suck," it doesn't mean ANYTHING until you construct a meaning for it. When a person fully grasps and accepts this idea, it allows them to ignore a host of insults, perceived slights, and all the slings and arrows that life may hurl every day. Words are just facts. Insult comes from the meaning you apply to them.

2. Hubris. This is really the most ugly and common failing of intelligent people. What's really dangerous about it is that it erodes and precludes empathy, perhaps the noblest sentiment and best claim we have to any value as individuals or as a species.

3. Impetuousness. You often seem to inspire, fuel, and thrive on flames and emotional drama. But you are hardly alone, as this cannabilistic human glee at being embroiled in conflict and seeing others suffer seems to be driving so many world events and media. But if semantic notions or empathy won't serve to provide perspective a particular issue, sometimes a consideration of greater time scales will. Will the transient and guilty entertainment value or the satisfaction of trying to prove something on an insignificant message-board thread -- really be worth having churned up and perpetuated so much vituperative and unhappiness and increased polarization 5 years down the road? Or 10? Or 20? I hope not.
