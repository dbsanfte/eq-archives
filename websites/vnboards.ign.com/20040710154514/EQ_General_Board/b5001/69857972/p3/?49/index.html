I'll just show you a qoute here that sums up quite well what I mean, originally posted by Scirocco, link to post at the bottom of this.
----------
I think it is a mistake to think of player populations in terms of casual vs. uber. Casual is a misnomer for the bulk of the population.

Casual implies that the player is not serious about his or her gaming. The true casual player makes up only a small percentage of the gaming population. The great majority of players, in my opinion, are in the "serious" category. They care about advancement, about getting better equipment, etc.

When Tiane says, "LoY was a non-event for 90% of the playerbase, and it was aimed right at the casual player," she is perfectly correct. What I think she overlooked is that fact that the casual player makes up only 10% of the playerbase. The remaining 90% of us are serious players.

I divide the serious folks into time-restricted and less-time-restricted. The latter is the smaller of the divisions, and includes all the folks who can log on consistently for substantial periods of time earlier during prime playing hours. It is from this group that the uberguilds form their core.

That leaves the rest of us in the time-restricted category. These players also take the game seriously, and seek all the same goals, but are limited in how frequently and at what times they can play. This could be due to family, children, spouses, jobs, time zones, or a variety of other RL factors. It spans the continuum from the true casual to the full-time uberguilder, and it contains a large number of time-restricted raiders and powergamers.

Earlier, content aimed at the full-time uberguilder also was content aimed at the remaining time-restricted players. The latter couldn't do the very top end material immediately, but had the reasonable expectation of being able to do it at some time in the future. This held true for the first couple of expansions.

The main problem I see is that the top-level raiding requirements gradually grew beyond what those in the time-restricted category could ever hope to meet. SOE responded to the challenge of providing new material for the full-time uberguilder by adding material that took more time to get to (i.e., keys and flagging) and that took more people to complete. This culminated in the PoP 72-person raids. And the indication from GoD is that lowering the numbers requirement for future top-level raids won't work. It doesn't matter if future expansions have raids for 36 raiders, if it requires all 36 to be equipped through earlier raids that required 72 people, for example. This is one reason why LDON raids were unsuccessful.

When you are in the time-restricted group and realize that it's no longer a question of merely delaying when you get to experience the top-level content (which admittedly may not be at the top when you get to it), but that you will never be able to experience it, you begin to question whether it is worthwhile continuing to play the game. Thus, the suggestion that it is the focus on uberguild raiding that has led to the decline of the game is, in many respects, true.

"You don’t need all of your spells. Just like some weapons, armor, and other stuff, some of those spells just may be out of reach for some folks."

It also is why this remark by Absor is so dangerous to SOE. A time-restricted raider or powergamer that perceives that that statement is really true does not remain a player for long.
