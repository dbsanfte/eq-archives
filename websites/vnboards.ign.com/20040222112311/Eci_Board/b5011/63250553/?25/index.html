 		Date Posted: 1/26 12:39pm Subject: RE: What's your worst job ever?
I have to admit that the work itself in this job was the worst ever, the pay was good and so was the scenery.


When I lived back home on the island, my dad ran a mobile marine business. He never had to advertise, people just knew when their boats broke down to come to him to get them fixed. He'd spend all day out in the hot florida sun practically standing upsidedown in some cases fixing any type of engine foriegn, domestic, inboard, outboard, I\O. He even got hired to work on a 40' sailboat once. That was a job alright.

In any case I grew up helping him out here and there, and eventually he let me get my own piece of the action since back then I wanted a new computer not the 100mhz 486 16mb ram brand new thing we had gotten a year before, lol.

So anyway, by word of mouth my dad gave me the opportunity to detail boats as well as clean boat bottoms.

If you go to a marina there's forklifts which can lift a boat up onto a drydock area where anyone can easily get under it and do their job.. but we didn't live in a marina so we had to engineer our own method.

We had davits on our dock, so my dad would lift the boat up on the davits for me, and i'd strap into a wet suit. Florida summers can be particularly hot, though, so i'd only wear a shorty. (short sleeves\shorts instead of pants).

I'd get under the boat wearing a shower cap to keep my hair up dry and clean, gloves so I woudln't get my hands scraped up by barnacles, a lifejacket since the water where I would do this was relatively deep, and flippers, so I could easily navigate against any tidal currents by our house.

So i'd swim underneath a boat in my gear with a gas-powered pressure washer in hand.. and i'd use it to clear off the gunk underneath the boat. Depending on how long a boat has sat in water without being dry docked, there could be up to 2 inches of barnacles, slime, algea, you name it.

As I would knock the barnacles off (Would later have to go and use a scraper by hand to get any ones that were really old\stuck on hard to the boat) there are little creatures, the equivalent to roley poley's that live in the barnacles. They would come out of their burrows as their homes were destroyed, and try to burrow into my skin.

Unfortunately only wearing a shorty suit I'd have to brush them off and constantly swim away from them. They could roll up into a ball but they wouldn't until they found a home.. i refused to let that home be my skin. Sometimes they'd stick onto you and you wouldn't know it and fall in the shower after I finishedt he boat.


Anyway, once the boat was clean I'd get this little raft I made that a metal paint pan would sit on, and get a paint brush and apaint roller. Marine paint is expensive so i'd charge the customers for the cost of the paint. Had to float under the boat looking up and always keep hands above the water so the paint wouldn't get touched by the water. Very tiring seeing as though it could take up to 2 hours to put 2 good coats of paint on the boat. The paint on the bottom of a boat in saltwater has special chemicals to keep away barnacles for short term use, and would get splattered all over my arms when rolling.

If you've ever rolled paint you probably know that most rollers have splatter from it.

The other hardest thing about this was that I'd have to take masking tape and tape off the sides of the boat since the paint line would come up about an inch or two from the bottom of the sides since the boat sits in the water a little ways. I'd have to not get paint above the tape and i'd have to tak the tape off the instant i was done since when masking tape gets heated it leaves nasty stickies behind. (Think of peeling labels off gifts at christmas time)

Now, if the customer wanted i could detail his boat too. This meant taking some form of rubbing compound and going over the entire interior part of the boat, then waxing where i rubbed. I had to use some chemical solution to clean the muck\fish guts\you name it off the floor of the boat, and if it had any wooden fixtures in it, take a teaking solution (spelling?) and renew the wood's look.

I got $10 bucks an hour for the detailing, and umm.. Something like $20 per foot of the size of the boat. That was enough to cover the cost of the paint as well as labor.

The work I did would keep a boat's bottom good up to 5 years, when most need to be redone in a year or two, the local redneck marina guys were really sloppy.

Glad I didn't work in the metal plant though eesh
