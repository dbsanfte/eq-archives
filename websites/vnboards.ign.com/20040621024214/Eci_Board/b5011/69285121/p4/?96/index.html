> OK, then just how stupid is it to expect a limited
> being to understand the motives or methods of an
> unlimited being?

This is the mysticism response that Hume gave to the Problem of Evil. (That's why I wrote that the argument in your previous post was either circular reasoning or mysticism; I couldn't tell which way it was going.)

The problem with this approach is that it pushes God out of the realm of understanding or consideration by human beings. Is it really the loving compassionate Christian God if this being's motives and actions are so far beyond us that they defy comprehension? How can we identify with such a being? Why would we worship a being? In fact, how? (More on this later.)

This position on God also calls into question the credibility and efficacy of the Bible. Why would such a being, who is from our perspective inexplicably indifferent to human misery, attempt to explain himself in a book? If he were omniscient he would know that any such effort is doomed, since his motives will always lie impossibly out of reach of human intellect. Besides, instead of inspiring a highly questionable book, it would be possible for an omnipotent being to simply embed a knowledge or understanding of himself into the minds of sentient creatures.

The bottom-line on this approach is that it plays a shell game with God's benevolence in order to address the Problem of Evil. It asserts that God's benevolence is so imcomprehensible to us that it seems like what we label "cruelty". But once you redefine "benevolence" this far out of its conventional usage, it is a deception to continue using it to describe God.

Some Christians will respond by asserting that Jesus is the bridge to the incomprehensibility of God, but this doesn't solve the Problem of Evil. If we accept that Jesus was human and spoke on behalf of God, why couldn't he explain God's motives coherantly? One of the critical tests of intelligence (and we would imagine likewise of supreme intelligence) is being able to explain yourself to those with less intelligence.

Further, believing in Jesus's promise of an eternal reward doesn't ameliorate suffering on earth by one iota. In fact, in many cases, believing this has been the justification for human actions that have caused more suffering.

> So I ask you, how does this invalidate the Genesis
> Creation story?

It doesn't, and this isn't what I'm asserting. As I've said, the Problem of Evil is tangential to the Evolution versus Creationism debate. My original thought in posting it was that we might obviate the need to defend the side of Judeo-Christian Creationism by realizing that such a being doesn't stand up very well to the Paradox.

Well, at least Zoroastrian-derived religions make a little more logical sense. Since the powers of good and evil are equally balanced, we need not wonder why there is suffering in the world, since the good is not able to fully overcome the evil.

But really I'm not as concerned with the umbrella of evil as a concept as I am with the suffering aspect of it. There is no conceivable reason why an omnipotent being would have had to design suffering into the world, or if he didn't design it, why he couldn't abolish it at any moment.

Or if we prefer, an omnipotent being whose reasons for not eliminating suffering transcend comprehension cannot be called benevolent using our definition of the word, which is the only definition we care about. Aquinas considers perfect benevolence to be a necessary component of perfect goodness, so this calls into question whether such a God is even good.

I think the best avenue for Christians who care about the logical soundness of their beliefs (which doesn't seem a requisite for most) is to capitulate on God's omnipotence. As soon as God steps down from omnipotent to very, very potent (perhaps still far beyond human beings), it is possible for there to be suffering in the world that is beyond his power to stop or that is part of some understandable tradeoff. This solves the Riddle.

Mollusks, unlike plants et al., get the carbon for their shells from ancient carbonate and not the atmosphere. Ancient carbon is already depleted in C-14.
