My name is Eric Malling, I am an attorney. I was, incidently, up until very recently an avid player of Everquest.

My carreer has comprised of 17 years of torts counsel work, as well several cases of corporate IP and branding litigation. I am appalled at your complete ignorance of federal IP statutes, and your misrepresentations of what constitutes "fair use" in this situation.
A few facts:
1. Neither Verant nor Sony in this case have established what in the "Mystere Story" constitutes their IP, and therefore any actions taken by Verant were summary and without merit until the facts can be established.

2. First Amendment law does not apply in this situation because Verant's actions against Mystere do not prevent "Mystere" from continuing to write his/her fiction.

3. Fair use ( Copyright Act of 1976, 17 U.S.C. § 107) of derived ideas from popular cultural icons has long been established...A Steven King novel mentions a psychotic killer quenching his thirst with a coke, or wiping his nose with a kleenex or going to bed and taking 2 aspirin. In each case it is understood that said occurences are ficticious -- the basis of the whole story is ficticious. Only in cases where demonstrable harm in done to the integrity of said IP is it actionable, and in cases such as this the onus is on the owner of the IP to prove actual damage. "The psycho killer drank the fetid coke that everybody knows kills many more people per year than cigarrettes."

Section 107 carves out an exception to the exclusive rights conferred in section 106, permitting members of the public to use copyrighted works for "fair" purposes:


Notwithstanding the provisions of sections 106 and 106A, the fair use of a copyrighted work, including such use by reproduction in copies or phonorecords or by any other means specified by that section, for purposes such as criticism, comment, news reporting, teaching (including multiple copies for classroom use), scholarship, or research, is not an infringement of copyright. In determining whether the use made of a work in any particular case is a fair use the factors to be considered shall include--


(1) the purpose and character of the use, including whether such use is of a commercial nature or is for nonprofit educational purposes;


(2) the nature of the copyrighted work;


(3) the amount and substantiality of the portion used in relation to the copyrighted work as a whole; and


(4) the effect of the use upon the potential market for or value of the copyrighted work.

"The four fair use factors "are to be . . . weighed together, in light of the purposes of copyright," Campbell, 114 S. Ct. at 1171 (citing Leval, supra, at 1110-11), and "the primary objective of copyright is not to reward the labor of authors, but `to promote the Progress of Science and useful Arts.'" Feist Publications, Inc. v. Rural Tel. Serv. Co., 499 U.S. 340, 349 (1991) (quoting U.S. CONST. art. I, § 8, cl. 8). Thus, the "fair use" concept embodied in section 107 may be understood generally to permit a secondary use that "serves the copyright objective of stimulating productive thought and public instruction without excessively diminishing the incentives for creativity." Leval, supra, at 1110"

Mysteres work was interpretive, origianal and based very loosely on fictional experiences Verant itself has purported to occur. Your legal standing is questionable and your reasoning flawed. Please do not insult the intelligence of "us" the consumer.

here is aonther analysis from the SOny Boards...

Here are some excerpts from the current user agreement and my thoughts. My thoughts should not be read as containing legal advice or read as creating an attorney-client relationship between myself and anyone else.

Please keep in mind that when a clause of a contract is ambiguous, the ambiguity is typically interpreted against the drafter of the clause – in this instance SONY.

USER AGREEMENT AND SOFTWARE LICENSE

6. We may terminate this Agreement (including your Software license and your Account) immediately and without notice if you breach this Agreement or repeatedly infringe any third party intellectual property rights . . . or upon gameplay, chat or any player activity whatsoever which is, in our sole discretion, inappropriate and/or in violation of the spirit of the Game as set forth in the player rules of conduct, which are posted at www.everquest.com.

In otherwords, you can be banned for infringing third party intellectual property rights which is not the case here. You can also be banned for engaging in inappropriate gameplay, chat or any player activity. Reasonably interpreted, this means you can be banned for your actions while you are logged on. This may also include posts to the Flame-play boards as well. Mystere posted his story on a third-party site.

7. Subject to the terms of this Agreement, we hereby grant to you a non-exclusive license to use the Software solely in connection with playing the Game via an authorized and fully-paid Account. You may not copy (except to make one necessary back-up copy), distribute, rent, lease, loan, modify or create derivative works, adapt, translate, perform, display, sublicense or transfer the Software. You may not copy any of the written materials accompanying the CD-ROM.

SONY here has stated that you may not create derivative works, etc. of the Software. Mystere did not create a derivative work of the Software. Also by specifically referring to the Software, one could make an argument that Sony authorized to create derivative works or copyrighted material apart from the Software.

8. We and our suppliers shall retain ownership of all intellectual property rights relating to or residing in the CD-ROM, the Software and the Game. The Software is a "commercial item.”

In intellectual property, ownership and use are two separate concepts. This is a standard phrase that insures that SONY retains ownership. There are some copyright cases that state, that upon delivery of disks, the entity in possession of the disk becomes the owner of that copy, which entitles them to make other copies of that copy. Obviously, one can see how that could become a problem for game developers.

9. You may not use our intellectual property rights contained in the Game or the Software to create or provide any other means through which the Game may be played by others, as through server emulators. . . . You may not sell or auction any EverQuest characters, items, coin or copyrighted material.

Sony is stating again that you cannot create or provide other means through which the game can be played and cannot sell or auction copyrighted material. Mystere did not do this either. Furthermore, this paragraph further reinforces the notion that we have a license to create derivative works of the Game as opposed to the Software, as long as it is not sold or auctioned.

11. As part of your Account, you can upload content to our servers in various forms, such as in the selections you make for the Game and in chat rooms and similar user-to-user areas (collectively, your "Content"). Your Content shall not: (a) infringe any third party intellectual property, other proprietary or publicity/privacy rights; (b) violate any law or regulation; (c) be defamatory, obscene, child pornographic or harmful to minors. We may take any action with respect to your Content if we believe it may create liability for us or may cause us to lose (in whole or in part) the services of our ISPs or other suppliers. You hereby grant to us a worldwide, perpetual, irrevocable, royalty-free, sublicenseable (through multiple tiers) right to exercise all intellectual property rights, in any media now known or not currently known, associated with your Content.

This is a very interesting clause. First, SONY acknowledges your ability to upload content to “similar user-to-user areas” – arguably fan-fiction on third party web-pages is covered. Then, SONY grants themselves a license to your content, which further reinforces the notion that you can generate derivative works as long as it is not from the Software. IIRC some derivate works are separately copyrightable. However, they do provide the caveat that your Content shall not be, obscene or child pornographic.

Overall, it would seem that you have a perfect right to generate fan-fiction free and clear of any claim of infringement based on Sony’s intellectual property – unless it relates to the software, it is not sold or auctioned, or provides a method of playing the Game through emulators. Note: Third party intellectual property means intellectual property that is not owned by SONY or Verant.

Thus, it seems to me that Verant’s allegations concerning intellectual property violations is as lame as Gordon’s excuses concerning hybrid defense.

I have not read Mystere’s fan-fiction and therefore cannot express any opinion regarding whether it is obscene, etc. However, obscenity is based on community standards and requires an appeal to the “prurient” interest. The EULA also states that California is the relevant jurisdiction. I expect that California has a fairly high bar for obscenity and I doubt that Mystere was appealing to the prurient interest.
