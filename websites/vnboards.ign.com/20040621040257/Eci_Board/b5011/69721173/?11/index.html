I noticed that the term "free will" came up a couple times during our various sidebars in the Evolution vs. Creationism discussion. So I am curious if anyone who mentioned the topic has thought about it much.

Do you believe that you have Free Will -- that your decisions are uncaused and that at any given moment you are free to take a completely unpredictable action?

Or do you believe in Determinism -- that each event in the universe, your decisions included, is the culmination of a long chain of events that, predictable or not, could not have happened otherwise?

If you believe in Free Will, here are some dilemmas to consider:

1. If you believe in an omniscient God, how could God know every decision you would (and will) make, and yet you still possess Free Will?

2. How can an uncaused decision-making entity (you) interact with a string of causes (the world). One is free from causation, the other seems to be heavily dependent on causation. Another way to state this is: how do you account for phenomena like learning and intoxication if the mind is free from causation? (And if the mind is *not* free from causation, how can it be said to be Free?)

But if you believe in Determinism, you're not out of the woods either:

A. If all actions and events are the results of long strings of causation, how can there be any notion of Responsibility? I.e., Under Determinism, why bother having laws and courts and prisons and award shows? How is it Jimbo's fault that he murdered his dad if events in the universe _could not have happened any other way_?

B. How do you account for your strong feeling of Free Will? When you stop to think about your mind and body, you feel like you could raise your arm or jump off a cliff at any moment without external stimulation. How could anyone have predicted that you might perform any action at any particular moment?

C. How do you account for Quantum Mechanics and the Uncertainty Principle?

As for myself, I predict that most readers will check out after the fourth sentence and reply with "Free Will is obvious d3vvd!"

Is it a wave, or a particle? It's BOTH!

This universe operates on a sort of fuzzy "probability cloud" model. For example, a classic experiment illustrated by Dr. Stephen Hawking in his first popular book, "A Brief History of Time," has a photographic plate enclosed in a box that has two parallel slits in one side. An electron gun is set up to fire one electron at a time at the box. One would expect a certain percentage of electrons to hit the plate through one or the other of the two slits, and the bulk of the electrons to hit the face of the box (missing both slits). When the plate is removed and developed, though, it will exhibit classic "interference patterns" that indicate that each electron was traveling 10% through one slit, 10% through the other slit, and 80% striking the outside of the box.

This has exciting implications for physics, of course. It means that a particle's trajectory is not a given path, but a cloud of probabilities. But the philisophical ramifications are staggering. We've all heard of Schrodinger's Cat, the thought experiment that shows us that unobserved phenomena can be expressed as probability wave functions, and observing the phenomena "collapses" those probability waves and leaves one observable result.

If these quantum-level functions are expressed in higher orders, that means that for every decision point, where multiple outcomes could have occurred, they all did, in the same proportions that probability would dictate. So there is a me that DIDN'T miss that other car and wound up getting in a collision, But we are in different probability spaces and cannot interact with each other of course.

That allows Free Will and Predestination to both be partially correct.
