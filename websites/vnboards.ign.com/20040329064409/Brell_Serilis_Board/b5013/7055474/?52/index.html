Hrm...trying to say this without seeming completely like a troll here...

I would like to start out by pointing out that I have been on the receiving end up the "that was gratuitous" accusations. I'll be honest and up front. Those accusations hurt. I don't know if you are as sensitive as I am (I acknowledge that I'm overly sensitive), so this may not relate to you. I just want you to know these are CONSTRUCTIVE suggestions (I'd hate to go so far as to call it criticism, I'll explain that more later).

The following are MY opinions, and mine only. Any may feel free to add or disagree with this, but any of you flame me for it and you can bugger off.

When I try to write something, I have many "voices." My "narrator's" voice is completely seperate from the characters that are involved (unless the "narrator" happens to be one of the character's involved, i.e. it's written from a first person perspective). As such, I feel it necessary to provide that distinction by having the narrator's voice be rather indifferent to the surrounding story. Confusing? Let me explain through example.

Generally, I feel the "narrator" is the vehicle through which the bulk of the story is told. This voice is the one through which much description is told (assuming a 3rd person perspective). As such, I generally have my narrator's voice be my most eloquent in "speech." What I'm getting at is that for the most part, my description avoids (or tries to avoid) derogatory terms in any way. Meaning the references to "cunt" and "cock".

Think of it this way: When a person reads something, they are generally placing THEMSELVES as the narrator (assuming again the 3rd person perspective). 4 letter words tend to place emphasis in the wrong place if "voiced" by the narrator. The emphasis that I _think_ was trying to be emphasized was the brutal and animalistic ways of the citizens of Paineel. This should have come through by their acts and their speech, but not, imho in the narrator's "voice". Make sense?

Again, this is not criticism, just pointing things out. One other thing that got me personally, but not likely many others, is that most Erudites (heretic or no) are viewed as rather reserved, and, well stuck up. This is one of the reasons (amongst many) they are referred to as "high men." Aren't they supposed to have evolved above ravenously eating what they have sex with like a black widow spider? *shrug* Just struck me as "wrong" is all.

Now, all that crap aside, I want to point out some things nobody else seems to have gone into much.

There is obvious talent here folks. The descriptions, while not entirely comfortable to some of us, are detailed, and do invoke images, however uncomfortable. This, as I mentioned back when I had posted something many didn't like, is the ENTIRE point of writing. To invoke emotion. To make you sympathise or hate one or more of the characters involved. While it may have seemed a bit disjointed, each image can be taken of its own accord and looked at.

I, for one, would like to welcome you to the boards. It takes ENORMOUS courage (some more than others) to post your writing for others to bear witness to. Each of us knows that essentially it is free to be judged, and that can leave the author quite vulnerable. So please, don't be discouraged by any responses you've seen here.

Additionally, I am flattered that you think my opinion is of merit. Thank you, and I look forward to hearing more out of you in the future.

I see no reason to resort to namecalling. We are writers enjoying not only our own process of writing but that of others. We will not all have the same tastes or sensitivities.

I applaud Mystere for her critique. I disagree in that because the topic was itself so crude, I found the use of "cunt" and "cock" appropriate to the subject matter. The tone was "this is corruption at its most corrupt". My 3rd person narrative often wanders into the minds of the characters. I generally opt for the "omnipotent" or "godlike" voice when I write. Thus I may be in one character's head for one part of a story and another character's head for another, but still using 3rd person narrative.

But Mystere's post got me thinking and since I think we can all learn from each other I am adding to this topic.

If we agree that the purpose of writing is to evoke emotion in the reader, then consider, if you will, which is the more successful. I will use film since that is the example this comes from.

A man gets his leg cut off by a train. How would you film it? You see a man on the track, his foot trapped, his leg stuck on the track. You see the train coming.

Graphic: As the wheels roll over the leg, it is crushed, blood and bits of flesh fly everywhere. The train passes on the tracks is the stump of a leg, bone and tissue mass in the deep read and black of a real wound.

Imagined: As the train gets closer, you cut to the man's face to see the panic, sweat beading on his forhead. Cut to the train, angle from the man's POV looking up at a huge iron monster bearing down on him. Cut to the man's face as he screams out while the train whirrs by.

Which evokes the most vivid sense of a man getting his leg cut off by a train?

I believe that the reader's imagination is far superior to mine in drawing it's own conclusions. By setting up a possibility for the reader to imagine what it must actually FEEL like to be that man with his leg cut off, you have drawn the reader into your story.

By depicting it graphically, you hold the reader apart, a 3rd eye looking down on the world. The only way to evoke emotion in the reader when holding him apart from the work as you have done, is through "shock value".

It is this holding us apart from the action and assaulting our senses with crudity that gives your work the label "gratuitous". Can you have the same scene evoking REAL emotions of revulsion not at the author but at the debauchery that is being depicted? If so, then you have a masterpiece for this kind of disgust belongs in the right story. Perhaps as a preface to a story in which another wood elf meets one of these woman. You know what is going to happen to him and you have now set up intense amounts of tension for your story to go forward.

But first, foremost and always, you MUST draw the reader into the story. If you fail to do that, whether XXX rated porn or fairy tail banal, the writing will fail.

And that concludes writing 101 for the day. Class dismissed ;-)

OOC: Please forgive me. I am deeply immersed in "The Fiction Writer's Handbook" and what makes fiction work is probably the foremost thought in my mind these days.
