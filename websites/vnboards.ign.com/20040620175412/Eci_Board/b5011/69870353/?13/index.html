I, Robot was a book with maybe 5 or 6 (I think, its been a long time) stories that presented the ramifications and moral issues associated with life with robots. I don't want to give anything away - if it sounds at all interesting to you, you really should read it. Its relatively short and gives you something to ponder. You'd be amazed at how well Isaac Asimov was able to put himself into the future to come up with these stories.

"I, Robot," by Dr. Isaac Asimov, is a collection of short stories previously published in "Amazing Stories/Analog." They were tied together with narrative passages starring an old woman, I believe her name was Dr. Susan Smith or something like that, reminiscing about her career as she stands on the cusp of retirement.

The stories themselves are varied, but all are consistent with one another, in a sort of "future history" style. The main theme throughout the stories are the relationship between humans and their artificial but intelligent creations, especially focusing on the Three Laws of Robotics and how they affect the natural human tendency to be repulsed by something that acts human, and even roughly looks humanoid, but doesn't breathe, or fidget, or give any body language symbols. The bulk of humanity is seriously creeped out by robots, and they are outlawed on Earth a few stories into the collection, so much of the action after that takes place off Earth and in various locations around the Solar System, where Mankind depends upon robots to perform important tasks in the hostile environments to be found in the Solar System.

Every story has its own unique point to make, and every story stands alone, but all of them fit together into a whole larger picture. "I, Robot" is a powerful and groundbreaking science fiction book, and a must-read for any serious science fiction reader.

The movie is probably "Terminator" meets "Gremlins," with Will Smith being chased down hallways by explosions every third scene, and hostile robot armies poised to take over the Earth with the help of Skynet, which Will Smith feeds after midnight.

Oh, by the way, in case you missed the second half of the 20th century, here are the Three Laws of Robotics by Dr. Asimov:
1. A robot may not harm a human being, or, through inaction, allow a human being to come to harm.
2. A robot must obey all orders given to it by a human being, except where such orders may interfere with the First Law.
3. A robot must preserve its own existence, except where that may interfere with the first two Laws.

Speaking of the "Foundation" series, Dr. Asimov continued them decades after the original set were published, and in "Foundation & Earth," as I recall, R. Daneel Olivaw, who was in Asimov's robot detective novels such as "The Robots of Dawn," creates a "Zeroth Law," which states "A robot may not harm Humankind, or, through inactivity, allow Humankind to come to harm." He had become a sort of de-facto Third Foundation unto himself by the time of the novel, taking upon himself the responsibility to use Hari Seldon's "Psychohistory" science to guide Humankind into a safe, sustainable level of civilization, to avoid future cycles of Empire/Dark-Age/Empire.

Man, Dr. A was awesome! He was a very good friend to Arthur C. Clarke, and the two of them had a funny way of describing each other: Dr. A claimed to be the second-best science fiction writer in the world, and Dr. Clarke claimed to be the second-best non-fiction science writer in the world, with each reserving the top title in these fields to the other.
