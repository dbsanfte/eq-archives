I played Ultima Online for 3 years, then for a couple of months (3 I believe) I beta tested EQ. I went back to UO with a passion because Everquest was so frustrating (the experience losses on death were awful, I literally became a 6th level Druid over 30 times, I finally stopped counting and just deleted it off my computer.)

A few months back I rejoined EQ having been told quite a bit had been changed, including a drop in the penalty for death. So I played, and played for a couple of months, long enough to get my Druid to level 14 (the number that seemed so magical). I realized that in all that time, I may have grouped 3 times, and beyond the few zone /yells to help someone out, interaction with others was the pits. No one responded to /says, even it I /tell'd (if that's a word. hehe someone I was frequently ignored.

I even tried joining a guild, which it isn't easy finding a guild with people at that level. While they were gracious, and a pleasure to talk to (when they were busy with high level things) I rarely if ever saw any of them.

But in all my time in UO, I never once was ever alone, or without someone to talk to. In my opinion, and on this board, probably ONLY my opinion, Ultima Online was far more conducive to Role Playing than Everquest was. Simple things like being able to create items, to participate in the world (albeit perhaps on a much more general level), and have reasons to be in the game besides killing everything that moved had that little something.

There was something, intangible, that Ultima Online had that none of the other MMORPG's has been able to recreate for me yet. I love Everquests variety of races, and the variety of spells are fantastic (I am personally biased towards the shape changing ones. . Asheron's Call has a nice concept for a social structure, and their attention to character animation and "emotes" was fantastic. But with it's limited graphics, single race, and questionable character customization (appearance wise, not "dress" wise), there was something that just.. clicked, in spite of the bugs and player killers. I persisted for 3 years, eventually being part of a wonderful player-run city, and was in the beginning stages of creating a story-based player run government for one of the NPC cities when I finally left.

When I finally did leave it was because of Real Life constraints. And while going back, from time to time still tempts me, I look back and realized UO was part of an era that is now gone. A player run town like the one I was part of is unlikely to ever exist again in games coming out in the future. Things now looked at as problems allowed that city to be built, such as how close buildings can be built, no limit on how many buildings a single player (an now account) can own, etc.

Lessons were learned from UO's Global Urbanization and following games will learn from it and prevent it, and in so doing, without the cooperation of a couple dozen people, starting up one of these player-run towns again will be a very difficult feat.

But I digress from my point. Which is no surprise, that's a bad habit I have. I guess the final point I'm making is this, despite their claims, Everquest was not developed as a game to create a strong desire to roleplay. From the outset they hadn't planned on trade skills because even before beta the Dev Team said that Everquest was to be and Adventure game, not a Sim-Life game.

Horizons takes that idea to the next level. Horizons is created a world with almost no limits. Everything in the game is craftable by the players, a wide variety of races gives players a vast choice in play style. Humans are good for commerce and trade, Orcs for battle, and Demons for the sheer pleasure of murder and chaos.

You cannot compare Horizons to Everquest, nor Ultima Online to Everquest. The simple reason being (to beat a dead horse with a cliche, a double-whammy sin. you can't compare oranges to apples. Each game, while thrown under the generic genre category MMORPG each had different goals in mind when in develop (or while IN development in Horizon's case), as such people will be drawn to each game for their own reasons.

Horizons is just shaping to encourage role playing by it's game mechanics than Everquest was developed to. Hence it has a higher number of RP posts than EQ. (Now I'd be curious to compare UO's number to HZ). On the flip side, the only way fans of the upcoming Horizons CAN role play is on the boards, where with Everquest and UO they can do this in game.

Okay.. I'm done. Sorry to take up your board space.

That's my two cents and a quarter.

Turyleon
