TurnipFarm said:
"At points in time there were some key decisions made in the way humans and animals developed. "

Who made these decisions? You can't have decisions without a decider, and "Nature" isn't a decider.

There were two or more evolutionary paths available. One of those paths was followed. That path might have been followed because it was more advantageous for the organism at that time, it might have been followed because the trait in question was linked to another trait that was more advantageous for the organism at that time, or it might have been followed due to a stroke of good/bad luck. No "decision" was made. Nothing sat down and decided to take path A over path B after weighing the consequences.

A common (often misused) example of evolution is a species of moth in England. For countless generations, the moth was an offwhite color that blended with the local foliage. A few darker moths appeared now and then, but they were rapidly spotted and eaten by birds, so their genetic patterns generally were not passed down.

Along comes the Industrial Revolution, dumping coal soot over the countryside. Suddenly the off-white colored moths are at a severe disadvantage and the darker ones are better able to hide from predators. White moths then became rather rare and darker ones were common.

There was no "decision" made. "Nature" didn't sit down at its breakfast table and say to itself, "Hey, I really should make these moths better able to hide in their new environment."

You're trying to anthropomorphize Nature and use it in the place of God as a being that has desires and goals. It does not. You might as well talk about decisions made by mathematics.
