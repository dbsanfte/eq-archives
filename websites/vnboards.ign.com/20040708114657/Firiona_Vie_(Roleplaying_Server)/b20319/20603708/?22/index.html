I don't know Martty, I think that on FV there are many more "exclusive" guilds than on the other servers.

The term "Websters" by itself is meaningless. Anybody can publish a book and call it "Webster's Dictionary." It doesn't even have to be a dictionary in the first place. I can slap together a collection of Klingon poetry and call it "Webster's Dictionary" if I want. Or just a slab of blank pages. Believe me, I'm not the first to think of this. Some people have already taken advantage of the lack of a clear trademark or copyright, and thrown out some wretched claptrap not worth the paper it's printed on -- but it's graced with the name "Webster's Dictionary," which people take as some grand seal of approval. <<The game is the child of the middle ages.>>

If it were, then it had grown up, and now resembles the Middle Ages as an adult resembles a child.

In real life, technology changes society. The culture and lifestyle of modern England, for example, is different from the culture and lifestyle of Medieval England for a number of reasons ; but a major reason, and arguably THE major reason, is change wrought by technology. It's only logical to assume that the powerful magic which is common in EverQuest would change Norrath every bit as much, if not more, as technology has changed England. (Insert obligatory quote by Arthur C. Clark here.)

Consider the ease at which people communicate and travel in Norrath -- and no, I'm not talking about /guildchat and /tells at the moment. Consider the letters the League of Antonican Bards exchange. A bard in Qeynos gives you a latter that she wants delivered to Freeport. You cast Gate and, voila!, you're there delivering the letter in five minutes, tops, totally in-character. Just think for a moment, how the ability to transmit letters across a continent in minutes can change a world.

How different is today's world from what it was in the Middle Ages because of medical advances alone? Now consider what most hybrid casters can do, let alone a full-fledged cleric. Forget about Resurrection. Cure poison? Cure disease? Cure Blindness? Instant Heals? Now imagine how big the impact on Medieval Europe that would have.

What if the Black Death had never happened?

Food production alone is a preeminent cause of how a society organizes itself. The Feudal system existed largely because it was vital to keep a vast number of serfs tilling huge swaths of land at all times. Now put that same Feudal system in a world where priest classes can cast Abundant Food all day long, and a tenth-level character can use a fishing pole to produce ten meals in a minute -- and every city in the world has a lake, river, or ocean which is never depopulated of fish. What happens to the Feudal system then?

There is no reason why we should expect the society and culture of Norrath to be exactly like the society and culture of Europe during the Middle Ages: quite the opposite, in fact. It's no real stretch of the imagination, therefore, to suppose that a Norrathian guild might operate very differently than a Medieval one.

And before you argue that we ought to use a different term for the organization, then, if it doesn't mean the same thing, consider how the meanings of words change over time. Does "abortion" mean today what it did in 1547? Does "gay" mean now what it did a hundred years ago? Why should the word "guild" be any less mutable?

ps. Apologies if I got my subjunctive wrong in the first sentence.

You know, this all comes back to one of the original threads on this board (the near-mythical "Ding" thread) and the question asked therein: Must you role-play to, or ignore the game's mechanics?

For me, I role-play to the mechanics. When I die, I lament the temporary loss of my body; I request that a cleric summon my spirit back to an old corpse so that I may regain some of the latent energies within the body. I absolutely role-play in guild-chat (for me, that's where much of my role-playing occurs); it's the "psychic bond" that exists between me and my brothers/sisters. [of course, I don't have a guild yet, but when I get in one, you bet I'll be IC in guild chat] I role-play link-deaths. I role-play summoning a GM to get my stuck body out of a wall. I role-play leveling events.

There are limits, of course - I don't try to explain away the little purple names over people's heads or the little white labels above corpses. I don't have an IC explanation for zoning times or accidentally inspecting someone during combat. But all in all, I use a massive steel cable to suspend my disbelief, and it is capable of supporting a huge weight.

I realize that there are a fair number of devoted role-players out there that can't get past the game-mechanics. Some because of a personal unwillingness or inability to do so, and some because it violates the "rules" for their character (someone posted here that their character was an "import" from the Forgotten Realms setting, for instance).

To each their own I reckon.

I don't think that his examples are too far off, or too far out of the realm of possibility.

A monastic order would certainly cooperate with other orders. In the Western world they would all be from the same church, so no conflict there, but a better example would be the East, where warrior-monks for various stripes would be co-opted into the local warlord's army for land, cash, threats or obligation.

Merchant guilds have cooperated across the ages, at least where there was mutual interest. Obviously there would be conflict over the core issues of economics, but the wine-merchants' guild may easily join with the gem-merchants' guild to fund an expedition to rid a road of bandits.

The knightly orders are perhaps the best example. The orders were all church-sponsored organizations with particular charters (in the beginning, anyway). Crusade squadrons would often include knights from the Templar, Hospitalar, Avis, etc. orders gathered together as a single fighting unit. Some orders would not fight along side other orders for any number of reasons, but by and large they would cooperate, occasionally merge and split, and generally act much like EQ guilds do today.

In a Quixotic attempt to clear up martty's obvious confusion about the immortality of Tolkien's Elves:

Middle-Earth is one continent on a planet named Arda. The name of the universe in which Arda is located is Ea.

The Undying Lands, of which Valinor is a part, were once physically located on Arda, west of Middle-Earth across a sea.
Valinor is where Bilbo and Frodo go at the end of the Lord of the Rings.
Numenor was an island west of Middle-Earth, but east of The Undying Lands

Men were forbidden to ever go to The Undying Lands. When Ar-Pharazon the Golden (the last king of Numenor) defied that ban and sent a fleet west, Numenor was utterly destroyed as punishment, and the world was reshaped so that the deed became impossible. The Undying Lands were physically removed from Arda. This is basically a retelling of the Garden of Eden and Atlantis myths.

(Note: by "myth" here, I am not implying that the stories are necessarily untrue. No offense is intended towards Christianity by the label.)

These events, the destruction of Numenor and the removal of The Undying Lands from Arda, occurred near the end of the Second Age. The events of "The Lord of the Rings" took place at the end of the Third Age.

During the Third Age, it is no longer possible to travel from Middle-Earth to The Undying Lands by ordinary means. It is given to Cirdan the Shipwright, however, the ability to craft ships which can make the journey. Call it "magic" if you like. Cirdan is himself an elf, though unlike most other elves.

When Elves are slain in battle (or otherwise unexpectedly die), their ghosts or souls travel to a place called "Mandos." Another name for this place is "The Halls of Waiting." There they are judged by Namo ("The Doomsman of the Valar," "The Keeper of the Slain"). Namo is also sometimes called Mandos, which can cause confusion. (Rather like how "Hades" is used for both the name of the Underworld and the name of its king in Greek mythology). After a time, Namo/Mandos sends the spirits back to Middle-Earth to be re-embodied. As stated previously, re-embodiment is very much like respawning in EverQuest.

A specific example of this re-embodiment occurs to Glorfindel. Glorfindel of Rivendell, the elf who helps Frodo escape to Rivendell in Book I of "The Lord of the Rings" is the re-embodiment of Glorfindel of Gondolin, who was slain battling a Balrog during the First Age.

When Elves tire of Middle-Earth, however, they are permitted to voyage to Valinor on ships created by Cirdan the Shipwright. This is a one-time-only and one-way-only journey. They are not permitted to ever return to Middle-Earth, nor, for that matter, would they want to.

To recapitulate: there are TWO ways in which Elves travel to The Undying Lands. One way is deliberate, occurs only once in an elf's life, and is permanent. The other way always happens unintentionally (through violence), can happen many times to the same elf, and is temporary.

Frodo and Bilbo were given special dispensation to permit them to travel by sea to Valinor. Normally, Hobbits would be forbidden, for they are mortal, just like men. (Hobbits are, in fact, "a special variety of the human race," to use J.R.R.'s own words).

UNDER GLORFINDELS OF GONDOLIN:
"The Problem of the Two Glorfindels

With the possible exception of Tom Bombadil's identity (and - of course - the wingedness or otherwise of Balrogs), there is no more hotly debated topic than the ultimate fate of Glorfindel. Were Glorfindel of Gondolin and Glorfindel of Rivendell the same person?

The only real resource we have to answer this question is in The Peoples of Middle-earth (The History of Middle-earth Vol. 12): XIII Last Writings, Glorfindel. Christopher Tolkien dates the notes he gives here at 1972, the year before his father's death.

These notes clear up one question immediately: at the time of the writing of The Lord of the Rings, Glorfindel of Rivendell was not conceived as the same character as Glorfindel of Gondolin. Tolkien says, 'Its use [i.e. the name 'Glorfindel'] in The Lord of the Rings is one of the cases of the somewhat random use of the names found in the older legends ... which escaped reconsideration in the final published form...'.

Tolkien was far from happy with this state of affairs, however, and it seems that he intended to reconcile the problem by uniting the two strands of the story. In summary, the notes tell us that Glorfindel's spirit returned to the Halls of Waiting, but was after a time re-embodied by the Valar. He then returned to Middle-earth (either in the mid-Second Age, or as a companion of the Istari in the Third). For the full story of his return, refer to The Peoples of Middle-earth.

The question of Glorfindel's identity, then, brings us to a much wider, and highly relevant, question. Can we accept a writer's personal notes, whether written in preparation for a published work, or simply for personal satisfaction, as part of that writer's 'canon'?

The importance of this question is highlighted by the essay entitled The Problem of Ros in the same volume of The History of Middle-earth. This is an extensive disposition on the origins and meaning of the syllable ros in names such as Elros. The details need not concern us here: what is relevant is the fact that, after its composition, Tolkien noticed a detail in the published Lord of the Rings that essentially negated the discussion. He dismissed the body of The Problem of Ros with four words; 'most of this fails'.

But what if he had not noticed this inconvenient fact (that Cair Andros had already been interpreted, and disagreed with his conclusions)? What if he had noticed, but had failed to record the fact? Would The Problem of Ros now be considered part of the 'Tolkienian' canon in the way that many regard the notes on Glorfindel? Questions like this show that we cannot simply take such notes on immediate face value.

Despite this, the Glorfindel notes lead many to see his re-embodiment and return to Middle-earth as 'fact' (and not a few have e-mailed us to remind us of this!) The purpose of this rather lengthy aside, though, is to show that we cannot view these 'events' in such concrete terms. This is the reason that the 'two Glorfindels' have separate entries on this site. This is not because we do not believe that Tolkien saw them as different embodiments of the same character (as we have seen, there are strong indications that he did), but simply because there is no definitive, published, proof of this.
