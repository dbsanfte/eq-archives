Muon,

I had no source for that, it was simply what I had gathered from fast reads this morning (which is why I prefaced it by saying it could be incorrect). With a little research, I find you are correct. Here is a set of formal procedures:

1) A death warrant is signed by a state court judge setting the execution date.

2) A clemency petition is filed with the Texas Board of Pardons and Paroles by the defence on behalf of the prisoner.

3) Members of the Texas Board of Pardons and Paroles review the petition and cast a vote on whether to recommend a commutation, conditional pardon or reprieve. They also decide whether or not a
hearing will be convened on the clemency petition to hear testimony from witnesses.

4) If a majority of the Board votes for a commutation, the Board recommends to the Governor that clemency be granted.

5) The Governor has full discretion to either accept or reject the Board's recommendation on clemency.

6) The Board has no independent power to grant relief, but can only make recommendations to the Governor.
 
Powers of the Governor
**  If the Board votes against clemency, the Governor has no independent power to commute the sentence. The Governor can only commute a death sentence upon the recommendation of the Board of Pardons and Paroles.

**  Under Texas law, the Governor has the power to grant a condemned prisoner one 30-day stay of execution. No recommendation from the Board is necessary for the Governor to take this action. Any further executive reprieves require approval by the majority of the Board, who then make the
recommendation to the Governor.

** The Governor may formally request that the Board consider convening a full clemency hearing to review the petition of a condemned prisoner.
 
Other Sources of a Stay of Execution

**  Aside from the one-time stay that the Governor may grant, both the Board and a court of law can issue a stay of execution at any time and for any duration.


Given the above guidelines, the Governors responsiblities are definitely less than what I had previously gathered. He certainly can grant a 30-day stay, and can then make recommendations. For a full commutation, it looks like the Board must first recommend it, then the Gov. may accept or reject the reccomendation.

In a fast look I have been unable to find out how many (if any) times the Board reccomended comutation and it was rejected.

So it looks like the Governor has a role in the decision-making process, but not always the FINAL role.

The power given to the Governor is to be used with discretion. The Governor was not meant to have the power to circumvent the law as passed by the legislature of the State (in this case, Texas). The Governor is meant to have this power for a last resort to stop the clock while a potential problem is investigated.

Lets look very fast at the way someone gets the death penalty :

1) The LEGISLATURE passes a law, allowing the imposition of the death pentalty for certain crimes;

2) The CRIMINAL violates the law;

3) The POLICE catch the perpetrator;

4) The DISTRICT ATTORNEY charges the perpetrator with violation of the law that has the possibility of death;

5) The JURY is selected (and those who are 100% against or for the death penalty are eliminated*) to hear the facts of the case and a) make a determination of guilt, and b) decide if the defendant is deserving of the ultimate punishment;

6) The JUDGE sentences the convict to the sentence decreed by the jury;

7) The BOARD OF PRISONS executes the convict.

How again does this equate to the GOVERNOR executing the CONVICT?
