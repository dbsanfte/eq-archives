Harmony is for pulling. it's an AE spell.

Lets say there are 3 mobs together and they are all aggro. You target the mob you want to pull out and cast harmony on it.

Since you made an offensive move, it will charge you. The other 2 will be effected by the spell and wont come to help their buddies. Just make sure you pull the 1st out of aggro range so his buddies wont come when the spell wears off.

Rinse and repeat for the others.

Here's a few tips.

After rooting the creature, snare it. This way way when root breaks (and it will, usually at the worst time) the creature is moving slowly. It also prevents the mob from running away quickly and will also stop them once their health is low enough.

Harmony helps when you just want to pull one creature from a group. It is short ranged so you have to get up close to the mobs. Start from a distance and move forward until the 'your target is out of range' message stops. It does have an area of effect. Cast it on one or two mobs near the one you want to pull. Then pull the one you are after. Don't root it in place. Once the harmony wears off, the other mobs agro range returns, and might rush you if the other one is rooted in place.

I would suggest you to do the harmony and then have your ranger/main tank bow pull singles out. Then root, snare, dot, etc.

It's been a while since I've used harmony so I may be off on the exact details. With my druid, (I solo a lot) I pull by harmony on the mobs (except the one I want) then I dot the one I want. Followed by root, snare, dot, dot. Repeat as needed.

Most of what I hunt are wandering or single spawns so I just root, snare, dot, dot, dot then sit and med if needed.

Hope that helps some.

You probably got clarity or breeze - neither of which you will ever be able to cast as a druid.

now, tips for playing:

let the RANGER pull the mob and take the beating.

However, before he pulls, you cast harmony on the mobs (if they are in a group and more than one will come - ie the orc camps in e. commons). Harmony will stupify the monsters so they stand there and do nothing while you plink their friend out (just make sure you pull it far enough away).

I personally would not bother rooting a single mob - especially since you have snare. cast snare when the mob is at 50% or so. Snare prevents the mob from running fast (and from running at all below 20% health when it decides it's time to run).

get a 1HS weapon. your epic weapon is 1hs.

if you are main healer, then keep nukes/dots to a minimum. but, if it's just hte 2 of you, be sure to cast your Shield of (thorns, barbs, etc) on the ranger. much better than a dot IMHO if you are MH.

Druids hardly suffer indoors anymore, the restrictions on spells are stupid, but arn't a huge burden. The only annoyance I have is not being able to use flight of eagles indoors. Partly because of the move increase, but mainly because of the long duration lev.
Hell even in close quarters a druid doesn't suffer much, just tank the mob and let your DS do some damage well you nuke it too. Just remember to stick yourself in a corner, as the major cause of spell interuption is not damage, but the movement caused by being hit.

And yes, I'm glad the druid epic is 1hs, hehe it was accutaly one of my few points of roleplaying I did in EQ before there even was an epic. I refused to use the starting 1hb, and used only 1hs, even just messing around, until into my 50s, and I still have never used a blunt weapon seriously, but I'm pretty much forced to if I want a nice proccing weapon. As the only good ones are Hammer of Hours and Sharkspine Club.

As for harmony, it's on the Lull line of things. You target a mob and cast it, and it reduces their reaction radius and their frenzy radius to pretty much nil for 7 ticks ( a tick is 6 seconds ). Frenzy radius is when you get low on hp, a mob will switch to you and not want to taunt off until you get your hp back up. Reaction radius is just the obvious, where it sees you/what you do, like pulling one of it's buddies standing next to it. Harmony is an outdoor only spell, and some mobs can be immune to. It's one of the few outdoor only spells that kinda sucks not being able to use indoors.

Your role in a group can be quite varied, it really depends on what your group needs, and what you feel like doing. Some druids are more of a healer role, some are more of a nuker, and some are like me and do everything at once. If your group has no other healer, you obviously need to be doing some healing, at least until they can find another healer if you don't really want to be doing it. But if your group is fine on healing, or you're fine on mana well healing them, feel free to fire off a few nukes. Also snaring is an important part of being in a group as mobs will tend to run and end up agroing their friends, and in a dungeon this can cause a HUGE train. If you're husband is with you all of the time, it's better for him to take over the snaring duty, but it's still something you should remember to do if needed. Into your later levels you can supliment your groups damage by adding a charmed pet into the mix for a nice boost of damage, depending on the available of charmable animals.

As for stats, a druids main stat in the low levels is a mix of hp and mana/wis, probably with more emphasis on wis/mana if you're just leveling up yourself for the first time, as you are. As you get into higher levels you start to focus less on wisdom, and your mana pool, as it will naturally be at an ok level, and what you're more concenred with is your Hit Points and you're Mana Regeneration, which can be obtained with Mental Clarity Alternate Advacement, or with items with the Flowing Thought effect on them. There is a maximum cap of constant worn mana regen (or commonly called the FT cap) of 15 points(15/tic). This includes effects like Composure which is combined 3 hp/mana regen,and any other effect that is constant and worn (not clicked or cast on you). The Mental Clarity AA will stack with this however.

Seriously, on stats, I could care less what my wisdom is, for a long time it sat around 240-250 unbuffed, the only thing your wisdom or + mana effects is your mana pool. So if you are dumping all of your mana fast, it's effective to have a deep mana pool (see wizards), but usually in a group setting you're slowly using down your mana, unless something goes wrong. Not dumping half your mana per pull. In this case, your mana regeneration rate REALLY comes into play. Gaining 4 FT has a huge effect on my constant mana level well duoing for experience, where as gaining +300 mana or 20 wisdom would hardly mean anything. Though I'm starting to obtain the gear where I get the best of all worlds. 100 or more hp/mana, flowing thought, good resists, and 10-20 in all stats. I don't have much of that yet though, and even when I was just in bazaar bought or self obtained gear, wisdom/mana still meant little. In the end though, it comes down to resonable balance. Losing that 300 mana for 10 hp wouldn't be a good trade off. Or losing that +20 wisdom for +5 sta, or +20 hp. Those are extreme examples, but it holds true, and you have to find a personal balance in there too, as every player is different, and druids especially with their wide and varied roles can be quite different from one to the next.

Well everyone has covered it quite well, as you'll soon learn pretty much everyone has a druid alt. They're just fun to play.

A couple of points to keep in mind:

1) Know the difference between root and snare. As a druid you will almost always use snare, and if you group with someone with root (wiz for example), you want to make sure they know the difference.

Snare will basically allow you to use it like a root, because you can walk backwards faster than most mobs move snared. This becomes more so when you get SoW. Snare is better for several reasons. It generally lasts much longer, and root can be broken if a large damage shot hits the mob. When a mob turns to run (usually at 10-20% hps, depending on mob), if snared they will turn away, unable to move. If rooted, they will continue to fight until dead or the root is broken.

2) While you are still relatively young, make sure you max ALL your spellcasting skills. If you wait until the late 20s or beyond, it becomes a tedious chore to get your lesser used skills (divination) up. A low divination skill for example, will mean your camoflage spell won't be reliable, and will drop right as you are sneaking past that deep red mob.

You're probably wondering what the spell "True North" is for. Since they gave all characters an on-screen compass, it would seem a pointless spell. It is a very low mana cost, quick casting divination spell. Memorize it, put that spell slot on a hot key, and during some downtime spam it. It will raise your divination skill fairly quick.

If you stay on top of ALL of your casting skills, you only have to raise each 5 points per level, which can be done quickly and easily.

3) Once you get sow, learn how to kite when solo. You can do it with snare and no sow, but it is much easier with both. Druids aren't very popular in groups at later levels, so you will be doing some solo.

You do have to use a skill to raise it, although you could train in it, it's expensive past the early levels.

Let me explain a little better what I meant above. Taking Divination again as an example, this applies to other spell skills you won't use as often, like conjuration.

In your normal playing, you won't likely use divination more than once or twice an hour. It covers spells like invisible (camoflage for druids) and see invisible.

My first caster character was a wizard, and I wasn't aware of this issue. I almost never used divination spells, and so at level 20 my divination skill was like 10 (max at 20 would be 105).

At this point I needed to use invisibility to get to certain areas, and to go AFK in unsafe areas with less risk. With a DIV skill of 10, my invisibility would sometimes begin to fade just seconds after casting it. When it held longer, it would always fall at the worst time.

Someone then told me to use the TRUE NORTH spell to increase my DIV skill. I spent about 4-5 hours over the next few sessions casting true north until out of mana, sitting till full mana, and repeating over and over. This is enough to make you want to cry.

As a druid you will also face this with conjuration, the skill that governs spells that create (conjures) things. In normal play only your DOT spells will be raising this, and since you will probably only cast 1-2 dots per combat at lower levels, it won't max itself. At higher levels when your DOTs become a big part of your offense, you don't want them to fizzle or get resisted often.

To raise up conjuration, use SUMMON FOOD/WATER in place of TRUE NORTH. It is a fairly low mana conjuration spell that you can use to make sure your skill is maxed.

Combat skills are tougher, especially once you reach the 20s and become 90% caster. You need to make sure you wade into combat every now and then (helping finish off nearly dead mobs) to keep those skills up. If you don't, you could find yourself level 30 with a defense of ~100, when your max would be 155.

Even as a cloth caster, maxed defense CAN keep you alive when a mob aggroes on you.

A druid's life changes in several stages:

From 1 to 13 it's stage 1, where you can melee, cast heal, and nuke, and cast a few buffs like skin of wood. This gets you into the basics of the game.

Stage 2: From 14 on you get to dot and sow (spirit of the wolf). This is when you get a first inkling of what being a druid is about. Dot is an important part of a druid's life, so is sow. Some druids start kiting at this stage. If your regular partner is a ranger, the two of you should practice kiting as soon as possible because in the long run it'll be your most powerful hunting tactic as a duo.

Fear kiting (both ranger and druid get to fear animals)with the ranger shooting arrows and the druid nuking or dotting an animal that is running in fear can be good, since neither of you will be able to tank real well around level 30 or so.

Fear pathing can be weird at times because the game controls the path of a feared mob a lot. You can also do aggro kiting so you can control the pathing better. (With aggro kiting, you get the mob mad at you, and it will follow you, so you get to control where the mob goes.) This means you snare the mob and sow yourself so you can out run the mob and, with the mob chasing you, you and ranger shoot, nuke, dot it.

For your druid/ranger duo, kiting is a good way to go. If you two plan to join others in a full group of 6 with a tank and cleric, then your role as a druid can be patch healing (if the cleric is too busy), dottting and nuking to help dish out damage, snaring to keep the mob from running away (a runaway mob can bring a train of mobs onto the group), damage shield the main tank, casting sow on the group so everyone can make a quick escape, and evacuating the whole group in a dire situation. (Make sure you get the evacuation spell at level 19. And always keep enough mana so you can cast evac when the cleric shouts OOM and the situation looks hopeless for your group).

Stage 3: From level 51 or so, kiting will still work for your duo. But your druid's healing spells are on the weak side, and you may find it difficult to get into a group. At 58 you will get a psudo complete heal, and groups will want you again. After that most often you'll be invited into a group to provide healing when they can not find a cleric. Hehe. Or if they really like you, they may invite you to nuke in a LDoN adventure. Sadly, druids have a hard time finding groups at higher levels. Most groups hire them as a last resort when a cleric is nowhere to be found.

Many people choose to be druids because they can solo well with quad kiting--rounding up 4 mobs, running them into a tight pack and aggro kite them with area effect nukes. But after hours of kiting it can become very boring and lonely. A soloing druid can also "root rot" a mob by rooting the mob and dotting it. Root rotting won't work well with your ranger husband because a rooted mob will reduce his bow damage. And root timing can be very boring as you sit and watch the mob slowly dwindle in health.

You can also charm hunt by charming an animal. It can be risky, but your ranger husband can help a lot in this case by helping you out when the animal breaks charm. This can be fun and exciting.

Your ranger husband will have a hard time mid levels. But once he gets true shot, endless quivers, etc. he'll be a highly desired class by most groups due to his high damage output. You can be desired as a healer in the end by most groups if they don't have a cleric. (This is stage 4: post 60's.)

Hope this is some help. Good luck and have fun!

[EDIT: oops forgot--Re: training up your spell skills, you can cast your lowest mana costing offensive spells at your husband while he is afk. You'll get a message saying he's protected from your spell, but your skill will go up. It really doesn't take too long to get skilled up this way, and it will save your training points for later use on other stuff.)

Short harmony guide from my experience.

First, you will never know if harmony worked unless you are close enough to actually see it work since there is no message. IE, if a the mob gets a spell graphic around it on your cast it affected the mob, if it didn't get the graphic, it didn't work.

Second, a lot of times if you are close enough to see it work the mobs will agro you anyway, so just cast it 2 or 3 times from a distance to be sure. Once you get past the mid teens this will take almost no mana, so it's better to be safe than sorry.

Third, Harmony drastically lower the agro radius of a mob, but it does not turn it off. In most cases this won't matter a bit because it lowers it so much that 95 out of 100 times nothing but the mob you pull will come, but try not to pull directly over the top of a harmonied mob.

Here's how I usually work a harmony situation whether grouped or solo.

Lets say there are 4 mobs at a camp, first you move in to casting range (find this by walking foward while spamming the harmony button until it starts to cast instead of the out of range message). Harmony the mob farthest from you first and then one or two others just to be safe. Then target the one you want to pull, turn around and cast whatever you want to pull it. I usually never snare right off because with sow I have time to do it later, and a snared mob has much more chance of agroing other mobs as one that's moving through an area quickly.

Once the camp is broken up you won't need to harmony very often, only when you break your rythem and more than one spawn at camp.
