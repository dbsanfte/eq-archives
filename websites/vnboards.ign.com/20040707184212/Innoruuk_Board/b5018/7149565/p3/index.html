I will leave the moral debate to others. Because that issue, and the issues of the rights of Verant Interactive and the person playing Mystere are two completely seperate issues.

First, copyright law. Verant claims that any name and other material transmitted over the Everquest servers are derivitive works of Everquest and therefore, they own the intelllectual property and copyright.

However, in order to retain a copyright, you must actively pursue all people who infringe upon that copyright. Assuming Mystere's story was in violation of that copyright (more on that below), that would also imply that ALL publicly distributed stories using character names and other EQ references are also in violation. Unless Verant actively pursues all of those violations, they lose the copyright. They cannot selectively pursue violations based on moral standards that they set.

There is also a good argument for her story not being in violation of copyright at all. For example, the phrase "Coca Cola" is trademarked and copyrighted by Coca Cola. If I were to write a book, and within that book say "Bob drank a bottle of Coca Cola.", that does not make my book the intellectual property of Coca Cola. This is due to a term known as "fair use". In fact, I can write a book on the history of the company, using that reference many times, and that work will still fall under the umbrella of "fair use", and the intellectual property of that book belongs to me and me alone.

Finally, the EULA is not the word of g-d, its legality is bound by the laws of the United States and the State of California. Do not be fooled into thinking because they basically say they can do whatever they want, that they actually can.

Verant's actions on this matter clearly violated Mystere's civil rights and they cleared breached the contract they made with that person.

This post, despite containing multiple references to Coca Cola and Verant Interactive, is not the intellectual property of either one.

10 Big Myths about copyright. Relevant info:

4) "My posting was just fair use!"

See other notes on fair use for a detailed answer, but bear
the following in mind:

The "fair use" exemption to copyright law was created to allow
things such as commentary, parody, news reporting, research and
education about copyrighted works without the permission of the
author. Intent, and damage to the commercial value of the
work are important considerations. Are you reproducing an
article from the New York Times because you needed to in order
to criticise the quality of the New York Times, or because you
couldn't find time to write your own story, or didn't want your
readers to have to pay to log onto the online services with the
story or buy a copy of the paper? The former is probably fair
use, the latter probably aren't.

Fair use is almost always a short excerpt and almost always
attributed. (One should not use more of the work than is
necessary to make the commentary.) It should not harm the
commercial value of the work (which is another reason why
reproduction of the entire work is generally forbidden.)

Note that most inclusion of text in Usenet followups is for
commentary and reply, and it doesn't damage the commercial
value of the original posting (if it has any) and as such it
is fair use. Fair use isn't an exact doctrine, either. The
court decides if the right to comment overrides the copyright
on an indidvidual basis in each case. There have been cases
that go beyond the bounds of what I say above, but in general
they don't apply to the typical net misclaim of fair use.
It's a risky defence to attempt.

Here, I think Verant would argue that this story contains "damage to the commercial value" of Everquest.

And, with regard to selective prosecution:

5) "If you don't defend your copyright you lose it."

False. Copyright is effectively never lost these days, unless
explicitly given away. You may be thinking of trade marks, which
can be weakened or lost if not defended.

But, having taken your "you know just enough to be dangerous" comment to heart, I decided to do a little more research on fair use.

There are 4 primary factors for fair use:

1) Nature of the use

Mystere's use was personal and non-profit. Both these points are in favor of falling under fair use

2) Nature of the work in question

Everquest is a published work, which favors Mystere.

3) How much of the work is being used

This factor tips the scale VERY heavily in Mystere's favor, since she is only making references to things in Everquest, not copying text from the game.

4) If this kind of use were widespread, what kind of effect would it have on the original

This again tips very heavily in favor of Mystere, because her work neither competes with Verant's, nor does it take away from any royalties from Verant may receive from people who licenses the use of Everquest text for their own purposes.

So to answer your final question, I don't think Verant has any legal ground to stand on. The court system may or may not agree, I think they would, but I can make a very strong case for it. And if you were a decent attorney, so could you.
