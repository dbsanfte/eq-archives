IMHO, Role-playing (whether in EQ or any other game, either computer or table-top) means that a person creates a character (ie. a simulated person, with a history, goals, motivations, likes and dislikes), and plays that character out as if he/she were that character.

Does that mean you have to speak in old-english? Well, if your character concept says so then yes, but it's up to you. Do you have to discriminate against opposing races/classes? Again, if your character concept says that your character doesn't like opposing races/classes, then I say yes, but it's up to you and your character concept.

Out of my two main characters, Kylldoran (on Tarew Marr) is a half-elf ranger who's out for adventure... to see new sights and experience new things. He's not concerned with the sqabblings between races and classes and is willing to help out anyone, regardless of race/class.

Yria (my character on Morell-Thule) is a dark elven necromancer who's only goal is to become the supreme being in her universe, through almost any means possible. She respects those above her in positions above her, but she knows that such positions are temporary and she will be greater than all of them some day. She tolerates everyone else of her race/class as a necessary evil, and regards anyone of lower stature than her in disdain.

Neither of these characters speak in old english, and only one pays any attention to race/class conflicts. Neither of them (nor my other, minor characters) will kill-steal or farm items, and they all have legitimate fantasy names (ie. not k3w1d00ds).

And *that* IMHO, is what role-playing is all about.

A large part of roleplaying, in my opinion, involves background. Who are you, and where have you come from? Too many people, it seems, play their characters as though they sprang into being whole cloth the first time the player clicked "Enter Norrath." And although technically they did, it makes it easier to have an idea of how your character would react in certain situations if you have developed a background for them.

For example, my human paladin, Greyjoy. He used to be an idealistic young soldier named Thomas Grey. He fought in a lot of bloody wars as a young man, and slowly came to lose his faith and belief in anything good and noble. The last straw was when he lost an eye in battle (I chose the blond, bearded face with the eyepatch.) He was dismissed from the military--the only life he had ever known--and cursed his bitter luck. He became a soldier for hire, taking any job, with no concern any longer for the morality of the fights. He lost his faith. He was a shell.

Then, after years of this, something changed. He fell in love. She redeemed him, she gave him back his soul. He gave up soldiering, sheathed his blade for what he thought would be the last time, and settled in to a quiet, happy life as a farmer and blacksmith in the plains of Karana. He and his wife had two daughters, and they were the light of his existence. He had something to believe in again. Then, a number of years later, the unthinkable happened. In the dark of night, foul undead beings stole into his home, and attacked his family. He fought back fiercely, but before he could ever even reach his long-sheathed sword, his wife and daughters were dead. He did manage to drive the undead off, but barely, and only barely survived, himself, though he wished he hadn't.

He sat silent and alone in his home, day and night, for many days. He didn't eat, he didn't sleep...he just sat there, slowly wasting away, his still-sheathed sword across his knees, reminding him of how he had been helpless to save those he loved.

Then, one week from the night of their deaths, as thunder and lightning rolled through the dark skies, his wife and daughters rose from their graves and came to claim him. He was fully aware of what was happeneing, and right up until the last moment, was about to let them take him, for he had no desire to go on. But in that last instant before their cold hands took his life, he realized that the souls of those he loved were trapped in this nightmare existance of living death. If he let them take him, they would simply have to suffer on like this, their souls unable to rest. He had failed them in life, but he could not fail them in death, and so he drew at last his long-neglected sword, and sent their bodies back to their graves, and their souls to their rightful peace.

Covered in their unliving blood, tears streaming from his eye, an animal whine of agony and despair tearing from his throught, Thoms Grey took himself out into his fields to die. He fell to his knees in the fallow fields, and raised his own blade to his throat. At that moment, the clouds at last opened up, and the rain poured down upon him. And somewhere in that rainfall, a miracle occurred. He was washed clean. As the rain sluiced the blood from his form, and ran down his parched throat, he felt his old self carried away like the dead, dry earth that is washed away to reveal the rich new soil beneath. He felt the Rainkeeper's touch upon his soul, and just as the grasses of the plains which die in winter are reborn under the Rainkeeper's touch in spring, so did Thomas Grey die. All the joy in his life had gone as grey as the sky during a storm, but he had new purpose. Thomas Grey--soldier, mercenary, farmer, blacksmith, husband, father--was dead. But Greyjoy--the chosen of Karana, who brings rest and peace to all souls who suffer the curse his loved ones did, who washes the land clean of the foulness of undeath, just as the Rainkeeper eventually washes all foulness away--lives. He is the cleansing storm, whose blade is the lightning, whose battle cry is the thunder. But he is also the nurturing rains, who heals and comforts. He is the grey sky of the storm, who's passage fortells the rainbow which will come after.

All of this makes me feel as though I know Greyjoy intimately. I know how he will react in any situation. He is always kind, always helping and nurturing to those in need. But he never appears happy, never smiles. He carries a greyness of melancholy with him everywhere, and yet is generally loved by most who meet him. He speaks fondly and reverently of the Rainkeeper always, spreading his teachings and blessings whenever he can, always speaking of the Rainkeeper's ability to wash anyone clean, and give them a fresh start. And he never lets the undead pass by him untouched. He will not foolishly attack those he knows can easily destroy him, for he knows that if he dies he is of no use to the Rainkeeper. It is not his purpose to die needlessly. But if he believes he has the capability, he will send any undead to their rightful rest, whispering "mercy on your soul" as they fall.

That's what roleplaying is about, to me. Know who you are, and you will know what you would do in just about any situation.
