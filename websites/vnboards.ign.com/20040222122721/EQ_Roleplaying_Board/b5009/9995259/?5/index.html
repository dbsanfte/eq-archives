Just put yourself in the characters shoes. Think like they would in certain situations. If you want to create a history of your character, do it. My half elf druid was orphaned at birth upon the death of her mother. Her father, a human enchanter, was killed by a cyclops in West Karana and her mother, a wood elf druid died of a broken heart, as her parents were deeply in love. My druid is now married to a human Paladin and has two children now. They have a son and a daughter, the son being a "mule" my partner created to hold things, the daughter I play regularly, and even now, has a life going of her own, as she is soulbound to an Ogre.

You can still "powerlevel". Think of it in terms as "overacheivement" and the burning desire to advance in your studies and be the best and over take your enemies, and outshine your peers, and reap the rewards of your plundered loot.

I hope that helped somewhat.

Hey! I am a roleplayer, though I don't pretend to be very good at it. I used to find that I would "break character" alot because his personality was really different from mine, so here are a few ideas I thought of. (Use them if you want)
-If you are new to RPing, pick a character that is similar to your own personality, as to avoid your "Evil Mage" having mercy when about to kill an NPC child (I often found that one of my first characters (who was supposed to be evil) was more like a priest than a drow.
-I find that writing a story about the chracter helps. Just sit down and write a short tale without thinking. Throw stuff at the character in the story and see what comes to your head for the character's reaction.
-If you have ever acted on stage in front of a live audience, that is roleplaying to the extreme. If you make a mistake on stage, you have to deal with it in character. RPing, I find, has a lot more to do with "spur of the moment imagination" that anything. You cannot plan what will happen, so remaining in character while trying to play out the situation can be fun, but also hard at times.
-I think it's mostly just practice, try RPing on a message board, and see how other RPers react with you. And then as you do it more, your character can build a personality with the help of the people responding to him on the message board and then you can transfer the personality from the message board to the game.

Anyway, enough of my ramblings. Hope that helps!
-Have Fun!

Writing out a history helps a lot but if you are not inclined to do so you should at least take a moment to think out a sketchy history. Think about the town they grew up in and their religion a bit. The enviornment your character was raised in shapes the way they think and act/react in a given situation and understanding that environment helps you to slip into their mindset even if you have not given them a specific or detailed history.

(Someone raised in the back streets of Freeport is probably not really going to automatically give their old equipment away no matter how good/noble they are. Good and Noble standards in back alleys of the human port-town might be to sell really cheap.)

I always tended to look at starting a character as their coming of age time. The leave their childhood home and choose their vocation. After that, I would keep a journal for them (a txt document on the computer. I would write in it just after playing). While still in their frame of mind I would jot down journal entries describing what happened in the game, embellishing a bit where it seemed appropriate, referring to NPCs in the same manner I might refer to PCs.
