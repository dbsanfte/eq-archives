I often feel Alikat is like the last Legionnaire trying to defend Rome from the Barbarian invasion, but in this case I think the Barbarians have a point.

She writes:
> It's not the intent, it's the method used.

In an ideal language, each word would be phonetically and denotively unique, cognatively (not cognitively!) intuitive, and completely precise (i.e. the entire dictionary meaning of each word would be encapsulated into its construction).

However, two seconds thought reveals such a language would be untenable, since each word would have to be long enough to convey its entire definition. Practically, we need a toolkit of slightly imprecise (but shorter) morphemes (as placeholders for longer and more precise definitions) that can be shaped and aggregated on an ad-hoc basis to serve dynamic linguistic needs from technical advancement to humor to cultural development to advertising to rap.

What I'm getting at is -- it would be nice if each word for an action (or in this case an actor/role) could uniquely convey the intention, methods, and results of it. But it's probably impossible and as things stand there are no cut-and-dried rules for naming things, societal roles included. For example, as Alikat has pointed out:
a bomber - bombs (the name conviently identifies his intentions, method, actions, and result)
a fisherman - fishes (likewise)

And there are many other good examples, such as:
a policeman - polices
a stuntman - performs stunts

But then there are other societal role names that have nothing to do with method:

a nobleman - has noble lineage. It used to mean he performed a rulership action, but I wouldn't say even then that it identified him by a particular _method_ of action.

a sociopath - is antisocial. This label has to do with behavior, but more a particular mental model for behavior which gets to intention, not method.

Personally, I'm not so worried about capturing the intentions behind something in its label as I am (particularly for violent actions) in accurately capturing the scope of the result.

A "bomber" (by implied result scope) could be bombing anything: satellite dishes, buildings, himself, or filled school buses. A "suicide bomber" could also be bombing anything, the only difference is we now infer that he took himself out with whatever he bombed. While this is a technically accurate label for the kind of terrorist attacks we're discussing, it feels inadequate (to some of us) for fully conveying the suffering and damage that the conventional "suicide bomber" has caused (the full scope of the result).

I guess what we're asking for is some term, "xyz bomber" for this new kind of bomber that implies he or she has killed unarmed civilians and him or herself as well. I don't see this desire for greater precision as a form of PC language pollution.

The reason that Fox News has changed ALL references to suicide-bombers into homicide-bombers is very simple.

And also, very ethical.

See, when you talk about suicide-bomber what that does is focus the debate or topic upon the bomber. Their cause or their reasons for doing the bombing. It ignores the fact that they just commited a heinous crime, and instead puts the focus of the discussion, on what 'we' or 'society' did wrong to push this quiet unassuming individual to commit such a sad and sorry act where he just felt that he couldn't go on anymore and had to take his own life.

Now when you talk about a homicide-bomber instead the focus is that this person HAS committed a crime, and that his VICTIMS are victims and not just bystanders to some sad act of suicide. That the bomber DOES not have a right or a reason to do what he did. And that it is NOT society's nor OUR fault that this occured.

that is the difference between a suicide-bomber and a homicide-bomber. One accordingly commits a heinous crime that is not justified, the other is a victim of society that has done them wrong...
