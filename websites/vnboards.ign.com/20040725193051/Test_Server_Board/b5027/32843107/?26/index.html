Weiss and Hickman's Seventh Gate series, or whatever the hey the name is for the set of books. Death Gate Cycle? Oo, that sounds right.

Haven't read a lot of fantasy I'd recommend, unfortunately; guess it's not really my thing to get into.

Eldridge Cleaver's 'Soul on Ice' is pretty interesting reading, as is John Howard Griffin's 'Black Like Me'. I'd also recommend Ralph Ellison's 'Invisible Man' and Richard Wright's 'Black Boy/American Hunger' (they were written separately, divided by a couple decades; just make sure you get the un'edited' 'Black Boy').

'Smilla's Sense of Snow' was easily the best thing I've read in a while. Heard the movie didn't turn out so well.

I like the not-so-well-followed Alfred Bester in sci fi, probably better termed as 'science fantasy'. Just make sure you start with the short stories or 'More Than Human' or 'The Computer Connection'. Anything but 'Golem1000' . That one's hard to like.

Asimov luvs you! Well worth reading anything of his you can lay hands on; he's just quality goods.

Hemingway's 'Farewell to Arms'

Elie Wiesel's 'Night'

Ken Kesey's 'One Flew Over the Cuckoo's Nest'

Joseph Heller's 'Catch-22'

hmm...

Alexander Solzhenitsyn's 'Day in the Life of Ivan Denisovich' (but I _like_ the Russian 'great book' authors...)

Dostoevsky's 'The Brothers Karamazov', ehh, but I was glad I read it. When I was done, at least. I hear 'Crime and Punishment' is pretty good.

George Alec Effinger's 'Marid' books aren't too bad.

'Memoirs of an Invisible Man' is fun, if not terribly good as a movie with Chevy Chase.

Robert C. Parker's 'Spenser' novels are mostly pretty good.

I went through Mickey Spillane's Mike Hammer books just to see what it was like, and was pleasantly surprised by how readable they were.

Ian Fleming's James Bond novels are neat to read, if you've seen the movies and cinematization they went through.

H.G. Wells' 'Island of Dr. Moreau', very good, despite what Kilmer and Brando made of it. 'Invisible Man' also good.

Tom Stoppard's 'Rosencrantz and Guildenstern are Dead', mwah! Good. Also a good movie, even if it wraps up kind of bleh.

Nathaniel Hawthorne's 'Rappacini's Daughter', a little mucky to trudge through, but he's just an interesting builder.


Ehh. Didn't mean to make this a laundry list of stuff, but oh well. That lead stuff (down to Catch-22) is all stuff I'd recommend reading under any circumstances. I guess the rest is just kind of a walking tour of things I've liked.
