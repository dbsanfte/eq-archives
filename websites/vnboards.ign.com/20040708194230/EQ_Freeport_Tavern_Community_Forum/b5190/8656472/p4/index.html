In regards to the issue at hand, the bottom line is this: from a legal perspective, the action that Verant has taken against eBay auctions is illegal. This is plain to see; there is no room for interpretation in this case, and I will explain why.

First of all, there is the question of the legality and validity of the EULA. As someone mentioned in an earlier post, restrictions are placed on these types of agreement, which vary slightly from state to state. In some states, they are prohibited by law. In the great majority of the other states, severe limitations are placed on these agreements.

For a moment, let us assume that in your particular state, there are no restrictions on the EULA. This is quite likely *not* to be the case, but let us assume this for the moment. The EULA states that no "selling" of "items" from the game will be allowed. This does *not* apply to the selling of a person's "time".

If, in your state, the applicable terms of the EULA are legal, using eBay to conduct these transactions would *still* be legal, *IF* the person doing the auctioning states within it that they are selling their "time". No laws exist on the selling of a person's "time". If, in your auction, you state that the bidders are bidding on merely your "time" to acquire an item, then you are technically not selling the item itself, but the time that it took you to acquire said item.

Within the game, trading of items is perfectly allowed. No selling of the physical item is taking place, and the intellectual property still belongs to Verant even though it is now possessed by a different player. Using eBay to sell your "time" to acquire items, and then simply providing the item in question to the winning bidder, is *not* a sale of the *intellectual property*...it is still owned by Verant. It is a sale of the seller's "time".

This is a legal viewpoint on the subject, and when this matter goes before a court, these will undoubtedly be the findings. I do not use eBay for Everquest-related auctions and so forth, but I find the issue very interesting from a legal standpoint, and wanted to share some thoughts on this, relative to the law.

I think the whole debate is moot. When you look at the argument (selling my time, and giving the account away for free), if this ever got to court, I'm sure nobody would care if someone wants to give you money for time you spend playing a game. However, they will have issue with you giving away the property of another, whether it is free or not. As an end-user of a service, we are paying for the right to access the service. By doing so, we don't gain any rights or ownership to that service. Any value we add to the service is done voluntarily by us. We cannot re-sell (or give away) that service to another without permission from the provider.

The only way to truly sell your time in EQ would be to have a person actually pay you to spend time doing something for them in the game. i.e., they buy a new EQ account and pay you in advance to help them for a certain amount of time. During that time, you help them make level X or gain items A, B, and C. Only at that point you are you actually being paid for your time, and not for a transfer of Verant's property (data within the verant databases) or investment in their service.

Let's expand the topic a bit. Since we're talking about intellectual property rights, what about strategy guides? There are whole hosts of strategy guides out there; some are official, most are not. Does this infringe upon the company's IP rights? Nintendo seems to think so. If companies are allowed to claim as their exclusive domain anything that pertains to their product, then we must agree that ALL of these websites and message boards and such are illegal. Where does it end? Is Consumer Reports no longer allowed to rate products because use of pictures and names of products violates trademarks and copyrights, etc., etc.? Of course, now you are sitting back thinking, "Well, that's silly, of course they are allowed to rate products; that's a far cry from selling EQ items on eBay." Well, *YOU* may have a clear distinction in your head, but that doesn't mean one exists within the context of the law. So, we get back to the fundamental argument of ownership and time. No one can say that camping the Glowing Black Stone isn't a considerable effort. The question remains, then, that once you've obtained the item, is it yours by virtue of the work you've put into obtaining it? Let's take a broader example, you build a character up to level 60 and decide you're tired of EQ. If you have a friend or family member who would like to play that character, shouldn't you be able to give it to them if you so desire? The fact is that most people do share accounts with friends and family even though it is expressly forbidden in the EULA. For every other piece of software out there, you care purchase it and then sell it to someone else if you wish (same thing for tapes, CDs, DVDs, etc.)--why, then, is Everquest exempt from this? The fact is that the only thing that makes them exempt is that they put it in the EULA. The only problem is that other companies have tried that and were defeated in court. The FACT is that consumers have certain rights and considerations where it comes to things they've paid for. THAT is at the very heart of this issue.

Let's examine this logically & calmly.

First of all:

"people who create programs using a commercial C compiler shouldn't get anything for their time either. After all, "The 'time' you are 'selling' is spent accessing [someone else's compiler]."

Your case here is flawed because even though I am using someone else's compiler, the code is still mine because I am the programmer.

The same thing can be said of licensing game engines. Sure "American McGee's Alice" runs on the Quake III engine, but the engine license was legitimately purchased & proper credit given to the Quake III team. The code to "Alice" itself was created by the original programers & them alone. As a result, "Alice" is a unique product & they are the creators.

At no point in time are you creating a module for EQ by merely raiding Guk, nor can you because it is against the EULA to alter the program as it stands.

The cold fact of the matter is that no one creates anything by playing EQ; you are merely generating what is already in the data tables.

What you believe is yours is already theirs &, in some cases, has been since beta. It existed before your character walked Norrath & it will be there afterwards.

"Well, probably the biggest issue at hand is establishing the ownership of your character. This may seem like a no-brainer; but if you stop for a moment and think about it, it's not so clear."

I am afraid to say that it is indeed a no-brainer. What you describe as "ownership" is merely an unhealthy fixation on a virtual self. Being a former DM in the tabletop genre, I have seen players fly into rage when their characters "die," & have felt it when one of my favorite characters bites the bullet.

You have put so much of your persona into the character that it becomes a part of you in a sense. You think you "own" it because "Badaxe, Lvl 60 Shaman" reacts a lot like you would in some situations (but what situations in Norrath resemble real life?) & in a sense, reminds you of yourself.

No one auctions D&D characters scrawled on a piece of paper regardless of how much time they spent leveling that character or how large the stronghold of your 20th level fighter is. They are concepts with no real value & your auction would get laughed off of eBay.

The only difference between that & EQ is that there is a market for the data & you can "use" eBayed items to an extent; it is more "tangible" than some character record sheet.

However, these concepts are too similar to be ignored. Beating a dragon & getting a +5 Holy Avenger makes you no more a creator of D&D than camping the GBS makes you a creator of EQ.

You are a paying participant & subject to the rules when you are allowed to play.

"The FACT is that consumers have certain rights and considerations where it comes to things they've paid for. THAT is at the very heart of this issue."

That is very true, but consumers also have a responsiblity not to MISUSE the things that they have paid for. Ammonium Nitrate use as fertilizer is productive & used as bomb formula will get you time in prison.

I can't use my apartment to make adult movies just because I pay $300 a month in rent.

If I break the rules, I suffer the consequences regardless of the time or amount of finances I sink into something.

Time spent & money invested does not equal ownership in certain cases, including this one. THAT is the primary issue.

The burning finality is that you do not have ownership over something that was never yours in the first place.

You played a game, some random numbers were generated by Verant's computers, the simulated outcome ended in your favor & the database generated a random number resulting in a nice piece of treasure for your character.

Time & how it is spent is indeed the crux of the matter. The time you spent looking for the GBS could have been spent getting a better job, meeting the woman of your dreams, or even getting some sleep.

I am not saying that EQ is a waste of time, because the game is still fun to me. I am only saying that I am not so wrapped up in Norrath that I have deluded myself into thinking that I am lord & master because my 60th level Warrior has been around since beta.

I am a paying rent to run my warrior around Norrath & the lease is the EULA: if I break the "lease" & the "landlord" finds out, I can get "evicted."

Painting a room red in my apartment doesn't give me ownership of that room, despite the COST of the paint or HOW LONG it took to paint it. The space still belongs to the landlord.

It baffles me how eBayers can refuse to accept this simple concept because it suits them.

The only regrettable thing about the loss of eBay is that now the threat of fraud is greater because the trade has been driven underground.

I completely agree that not allowing item exchange is bogus & sad, but if it is part of the rules of the game & I want to continue playing, I will follow the rules.

If I don't agree with Verant's stance on eBay, then I wouldn't give them my business & would persuade others not to do so as well.

Why attempt a lawsuit to change the policy of a company that is unwilling to change?

The answer is simply not to buy or play EQ if you find the EULA distasteful.
