You make the assumption that DUI incidents will automatically rise with the legalization of drugs.

The problem in this case isn't just the law, its an entire culture of people that say "drugs are bad" and refuse to learn or provide any actual information on the subject, as well as an epidemic of crappy parenting. When something is legal, that pretty much guarantees that there will be more information easily available on it, and parents who actually talk to their children instead of just saying "don't do drugs" and leaving it at that have a real effect. If the focus is on responsible use, you have a lot more control over the situation. Why do you think alcoholism is less of a problem in countries where there's no drinking age? Why do you think STDs are less of a problem in countries with a more casual attitude towards sex? The more taboo something is, the more people, especially teenagers, will do it.

As far as people who use drugs being "weak", there is no such thing as a human being who isn't "weak" like that. Every last one of us has our distractions and our hobbies, and every last one of us uses them to escape from the stress of life. Drugs are simply more addictive and destructive by comparison.

As for leagalization of drugs the benefits go much farther than that. Currently about 75% of the US prision population is behind bars for drug related offences. How much money would we save if they were not there?


You seem to have hit a nail on the head here, but not quite for the reason you think. One of the reasons the drug laws were created was to increase the prison population. Prison labor is incredibly cheap compared to almost any kind of labor, including labor moved overseas. If you saw just how much of our consumer products (or in some cases the precursors to our consumer products) are actually made by prisoners you would be astonished. The drug laws were passed for a purpose and that purpose has been achieved.

Now I agree whole heartedly with Rrail on this issue. I say legalize it, lease expensive licensing to create the stuff on it, and put a big fat federal sin/luxury tax on it (say 10% or 15%) on top of your normal state sales and sin taxes.

I remember reading a study done several years ago that had to do with the legalizing of drugs, and the taxing of them with a 10% sin tax. It said that the national debt would be paid off entirely in like 8 years. If that study was based upon a debt that was at most $1 trillion dollars less then it was today, then it would still be based on a national debt of over 6 trillion dollars. That means it would take about 10 years to pay off the current debt (which is increasing 2 billion dollars a day thanks to our current government spending plans.) After those 10 years, that extra $750 billion in yearly federal income could be used to do things like send people to college, pay for new schools, create a better healthcare system. Now this doesn't even include the ever increasing $40 billion per year that we are spending on the "war on drugs" currently.

Another study comparing the cost effectiveness of the war said this:

RAND Drug Policy Research Center found that treatment is 10 times more cost effective than interdiction in reducing the use of cocaine in the United States.

The same study found that every additional dollar invested in substance abuse treatment saves taxpayers more than $7 in societal costs, and that additional domestic law enforcement costs 15 times as much as treatment to achieve the same reduction in societal costs.
