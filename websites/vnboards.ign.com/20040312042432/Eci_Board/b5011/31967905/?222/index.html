It was a cold war legacy. During the 50s a senator noticed that the pledge could just as easily be recited by a soviet as by an American citizen. To distinguish Americans from the "godless commies" they added the "under God" portion.

The interesting thing is that the case was decided under the endorsement test favored by Justice O'Connor. That test, IMO, is closer to the Early Republic attitude towards religious establishments than the other constitutional tests that have been tried in the last few decades. It indicates that the circuit courts are moving to make endorsement their standard.

Of course, most of the founders would be upset at the decision. But that is because they never realized that their principles were contrary to their assumptions.

Snacko asked about the endorsement test.

The first clause of the first amendment reads: "Congress shall make no law respecting an establishment of religion"

What does this mean? Basically, the federal government was not to mess with religion, religion was a state matter. Early in the 20th century the court decided that the Civil War amendments had made the Bill of Rights applicable to state and local governments. At this point the establishment clause became murkier than it had been.

For a while the court attempted to decide questions based on whether the laws were creating excessive entanglement between government and religion. This approach was useful in some cases, especially the released time cases, but was not so clear in others.

The endorsement test hinges on whether a reasonable observer would conclude that a particular practice, law, or language constitutes an endorsement of any particular religion, faith, or creed.

As such it is very well suited to questions of public statements of civil religion, creches in front of court houses, and so on.

It is important for this case in that at the moment the supreme court is divided 4 4 1 on church state questions. The Scalia-Rhenquist block always votes for civil religion, the liberal block always votes Jeffersonian. Justice O'Conner is the swing vote, and she prefers the endorsement test. As a result constitutional lawyers have been keying their church-state briefs on the endorsement question and attempting to convince O'Conner to agree with them. Based on the AP account of the decision, if it goes to the Supremes O'Connor will probably be the swing vote and may well write the decision. I expect that it will be upheld.

As a practical matter, the only reason "under God" and "In God We Trust" have survived so far was under the principle that they were meaningless trifles not worthy of the blunt hand of legal correction. The endorsement test is aimed at this sort of civil religion and as long as it is used to determine the meaning of "respecting an establishment of religion" then much of the language of civil religion that was put in place during the Civil War and during the Cold War, that language is likely to be removed again.

Tami,

You would be better off to say that the founders were compelled by two notions. The first was liberty of conscience, no person has the right to dictate another person's religious beliefs or to force them to engage in acts of worship.

The second was civil religion, a society is best organized and best lived if its members are religious. Civil religion is a fuzzy concept, from Jefferson and Franklin and Tocqueville who thought that all religions were "good enough" if they taught morality to the folks who believed that the nation was under Special Providence and would be punished if its leaders were not professing Christians and good Calvinists.

You appear to have confused the Puritan migration with the national founding, or to have accepted the 19th century notion that the American Revolution began when Martin Luther nailed his protest to the church door.

This decision, like many of the church-state cases, tries to make sense of a conflict between freedom of conscience, broadly defined, and civil religion, fuzzily defined.

Snacko,

Here is the AP write up, which has more details than the Reuters.

http://www.washingtonpost.com/wp-dyn/articles/A49515-2002Jun26.html

The key paragraph reads:

"A profession that we are a nation 'under God' is identical, for Establishment Clause purposes, to a profession that we are a nation 'under Jesus,' a nation 'under Vishnu,' a nation 'under Zeus,' or a nation 'under no god,' because none of these professions can be neutral with respect to religion," Judge Alfred T. Goodwin wrote for the three-judge panel

This ties into something I am writing about in my own work. Civil religion, broad fuzzy language about religion, is popular and acceptable ONLY until someone insists that it be defined. At that point it collapses; either it is meaningless or it is specific and doctrinal. In this case the panel tried some word-substitution and decided that "under God" was doctrinal.
