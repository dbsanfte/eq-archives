>>"you guys ever wonder why icecubes float? its just like water floating on water! I guess just one of those things I ponder about alot"<<

Because when water crystallizes (say, in the freezing process) it expands slightly. Since it has a slightly increased volume but the exact same mass, crystallized water is less dense than it was before it froze. An object submersed in liquid that has a higher density than itself will float to the surface.

Hot air rises and cold air sinks for similar reasons. Cooler air is denser air. The reason that less-dense things float on top of more-dense fluids is because the lower-density material is displaced by higher-density fluid. Put a ping-pong ball into a jar of walnuts of similar size and shake it for a while, and the ping-pong ball, if it isn't crushed in the process, will eventually rise to the top because it is displaced by the more dense walnuts. In a real fluid, shaking isn't necessary, because fluids "shake" at the molecular level in something called "Brownian Motion."
