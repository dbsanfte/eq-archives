With regard to the class action lawsuit proposed by Gravityspot.com, it was inevitable. There is a side to this that most people are not seeing. Most people are only too glad to see eBay transactions terminated as they believe it adversely affects them in-game. However, what is at stake here is not the right to sell fantasy equipment on eBay... it is every person's right to play this or any other game in the manner that he or she decides. Verant has this virtual world which they charge you an initial fee to access (cost of the game), a monthly fee to continue accessing and a fee every time there's a significant upgrade. As consumers of a product, we have distinct and definitive right under the law. Those of you that think this lawsuit doesn't stand a chance because of the End User Agreement, think again. The End User Agreement is just a glorified shrinkwrap agreement. Shrinkwrap agreements are NOT legally binding in many states and are very limited in others. Verant does NOT have the right to dictate how every aspect of the game is conducted. By allowing them unchecked authority over HOW the game is played, you end up with situation where people are banned for being SUSPECTED of cheating without any actual proofs. If you think this can't or hasn't happened, think again. As a former guide, I can attest to the fact that people have had their accounts terminated because of the appearance of impropriety. In at least one case, it turned out to be a game bug; however the user's account was NOT restored. What it all boils down to is this: MONEY. The fact of the matter is that when a person purchases an Everquest game and account, it is worth about $50. However, through that players WORK, he or she is able to level his characters up to 50 and all of a sudden that same account is now worth hundreds if not thousands of dollars. THAT is what a judge is going to look at and make a ruling on. If eBay has done anything it has demonstrated that these fantasy items, and the time it takes to aquire them, are worth real-world money. Verant is saying that selling stuff on eBay infringes upon their Intellectual Property rights. Bologne... they retain possession and control of everything in the game. Nothing is being copied, illegally accessed or illegally transferred. Both the buyer and seller are authorized users of their system engaging in a transaction which would be legal if done in-game for platinum or even on out of game websites also for platinum. People write fan-fiction all the time; but when one player wrote a rather dark and adult piece of fan-fiction (referring to Mystere), he was banned because Verant didn't like it. It is these inconsistances and double-standards that will prove Verant's undoing in any class-action lawsuit. I think a lawsuit would be a good thing, ultimately, because it will firmly establish player rights in the eyes of the law. This is extremely important as a whole cornucopia of persistant-world MMORPGs are soon to make their debut. I would urge people to think about what I've said rather than just defaulting to a knee-jerk reaction of telling me I'm wrong and I'm some eBay camper (because I'm not).

Did someone on this thread ACTUALLY cite to the EULA of playerauctions.com as proof that they can override the EULA of Everquest (I'm looking in your direction, AyonaeJack...)? In the law, we have what's called a "giggle test." If you can't make an argument without a straight face, better not try it in court. If you did make that argument with a straight face, you really can't know what you are saying.

It comes down to this.

1. People want to be able to profit off of Everquest, even if it means rationalizing around the law to do so. (e.g., "Ebay payed for a large part of my honeymoon... so I think it's good")
2. Selling another company's intellectual property is not something we are likely to get caught doing, or to be severely punished for if we did.
3. In America, if we don't get caught, it's not illegal in our minds(like copying your friend's copy of MS Office, or burning a friend's CDs, or speeding on roads that aren't patrolled).
4. When confronted with the clear intellectual property laws which we agreed to every time we played Everquest, we will say something intellectually meritless to justify our violations of the law ("First off Verants intellectual property is not being sold. No real peoperty, with the exception of accounts being sold, ever changes hands. People are selling their time spent to aquire such imaginary items")
5. Despite the fact that no one has ever sold "time" (even me, as a lawyer, sell my work over time, not the time itself), since no other excuse works, we will use it to justify our actions.
6. We know that the worst thing that could happen is we would have to buy another copy of Everquest or get a different game, so we feel the risk is less than the reward.

Here's the fact, and I guess I have to keep repeating it on every board until someone listens, WHEN YOU SELL ON EBAY IN THIS FASHION YOU ARE SELLING THINGS THAT ARE NOT YOURS. They never were, you "borrowed" them as a license holder. It's really that simple, and the rest of this nonsense is just trying to rationalize behavior and to continue to cash in. Verant had (and has) the authority to stop these practices, and it has. It will go after every other site listed here and elsewhere next, because one of you will leak it out in a place that a Verant employee can see it (do you think Playerauctions has any way to determine that MrJones@hotmail.com isn't Brad or Alan or Gordon? do they have a magic webserver that can see when they go home to a different IP address?), or because people will tell them.


Please get a grip. Your source of money from a game you spend $120 a year (plus the initial $50) on is drying up. Go join a multi-level marketing scheme or something and leave us alone.

This is from their press (gamers.com)

GameRealm has opened PlayerAuctions.com, an auction site devoted to online roleplaying gamers. Swapping rare items and high-powered characters for cash can be a cheap, quick way to reach the elite levels in games that would otherwise suck away hours of time. But there are drawbacks.

"We felt the current outlet for online auctions was not good enough," administrator Shane (last name withheld upon request) said. "PlayerAuctions is a safe and secure auction site for all online games that is both better directed and cheaper then eBay. In addition, it has a private message system so e-mail addresses are not exposed to the prying eyes of gaming companies."

PlayerAuctions also has a user agreement clause that specifies:

13. No employees, contractors, or agents of the following companies are eligible to use this site: Microsoft, Verant, Sony, Turbine, Sierra Online.
14. No person who is designated to act in an official capacity within the following online games are eligible to use this site: EverQuest, Ultima Online, or Asheron's Call. Official capacity will be construed as meaning the ability to ban or suspend accounts, or to modify character statistics not normally within the control of players.

Whether or not this will save players from being banned at their MMORPG of choice for participating in auctions remains to be seen. Origin's Ultima Online encourages auctions and permits account transfers. Microsoft's Asheron's Call does not endorse auctions, but does not prohibit or police sales. Verant's EverQuest forbids the transfer of accounts, and the sale or auction of any characters, items, coin or copyrighted material. Verant, however, does not actively seek out auctioneers.
Shane said that GameRealm had consulted a lawyer prior to creating the site. "Our understanding of the legal issue is that the agreement not to sell items is between the user and Verant. Not all games have this same rule, and there is no law that prohibits these types of transfers. Therefore, auction providers like eBay, Bidbay, and PlayerAuctions are not responsible for what the users decide to sell.

"We fundamentally believe in a user's right to profit from their own time. That is what the free market is all about. Can you imagine if Microsoft took the same position? Users of Office would not be able to sell information or content that they had entered into Word or Excel. It is ridiculous."

Gordon Wrinn, the voice of customer service for Verant, pointed out to EverQuest messageboard users that auctions may not be such a good idea. "If player A sells the account to player B, and later steals it back, then player A has committed a crime, but that is between player A and player B, and really has nothing to do with us.

"The extent that we get involved is to ban the account if player B calls us up and proves that he bought it from player A. We do not however compensate player B in any way, since he acquired access to the account through a violation of the user agreement by player A. It is then player B's option to file legal charges if they wish, but that doesn't involve us.

"See, this is why we don't allow account transfers, because we can't be responsible for all the fraud that goes on. Someone might look reputable on those auction sites, but takes only a couple of days and costs a couple of dollars to work up a convincing profile. There is more fraud involved in account and item sales than you can possibly imagine. I wish I could tell you how many calls we get every day about this," he said.

PlayerAuctions is working to provide a safe trading environment, but the security comes with a price. Like most auction sites, it has a fee schedule, with rates starting at $0.25 for a listing, regardless of opening value. For an item that sells for $25 or less, PlayerAuctions collects 4.0% of the sale. eBay would scoop up 5.0%. "Our goal is to maintain this discount," Shane said.

Did someone on this thread ACTUALLY cite to the EULA of playerauctions.com as proof that they can override the EULA of Everquest (I'm looking in your direction, AyonaeJack...)? In the law, we have what's called a "giggle test." If you can't make an argument without a straight face, better not try it in court. If you did make that argument with a straight face, you really can't know what you are saying.

It comes down to this.

1. People want to be able to profit off of Everquest, even if it means rationalizing around the law to do so. (e.g., "Ebay payed for a large part of my honeymoon... so I think it's good")
2. Selling another company's intellectual property is not something we are likely to get caught doing, or to be severely punished for if we did.
3. In America, if we don't get caught, it's not illegal in our minds(like copying your friend's copy of MS Office, or burning a friend's CDs, or speeding on roads that aren't patrolled).
4. When confronted with the clear intellectual property laws which we agreed to every time we played Everquest, we will say something intellectually meritless to justify our violations of the law ("First off Verants intellectual property is not being sold. No real peoperty, with the exception of accounts being sold, ever changes hands. People are selling their time spent to aquire such imaginary items")
5. Despite the fact that no one has ever sold "time" (even me, as a lawyer, sell my work over time, not the time itself), since no other excuse works, we will use it to justify our actions.
6. We know that the worst thing that could happen is we would have to buy another copy of Everquest or get a different game, so we feel the risk is less than the reward.

Here's the fact, and I guess I have to keep repeating it on every board until someone listens, WHEN YOU SELL ON EBAY IN THIS FASHION YOU ARE SELLING THINGS THAT ARE NOT YOURS. They never were, you "borrowed" them as a license holder. It's really that simple, and the rest of this nonsense is just trying to rationalize behavior and to continue to cash in. Verant had (and has) the authority to stop these practices, and it has. It will go after every other site listed here and elsewhere next, because one of you will leak it out in a place that a Verant employee can see it (do you think Playerauctions has any way to determine that MrJones@hotmail.com isn't Brad or Alan or Gordon? do they have a magic webserver that can see when they go home to a different IP address?), or because people will tell them.


Please get a grip. Your source of money from a game you spend $120 a year (plus the initial $50) on is drying up. Go join a multi-level marketing scheme or something and leave us alone.

Any lawsuit along these lines is a huge waste of time. It will only benefit lawyers.

The EULA does clearly state that you can't do such a thing. For whatever that is worth. Illegal restricitions in a license can be over-turned, but IMO there are none here.

You do not, in fact, own any of the virtual items, nor does your time invested in getting them mean anything. They are being rented to you in consideration of your membership fees.

There may not be direct financial loss, but I feel Sony can argue with some success that continued EBay type sale undermine the integrity of the game, and therefore reduce the value of the game in the marketplace. Indirect loss due to IC infringment is quite valid (Ask Disney - they know ALL about that).

You MAY NOT sell something legally with an illegal item being added 'for free'. That has been judged and denied. Don't count on the 'only selling my time' arguement to mean squat before a judge - it won't.

As a concept - I support EBaying items - but EQ is not designed to support that. Future games might be, and I imagine some will integrate auctions into the game ala UO.

Bottomline - it is up to the game company to decide. Verant must figure that those offended by auctioning are a greater concern then those profiting from it.
