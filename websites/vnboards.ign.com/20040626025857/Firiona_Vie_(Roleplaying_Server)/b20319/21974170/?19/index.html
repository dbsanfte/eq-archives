As with languages in real-life, some people seem to find learning to communicate easier than others. There are many factors, and the intelligence and/or wisdom of your character is just one of them. But if all the stars align, and heaven smiles upon you, then it is possible to completely learn a new language in an hour.

Now, this may be the same "possible" that has our friend the snowball calling his travel agent, mind you. But, yes, it is possible to learn a language from scratch in an hour.

If you are going to work on learning a language, there are some things you should know first. For one, take a look at http://everquest.allakhazam.com/Skills/Languages.html. I admit it's somewhat out of date, but it does have some useful information.

Next, keep in mind that you don't have to see the spam on your screen to get the improvement. So go into your options and turn groupchat OFF. Then use the "/serverfilter on" command to tell the server to not send you spam you can't see anyway. This will greatly reduce the liklihood that you will go linkdead trying to learn a language.

My thought on this matter begins with a question: In a roleplaying world, why do we want to learn a common language?

I love the fact that we have language barriers within this world! While it is completely frustrating for this human to find an adventuring party in a forest of elves, it's also quite the cultural experience. As someone of another race, you tend to feel unique and on display, yet isolated and silent.

In real life language barriers separate mankind into little pockets of cultural diversity. Each pocket has its own unique, interesting way of meetings its needs and desires. Outside ideas are filtered out due to the language barrier. This creates a world within a world concept.

If the people of Norrath spend all their time "skill building" to learn all the languages, they defeat this language barrier which creates the cultural diversity.

From a RP standpoint, you may not want to. For a while, I wanted to make my dwarf uncommunicative - I was going to learn the Common tongue (whatever it turned out to be) so that I could understand what was going on, but refuse to answer in anything other than dwarvish and descriptive emotes.

In practice, my character concept changed enough to where this just wasn't attractive anymore, but I can still see where it would have been fun.

> In real life language barriers separate
> mankind into little pockets of cultural
> diversity.

True, but in the real world, there are those that usually take the time to learn other languages or at least the "Lingua Franca".

These people fall into two major categories: merchants and adventurers.

Swahili developed as a "Common Tongue" in (East) Africa for economic reasons. The coastal Persian/Bantu cultures aggressively moved south and west economically and brought the language with them. As trade increased across the continent the language spread further, and if you wanted to do business, you learned it. Today, English is the "Common Tongue" of the merchant class since the USA is the dominant merchant culture; a couple generations ago it was French.

Adventurers across time have usually taken the time to learn a couple new languages, and one of those was almost always the most prevalent language of the era. "Adventurers" would be soldiers of fortune, explorers, carpetbaggers, diplomats, etc. Basically the types of people our characters are supposed to be.

So, you may expect a typical halfling to know his own language and very little of others, but a sell-sword or adventuring priest is not a typical halfling.
