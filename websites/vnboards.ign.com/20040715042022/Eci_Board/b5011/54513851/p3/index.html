Groo,

I'm glad someone is reading what I posted.

I must admit that you hampered the likelihood of being taken *too* seriously by me when you wrote, "Japan deserved what they got" last night. (A statement I still think you should consider retracting, revising, or at least qualifying.) I am normally careful not to reply to people who post such inflammatory generalizations, but I've already broken my rule.

To begin by softball-ing your question, there have been many "conditional" surrenders in history -- some good, some bad. It kind of depends on what you mean by "conditional."

Actually, the matter of clearly defining this word is (and was) at the heart of understanding the decision to and subsequent justification of dropping the Bomb(s).

In my opinion, it was the ridiculous insistence on and lack of precision in defining this term by the US leadership coupled with a misunderstanding of it by the Japanese that probably closed many avenues of surrender and peace that would have been mutually agreeable (short of nuclear warfare).

General Marshall admitted in early 1945 that "unconditional" surrender (with respect to Japan) "we all agree is difficult to define." He and the Army unsuccessfully pressed the Executive Branch over the course of 1945 for a "precise definition."

Acting Secretary of State Joseph Grew, as early as May 1945, correctly foresaw the roadblock that a likely (mis)interpretation of "unconditional surrender" by the Japanese might present. This prompted him to suggest a tiny verbage modification to an upcoming Truman speech that, had it been incorporated, might have allayed Japanese fears and paved a route to peace.

In a meeting on May 28, 1945 Grew is quoted as having said to Truman,

"The greatest obstacle to unconditional surrender by the Japanese is their belief that this would entail the destruction or permanent removal of the Emperor and the institution of the Throne."

He then proposed the addition of the following statement to a public address Truman was scheduled to make on May 31,

"This [the new Japanese government] may include a constitutional monarchy under the present dynasty if the peace-loving nations can be convinced of the genuine determination of such a government to follow policies of peace which will render impossible the future development of aggressive militarism in Japan."

But Secretary of War Stimson objected to this statement, which probably persuaded Truman to omit it.

The US Army was also advising Truman against removal of the Emperor and the existing Japanese beaucracy even should "unconditional" surrender be achieved, due to the difficulty they felt it might present in administering a post-war Japan.

At the same time, there was, unbeknowst to US leadership, much concern and debate in Japan about what the ultimate consequences of this ill-defined term "unconditional" might be.

I think it is generally believed that Togo, Sato's correspondent in the intercepted communiques (in conjunction with the other Japanese leadership) unfortunately understood "unconditional" to mean the deposition of the Emperor, something they wouldn't abide. (and which the US leadership didn't want anyway!)

One should also consider the influence that US news and other media reaching Japan during 1945 may have had on their interpretation of "unconditional".

At that time, 13% of US public poll respondents wanted to "kill all Japanese"; 33% wanted to destroy the Japanese state. Life magazine featured photos of hollowed out Japanese skulls sent home by US GIs as war trophies.

Japanese press understandably responded with statements such as,

"American Savagery Unveiled"

and,

"Barbarism is a conspicuous characteristic of their history," [if Japan capitulates, America will] "inflict a general massacre and defile the purity of our blood. All our men would be made into slaves and all our women would be prostitutes."

This climate of brutality and justified fear (on both sides), coupled with a lack of communication, a lack of understanding of Japanese culture by anyone in the US leadership (except perhaps Grew, who was ignored), and a refusal on the part of Truman to explicate his "unconditional" intentions for post-war Japan (particularly with regard to the Emperor) certainly didn't aid the cause of reaching a peaceful resolution to the war prior to his decision to incinerate ~300,000 Japanese civilians.

>>Acting Secretary of State Joseph Grew, as early as May 1945, correctly foresaw the roadblock that a likely (mis)interpretation of "unconditional surrender" by the Japanese might present. This prompted him to suggest a tiny verbage modification to an upcoming Truman speech that, had it been incorporated, might have allayed Japanese fears and paved a route to peace.

In a meeting on May 28, 1945 Grew is quoted as having said to Truman,

"The greatest obstacle to unconditional surrender by the Japanese is their belief that this would entail the destruction or permanent removal of the Emperor and the institution of the Throne."

He then proposed the addition of the following statement to a public address Truman was scheduled to make on May 31,

"This [the new Japanese government] may include a constitutional monarchy under the present dynasty if the peace-loving nations can be convinced of the genuine determination of such a government to follow policies of peace which will render impossible the future development of aggressive militarism in Japan."

But Secretary of War Stimson objected to this statement, which probably persuaded Truman to omit it.<<

Actually, the people who were most opposed to the retention of the Emperor were MacLeish and Acheson at the State department. It didn't help things that only 3% of the public supported the idea in public opinion polls, hence Byrnes' famous line about Truman's "crucifixon" if he agreed to anything short of unconditional surrender.
