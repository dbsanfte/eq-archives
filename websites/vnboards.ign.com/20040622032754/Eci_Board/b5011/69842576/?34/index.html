D-DAY'S ENDURING BOND
Despite U.S.-France tensions, Americans will always be heroes in Normandy

Elizabeth Bryant, Chronicle Foreign Service

St. Mere-Eglise, France -- The Americans arrived one predawn June morning, waving at 17-year- old Eugene Jouan as they marched down a country road and then leaving behind a dewy field speckled with parachutes that town residents would later sew into clothes.

Weeks later, long after the Germans had fled and many celebratory bottles of Calvados liqueur had been consumed, one departing GI handed Jeanette Pentecote his pocket knife.

"My mother told me to give this to a French girl," he told the 16-year- old butcher's daughter. She never saw him again.

Across the buttercup-sprinkled countryside of northwestern France, World War II survivors like Jouan and Pentecote are again dusting off memories of that fateful day in 1944 when they were liberated by U.S. soldiers.

On Sunday, the 60th anniversary of D-Day, President Bush and 15 other heads of state join tens of thousands of veterans and war buffs for lavish June 6 ceremonies commemorating the Allied landing in Normandy.

In between the barbecues and parachute drops, the world leaders are expected to issue messages of peace and of transatlantic cooperation, intending to heal bitter divisions over the U.S.-led war in Iraq. Few in France, which led last year's anti-war campaign, expect that will happen.

But in Normandy, American soldiers are still remembered as heroes and liberators. U.S. flags fly proudly alongside French, British and Canadian ones at World War II cemeteries and memorials.

In St. Mere-Eglise, bakeries sell American-style chocolate chip cookies along with their croissants, and the town's Gothic church bears a life-size model of an American paratrooper hanging from its spire, just where Pvt. John Steele got snagged on D-day.

'Especially for the veterans'

"We will never forget that the Americans gave us our freedom," said 77- year-old resident Raymond Paris, his sparkling blue eyes misting over. "What is happening in Iraq can't change our feelings of friendship, especially for the veterans."

Paris was a young notary clerk at the time of the Allied invasion. A few months before, he had gone into hiding to avoid military conscription under France's Vichy government. But in early June, Paris returned to his parents' home in this small, plain gray town, five miles from Utah Beach. On June 5, he switched on his radio to hear the nightly broadcast from Britain by the Free French Forces.

"All of a sudden, I heard the sound of dice being tossed," he said during an interview at his small two-story home, recalling the message crackling from across the English Channel. "It was the signal for the invasion, of course. But you don't know what a shock it was."

The Americans arrived a few hours later -- the 101st and 82nd Airborne divisions, parachuting through inky skies from low-flying planes. German soldiers, who had occupied St. Mere-Eglise since 1940, traded fire with the U. S. paratroopers.

But by dawn on June 6, the Germans had fled and an American flag fluttered over the town hall. St. Mere-Eglise became France's first community liberated on D-Day.

"We were waiting for the British," said the 77-year-old Pentecote, who watched the parachutes floating down. "But we got the Americans instead."

Paratrooper Howard Manoian landed in the town graveyard, nearly two miles from La Fiere bridge, which he had been ordered to seize.

"I banged on doors -- I had a French phrase book," recalled the Massachusetts native and decorated war veteran from his home in Chef-du-Pont, two miles from St. Mere-Eglise. "I said, 'Me, La Fiere.' They said, 'No. St. Mere-Eglise.' "

It took Manoian two days, ducking gunfire from reassembled German forces, to get to the bridge. His platoon kept moving, later crossing the Ardennes into Belgium and then into Germany. In April 1945, Manoian was sent home. A few weeks later, the war was over.

Scraps of D-Day lore are tucked into every corner of St. Mere-Eglise -- from small anecdotes etched on gravestones of the town cemetery, to streets and even a restaurant named after U.S. veterans, to the musings of local seniors.

"If the Americans hadn't come, the Germans would have deported us. Who knows where? Maybe to Siberia, to die,'' said Jouan, 77, a sturdy onetime farmer who once presented a model of St. Mere-Eglise to the 82nd Airborne at Fort Bragg, N.C.

Today Jouan, like many D-Day survivors, keeps a thick folder of fading photos and curling letters from American and German soldiers who fought here. Veterans from both countries are feted when they return on visits.

In 2002, President Bush also stopped in town, where he honored those who had died in combat -- before Bush and French President Jacques Chirac split bitterly over the war in Iraq. The two leaders are scheduled to meet in Paris on Saturday, in the latest effort to heal their differences.

"It's quite odd to think we're still waging war after so many years," mused British tourist Guy Parker, 46, as he sat on a sun-washed bench watching tour buses rumble by. "You would have thought it would have come to an end by now. But it hasn't. Not by a long shot."

Manoian returned to Normandy in 1985 after a career as a New England cop. He moved to the small hamlet of Chef-du-Pont, where a sign grandly stating "Chateau Manoian" hangs over his one-story home.

A small man with bifocals, rudimentary French and a quick wit, Manoian is a popular fixture of St. Mere-Eglise, where the mayor's top aide is a drinking buddy.

From his sun porch one recent spring evening, he delivered D-Day memories between gulps of beer. A faded cross-and-heart tattoo from Worfld War II days is etched on one arm. A Desert Storm sweatshirt, a more recent acquisition, is rolled up at the sleeves.

Talk about partying

He talks about partying with French and German veterans. He recalls the day in 1944 when he almost killed a well-meaning French farmer who mistakenly handed him Calvados instead of water and he chugged it down before the brandy's heat hit him with a wallop.

A car rounds the bend and honks. Manoian waves. It is the mayor of Chef- du-Pont.

"It's been a while since our esteem for the United States has gone down," said Jean-Michel Selles, who owns a war memorabilia store in St. Mere-Eglise. "But people here venerate veterans like Howard Manoian. For us, the Americans from the landing will always remain a cult image."
