"Studies have long since proven that Internet addiction leads to depression. Do the math."

I don't know of any studies that have firmly established causality with Internet addiction and depression. Certainly, there's a huge body of evidence about the relationship between depression and all sorts of addiction. There are specialists in addiction medicine who approach cases by finding out what people are trying to treat with the addiction. I heard a lecture from one such psychiatrist, where she told a particularly interesting story about an alcoholic who had been drinking since she was 11 years old. It turned out that she was schizophrenic... and she'd been hearing voices since she was five. The alcohol "drowned them out."

I used to spend too much time on the internet, in game rooms, online with my "friends" there. I was a sysop on the MSN Gaming Zone. I spent most of my free time there. I was also in a miserable marriage to a man who, it turns out, is bipolar and has Asperger's Syndrome (high-functioning autism). As soon as we finally split up, I had little desire to go back to the Zone. I was going there to escape from my life, not being pulled away from my life by my desire to go there.

Just as the vast majority of people drink alcohol without becoming alcoholics, so do people play games like Everquest and go to chat rooms without becoming addicts. The definition of addiction is dependent on how it affects your life; if you continue to use a substance even though it is having negative effects on your daily life, you're addicted. Some things are more addictive than others; cigarettes and heroin are extremely addictive, while marijuana and LSD are difficult or impossible to become physically addicted to (but a few people develop an emotional dependency). Certain aspects of online gaming make it more addictive than the Internet at large, but on the whole it's not much different from alcohol in my experience.

There's too much scapegoating these days. Television, movies, games, etc. don't cause people to kill themselves. They are sometimes symptoms or make it easier, but if we aren't willing to take responsibility for those we care about, and saying "Hey, there's a problem here, let me help," it's not going to bring them back to blame it on a game.

EDIT: Oh, man, just read a bit more of the story... the kid was epileptic and schizoid! And they think that the *game* is responsible for his death? The game was probably responsible for the only bit of enjoyment he experienced.

You have a lot of good points, but I think you're still falling into some causality traps. There's some who will tell you of a correlation between pornography exposure and negative attitudes toward women, but truth is, there's also a lot of studies that contradict that. A famous study cited by the "anti-porn" folks is the Michigan State Police Study, where they allegedly went through records of thousands of sex crimes and found that pornography was linked to a significant proportion.

Turns out, that study *never happened.* Furthermore, when the Detective in charge of the division of the Michigan State police that supposedly did the study tried to replicate it, he couldn't. Michigan does have the most comprehensive database of crime data in the nation, covering over 75,000 incidents dating back to 1950. When Detective Sergeant David Minzey tried to correlate sex crimes with pornography, he found that the correlation was weak at best... and that other factors, such as alcohol use, had a much stronger correlation with these incidents.

What is probably true is that:

- People who are addicted to sex are likely to become addicted to pornography.
- People who have sexual addictions may have a family history which skews their perceptions about sex and members of the opposite sex.
- Men who are addicted to pornography may be so for reasons that also affect their attitudes toward women.

If there is a correlation between pornography addiction and negative attitudes toward women, it is just as likely to be the opposite from what is often supposed: that men who view women as objects or as inferior are more inclined to become addicted to pornography. There is no solid evidence of any correlation though.

The internet, and much of modern technology, is an amazing new way to get exactly the kind of information we want instantaneously. But it doesn't change human nature, or the nature of addiction. Part of the problem with internet addictions is that people think they're different from other sorts of addictions; essentially, they're not. Some people have "addictive personalities;" they are more likely to become addicted, but whether to alcohol or cigarettes or heroin or overeating or cybersex isn't fundamentally different.

A lot is still to be understood about the nature of addiction. There has been a lot of success in treating addiction as a symptom rather than as a problem in and of itself, as I mentioned before. Certainly this was true when my mother quit smoking; she tried and tried and tried for 10 years, and never did better than three weeks. Finally, she went through regression hypnosis to find out when she started (because she truly couldn't remember; as far as she knew she had smoked all her life) and discovered not just that she had not always been a smoker, but that she had her first cigarette to "calm her down" after a very traumatic experience. That didn't make it easy for her to quit, but it made it possible.

The kid didn't commit suicide because he played EQ too much. He played EQ too much because he was epileptic, clinically depressed, and schizophrenic, and it let him forget about all that and be someone else for a while. He also killed himself for the same reasons. Certainly, his overindulgence in the game was a warning sign and a symptom, but it was in no way a cause.
