>>Low population<<

That's arguably a benefit - makes it possible for a relatively small and cash-strapped campaign get its message out via retail politics as opposed to the inevitably media-driven contests in California and New York.

>>one of the most homogenous populations in the country (white bread and miracle whip anyone?)<<

True enough, that is arguably a problem.

>>just about zero industry other than farming and food processing<<

It's not so simple - see http://www.census.gov/epcd/susb/2001/ia/IA--.HTM#table3

>>That state is nothing like the rest of the nation as a whole<<

It's true that the population of Iowa is a little older than the rest of the country and contains fewer unwed mothers, fewer minorities and city dwellers. However, it's rather even divided between Democrats and Republicans, at least in presidential elections, so in a way it's representative.

>>yet one of the top contenders for Democratic nominee is dropping out after Iowa because of placing fourth.<<

Not just any contender, but rather a contender who had cultivated the state for years, had a district next door, and won Iowa in 1988 when he last ran for President. Also, he had no money in the bank and bet everything on an Iowa win.
