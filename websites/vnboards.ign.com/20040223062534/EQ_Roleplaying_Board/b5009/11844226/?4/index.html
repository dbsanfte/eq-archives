Ah ok.....inside the Inn.

I see it to be a fairly large tap room with the hearth off to the left and facing the entrance and the bar itself to the right, also facing the doorway. The doors themselves have been oft replaced and quite possibly sag a little from the last time a certain High elf and Troll attempted to replace them, despite obvious lack of carpentry skills.

Behind the bar and hidden from direct of those in the tap room sight by a wall and a flight of stairs leading to the upper guest rooms is the kitchen. The guest rooms are above the kitchen itself, I suppose so that they are kept warmer by the heat from the kitchen. There is a slightly dripping hole in the often repaired roof, currently blocked by an oilskin pinned there by Gales most excellent arrows. And also the scars of previous repairs can still be seen, though obviously of a more professional calibre than either Gale's effort on the roof or those on the doors.

The bartop itself still bears scars of the occasional dagger blade driven hard into the woodwork, though if the Innkeep wasnt such a lazy sort he'd surely have repaired those by now.

As for outside...

Well the double doors open, slightly difficultly, onto a view of a pine crested valley and sloping hills on the far side, again densely populated with pines, there could well be a path or maybe even a cobbled road leading downhill to the left towards a possible nearby town. There have been times when the town was right outside the Inn but I have never seen it that way. Possibly its some kind of seasonal expansion of the town, for a faire or some such when people overflow to the spaces near the Inn and pitch tents and temporary structures or the like.

Tucked against the right hand wall (when facing the doors and the Inn) is a sadly empty stable.

I see the building itself in Tudor style, with white plaster and red timber facing.

Um well thats how I see it anyway. Hope that helps.

Hmmm, perhaps I should have read this before I started posting! Ho hum, If i may make some additions... (correct me if inconsistant anywhere)

To embellish upon the already rich scene described so eloquantly above, I will describe the elevated areas that oft escape the notice of the less observant guests of this excellent establishment. I shall then describe the hearth that has warmed the toes of many a brave soul to snuggle close to its warm embrace!

The roof of the bar is held up by a veritable maze of cross beams. Those with exceptional night vision would, peering into the shadows see critters of various persuasions, some building nests and webs, others scurrying along those elevated dust strewn highways, intent on their own uncomplicated animal business.

At on end, situated just above the bar and to the left is a recent addition: a dovecot. It houses a rapidly diminishing population of doves, who are beginning to learn who amoungst the inn's customers they should consider to be preditors. A wooden ladder of simple construction leans noncholantly against the wall to provide access to their straw lined home.

Over the years, a good many of the bolts holding the intricate structure together have rusted their way out of their sockets and plummeted to the wooden floor below, to be swept into the corner by the hearth, where a dusty necropolis has slowly acumulated for years. Occasionally these plumeting missiles have prompt a violent brawl, as would anything dropped into an Ogre's pint.

The hearth itself is of hardy construction, made of roughly hewn, soot blackened granite. It was a necessary replacement of the original brick fitting. The latter had suffered unnecessarily over the years from many stray blows of weapons and fists, back in those less civilised days when tavern brawls were an almost hourly occurence. Finally, one still frequently recounted day it collapsed into a pile of rubble, closing the establishment for one whole lamentable week

The victim and ultimate protagonist of its collapse was one Struga Stormsinger, a Dwarf maiden famed for her operatic voice. As she belted out her final climactic note of her tear inducing performance, the hearth, obviously not counted amoungst her biggest fans, decided to spare the suffering audiance. Slowly cracks appeared and widened, and then, with an ominous rumble the fireplace toppled forward, burying her up to her neck. Like a true performer her voice barely wavered and, when the dust cleared enough to reveal her proud visage poking out of the pile of bricks and morter, she was awarded a standing ovation.
