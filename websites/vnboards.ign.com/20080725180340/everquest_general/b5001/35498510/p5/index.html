Some comments in addition to the good info already posted.

Bard - kind of a jack of all trades since they can sing just about any spell effect in the game. This can be good and bad. A well played bard is a phenominal group member - but due to the fact that bards have so many options it's easy to be a poor bard. Bards need to learn how to maximize the use of their wide variety of songs. Bards can be excellent pullers with lament.

Cleric - Probably the most sought after class in the game for groups for their heals (and usually buffs too). Clerics can be vital to group success with their lull/sooth/calm/pac line too, not just healing. I've grouped with some clerics that can pull better than many tanks I've grouped with.

Druid - Very versatile, often found soloing, and often not the best in groups due to the fact that they solo too much! A well played druid is an asset in any situation though.

Enchanter - Crowd control specialist, mana regen buffs, many other handy buffs, fun illusions spells, decent slower - an all around fun class to play and highly sought after for almost all group situations.

Magician - Very powerful class that can summon all kinds of fun and usually useful stuff, and has a very powerful pet. Able to solo decently and a good asset for a group.

Monk - I'm not happy with the evolution of monks - I would've prefered that they never started wielding weapons! But, I guess it gives em two more slots to get +stats from. Classically monks were the ultimate pulling class and in many cases still are. In my experience FD pulling is often slow compared to using pacify or mezzing adds. Monks have lots of cool looking special attacks over time and are a great damage dealing class - a good compliment to a tank that can hold aggro and take damage.

Necromancer - Versatile class with a wide variety of fun spells and a decent pet. A strong solo class. Exceptionally strong vs undead but effective vs any critters. (see closing notes on strong solo clases!)

Paladin - A holy warrior that can wear plate, often wields two handed swords/axes and/or nice paladin only weapons, gets some decent cleric-oriented spells, and is generally highly sought after for groups, particularly LDoN. Paladins get the pacify line of spells which allows you to pull singles all day long and IMO are often the best pullers in the game (pac, plate armor, easily hold aggro). If you can consistently pull singles and keep aggro on one tank your group will mow critters down.

Ranger - Can become decent archers, usually later in levels. Tracking is sweet. High damage output class that compliments stronger damage taking tanks very well. Nice assortment of druid-oriented spells at 9+. Some people consider rangers the best farmers in the game because they have the best tracking - and often farming ability is determined by ability to detect and then locate specific critters.

Rogue - Super damage dealers but rely on being behind a critter for maximum damage output. Not the best at taking hits. A very nice compliment to someone who can maintain aggro (to keep the critter's back to the rogue). With LDoN rogues are sought after to open chest and more importantly, for high DPS.

Shadow Knight - Somewhat of an anti-paladin. Gets necro-like spells starting at 9th. A nice medium tank that can hold aggro well. Often wields two handed swords/axes and/or nice SK only weapons. Gets a special ability called harm touch that can do a big chunk of damage (sort of opposite of lay on hands of paladins). Like necros, they are considered an 'evil' class and occasionally have faction issues.

Shaman - Shaman are also the "kings of slowers" - although other classes can slow and at the highest levels some have nearly as strong as slows. Shaman can solo with their pet. Shaman are generally highly desired for groups as they are group enablers - their buffs, slows, and other abilities enhance group effectiveness and efficiency.

Warrior - straight forward slug it out class that is best with groups - because they have no significant way to heal themselves and sitting and regenning health take forever. Warriors get the cool melee skills like dual wield, double attack, riposte, etc earlier than any other class.

Wizard - A high damage output class that is easily squashed (like most casters).

Beastlord - A very powerful class that's good solo or in groups. They get many shaman spells including slow over time...and also get a series of mana/hp regen spells that are very nice. They have nice pet buffs and heals for their pets. They can tank a bit with their pet but wear light armor. The add serious firepower to any group.

In closing a comment on solo classes. My feeling is that any class that can solo well should be a good asset to a group. If they are strong enough to kill critters solo, they should add to any group, yes? Druids are sort of the exception here, as druid typically solo by killing their prey over time - rather than killing things fast like groups want to do. Necros can use simlar tactics but do have more frontloaded damage options. Beastlord pets are ridiculously powerful and BLs have some instant offense besides their pets.

I typed this out for someone who is new to the game, and recommended he just print it off. A lot of this may seem menial, but to me, these were a few of the things that I was very confused about when I first logged in:

---


When you first log into the game, you will see a screen full of windows, they are everywhere, and you will wonder, "where is my character?". This is because you are in first person view on default, you can use your mouse wheel to scroll back and be in 3rd person. Also, using F9 will show you a few other camera views, I think if you hit it like 5 times, it will put you back to your default first person, at which time you need to use the mouse wheel again to return to normal. (Normal to me = what it looks like when you play AC. 3rd person, looking at your char's behind).

A lot of the windows you see are "tip" windows, which are helpful. But really, I pretty much click on "don't show any tips" everytime I log in a new character, as they were more confusing to me than they were helpful. But this is up to personal preference. The other windows you see are a Pet window, effects window, actions window, chat window, hotkey tab, target window, group window, and a window with your name on it, showing your health/mana/stam. There is also a tab at the top of the screen (horizontal) with a bunch of icons, and a compass with N, E, S, W on it. I will explain how I have my stuff set up, because this is how *I* think it most useful for me. Again, it's up to personal preference.

The vertical tab that's a "spelltab" with 8 slots: I have this at the edge of my screen on the left, directly above the "hotkey" tab. Above the spelltab, in the top left corner is my pet window (if a pet applies, ie. Necro, beastlord, etc.) If no pet, the space is blank.

The horizontal tab with all the icons is at the top of my screen, in the center, and the "compass" is right next to that, on the right.

Top right hand corner, starting from the top down: The window with my name (showing health/stam/mana), group window, target window, actions tab, effects tab.

I have 2 chat windows on the bottom of the screen:
Chat 1 is for damage: ie.: my hits, others' hits, how much I get hit for, how much my pet hits for, and my misses, as well as my enemies misses.
Chat 2 is for chat: group chat, guild chat, tells, open chat, and chat channel chat.

When you log in, your main chat 1 box will be quite large, you can resize it, as you can resize and move most all your tabs and boxes. I made it smaller, so it only goes halfway across the bottom of the screen, and is also shorter from top to bottom (ala AC). To make a second chat window, right click at the top of chat 1, click on "new window". Move the 2nd window so that it is next to the first, and resize it so it all fits at the bottom evenly. To specify which kinds of chat you want in the 2nd window, right click the top of it, and click on "filters". Click on whatever filters you want in that window (ie. Guild, group, say, tell, chat channel, etc.)

I find this method is easiest, that way all communication is not lost in all the battle messages in the 1st window.

The horizontal tab at the top is important, never remove it, lol! The little icon that looks like a stick figure person, is your inventory. Opening your inventory shows not only your inventory, but also what type of character you are, your class, diety (religion), and also shows what level you are. There is also an experience bar there which shows you how much experience you need to gain a level. The Inventory tab can be opened by clicking on the stick figure once, or by hitting "i" on your keyboard.

You can move your mouse around over the top of the horizontal bar, and it will tell you what each icon means. There is an "O" shaped thing, that is your "options" tab. You can change just about anything in this tab, graphics, font, keyboard functions. I changed all my keyboard functions around so that they were like AC (ie. W to run forward, End for attack), because I was getting frustrated.

Another important tab is the "actions" tab. I always have this open. This is how you sit to meditate, camp to exit game, and use the "find" option, which as a newb you will need for a while. This is also where you will create your own hotkeys. Using "find": click find once, it brings up a box. Lets say you want to find the bank, you find the "banker" and click "find". A yellow transparent path will form, and you will follow that, it will lead you to the banker, and will disappear when you reach that NPC. Some cities, like Neriak, are very confusing at first, and span through more than one zone. You have to find your way to the next zone part of the city sometimes to find the merchant you want. For backpacks (for some reason backpacks were one of my first concerns) : click find, find the "satchel" vendor, and walah.

The Map: Learn to use the map!! It's a godsend when you figure it out. To open it use the icon that looks like "pliers" on the horizontal tab, or you can hit backspace. To the right of the map, you will see a ton of options. One of them is the "height filter". Clicking on this will make it so you can see things above you and below you on the map, instead of just things at your level. Unclicking it will turn it back to normal, so you can only see things on the map that are at your elevation. You can hold your mouse button down on the map, and drag it around, you can use the mouse wheel to make it bigger or smaller, or you can use the arrows at the right of the map. Hitting backspace again, or clicking on the pliers, will close the map. Also, you are the "arrow" on the map, as you run the arrow moves, that's because the arrow is you

Using your inventory, and NPC's: If you are coming from Asheron's Call, you are going to find this system confusing, at least I did. Forget the days of drag and drop, and double click. When you enter the game, there should be some sort of weapon in your inventory. To wield it, click it once, your mouse is now the weapon. You can either: drop it in the big empty black square in your inventory, or you can place it in the "primary" slot under that box. To put it there, simply move the mouse to that spot, and click once to "drop" it onto that square, or slot. Also, upon entering the game, you will have a note in your inventory. You need to give this to the GM (guild manager} you are standing in front of. You also need to talk to the GM, by "hailing" him. To talk to him: click him once, so he is targeted, then type "/h". In the chat screen you will see, "Hail, Guild Manager" (actually it will be his name). He will then say something in open chat, and you will see some words in [brackets like this]. You repeat the words in [brackets], they are keywords, and upon doing so, the GM will tell you more. It basically translates into "give me the note". To give him the note, click it once, holding the mouse button down briefly. Your mouse cursor is now the note, then click once on the GM. A window will open up, giving you the option to "give" or "cancel". Click "give", and you will give him the note, in turn, he gives you a nifty little shirt or robe. Check your inventory, click the shirt or robe, and place it on either your "chest" slot, or the big empty black box. You are now wearing a shiny new shirt. **This "give" option is also what you would use to give something to another player. By clicking item, then character, it will trigger a "trade window".**

Right clicking the GM will also bring up your skill window, this is where you train new skills, and raise some of the skills you deem neccesary. You won't need to worry about this for a few levels.

If you are using a merchant that buys and sells goods: Again, forgot double click, and drag and drop. In EQ, you right click the merchant, and his inventory will open up. By clicking on the items in his inventory, he will tell you the price of such items. If you have enough money, you will be able to click "buy" and purchase the item. To sell, you click on an item in your inventory, and he will tell you how much money he will give you for it. You then have an option to click on "sell", and the exchange is made.

So now, you have customized your UI to your liking, you have a shiny new shirt, and you have the map sort of figured out, what next ? Of course, you are going to want to kill stuff.

Most starting cities have zones outside full of newbling monsters. You need to find your way out of the city. The purple lines on your map represent a "zoneline" which is just an entrance to another zone. It will usually be labeled, ie.: "To Nektulos Forest". Once you find your way out, and you see a monster, you should first know how to "con". Con is short for "consider". To con something, you right click on it, a colored circle will appear around the creature or NPC. Light green = easy, you will not get xp. Light blue = pretty easy, you will get a tiny bit of xp. Dark blue = you might win the fight, you will get xp, it may be challenging though. White = even con. The creature is the same level as you, 50/50 chance of winning, good xp. Yellow = you will probably die, if you do happen to kill it, you will gain good xp. Red = you will die, you should run. When conning something, you will always see in your chat window something to the effect of, "what would you like your tombstone to say" or "ready to attack". This is how you know whether or not you should approach the creature, or avoid it. Ready to attack means if you run past it, it will chase you.

To target, conning will target the creature, so will clicking on it once, and so will the F8 key. Then hit attack, and you will find out how well you do at this point. When the monster gets to a certain health, it will try to run off. If you are an AC player, this part will confuse you at first. Your character will not automatically keep chasing the monster, you have to do that yourself. Also, by moving your character around while in auto-attack mode, you will not shut off auto-attack, so there is no need to keep hitting it. You have to be facing your target, and be close to it, so just hit auto attack and move about freely until you continue to do damage. If you play with sound on, you will notice that the music changes when you are in auto-attack mode.

To loot a monster after killing it: right click it once, your character will kneel down, and to take the items, right click on them, they will automatically move into your inventory. After you have looted what you want, click "done", and your character will stand back up.

Casting spells: click on target, click on spell once.

Linking an item into the chat window: right click the item, hold down the button, move cursor over the ID box. Click on the actual picture icon inside that box, and it will create a link in the chatscreen.
