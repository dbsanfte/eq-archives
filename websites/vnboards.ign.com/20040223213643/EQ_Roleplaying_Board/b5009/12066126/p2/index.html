The bard was weary, despite being exceptional at traveling. And she had just made a mistake that might prove to be her undoing. She knew that soon she would be dead if she could not get free of the great beast that followed on her heels. She absentmindedly sang the song that gave wind to her feet, and searched the horizon in vain for some hope of aid.

There, an outbuilding of some kind! Perhaps an inn. Once, in her travels here, she remembered that a gaurd occasionally passed near one of the inns here, and so she headed in that direction, her footsteps lagging. She hovered near death, and she knew it. So near to death was she, that she heard the breathing of the griffon she had foolishly tried to kill, as it swooped towards her.

Within moments, she realized her mistake. Upon the ground at the inn were the corpses of a guard, and a griffon. And, nearby, stood a troll, facing off with a warrior, who turned and walked away as she sped towards them. Surely, it must have been the troll who killed the guard, and there would be no help for her here, as the warrior was leaving, and she heard cries echoing from within the inn. Amris recognized them to be cries of suffering. What strange things transpired here?

Her elvish features, marked with a deep sorrow, grew taunt as exhaustion, fear, desperation, and confusion warred inside her. Cry for help, or keep running?

The Warrior's footfalls fell determined, his unseen face grim, a jaw seemingly set in steel. Sethix knew the pain of battle, he could feel the agony of the Griffon's evil talon upon his torn chest, it was like coals of fire and jags of ice driven into his chest.

He knew that time was running short, he had no Cleric at his side, for Alefrothe had remained behind with the rest of his companions in the underfoot, seeking the Queen. Nor could he turn his gaze upon his always smiling friend Arick and request the Druid's healing touch.

He was on his own, a determined Warrior, with none of the companions he so needed. He knew that the final whisps of adrenaline would soon fade, leaving him in a pain-induced stupor, vulnerable to the attacks of even the weakest of beasties that skittered about in the night.

With a growl, the proud Warrior began to question his wisdom in leaving the Inn and the Casters there-in.

His determined step faultered as yet another lance of agony pushed it's way through his barrier of adrenaline and discipline. He shrugged off the pain as only a hardened Warrior could and continued his now pain-laden march.

Darkness and starbursts tickeled the edges of his vision as he slithered to a hault and fell wearily to one knee. His gauntleted hand pressed the flawless plate covering his wound. Through the pain and fog of battle damage, he slowly came to see that this was no normal wound, this was the work of something evil, something horrible.

Sethix had seen and felt the curse falling over the splendor of the Karanas, had felt the itch and the dull ache of it's ailment. This tasted the same, with sudden shock, a question popped into his cottony thoughs, "Had Bertoxxulous come to the Commonlands, to his home of Freeport? Would the god of horror and disease lay his fetid hand down on his people as he had started in the Karanas, where even the clensing rains brought a eery green mist?"

Even through his confusion, he knew that it could be. Bertoxxulous was not so powerfull, Marr watched too closely the people of Freeport, would never let the lord of plagues a step on their lands.

Still the fear nagged at him as he limped, stumbled and crawled his way back towards the inn, fading from reality fast as his mind let go, seeking the calming abyss of darkness.

Oddly enough, it was the smell of food that brought Amris out of the unconscious state she was in. She came out of it instantly, jerked rudely to her feet. She lurched unsteadily for an instant before sitting heavily back down. The world seemed surreal to her, an instant ago, she had been near death, and now, she sat among strangers, with one handing her a meal as calmly as any daily scene. She felt peculiar, as if she were slightly out of things, set aside in some way. She watched the druid serving food calmly. The distinctive earthiness of the druid calmed Amris, a reminder of safer times, of youth. Still, the feeling of being outside held her firmly, unready to relinquish its hold.

Accepting the food, she ate quietly, her movements slow and measured. Standing slowly, she invited the injured to join her in the magical grouping that would allow her songs to weave around them. When those who wished it had joined, she began to sing. Her song was soft, her voice compelling and gentle at the same time, weaving intricate notes that danced and sparkled in the air around her, soothing injuries, encouraging muscles to mend, bones to knit. Calmly, she bandaged herself, using the silken bandages she always carried to pull the worst of the wounds together. She was lucky that the Cleric had cast the healing that she had, as the last blows of the griffin would have claimed her surely, otherwise.

Reminded of the great beast, she jerked to her feet, instantly changing the beat of her song, weaving notes that would carry her swiftly. She fairly flew out the door, suddenly sure that it lurked outside waiting to attack again. Her heartbeat roared in her ears, her stomach knotted, and she felt suddenly anxious. The others jumped, startled by her sudden movement. As she reached the door, she realized that the wreckage was the body of the griffon that had pursued her. Just as she relaxed, she saw a figure lying in the path, barely discernable to her in the shadows of the nearby trees.

She didn't hesitate. Hastening to the stranger's side, she began to bind his wounds, just as she had her own, moments ago. She raised her voice, crying out for aid, hoping that one of those inside might have within them the reserves to cast a spell that might keep this stranger from losing his life.

She busily staunched the blood that poured from greivous wounds, grabbing a nearby treelimb to carefully hold a broken limb still, all the time speaking softly to him, warning him to be still, comforting with her voice even as she saw him wince from the pain that the bandaging caused. She was afraid that, as hard as she was trying, she might be too late to save him.
