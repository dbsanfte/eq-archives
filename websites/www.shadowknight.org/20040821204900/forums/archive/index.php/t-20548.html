Josahd
The world's 'best supercomputers' still aren't fast enough to model the level of granularity necessary to truly get a good picture. They split the world up into 'cells', and faster computers means smaller cells, but splitting the entire atmosphere and oceans into oh, 1x1x1 mile cubes (just a guess, I'm sure it's better now) really doesn't give anything but a rough idea of climate changes.

And even if you COULD model every single molecule, there are factors in the environment that simply aren't known, therefore aren't possible to model.

Just because you've got a piece of software running on the fastest computer ever created DOES NOT mean it's right. Actually, since I sorta work in the modelling and simulation business, there's an axiom I've had told to me several times.... 'no model is right, but some are useful.'

Can a computer model tell you there's some sort of warming trend? maybe. Can a computer model tell you exactly what the average temperature increase will be every year for the next 100 years? Not real likely. That's the problem when you have dumbfucks interpreting the data... they try to get more out of it than it's really able to say.

BTW we aren't trusting temperature measurements from the 11th century... there was no accurate way to tell what the temperature was then. But there are ways you can see how temperature affected other things... such as plant life, chemical processes, etc, that can tell you with a pretty good degree of accuracy what the average temps were, or at least relatively speaking, if there was a warming or cooling trend from year to year.
