Melee balancing II looks in the near future, vitually guaranteeing knights' continued fall into mediocrity;
while warriors will likely deal even more damage, tank even better, and have even more flexability added by adding half the spellbook
of a hybrid to their Alt-C window.

Its quite frankly time to add something for the knights to be ABLE to tank the big dogs, if only for a few minutes.
Knights should NOT have to be "knightemized" in order to do the only job they are qualified for...
(warriors are now superior or at least equal add tanks as well...)


I will work with the following principles as a basis for the changes I suggest.
-Warriors should remain superior at absorbing consistent damage, and maintain their reason to normally tank boss encounters
for most of the encounter.
-SOME sort of tank stacking should be possible
-Knights should be DIFFERENT tanks capable of filling certain places in the Tank list due to certain talents.
-Above all, knights should be superior raid tanks over monks and the other damage dealing light-armored classes.


Suggestion:

-Add raid-tanking abilities for the knights that essentially do the same thing as the knights' primary spell lines, and that cost mana.
-These should be a level 65 discipline or AA or Spell...
-basically, every time the knight is hit, it costs him or her mana, and the damage done to them is reduced in some way.
-Paladins will auto-heal themselves every hit, and shadowknights will auto-lifetap every hit.
-This auto-mode should not be as efficient in healed/point or damage done/point as if the knight simply cast those spells normally.
-Auto-mode WILL be more efficient in healed/time than normal however...
-The basic idea is that the knight must use a large chunk (and perhaps all) of their mana to tank closer to a warrior for a short period of time.


First, the shadowknight:

Lets say auto-lifetap heals 25% of damage done to the shadowknight during its duration (1 minute max, 30 minute re-use)
Lets say it auto deals half of that back to the mob and costs half of that in mana each hit.
(I am not saying these are perfectly balanced, I just picked numbers for comparison)

I have about 7500ish mana raid buffed...

Lets say the mob is around 1000DPS... my mana will auto-lifetap for 250 healed and 125damage/sec and cost 125mana/sec
My auto-lifetap will last 60 seconds at this DPS. (total of 15k healed and 7500 damage if the SK lives for 60 seconds)

Lets say the mob is around 1300DPS... my mana will auto-lifetap for 325 healed and 162dam/sec and cost 162mana/sec
My auto-lifetap will last 46 seconds at this rate ( total of 14950 healed and 7452 damage if the SK lives for 46 seconds)


Lets say the SK is NOT tanking...

I can alternate Touch of Inny and Touch of Volatis for 470 and 350 every 12 seconds for 820 lifetap for 610 mana.
In 60 seconds, I can do that 5 times for 4100 lifetap for 3050 mana, and thus still have mana left.
In 120 seconds, I can complete 8200 lifetap for a cost of 6100 mana.
This is NOT COUNTING focus items or criticals...

Or, I could actually use real damage spells and not bother with using solely lifetaps for damage.
(the lifetaps are used for comparison...)

Now, why would a SK need this ability? To concentrate his power in a short period for the use of tanking something.
Lifetapping usually does little good on tanking these mobs... Time-equipped knights will be lucky to get a few spells out while being jacked for 2k.
Would a SK use this ability in XP groups? I doubt it. This is HIGHLY inefficient use of mana. (dead mobs don't hit back...)




next, I must apologize to the paladins, as I am less informed as to what would help them most.

I'd say a similar idea as above, but minus the damage shield.
It should Absorb more per hit (35%?), and be more efficient per mana point than the shadowknight one.


results are:
WARRIOR - absorbs 55% (plus normal 12%) discipline lasts 2 minutes, refresh 10 minutes
PALADIN - absorbs 35%, discipline lasts 1.2?? minutes, refresh 30 minutes
SHADOWKNIGHT - absorbs 25%, discipline lasts 60 minutes (max), does 12.5% damage shield. refresh 30 minutes.


Warrior is still the best, but Paladins are quite decent (>20% below warriors), Shadowknights would become shock troops used to deal 15k to the mob (HT+tank damage shield) very quickly.
(remember, that given time, their mana will deal quite a bit more damage if the mob survives longer than 1 minute)
Note, afterward, the knight is naught but a weak warrior barely capable of off tanking where as the warrior suffers no downside. (save having to wait for 10 minutes for repop).
Either Knight could stand most mobs up for a short time and be able to live given reasonable heals...

Prior to GoD, most people will agree that single group tanking is at it's most balanced point it's ever been. Warrior aggro pre-time isn't that hot still, but it's acceptable. In the spirit of healthy debate, let's be certain to not confuse issues here.

Issue #1: Did the melee changes affect GoD issues?

The answer is a resounding no, with a single exception, that unlinked discs makes it easier. These would still only be defeated through defensive, and if not a single one of the melee changes made it through, we'd still be having this discussion considering the culprit is /disc defensive, which was around prior to the melee changes.

Issue #2: What benefit did the melee changes give warriors?

It's limited to a straight 5% shaved off of each hit (from a rounded minimum). No more, no less. The 3 major areas that impacted warriors from the melee changes were... Incite, which while it's nice, isn't nearly the panacaea that it's cracked up to be, but completely acceptable as an "on demand" aggro generating skill which was desperately needed. Next was the 5% mitigation "bonus" for warriors, which in my view was justified, although I am not real happy that knights dont' have a spread between them and non-tanks. Third was the unlinking of discs, which was the #1 boon that warriors got.

Issue #3: Did warriors have a mitigation bonus prior to the melee changes?

The answer is a little more vague. Not strictly as defined by the 5% mitigation type (i.e. now warriors actually take hits for different numbers across the entire 1-20 line, whereas before the 20 values were identical), but there's evidence that they made a little greater use out of AC than anyone else, which you referenced in the "taking hits more smoothly". It seems to be that there's data to support the notion that warriors took less high rolls than others. The interesting thing though is there's also data which supports the opposite view (notably, the cleric parses which show a similar mitigation spread throughout the values with identical AC). I think that enough data exists to support the theory that warriors "use(d)" AC a little more effectively than others. The difference is relatively non-dramatic though.

Finally, the issue arises of "warriors have more AC/HP". The truth of this is, yes, warriors can gain about 1khp more than their knight counterparts with enough AA. Again, I don't find this inherently problematic. AC on the other hand isn't as cut and dried, since warriors and knights have an identical opportunity to gain AC to the same level, since the defensie skill is identical. The only real reason you'd find AC variations would be to account for knights selecting gear to support both sides of their character development, and sacrificing AC. Again, I don't find this all that problematic since the opportunity exists to match warrior AC, and in some cases even surpass it. While a warrior tends to have less stats to bother themselves with, and can be less picky with gear, most gear at the levels we're referring to dont' have a whole lot of differences, and are beneficial to both warrior and knight, and are popular choices among both.

Total hitpoints are becoming less and less of a benefit, due to a couple of factors... first being the nature of the GoD healing paradigm, second because of the hard caps on complete heal. Essentially, the warrior with 1k more hitpoints will delay the inevitable by less than a single hit by mobs of the environment that we're referring to, and those hitpoints are rarely completely refreshed any more, so it's merely delaying the inevitable.

Xanathol, I'm going to try to address your points in as polite and simple manner as possible. In order to understand the problem with what you propose, we first have to examine some of the most basic issues in the game.

First, we have to agree with the premise that warriors are supposed to be undisputed tanking kings, especially for RAID BOSSES. If you disagree with this, you are in the wrong forum, because you will not find much sympathy from the vast majority of serious knights nor from ANY warrior. That does not mean knights should not be "viable" tanks for raid bosses, just that warriors should always have a distinct advantage on any raid boss.

Second, you have to recognize the incredible gulf that exists in aggro-generation between pre-Time (and especially pre-elemental) warriors and post-Time warriors. The weapons warriors can get in PoTime are game-changing for them. See Brael's logs on aggro generation to get even a small understanding of just how bad pre-Time warriors had it prior to the addition of new disciplines like Incite/Bellow. Yes, these new abilities do help ALL warriors, but the gulf in aggro capabilities between knights and warriors was so huge to begin with that it's like trying to put out a fire with a water pistol.

Third, you have to recognize that when choosing an appropriate tank for a raid boss, hit points, AC, and disciplines (surviveability) are not all that have to be factored in. Of equal or even greater importance is for the tank to be able to hold the attention of the mob being tanked. It does absolutely no good to have 3000 AC and 15k HP if the mob isn't beating on YOU! Can we all agree on that?

Now, in your normal pre-Time raid, warriors have always held enough of an advantage with their superior HP and mitigation abilities that it would take a knight of incredible gear to warrant a switch from a warrior to a knight tank. To put it another way, our (knight's) superior aggro abilities were never enough to justify the incredible difference in healing it would require for us to survive a boss mob, pre-Time/elemental. The only knights that could realistically challenge a warrior for the MT role on raids were those with gear that was way beyond what is required for the encounter itself. In other words, for a knight to tank a mob such as the Avatar of War, he would need to be wearing all of the best gear available in all of Velious...in every slot...and maybe even some Luclin gear. And even then, he would still require more healing than an equivalently geared warrior.

The reason why a warrior's MT role CAN be threatened in such a situation is because there is a point in a guild's progression where they have an almost infinite capacity to heal. In other words, they have enough clerics in the CH chain, and with sufficient FT and mana pools, that they can literally heal a knight almost as effectively as a defensive warrior.

So, in pre-Time scenarios, giving a knight a defensive discipline closes the gap between warriors and knights surviveability enough to justify choosing a knight over a warrior for their additional aggro abilities that are WAY above a warrior's in equivalent gear.

Now let's factor in PoTime and GoD. With PoTime, warriors can actually close the gap quite a bit between knights in terms of aggro capabilities. Furthermore, there is a point where additional aggro becomes nothing but insurance against bizaar and unusual events. When you reach that point, you effectively kill the argument that a warrior's MT role is threatened, because they STILL surviev better and they NOW can generate sufficient aggro so as not to be a liability when MT'ing.

THAT is why a defensive discipline should be limited to only people that will use it almost exclusively in GoD and other post-Time encounters. You simply can't give it to pre-Time knights without seriously threatening the warrior's most important role, unless of course you further increase their pre-Time aggro capabilities to be even closer to a knight's.

Another thing that has to be considered is the great difference between the raid environment and the single-group environment. In a raid encounter, there are far more "roles" to be fulfilled than in single-group encounter, typically. Raids need good pullers, add-tanks, CoH, mana dumps, resist songs, spot-healing, heals for healers, hot-rezzes, and lots of other things.

In most single groups, many raid roles are completely marginalized, such as CoH and add-tanking. Pulling can usually be done by multiple classes, because the mobs are tuned for that, whereas on raids, things like lull-pulling aren't near as useful, and FD pulling becomes more handy. On the flipside, certain roles become even more important in single groups. For example, almost all single group encounters CENTER around tanking, slowing, and healing, with CCing coming in slightly behind.

What this means is that for single-group encounters, the versatility brought to the table by many "hybrid" classes for raids becomes less valuable in single groups. Yes, pallies can rez in single groups too, but in the traditional single-group encounter, pallies aren't NEEDED to rez that often, whereas on raids they rez quite frequently.

Furthermore, there is much more room on raids to find a role for everyone. When you can bring 72 people to a raid, it's easy to find a spot to make a pally or SK useful. In a single group, when a knight's utility value has already been reduced, we have to rely more on our primary ability - tanking - in order to be attractive to the group. There simply isn't enough room in a 6-person group to fit in every utility role that exists in a raid. And this becomes even more extreme in GoD than anywhere else. You HAVE to have a stout tank. You HAVE to have at least one cleric. You pretty much HAVE to have a spot healer. And you HAVE to have a slower. That's 4 slots in the group taken up right there. Rogues are still heavily desired, because they bring fantastic DPS (primary role), along with great utility for CR and scouting (secondary role).
