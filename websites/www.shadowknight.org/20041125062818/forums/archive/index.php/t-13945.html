Groff Gorespiller
According to "Mail Call", a series produced by the Discovery channel (or the History channel or TLC or some such other informational cable tv networ), the average weight of a suit of plate mail armor, including padding and chain mail underneath the bendy parts was around 75-85 pounds. While the armor was balanced as best they could, it was by no means a perfect system, and obviously the larger pieces weighed more than the smaller ones. This is why generally there were no covering over the backs of the thighs, calves and upper arms. He helms in and of themselves are supposedly about 10 pounds and limit visibility considerably. The curiass was the heaviest piece and having seen R.Lee Emry fall on his ass with one of them on, I'd say that you'd have a considerable difficulty getting back up while being beaten upon by an opponent.

However, the biggest danger when falling off of a horse in the midst of combat is not from your opponents, nor from dehydration, malnutrition or delerium. It's the damn horse. Heavy cavalry horses were often shoed with extremely nasty shoes which could crush, crease or cut through varying qualities of plate and chain mail, the horse itself weighed much more than the rider, not including the saddle and all the other crap they strapped to them, the battlefield was a chaotic place which often frightened horses into near blind terror especially when they were without rider to control them, and most importantly, if you got hit hard enough to knock you from your horse, you probably damn near pulled the beast down on top of you.

Oh, and contrary to popular imagination, most mounted soldiers did not wear full platemail, nor were they "knights". The light cavalry was used much more often than heavy cavalry, was less armored, quicker and more effective against ground troops. Heavy cavalry were most often used against pikemen, in situations where their army was seriously outnumbered, or in the event of the other army pulling out it's heavy cavalry for a charge.
