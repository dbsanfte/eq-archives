The Story of Dosoloth (Part 1)

I am in hiding. I am coiled, a serpent with umbral steel scales, and I am waiting for him to rise again. I know he will. And I will cut him down once more, under the black rage of the Father. Innoruuk's will be done.

Of the history of my young life I know little: I have lived but twenty years upon barren soil. What I can recall from the cloudy remnants of a child's memory I shall tell you, should you care to listen. These words are the first and last I shall ever chronicle.

I have no knowledge of my mother or my father, save that they were Teir`dal, and exiled from Neriak to the granite walls of Castle Mistmoore to serve the Great One, Mayong. I was taken from Lord Mistmoore's abode as an infant, and left to die in the wastes of Dagnor's Cauldron. Apparently I was an unexpected arrival. My savior, who I would grow up to know as my father, swept me up from under the pallid moon and to his benighted Estate, known to the sparse local peasantry as Unrest.

My father was a man composed of faint luminescence and hushed whispers, spectral and cold. His chill touch tried to comfort me as I cried for living warmth, but he was much too far from life to grant succour from my infant terrors. I matured in the cold.

He was a man of honour in life, and so in death. When I grew old enough to speak, I learned the Codes of Chivalry. When my arms grew strong, he mercilessly taught me the art of war in the courtyard of the Estate, using his frozen fists to scourge me should I tire or drop my blade. When he was angry, his eyes could tear holes through my pain, through fatigue and frustration, though above all I raised my weapon after countless hours of practice out of my devotion to my lord. Lastly he taught me faith to the Father, Innoruuk. In the darkness of the Estate's cellars, amoungst the cobwebs and living shadows, cloistered within a crypt which festered with those mortals who hated enough to continue hating even after death, I spoke to my god. And his voice was terrible with the howling of the mad and undying, crippling in its intensity yet innervating in its ire. I buckled before the rising rage of the father while my very will flared as a feather ignites before a holocaust. I was branded by the Father, a furious welt upon my heart my sign of allegiance, and I knew upon the hissing strike of the brand which scarred me that I was his vessel for all time.

Upon reaching the age of sixteen I bade my father farewell, and the gates of Unrest closed behind me for all time. I took with me my lord's red-bladed sword and a suit of plate he himself had forged for my use in the bowels of Unrest's furnace. I lusted for living blood then, in my youth. I headed to the Faydark to quench that thirst.

I had not seen a living being my entire life -- only the dead. The first living being I had ever set my eyes upon was not a Koada`Dal: she was dark elven.

Her name was Sularyn, of the Spurned, she said. Her skin shone in the pasty light of the Faydark moon, the only beauty in a place of oppression and misplaced arrogance, the homeland of the Koada`Dal. Her verdant eyes struck a spark in my heart I had never felt before in my father's house. I took her in my arms and held her. She was warm! I was shocked to feel her soft body in my arms, her heart beating strongly under her breast. In all the world I swore no living being was as beautiful as Sularyn, my lady. I swore that I would take her back to our rightful home in the proud city of Neriak, although at the time I had no idea where the fabled bastion of the Teir`Dal was. It did not matter really: we would find our way home.

She whispered in my ear that she must prepare to depart, that in the morning we both would flee from Faydark hand-in-hand, but first she must prepare herself away from me in a secret place. My chest ached to see her leave even though we had only met hours ago, and she would return after daylight passed in favor of the protection nightfall grants the dark elven race. My pain was so great I could not bear her to escape my sight. I followed her.

Through the woods, past glens of wildflowers I followed her. She left a trail of glimmering sliver, like argent pollen, blowing behind her gracefully and sweetly upon the midnight breeze. She found a road and followed it; I did the same, from a distance. She finally reached a darkwood tree which grew alone in a glen of fragrant cedars, and pressed her hand to its rough bark. The bark shimmered into nothingness -- a door nestled in the trunk. She opened it. And in the strange light of the tree's inner room I saw her indigo skin slide away as a serpent's skin to reveal soft white limbs and a mane of pale yellow hair. Sularyn was certainly not of my kind. She was one of those few who could whisper words of artifice to the skies and mask their true identity. Sularyn's beauty still scorched me, but the flame was different: this was my sworn enemy standing lightly in her place of enchantment. This was Koada`Dal.

I died there in the darkness, my heart riven by betrayal. Behind a cedar tree I wept fierce tears, blazing and molten tracks of my anguish down my cheeks. I grasped for the one safeguard I had been equipped with as a Knight of the Father: my faith. I half whispered, half moaned quiet prayers to Innoruuk, for council.

And he responded, his pronged and silvered tongue a hair's breath from my ear it seemed.

"Kill her."
