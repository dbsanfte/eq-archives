    Noam Chomsky Interview

Check out this interesting interview with Noam Chomsky at Infoshop.org.

From the Interview:

At first the US used the word "crusade," but it was quickly pointed out that if they hope to enlist their allies in the Islamic world, that is a serious mistake, for obvious reasons. The rhetoric therefore shifted to "war." The Gulf war of 1991 was called a war. The bombing of Serbia was called a "humanitarian intervention," by no means a novel usage. That was a standard description of European imperialist ventures in the 19th century. To cite some more recent examples, the major recent scholarly work on "humanitarian intervention" cites three examples of "humanitarian intervention" in the immediate pre-World War II period: Japan's invasion of Manchuria, Mussolini's invasion of Ethiopia, and Hitler's takeover of the Sudetenland. The author of course is not suggesting that the term is apt; rather, that the crimes were masked as "humanitarian." But the pretext of "humanitarian intervention" cannot be used in the normal way in the present case. So we are left with "war."

To call it a "war against terrorism," however, is simply more propaganda, unless the "war" really does target terrorism. But that is plainly not contemplated. Perhaps I may quote political scientist Michael Stohl: "We must recognize that by convention -- and it must be emphasized _only_ by convention -- great power use and the threat of the use of force is normally described as coercive diplomacy and not as a form of terrorism," though it commonly involves "the threat and often the use of violence for what would be described as terroristic purposes were it not great powers who were pursuing the very same tactic," in accord with the literal meaning of the term. Under the (admittedly, unimaginable) circumstances that Western intellectual culture were willing to adopt the literal meaning, the war against terrorism would take quite a different form, along lines spelled out in extensive detail in literature that does not enter the respectable canon.

    What's in a Label
I myself never wear T-shirts with logos. I don't like the idea of advertising brand names, especially when I have no idea who is behind the brand. I come from a school of thought that suggests if you wear someone else's logo your subscribing to their beliefs and ideals and letting the world know where you stand. Once people whore the "logo" of their king, country, religion or other group to show they "belonged" to that group. People like that sense of belonging and so we continue this tradition in the displaying of labels. But in modern society labels are nothing more than a display of a demographic. Many do not even considering who is behind the logo and what they represent. Logos and allegances are switched as easily as buying a new shirt or changing who makes your latte.

Lauren Raheja in an article in the Star Tribune discusses the duality between a corporate entity and the image they attempt to portray using Urban Outfitters and Chipotle restaurants as examples. I knew of Urban Outfitter's very conservative (and anti-gay) corporate leanings way back. I am not in the business of telling people where to shop and who to support, that is an individual descision. But I do urge we all take a moment and check out who we are supporting, but even that is a daunting task as no one is really forthcoming about their personal ideology when they can hide behind a corporate image.

It doesn't seem like a big deal to many that they buy a shirt from say, the Gap. But consider how much of the money you gave makes it to the person who made it, delivered it, sold it or even put it on the shelf? Then think about how much goes to some executive and where is his or her money going? Does the company finance local community projects or do they donate money to political campaigns? Why does it matter?

Well lets say you paid $25 for a shirt. What did it cost the retailer to make this shirt? An informal guess based upon my experiences in retail is that the final price is 50%-100% higher than the cost to the final retailer. That means $12.50 is split between the manufacturer and retailer. That isn't much but lets go further. Most manufactured products cost about 50% to manufacture of what they are sold to the retailer for. So we now widdle $6.75 out and split that between the manufacturing, shipping and packaging, etc. The other $6.75 goes to the company, which is used to pay executives and staff.

Now lets reverse things a bit. Say there are 150 stores, and each store sells about 2 of these shirts a day, or 300 shirts per day. Doesn't seem like much but lets go a step further and average out the sale of this shirt to a lower 1 shirt per day over a 3 month sales season (90 days to make things easier). Well now we have 13,500 shirts! It's likely they made many more to save on costs and are dumping the excess into odd job lots or selling them off to overseas markets, but we will not deal with that.

At $6.75 profit per shirt, this company has made $91,125. Not shabby for a run of a single shirt. Now what if the company's HQ is in say Vermont? Vermont might want to enact a higher corporate tax, or maybe enact stricter hiring guidelines. Maybe the CEO and a couple buddies on the board are against a specific group or maybe the government is getting tougher on their importing the shirts from soem third world country and they are going to have to pay workers $.50 per shirt instead of $.25. So the CEO begins to donate his own money and gets the company to make corporate donations to a candidate that is amicable to their line of thinking. Maybe he even goes as far as donting wardrobes or even letting the candidates use his shipping warehouse for a friendly photo-op.

There are lots of ways how your small support combines to make a big difference. Money isn't the only thing that supports a company. Public consumption of a product is an endorsment, and trust me anything to promote a product is good advertising. Why do you think every wrapper from a fast food chain has it's logo? Why do clothes have a label? So that you can advertise as you consume! By purchasing and using a product you overtly support it and the company behind the product.

Should this politian write you a thank you note for your $6.75 donation?

    Can I Have What He's Drinking?
I can only conclude he is drunk or stoned. After reading the speech President Bush gave on Monday (see www.whitehouse.gov) one can see how Mr. Bush has no concept of how he fits into history.

Forget that such a speech is about six months too late, or that there was no details given as to how any of the proposed goals will be achieved. Let’s not even discuss the issues of the U.S.’s new plan to incorporate the very militias we have been fighting into the new Iraqi army and police force. These are all issue that bother just about everyone.

What baffles me is how Mr. Bush can continue to grasp at the straws of “weapons of mass destruction” (which really applies to N. Korea and various other states), deposing tyrannical rule, and the “war on terror” and even bring democracy as causes for this war. I am still not sure how our government was duped into going along with invading a foreign country in the first place. His speech spoke not at all about his actions, or his cabinet’s actions in this whole affair. Instead we are supposed to believe “this is the world as we find it” and that “we did not seek this war on terror.”

So we didn’t invade a country which had no known connection to terror since 1994? A country that attempted to comply with rather harsh investigations and sanctions and still attempt to keep some show of dignity? In the face of contrary evidence the Bush administration continues to maintain their reasons for going to war against a very poorly armed nation. And then “some of Saddam's elite guards shed their uniforms and melted into the civilian population.” What did Mr. Bush and Co. expect? What invasion ever did not face some sort of a guerilla insurgency? Is the president completely delusional? Does it make sense for use to cry foul when others don’t play by our rules?

“They hope to wear out the patience of Americans.” Forget them, how about you Mr. Bush? You are wearing out my patience pretty quickly.

“All along, some have questioned whether the Iraqi people are ready for self-government, or even want it.” Well I suspect they do. Is this something that needed reiterating? Did anyone actually have to ask if they wanted self rule? The best part is we are still not giving it to them. We have declared that we will turn over Iraq to its own devices with advisories of various governments but we have yet to even begin gathering these advisors. We certainly won’t actually go and ask for advice. And we are still forcing the Iraqi authorities to report to us. We are not even removing any troops. What kind of pull out is this?

It isn’t one. We are lying to everyone. And no one is fooled, unless you’re an American citizen. In that case you probably didn’t even see, hear or read the speech, because you were watching American Idol or even worse were absorbed by “The Swan” – a TV show about low self esteem women who get plastic surgery and compete in a beauty pageant after.

Maybe I should be happy no one saw this.
