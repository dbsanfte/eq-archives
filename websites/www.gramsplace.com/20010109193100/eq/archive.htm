-(This person, like many of us, has obviously made the laughable attempt at playing a straight wizard or cleric in UO.)

The Lag Monster
By Razor

"Man this game is too lagged"

How many times have you said this while playing a game online? The dreaded screen freeze - as you watch your character killed and then looted in slow motion.

--(Who, me? Never. The lag in UO always gave me the chance to do my taxes between sword thrusts.)

I am a network engineer. I have spent the last 11 years designing, implementing, and supporting networks. I dont have a cure for network lag, but I can explain some of the causes for it.

Ive been playing multi-player games since the 1980s. I got my first taste of network gaming playing Muds Multi User Dungeons). I havent played a single player game since!

"What the hell is a MUD?"

Mud is a software program, running on a server, that accepts connections from multiple users. Each user functions independently. He or she can interact with other users, killing creatures, socializing, or even falling in love and getting married? Well some did. Im a fighter, not a lover (evil grin). The game is entirely TEXT. You have to be able to read (and in some cases read fast) to play. Each area is a square, which contains a room description, creatures, and objects. Soon after Muds came Moos, Mucks, Mushs, Mugs to name a few.

--(For those ancient PC users out there, just set the Wayback Machine to the days of Infocom's famous original Zork series. Except this time it's real people who can't understand your sentences, instead of it being the game itself.)

"Were you lagged while playing Muds?"

Yes. The lag monster was around in the old days as well, although the cause, for the most part, was for a different reason. Lag from over utilized servers was more of the cause then.

--(Funny you should say that, since it's the servers that are the major cause of lag today, too!)

"Ok. So you had lag. What the heck is it".

Games on the Internet use TCP and UDP datagrams to carry information from the Server to the Client (the client being your home PC). Lag is the interval between the time a piece of data leaves the server, travels across the Internet cloud, and arrives, and is processed or reassembled on your PC. Let us follow this process from start to finish. Ill try to keep it simple, so bare with me.

You just loaded EverQuest and installed the game. YEA!!!!! (hey its my story I can dream if I want to!) You hit the connect icon. Hopefully by now you have already connected to your Internet service provider or ISP. This connection between you and your ISP is one hop. The Internet is full of these hops to get from one destination to another.

There are also multiple paths from point A to point B that can be traveled to get to a destination. That is where routing protocols come in, but thats a future issue. Now your PC sends a message requesting a connection from the game server out in the cloud somewhere. When a TCP connection first opens, both ends can send the maximum datagram size they can handle. The smaller of these numbers is used for the rest of the connection. A connection is then established after a series of messages back and forth. Now game information is broken up into datagrams and sent out. It might take 15 datagrams to complete one piece of information. These datagrams are numbered so that the receiver can reassemble the data in the correct order so it makes sense.

So lets say a trace route shows that its 22 hops from your PC to the Game server, with an average ping (a program that uses ICMP replies) of 220ms. Thats the time it takes a packet to leave your PC, travel to the destination address, and back again.

Each of these 15 datagrams leaves your PC, travels across the Internet through each of these 22 hops, and arrives at the server, where they are reassembled in order, and forwarded up to the application layer. Then answered and sent back to you.

Okay now with this awareness of networking, lets say that for some reason; one of the 15 datagrams did not reach the server. The server has 14 of the 15 datagrams. He must now send a request back for the lost datagram while holding the other 14.

Although these processes happen in milliseconds, you can see if a packet is lost, or delayed repeatedly, it can really cause a lot of problems in your game.

--(Whoa, whoa, whoa... suddenly the term "lag" is synonymous with "latency?" Latency in TCP communication is one possible cause of lag, but they certainly aren't the same thing. To the game player, lag is defined as anything that causes their gameplay to pause or cease unnaturally. Be it network latency, server load, or Donna Dixon posing in a string bikini, it's all irrelevant.)

"Ok. Now Im totally lost. What does this all mean?"

All Im saying is to truly evaluate your connection to a game server you have to look at more than just the ping time, but also be aware of the packet loss, and take into consideration your through put. For example is your modem 9600-baud? Also keep in mind that not only does the game server have to reassemble datagrams, but your PC must do this as well. If your computer is loaded down with background processes like ICQ, Real Audio, News Ticker, and Netscape Mail checker all going at once, not only are these taking up resource space, but they are also processing packets of their own! You could be the biggest cause of lag.

--(Pardon me while I unload my breakfast... unless you're running Windows 95 on a 486 or a very early generation Pentium, your background internet utility processes will have a minimal effect on the performance of the game you're playing. I suppose it would make a difference if you're downloading umpteen files at a time, but how often does this happen when you're gaming? As far as packet parsing goes, this burden on a workstation's processor is like a flea on a camel's hump. You only encounter a heavy workload in this manner when you're on a server and it has to parse thousands or millions of packets at once.)

Also run trace routes through your ISP. Check the amount of packet loss from you and your ISP. You can even check this information between your ISPs ISP. If the packet loss between these connections are greater than 3%, I would find a new Internet service provider. In addition the average ping time between these should also be below 40ms.

--(Definitely good advice. If, for some ungodly reason, you're losing packets from your ISP and its local gateways, you've got to be hating life. But this should be an issue for you only if your ISP's technical support e-mail address is "satanic.demonspawn@slownet.rip-off.org".)

I sometimes get frustrated when I hear someone say, "This game sucks! I cant deal with this freaken lag!" while not knowing what "lag" is, or its source. EverQuest has stated they are keeping the traffic minimal enough that even a 28.8 modem will be able to handle, which means fewer packets for your computer to process as well. A focus on coding an application around latency is a very good approach to client / server communication. So when you get youre copy, and start playing EverQuest this spring [another evil grin], and you happen to experience some periods of lag, remember back on this article, and check those hop counts and packet loss ratios. Look to see what applications youre running in the background. Dont just run around screaming, "LaaAAAAAGGGGGGgggg" spamming my screen.

--(I always get frustrated when I see someone write an article like this where they address an issue I believe is completely non-sequitor. Latency due to slow and unreliable internet connection is always a factor, and there's little anyone can do about it until our communications standards improve -- true. However, the cause of 90% of our woes in experiencing lag in massively multiplayer gaming is not latency... hence the usage of the term "server side lag" ["SSL" for short]. SSL is a correctable issue, one way or another. Be it through the coding of the server software, or through the usage policy, there are ways to correct it. There exist very few valid excuses for users experiencing SSL from a company making hundreds of thousands of dollars a month in subscription fees. I find the comments Brad made about EverQuest to be promising. We'll cross our fingers and see if they can deliver.)

First off, thanks for writing, Angus!

Actually, I did see that line, and it caused me some distress at first. I don't like the idea of magic-users being able to cast spells at will, while fully equipped with an M-16, or equivalent weapon, pointed at your head as well. It seemed wreckless, and an unwise thing to do on SI's part, considering the character class system.

I was about to comment on it, when I reread what Brad said about limiting what class can wield and use which items. Reflecting back on my AD&D games, I remember how many weapons a wizard could use... I can count them on my left hand. Predominantly, all they could use were daggers (depending on your DM's particular deviation of the rules). I know I may be assuming a lot, but my guess is that Brad and the team have made it so that wizards in EQ have roughly the same level of restriction.

This being so, a wizard's ability to defend him- or herself by non-magical means is limited to begin with. We haven't been told a great deal about how magic is going to work in EQ, but assuming there's a time delay in spellcasting, coupled with the idea that a magic-user must have their hands free to cast the spell, they would end up being at the mercy of their opponent for this span of time.

Conversely, if they were allowed to have their little dagger in-hand while casting spells, it at least provides them a means of keeping the enemy at bay (barely) until their spell is completed. To me, this allows for a greater balance of power overall. On one side, the wizard isn't helpless during spellcasting, and on the other side the opponent isn't attacked by an enormous Two-Handed Sword Of Ultimate Death while being bombarded by fireballs either.

This, I believe, is why weapons can be equipped during spellcasting in EverQuest. In Ultima Online, where equippability of items rests soley on strength, it results in the warrior/mage character stigma we're all painfully familiar with. In the case of EQ, where item equippability is determined by class, level, race, and eyebrow color, I think it will be sufficient to keep the classes seperate from each other.
