A Brief History of My Life

I've been playing video games for a long time. How long you may ask? I can remember a time when the number of MegaMan games was less than two, or even less than one for that matter. I have saved money to buy Atari 2600 games. I have dragged my mother into my room to take a picture of my high score in Pitfall so I could get an 'Official Pitfall Harry Jungle Explorer Club' Patch*. I know that the little guy in Pitfall is named Harry. I know that Mario used to date a girl named Daisy before that bitch princess showed up. I used to read advertisements for Atari 2600 tennis games that proclaimed "Tennis so real, the ball has a SHADOW!"

* The picture of the television screen was far too blurry and did not show the score at all. I was emotionally crushed. Maybe someone in charge of the Official Pitfall Harry Jungle Explorer Club will read this and make me an honorary member.

I don't feel like blowing out the inside of an Atari cartridge for 15 minutes so I can play it without that BRRRZMZMZMM sound that the TV makes when you try to stick something in the Atari that doesn't belong, like half a can of Mt. Dew. I don't think it's worth all that just so I can get a screen shot that 3 people will spend 2 seconds looking at. So, to the right is a picture of my highest score in Marvel vs. Capcom 2. Sure, it's not Pitfall, but one BILLION points beats the shit out of any score I ever got in in Pitfall. And ABBA never showed up at my house and got the fourth best score on Pitfall either. It's also got Wolverine in it and I'm pretty sure he could kick Pitfall Harry's ass any day of the week.
I have also read about games. Oh my God, have I read about games. I own more pounds of magazines than I do pounds of automobile. They're not even good magazines either! It's not like boxes and boxes of Playboys that retain their value over time, these are Nintendo Power, and Sega Visions, and GamePro, etc. The content of these magazines is questionable at best but the Fan Mail sections are *almost* the stupidest thing I've ever read.

For instance, to the left is Steve Bell, the greatest gamer that ever lived. His tips for Zelda were to "collect all the hearts and weapons you can find". And to think that I'd been leaving them on the ground for years! If my brother hadn't told me his tip for the game: "You're not supposed to run into the damned monsters.", I might still be trying to figure out why the game was so ridiculously easy.

When I read this letter from Steve again yesterday, I got excited. If that's all it takes to get published in a magazine, I could send in my Pitfall tips (Don't fall into holes. Collect all the treasure you find and timing is everything! Don't use a polaroid camera to take a picture of a television screen.) to an Atari magazine and plug my web site at the same time. But alas, no one publishes an Atari magazine any more.

Does any one have Steve Bell's e-mail address? He's the only one who would understand where I'm coming from here.

Of course, the people that wrote these magazines were often just as clueless as the people that read them. One issue of Nintendo Power announced that PCs don't yet match the "graphic power" that the Nintendo 64 posesses. And just to be fair, one issue of Sega Visions claimed that just because the Genesis version of Street Fighter 2 took up 24 megs of space on the cartridge compared the Super Nintendo versions' 20 megs, the Genesis version was superior.

The magazines, although aimed at the 13 and under crowd, printed articles that listed specs such as internal bus speeds, the size of the cache dedicated to textures, and the CD data transfer rate. That's all great information if you're programming a new game for the system, or modding your Saturn to illegally play Karaoke CDs, but for nearly all of the Sega Visions readership, they could have listed the specifications of a curling iron and no one would have been the wiser.

The advertisements in these magazines were no better. They were totally unrealistic as is the one to the right. I mean, what kid has wicker furniture in his bed room? Besides that, suppose you could actually find a beret that would fit a nine year old, would you let anyone you know go out of the house dressed like the Big Game Hunter over there? I don't know if it's the hole in the knee of his jeans or that semi-dangerous looking Nintendo Zapper he's holding in his one gloved hand, but something about this kids' outfit says "Beat me up, mercilessly."

Sunsoft apparently felt differently and included this at the bottom of the advertisement. Remember, it's FREE!

I had a point in mind when I started this but I've rapidly gotten away from it. The point is/was I've spent countless hours (years) reading about and playing games but almost no time talking about them. I don't mean talking as in writing long and boring articles and posting them on an inane website. I mean actually speaking about video games with people I see everyday. Sure there were the ever present Nintendo vs. Sega "debates" in the lunchroom when I was in middle school and they usually ended in fist fights or "Yo momma" jokes or even crusty cremed potatoes in the shape of a scoop of icecream being flung across the room. But as far as video games entering my normal day to day conversation, it just doesn't happen.

Video games are a hobby much like golf, fishing, or building those tiny ass boats inside bottles. People won't hesitate for an instant to tell you, in great detail, about the big fish they almost caught last weekend or the score of the baseball game they watched, or how many rolls of pennies they stuffed down a stipper's G-string but when I've just spent a weekend playing video games and someone asks me at work what I did over the weekend, my reply is almost always, "Oh not much." Wanna know why? Because I sound like a psychotic geek when I talk about video games.

Try these suggestions or just say them out loud as you read and you'll understand how ridiculous it sounds. The next time someone asks you what you did last weekend, tell them, "Well, I managed to get my dark elf necromancer up to level 44 and buy his new spells. I've got a really awesome pet skeleton now and this other spell lets me turn into a skeleton. It's a lot of fun standing around as a skeleton and it makes my mana come back faster!" Most people just back away with that look in their eyes that says, "He's gonna snap one of these days and I hope I'm not around when he does."

Describe Space Channel 5 to someone who doesn't play video games. "Well you play as Ulala. She's a news reporter from the future with purple hair and a plastic orange skirt. You have to save the hostages from the aliens by out dancing them. Left...up....left...CHU CHU CHU hahahahahahah" If the person you're talking to doesn't say, "Oooooooookay" then I'd look out for men in white coats.

Call a department store that sells video games. Store clerk's comments in red. "Do you sell Seaman?" "Very funny. If you call here again, we'll trace the number." "Not semen, SEA..........MAN, it's a Dreamcast game!" "No, I don't SEE MAN! I don't think you see either!" And, since Abbot and Costello are dead, I'll stop there.

My best internet tips: Use a mouse. Click all the blue letters you see. Do your best and timing is everything!
