1-4:  Rarely can an enemy of your level or lower take you out at levels 1-4.
Your DD won't do much to them so save the mana for healing.  You can keep up
Endure Fire if you want, but it only really helps against another druid.  If
you're fighting a melee class, Snare Snare Snare.  It's then time to keep
moving and dart in and melee and then step back and hit them with a DD or 2.
Flame Lick should be your first spell after Snare.  It won't help too much
against a warrior in patchwork but every little bit helps.  Against a caster
use Flame Lick first.  With their already low AC this spell will make
hitting them easy.  At some point they have to stand still to cast.   You
should rarely miss them.  Save your mana to heal and then snare them when
they try to run.

5-8:  At these levels you'll find you can't melee with tanks and you can't
kill with spells.  Use the methods in the previous section.  The only real
difference is that the fights will last longer.  At level 5-6 do not even
try to use your new DD spell, Burst of Fire, against another player.  Too
much mana, too long to cast, and too many fizzles.  A couple of fizzles in a
row will get you killed.  Keep your skills maxed to reduce fizzles.  Before
you go picking fights, practice Whirling Wind.  Get your Conjuration up to a
respectable level and then you're ready to use it on another player.  It
does ok damage/mana and the stun can help and does disrupt spells.  When
you're in trouble never try to Camo.  Gate Instead.  Even though you have
Ward Summoned, don't ever bother with pets.  It costs too much and is slow
to cast, so just snare the pet and go after the caster.  You can use
Grasping Roots if you're picking on easy prey but it gets resisted too much
for me.  You can try to heal yourself in a fight but the damage you're
taking at this level will make this useless.  The quicker they're dead the
less damage you take.

9-12:  This is where you really find out what a druid can do in PVP.  Just
be careful at level 9.  You've got the spells but not the mana.  It's
depressing to cast off a couple of Ignites and find you don't have any left
for healing.  The best tactic I've found is to buff yourself with everything
and then melee while casting Whirling Wind at them.  Make sure to Snare
first.  They'll have to melee you at that point and your damage shield
really kicks in.  Firefist becomes necessary to kill tanks and Endure Cold
is something you have to have against Wizards.   Whirling Wind will get
resisted some.  Some players use Ignite instead but I almost never seem to
fully hit with it.  It's alot of mana to use for 15 pts of dmg.  Once your
short buffs run out you're in trouble.  Try a mana dump or just get out of
there unless you've got them really hurt.  I've had almost dead warriors
drop me from half life to nothing while I tried to finish them with my
staff.  When you're tricks run out, it's time to leave.

It's been fun to fight other players with a druid.  They're never really
sure how to come at you.  The biggest problem that I've had are clerics.
They're the ones for us to watch out for.  Just when you think you've got
them, a couple of heals....

When people talk of good classes to use in a PvP environment you
typically hear about the hybrids (SK, PAL) or the power-casters (WIZ,
NECRO)
Smart players will often mention Clerics or Encanters, and rightfully
so.

But dont overlook the Druid. Druids have an amazing range of tools at
their disposal. Lets look at a few of them and their impact in a PvP
setting:

 Equipment:

1) Weapons available- 1h and 2h blunt and 1h slashing with a scimitar.
Druids have access to some of the best, readily attainable weapons in
the game. Including the sreaming mace, bloodorge hammer, various fine
steel, combine and others such as the Ogre War Maul etc. This far
outweighs what other mage type classes are capable of getting and using.
Giving us a huge melee advantage when compared to other pure casters,
even the cleric may be at a disadvantage when comparing variety of
weapons available. Many of the best weapons for mage types are also
usuable by us. While few of ours are usuable by them.



2) Armor available- While the cleric certainly has us beat in this
department, no other class with spells even remotely as powerful as ours
comes close. Wearing up to leather level armor sure beats wearing cloth
for 35 levels then finally getting some goissmer or other cloth based
mage armor. From the start we can be equipped with patchwork, leather,
studded, or if we have a kind tradesman friend, even the high AC
reinforced. Some of the more easily available choices are the snakeskin
items from PAW the Lockjaw vest from Oasis, or the high level drakeskin
arms and legs I hear are coming from Solusek. Most of these are pretty
easily obtainable, and in general offer much better protection than
similarly powered casters of other classes have avaiable.


Skills:
TRACKING- At level 20 druids get the skill of tracking, while our
tracking is pretty weak compared to that of a ranger it is still
incredibly useful. Use it often to keep tabs on who is around you. Wise
use of this skill can prevent you from getting into a vulnerable
position in the first place. As well as enable you to stick to fleeing
opponents like glue.

Forage- Might not seem like a good pvp skill but when your fighting your
way back to a body in enemy territory, or setting up an attack run the
last thing you need is to have to run to a friendly town to get food and
water, or wait for teamates to do the same. With high foraging you'll
save many needless trips to town. I expect this and summoning to be used
greatly during prolonged territorial sieges on Talon Zek.

Hide and Sneak: Not available to all races and not much to worry about
either. Since both of these skills are capped very low they aren't what
you want to have your survival riding on. Depending on these skills they
will get you killed.

SPELLS:

Spells are what it really comes down to. I'll list a few of the better
ones and their applications.

Offensive spells:

SNARE!!- Snare is one of the most powerful spells we recieve. Being able
to slow your opponents movement to a crawl is an obvious advantage.
Snare uses next to no mana and even when they do resist it you can
usually just run a bit and cast it again.

Direct Damage- Although some of our DD's have a higher than average
chance of being resisted, overall we get enough of them to take a
serious chunk out of most opponents. Careless lightning is possibly the
most popular of all of them, it is difficult to resist or prepare for,
since there are many more items that give fire resistance than magic
resistance you will find occasions when you may prefer the reliable
damage of careless to the better ratio of the more easily resisted fire
based spells. In general it's better to rely on our DoT's than DD's
though.

Damage over Time- The swarm->crud->drones line of spells may be our best
offensive weapon. They aren't easily resisted and do deceptive amounts
of damage. I prefer using them to the DD spells when time/circumstances
permit. Immolate is ok when you can risk the mana.

Root- Our root spells are pathetic, easily resisted, do a tiny amount of
damage and have an VERY short duration. Use snare instead.

Area of Effect- You would think these would be very useful in a racewar
or group PvP battle but most of the time people will be spread out too
thin to hit them all and you will waste mana. Another negative side
effect is that you will often hit nearby MoBs by accident and end up
fighting them too. The aoe spells can be useful when fighting dumb
opponents but it doesn't take long for most groups to realize they need
to spread out a little. The best application is for diversions. Say a
pack of darkies is guarding the entrance to someplace you want to go-
use a rain spell on the lot of them and you can easily lead them on a
merry chase while your camoflauged groupmates sneak past or set up an
ambush point for the chasers, since they will often return one at a time
to the original point. You can take out large groups quickly using
diversionary tactics like this.  (example: 8 darkies take chase after
you tremor them, as you lead them around with sow or cheetah you will
innevitbly lose some of them, they usually wander back to the last place
where their party was assembled, where  your 5 teamates come out of
invis and whack them fast, 5 on 1, your teamates then re-invis, rinse
and repeat as long as they can.) AOE spells can also make good opening
spells when you have the benefit of surprise attack

Buffs/Defensive spells- Our skin like wood line is about the most useful
buff useful buff we can share with others. With two obvious exceptions.
SoW and the Shield of Barbs line. Sow gives the speed advantage and the
damaging effects of the shield line can amount to huge numbers in a long
fight. You already know to keep sow in effect but don't forget to have
the shield up ANY time you'll be taking melee damage. Our succor spells
can save lives occasionally but they take so long to cast that you will
rarely get them off in time. It's more important to have SoW memmed to
recast on your teamates when an enemy root or snare has nullified their
SoW.  Spirit of the Cheetah can save your life over and over, just make
sure it's available before you count on it. Cancel Magic can be very
useful but you have to go through alot of preparation to make sure that
you cancel the effect you want to, and not just your teamates buffs.
When I think I may be needing cancel magic then I put a dummy buff in
the #1 posistion on the buff stack and remove it after i have casted all
my other buffs, this means that if im rooted, for example, root would be
top on the list of spell effects and therefore the first one to be
effected by use of the cancel magic spell.

Animal Control Spells- You would be shocked how handy a griffon defender
can be in a pinch, just make sure that whatever animal you charm is high
enough that it will defend you against your enemies. This is more of  a
last ditch effort to get them off your back while you flee than anything
else. But it can be ALOT of fun in a hit and run raid on a group.


Some situational tactics:

For a 1 on 1 battle vs melee class: Snare them 1st so that you can
control the pace of the battle and gain the distance needed to cast your
DoTs. One combo that is nice but doesnt let your opponent know how much
trouble he's in until it's too late is this: Step1) fully buff,
including your damage shields. 2) meditate to full mana. 3) snare a
melee'r. 4) DoT him. 5) Melee him a bit(while your dots and damage
shield chew away at him.) 6) run a few steps and re-DoT him. 7) Heal
yourself fully. 8) repeat step 5.  You can frustrate almost ANY solo
meleer this way.

For 1 on 1 vs casters with a pet: You can try this two ways: First
inclination is to think: "take out the pet early" since we get the
dispell summoned line of spells this can be pretty easily done, simply
snare the master and run with his pet chasing you to a safe distance.
Then you can snare the pet snd dispatch him with a few dispells. The
PROBLEM with this tactic is that by the time you have taken out the pet
and made your way back to the master he has had time to summon another
pet or at least has the advantage of being at full mana, while you just
spent a big chunk of yours on his pet. A better method is to start by
snaring the pet and DoTing the master, then lead the master well away
from the pet and snare/dot/nuke him the best you can. Alot of
pet-dependant casters wont know what to do when you've taken their pet
out of the equation. The idea is that you lead them well away from their
pets and let them think they are chasing you, then you stop and stay in
their faces with melee and DD's to disrupt their spells- all while you
cast and use the speed advantage to keep them dotted and the fast
casting of your DD's when your at point blank. DO NOT GIVE THEM TIME TO
CAST. You must keep bombarding and disrupting them.

For one on one with a petless caster- Wizards are the biggest problem,
they can DD you for more than you can DoT and DD them in the same time
if you let them. However since they have no pets you can cause them to
become disrupted pretty easily. We also have the advantage of being able
to speed off the screen and heal ourselves. But stay ON them as much as
possible, your better melee skills will help keep him disrupted while
your damage shield and DoT chew on them. Even at mid levels careless
lightning can be useful here, fire resistance doesnt help them and the
low casting time/mana consumption keeps theyre health dropping. Watch
out for being rooted, and keep cheetah ready in case you do get rooted.
While rooted dump mana into staying at full health. Since most wizard
will root, back off and nuke you may be able to force a stalemate with
your heals. If you outlast the root immediatly cheetah to the nearest
zone, with a pause to fully heal before you zone. If you need to keep
the fight going then cheetah to the zone, then re-sow, camo and possibly
even med a bit first near the zone as he tries to catch up with you.
Like all enemies keep them on tracking as much as you can. Remeber
cheetah's recast rate means you wont have a second quick escape until
you re-zone.


Solo against a group: No matter how you slice it this is always risky
business. You best hope is to use trickery to seperate them. Some
tactics I have found to be succesful are:

1) When there are only 2 enemies: Snare one and let the other chase you
while you DoT and or Nuke. Get a good distance then snare him as well.
Now DoT, Nuke and Melee as needed, the more mana you can save on the
first kill the better your chances of taking the second opponent. Who
will, by this time looking for you with a vengeance if he's worth his
salt. Like I said always keep cheetah ready cause you never know when
things will take a turn for the worse.

2) When there are three or more enemies: You're taking a greater risk
but you can sometimes have success using extreme trickery. One method
that works sometimes is to bind on one side of the enemy group then
circle around behind them to sneak up on them and snare one their weaker
members. Then you lead the unsnared pack of enemies in one direction.
You may have to use cheetah but if you can get enough distance you can
gate back to where you bound and run up behind that weaker member you
snared to begin with. If you led his teamates far enough away you can
often kill him and escape before they return. Try shouting something
like "...and then there were five!" after a successful use of this ploy.
Just for fun. Make sure that where you bind is close enough to a zone
that if you do die you will be able to escape a second quick death to
the same enemies. A less risky variation is to do it only in zones that
have rings you can teleport to. So that you don't have to bind and risk
being killed twice in a row by the same group.

Group vs Group: These situations vary so greatly that I couldn't begin
to cover all the scenarios. Some general tips include: Keep everyone
sow'd and be ready to re-sow them as often as needed, especially when an
enemy spell has made it lose effect. Heal them and keep them
damage-shielded in melee. Use DoT's instead of nukes, they draw less
attention your way and that allows you to heal more or even succor some
times. Stay buffed and keep your teamates buffed. Remember your solo
tactics and use them when you can. Try and break up large enemy groups
and go ahead and use AOE spells when they make the mistake of letting
you. Use every advantage you have. Especially the speed advantage. Don't
over-rely on your speed or any one thing though, mix it up alot. Excpect
to have to snare the enemy to outrun them and assume they have sow or
jboots too. Most importantly- appoint a leader for the group based on
performance, just because im higher level than you doesn't mean im a
better leader or tactician. You need one guy assigned to taking the role
of leader on the battefiled and keeping eveyone doing what the group
expects of them. Assuming your teamate is doing one thing when he's
doing another can lead to a quick death and lost battle. Since druids
have the most wide ranging set of abilities they tend to be versed with
lots of different types of scenarios and often make excellent leaders,
but it's really an individual thing. You want a leader that feels that
the only things more important than his survival is the survival of his
groupmates and accomplishing the mission.

Hope this comes in handy for the time being.

-Exedor Jerricho
