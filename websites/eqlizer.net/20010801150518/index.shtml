Sullon Zek, Continued
 5:17 EST - jcsmith

Absor answered a couple more questions on the Sullon Zek point system changes at the EQ Developer's Corner earlier today. Here's one...

Q: It says on the point system rules that the VICTIM must be over level 20 to get points, but it says nothing about whether the killer needs to be over level 20. If I am level 19 can I kill folks over 20 and gain points with no risk of giving them points if I lose?

A: Yes, it's all about the level of the person killed. A level 19 that kills a level 20 will indeed gain points and be able to collect an insignia.

Keep in mind, we may change all this again… We're still keeping the records and just adjusting the point values for actions. You all know that we're not going to award toasting folks much lower level than you are, so if you're worried about points, try to avoid it.

Sullon Zek Point Changes
 12:56 EST - jcsmith

Perhaps the smartest move yet on Sullon Zek happened over the weekend. Here's Hobart with the details...

Yesterday we made a change to the way kill points were calculated on Sullon. Sullon Zek, Continued
 12:43 EST - jcsmith

Absor answered some more Sullon Zek related questions today at the EQ Developer's Corner. One of them was in regards to players losing experience for being killed by lower level players, but the lower level players being unable to loot or receive an insignia...

Characters will only be able to loot other characters they kill if they are within the five level range.

The intent of the experience loss is to add a level of risk for those of higher level that want to attack those significantly lower level than they are, not necessarily to reward those that kill someone a lot higher level than they are.

The PvP points are not something that are given out when you kill a player character. What is stored is the fact that X character, of Y level, killed Z character of U level. No points are passed out. Points are calculated from the stored results about who did the killing.

We're aware that this isn't a perfect system. But it works out well enough. And considering that the game was never set up to keep a PvP killer rating sheet, it works very well.

As I said, support classes probably won't score as many points as others. That's just the way it is in the violent world of Sullon Zek, people recognize the ones with the blood of their foes on their shirts, not the enchanter that never dirties his fingers…

Hack and Slash
 20:28 EST - jcsmith

After a long editorial hiatus I have put together a new article which takes a look at the Hack and Slash nature of MMORPG's and ways we can go about making these titles more enjoyable. Here's a text bite...

I think that most people who come from a pen and paper background would agree that the module based approach to those games was more interesting than the level quest of the MMORPG. The interesting quests and storylines kept the games going. Yes, we leveled, but we were constantly thrust in to a problematic situation that we had to solve. The resolution did not always require combat. When it did there were usually options and different ways to reach the outcome. In the next year two interesting titles by the names of Neverwinter Nights and Dungeon Siege will attempt to recreate the fun of the pen and paper titles. I have no doubt that both titles will be large scale successes. I also feel that they will fail to reproduce the social aspect of the MMORPG.

    On 8 Spot Slots
         12:51 EST - jcsmith

    Absor responded to a user's question as to why Verant has never increased the eight spell slot limit on casters. Absor gave a list of reasons why this limit was desired. Now having been in this community for a long time, I believe that it was originally stated that this was a UI problem and that the limit could not be increased. Here's another explanation...

    Well, there is a strategy element involved both when choosing what spells to memorize and when deciding which ones to cast. The more spell slots available, the less strategy required when memorizing spells. One could argue that this increases the amount of strategy required when picking which spell to cast, and that might be true.

    But the real strategic part of playing a caster is deciding what spells to have memorized. When going into unknown territory, does a wizard need fire, cold or magic based spells? Do you choose to take all three until you learn which works best and reduce the other types of spells you take, or do you guess and take one type to increase the range of other spells in your arsenal?

    Choosing what tools to have at hand is just as important and choosing which one to use.

    But the most important thing to understand about the restriction to eight spell slots is that a lot of the game is balanced around that particular restriction.

    If you don't like to swap your buffs, bring another character of the same class and decide between you how to split the buff duties.
