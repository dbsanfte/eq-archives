ISSUE # 132

 July 15th - July 21st, 2000


The Magic Connection

It intrigues me that the EQ Gathering 3 was held on the same weekend that zillions of kids were waiting at bookstores around the country for the release of the fourth Harry Potter book, my daughter among them. (Yes, she had it finished before I returned home from Las Vegas and had already lent it to a friend which meant that I didn’t get to start it until today.) Not that there is any intentional connection, obviously, but it fascinates me that EQ’ers and the Harry Potter readers seem to share (with roleplayers, gamers and fantasy readers) a happy and rapidly growing involvement in imaginal worlds where magic resides naturally with the mundane. It’s curious, isn’t it, that these books and games, like many roleplaying games and fantasy/magic books, are most frequently evaluated and measured by standards imposed by academic/literary ideas and business goals? It seems that neither the literary critics nor the “bottomliners” take the time to sit back for a moment to appreciate and wonder about the phenomena of children and adults, all over the world, alone and in groups, re-connecting with the magical and fantastic in these books and games and then trying to share it with others who have enjoyed the same experiences.

The EQ Gathering was certainly a portal into this shared involvement and an opportunity to mull it over. I remember walking through the lobby of the hotel, wondering who was there from EverQuest, staring at hair and eyes, wondering if one person played a troll and thinking that another person must have a gnome character, watching for costumes and noting the obvious hint of a “Casters” t-shirt. I speculated to myself about why all these people would play and why they would come to Las Vegas. I wondered if like me, these EQ’ers sometimes had an ache for something special and magical to experience. Even as I began to meet folks, diving into a nostalgia fest about beta, talking game talk and listening to bits and pieces of Verant gossip, the question never left me: What draws me and all these other people into these imaginal worlds and what needs of psyche, body and soul are met by entering into the life of a magical realm?

Of course, that one question spawned many others. Is there a psychological theory or model that can explain why we enter into these worlds and draw together with others who do too? Why do people who claim a lack of creativity find themselves making art, writing stories and poems, and developing and expressing new characters in roleplay? Are the archetypes of magic simply re-appearing to another generation from repeating patterns throughout history, or is the involvement different this time? Is the pattern of groups of people joining in to share magical worlds a cultural reaction rather than an individual psychological one? Are there no real reasons at all and in a zen sense, are these questions silly in the first place? Is real life too dry and cerebral so that respite is found in the magical and absurd? Is gaming or reading fantasy devoid of psychological weight and simply a fun way of mental exercise?

Perhaps there is a charm in the underlying magic contained in these questions which makes it impossible to ever answer them with finality. However, it was soon apparent at the Gathering that the questions were occurring to others too. I was lucky enough to spend a few hours with friends there mulling over some of the possibilities. One theory we discussed says that when we create a new character in a fantasy world, it is like creating a new “real” life. We go through the same stages of personality development with our created characters as we do with ourselves. Another theory, Jungian in general, says that we are here to individuate, become who we truly are, and that these imaginal settings attract us so we can engage with the archetypes presented in roleplay just as we traditionally do with the images we find in dreams and active imagination. Yet another theory says that we are simply victims of addictive, neurotic behavior and the sooner we rid ourselves of our magic games and literature, the sooner we’ll be healed.

My guess is that the 6 people I listened to, sitting around a spa on a warm summer’s night, would have no problem thinking up even more possible explanations about why people are drawn into worlds like Norrath or why millions of kids would leave play consoles, televisions, computers or sports to immerse themselves in a 700 page book about a wizard and his friends. However, even if no one idea or set of solutions can really fully explain this magic connection, it’s my guess that, like roleplaying gamers and fantasy readers, would agree that before we can even approach trying to understand this phenomena, we have to experience it and the experience itself is often the answer.

    Essay by Nepenthia
