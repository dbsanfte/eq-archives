Intermediate Group Combat

Introduction
Alrighty. I've played EQ for a while now. Been solo, been grouped. Been in groups that were really amazing, and with groups that were anything but. If you have done any of the following (and not learned it was BAD), please read this document because it is designed for you.

Bad things to do while grouping:

  1. Play a Wizard/Mage/Necro or any other direct damage wielder and burn the mob (monster group) all of the way down because you think that's what a Wizard/Magician/ETC does.
  2. While playing a cleric/druid/shaman, you cast only healing, because that's what you're supposed do.
  3. As a fighter, took _all_ the hits in a fight. Leaving you at 20% or so.
  4. Run ahead or opened doors especially in dungeons while the rest of the group was weakened and/or resting.
  5. Gotten the entire group killed because you were a dumbass (see #1-#4)
[Note this list is in no way complete, and just provides a few examples of common, but violently stupid grouping behaviors]

So you're either a total scrub (above) or you aren't. For example if someone asks you why are you not using Taunt, Lay on Hands, Harm Touch, or Backstab and your response is "What is that?" you are a _scrub_. Check out this text, you might learn something that will make your stay in Norrath just a bit easier and more fun. Also read the eqmanual and maybe check out a few of the other documents floating around the net. (PPI will have guides for most classes relatively soon.)

This is Group Tactics 101. Don't expect to see anything else.

While you are in a group, each class has a pile of skills that make a group more efficient. This is assuming that each character knows something and isn't a glowing pile of scrubbiness. We'll cover general group issues first, and then move into specific hints for individual classes while in groups.

General Grouping Strategies

1) You have many people fighting with lots of health. Why should you _ever_ let one person get mangled? Trade hits. Figure out how much health everyone has (ask if nothing else) and then try to spread the damage across the entire group. This way you have 2-6 people regenerating hitpoints (6 hps/tick) instead of one (1 hp/tick). Easy to understand. If you get messed up, back out and let someone else take the beats. Watch out for frenzied monsters... they'll concentrate on one thing until it's dead. ALWAYS back out before you get below 40% hps (2 bubbles). Otherwise there is a good chance you won't get out.

2) You have lots of people hitting the monster. Do it from all sides. There is a bonus for attacking a monsters backside. USE it.

3) If anybody realizes that the world is a bad place (ie: that Goblin Wizard was red and no one bothered to check it), and yells RUN!, everyone should BUG OUT, but do it in a controlled fashion. Most likely the person that yelled run is going to run as he calls the retreat which means that staying becomes an even worse prospect for you. People with more health stay back and take hits so those with less health can get away. After your escape, confer to decide if what you ran from is worth taking another try.

4) Know when to let people die (from #3). Only attempt to buffer people if you do this if you can do so safely. It does no good to get the entire group killed if you could have saved a few people. Leave sacrificial lambs if you can (pets, monks, or people you don't like :).

Class Specific Strategies: Fighter Types
My subdivisions are really sketchy and vague, and many classes fit on boundaries and borders between types. Adjust for your group composition and adapt to fill whatever roles you need to.

(Fighter Types: Warriors, Monks, Rogues, Paladins, Rangers, Shadowknights, Bard)

Most often, if you just follow general strategies you'll be fine. Fighters are straightforward. Fighters are the ones that are taking the primary damage, and hopefully returning the favor. It's your job to annoy the monster as much as possible to allow the others in the party to get in free hits. You have a lot of responsibility. You must keep watch on your health and everyone else's, especially in multiple enemy combat. Attack anything that is attacking your spellcasters, and taunt mobs that won't get off of more vulnerable characters. If you're a good tank, you'll have good armor and relatively high agility, so you'll take _much_ less damage than would another member of your party. However, you can't take _all_ the damage.

Here's the part that is really hard. You must know -when- to keep the mobs on you, and when you can let other people take some hits. In a good group, no one leaves combat unscathed. This is because it's more efficient to have everyone healing (especially if you have a Bard in the group) than just one. So, tick off the monster, and then rotate the target, saving weaker members as needed.

Specifics:

Taunt: If you have taunt, practice it. It makes a monster think you've done more damage to it. Brief explaination of monster AI. If you hurt them, they don't like you. If someone heals the person their fighting, they don't like the healer, etc. Taunt effectively makes a monster hate you as if you'd done damage. This allows you to keep the monster's attention so it doesn't go beat on that spellcaster who just burned the monster for 50 damage. This is a _good_ thing (assuming your spellcasters aren't total nimrods and burn before you've taunted the mob). The ideal taunt is one that -just- keeps the monster on you, and if need be, you can let someone else take the darned thing. You do _not_ want to taunt a monster too much, because then it will stick to you until you die and nothing anyone else will do can get it off. This is called frenzy. It's guaranteed to get you killed if you're not careful.

Bows: Every fighter than can use one (W,Ro,Ra,Sk) should have a bow. It allows you to back away from a monster and continue to deal damage. This is very useful in a dangerous fight. Also expensive unless you have a Magician along. Still is worth it. Once you are past about level 12 the expense of the arrows is well justified. The mobs begin to do much more damage and also begin to give more loot per mob. Soon spending a gold or two for ten arrows will seem like a neglible loss for such an invaluable skill.

Lay on Hands: The ultimate party Paladin panic button. If you can't figure out how to use a 'complete healing' spell... well... this guide might be too advanced for you. Never blow your load on this one and heal someone while the healer of your group still has mana and is not being attacked by a mob. Unless the healer calls 'no mana' (or OOM for out of mana in shorthand), then you're the last line of defense.

Harm Touch: Amazing when trying to take out a big nasty. Use as softening to really 'MEGATaunt' the monster. Harm touch really annoys monsters because of the big damage it does. Basically hold this one until you get a mob that you MUST get rid of fast . An example of this would be a mob that hops on when your party has already been severely damaged.

Bards: Wow. Get one of these in your party. At L6 they become the best thing this side of sliced bread. Group healing is an amazing addition because when people back out (which they should do at around 80% in a Bard powered party) they come back at fu ll strength. Wow. Their other spells and skills also make them a very well rounded type of character. They have nothing that defines them as a class except for their amazing range of abilities (and the ability to use them indefinitely with no mana cost ).

Monks: While unable to use weapons, these fighers make up for it by sheer number of attacks. At high levels, a monk can get off 3-5 attacks a round. EASILY. This makes them invaluable for tanking enemy spellcasters. As all of you casters know, if you're being hit constantly, you can't get a spell off. Monks, remember this advantage and use it to interrupt and beat the hell out of those wizards and shaman that are about to make crispy critters out of your party-members. Another _invaluable_ skill is Feign Death. Practice this one as much as you can. It allows you to immediately drop out of combat, either to switch mob target, to mend, or to wake up the clerics in the party (no cleric likes to see a party member crumple in combat, makes their heart flutter). Feign death makes the monk _the_ single best sacrificial lamb the party has. Fight going bad? Everyone but the monk take off. Monk, run the _other_ direction, and before you get too low, Feign death. You'll have given your party a good 20 second head start. This sort of advantage cannot be underestimated with the new bonuses to back/fleeing attacks.

Rogues: Your best place to be in combat is _behind_ a monster. You should _always_ be behind the monster. Backstab is the single most effective melee combat in the game. If you have another fighter who can taunt effectively, you have a tremendous advan tage. If you have a mage who can attract attention with burn, you're equally set. (See Mage section below for tips).

Class Specific Strategies: Burn Types
(Burn Types: Wizards, Magicians, Necromancers, Enchanters, Shaman, Druids, Clerics)

Rule #1: Burn is _not_ a caster's version of melee.

Almost all of the casters (including the hybrids) have a way to deal direct damage. Direct damage is a good thing, but is so often misused that I've almost broken my monitor in frustration. Burn is for four things:

  1. Attracting an enemies attention - either to start a fight, or to try to get if off of another party member (be careful with this one, cause you're next then).
  2. Finishing an enemy - Either one that is trying to escape, or it is much more efficient to use one last spell to knock it down rather than waiting a few rounds of combat for it to get killed in melee.
  3. Panic button - If suddenly the world is a bad place, everyone better be lighting up the enemy in question if the party has any chance of survival.
  4. Mid fight - Your warriors have been dealing a healthy amount of damage to the creature. It is now time to use a _little_ burn. Deal enough damage to use your burn abilities to make the combat go quicker but not so much that you A. draw the mob onto you or B. Burn up a ton of mana requiring the group to have a long downtime waiting for you to recover.
One more time: Burn is _not_ a caster's version of melee.

Fire support does not mean 'always firing support'. You don't call in an airstrike in _every_ situation. You save it for those instances when standard troops (ie: fighters) are not sufficient. Mana should be saved whenever possible, and then used whenever necessary. Remember that if you have no mana, the party should stop and wait until you do, and if you're being stupid and burning like a flame-thrower, you're just slowing the party down. (You'd be _out_ of my party in a big hurry.)

At this point the question is "What _am_ I supposed to be doing?" during the times when the fighters are damaging the creature to the point where burn is effective. This is the time for your weak melee. Let the fighters land two hits and then step in. Your dagger or staff will never catch up to the fighters melee but will do a little damage. Every bit helps and it also allows you to take your share of the hits for more efficient group healing.

If you want to be an effective party member, you save the burn for those occasions when it is needed, and otherwise, you attack with your puny melee and try to avoid getting gacked. If the monster decides it likes you, you back out of combat and wait unt il the fighters have it's full undivided attention. Then you continue to prick it as much as you can. When the monster runs, -then- you torch it, because that's more efficient than having your fighters chase it, possibly into more danger. (Root also wo rks well for this purpose, allowing the fighters to finish quickly).

There are situations where a bit of judicious burn is necessary, but please err on the side of not burning rather than becoming a torch.

Specifics:

Root: Wizards, Shaman, Druids, Clerics, Enchanters. If something is running, DON'T LET IT. Also can be used to stick a big ugly melee mob with no ranged attacks in place. Then the fighters arrow it to death while you fill it full of flames. (One use for a torch, but this is very inefficient killing.) Root is also resisted quite often. Its mana cost seems small at 30 but really the cost is somewhere around 45 or 50 depending on your level and ability with alteration because of these resists.

De-buff: Enchanters, Shaman, Necromancers. If you can get a de-buff in at the beginning of combat, most of the time it's like burning the whole way through because the fighters are that much more effective. Much more mana efficient too. It is one of t he most subtle of all the spell types since its effects aren't immediately apparent but if you compare debuffed mobs to mobs at full strength the difference is amazing.

Pets: Check for more PPI documents on Magicians and Necromancers, but generally consider them another fighter. Control them as such. If they get messed up, back them off so they can heal. In general pets are not as effective in a group if you are taki ng single or dual targets. Against multiple targets they are invaluable. Sicking them on an attacking creature you can put one to be dealt with later while you concentrate on the mobs that are actively attacking your living party members.

A much more important party skill for casters is handled under the Healer types: buffs.

Class Specific Strategies: Healer Types
(Healer Types: Cleric, Druid, Shaman, Necromancer, Enchanter [what?! Read on])

Sure, healers can heal, but they have one more skill that is _so_ much more important that healing pales in comparison.

What is this wonderful thing, you are thinking?

Buffs. Those magical enhancements that make you faster, better, stronger, difficult to hit, and difficult to damage. The PRIME function of a caster of this type is to buff everyone in the party as much as possible. The secondary function is to keep tho se buffs from wearing off by keeping the people wearing them alive. Usually by casting healing spells to restore health.

Clerics and Shaman have the best combat buffs. Enchanters have good buffs and excellent de-buffs (see Burn Types). Buff everyone and watch the monsters writhe. An example of good buff/class synergy is 'faster attack' buffs (Bard or Enchanter) on a Monk. Monks _already_ get 3-5 attacks a round. Increasing their speed, even by 50% moves that into th 4-7 attacks per 'round' range, making it nearly impossible for an enemy caster to get a spell off. Buffs on any fighter type is good. The more attacks one has, the more effective the buffs.

Healing should only be used in dire emergencies. Again, mana efficiency is key. If someone drops below 60%, it's a good time to heal them. Also, healing during downtime can be useful.

Healing becomes almost unnecessary when you have good defensive buffs on your tanks. The difference they make is amazing. Again just like de-buffs it is hard to see the difference they make at first but once you realize their power you will never let an yone go off without them.

Conclusion
Hopefully this document will help all of those people out there who are group-impaired. This new knowledge should help prevent people in your group from:
  1. Leaving you to die
  2. Killing you themselves
  3. Swearing at you because you created long down times for the group by wasting all of your mana/health.
  4. Kicking you out of a group because you failed to follow commands of your group leader (assuming he/she is competent).
Please take some of this information and think about it. It will help you in your quest for adventure in EverQuest. And, remember, I for one, have no problems kicking people out of groups for being scrubs. I doubt others will either.

Good luck.
