Compiled by Milena, with much help from the EQS Warrior board members.

Note: I define powergaming in this FAQ as maximizing your bonuses and minimizing your penalties. I am not implying any negative connotations nor am I making any accusations or pointing any fingers. In this FAQ, the term is used strictly to define a playing style. The same goes for the term role-playing.

A. Preliminaries

1. Why do people play warriors?
People play warriors for many reasons. The item-collection aspect is considered by many to be a lot of fun. People like being in the thick of things, like having lots of hit points and a high AC, or enjoy the role-playing aspects of it. Others enjoy the role a warrior plays in a group.

2. Isn't the complete and total lack of spells really limiting?
Yes, very. But people who play warriors either learn to work around that admittedly large restriction, or switch classes.

3. But it doesn't seem like it'd be any fun.
Whether a class is fun or not is totally a personal thing. Certain people enjoy certain classes for reasons they may or may not be able to explain. You are encouraged to try every class/race combo that seems even a little fun until you find the one (or more than one for some people) that you can enjoy the most.

Section 1 - Defining a warrior's abilities and characteristics

1.1 What stats are important to a warrior?
Strength, Stamina, Agility, and Dexterity. The order of importance is up to the individual to decide.

1.2 What do the stats actually do?
For the melee stats we have received no formulae from Verant detailing their effects, only generalized statements. Independent testing by warriors and non-warriors alike has supported each of the statements, and at this time, all are believed to be true. They are:

Attribute Effects
DEX How quickly you learn weapon skills; how often weapon will proc; how quickly you learn rogue skills; how hard you hit with bows (per Abashi, dex also increases the chance of critical hits).
STR Determines how much you can carry; influences maximum and average damage; influences how quickly you learn many offensive skills.
STA Affects how many hit points you have, how long you can hold your breath.
INT Directly affects how quickly you can learn most skills; affects the amount of mana for bards and all mages, as well as shadow knights.
WIS Affects the amount of mana the priest classes have, also rangers/paladins; also affects how quickly you can learn many skills if your wisdom is higher than INT.
CHA Affects amount you will be paid for goods by NPC merchants, and how much they will pay you; affects the saving throw on certain bard and enchanter spells (charms in particular).
AGI Affects how quickly you can learn some defensive skills, how difficult it is to hit you, and how much damage you take when you are hit.

Testing has also revealed that stat increases don't have a very dramatic impact on real-world performance. It takes a large increase to see a small difference, and in most cases, the trade-offs required to make the large increase are not worth the minimal increases gained.

1.3 What other stats are important to warriors?
Your armor class (AC), your ATK (attack), and your hit points (HP) are all very important.

1.4 What affects AC and what does AC do?
Your AC is a combination of your Defense skill, your Agility, and any items you have equipped that give AC. Iksars also have an innate AC bonus, approximately 40 AC at 50th level. AC is a composite skill, comprising both your ability to avoid damage, and in the event of being hit your ability to mitigate the damage. The higher the better, and warrior armor has the highest AC in the game.

1.5 What affects ATK and what does it do?
Your ATK is affected by your strength, your Offense skill, and your skill with the weapon type you are currently using. ATK is a composite of your ability to hit and your ability to do damage. Strength does not increase your ability to hit, only to damage. No stats affect your ability to hit.

1.6 How are my hit points determined?
You gain a certain number of hit points per level (starting in the mid 20's and eventually rising as high as 40 per level for Ogres with max stamina). At 20 you gain approximately 2x what you received at 19th level. At 30 you receive approximately 2x what you received at 20th level, and at 40 you receive approximately 3x what you received at 20th level. There is also a large HP boost at level 53, and possibly more HP boosts. (More information on this will be forthcoming as the facts become available.)

In the 40's, your naked HP can be accurately determined with this formula: HP = Level * (27 + STA/11). Note that this formula is inaccurate at lower levels due to the rising number of HP gained per level. Warriors have the most hit points of any class.

1.7 What is berserk?
Berserk is what happens when a warrior gets beat to 40% health or less. Strength increases a bit, thus increasing ATK somewhat, but more importantly, it enables you to land crippling blows, which can do large amounts of damage.

1.8 Why are my magic resist stat in green?
Warriors have innately higher magic resistance than other classes. It goes up one point every even level. If your base magic resist is 25, at 50th level it will hit 50. A later section explains resistances more fully.

1.9 What skills do warriors have

  • Offensive skill: kick, bash (slam), 1-hand slash, 2-hand slash, 1-hand blunt, 2-hand blunt, piercing, throwing, hand to hand, archery, offense
  • Defensive skills: dodge, parry, riposte, defense
  • Miscellaneous skills: dual wield, double attack, bind wounds, taunt, disarm
1.10 What do all the skills do?
  • Kick: Allows you to kick an opponent for low to moderate damage. This is a secondary attack and functions on its own timer, which is shared with bash or slam (you can't do both at the same time). At level 55 kick gains the ability to stun and is about as effective as bashing. Warriors start with kick.
  • Bash: Bash is an attack that uses a shield to do minor damage to a target, with a chance of stunning it. A stunned monster or player will have its spell interrupted and will stop moving and fighting for a brief period. The bash skill shares its timer with kick and you must have a shield to use it. Ogres, Trolls, and Barbarians have an ability called slam which allows them to bash without using a shield. All large race members have slam, even non-warriors. For warriors, paladins, and shadow knights, however, upon gaining the bash skill at level 6, slam starts to use your skill level in bash to increase its damage and chance of stunning. To slam creatures that require magical weapons, magical shoulder armor is needed.
  • 1H Slash, 2H Slash, 1H Blunt, 2H Blunt, and Piercing: These weapon skills are used for weapons of the appropriate type. Right-clicking and holding on a weapon will show you what type of weapon it is. With the expansion Ruins of Kunark there are good warrior-usable weapons in all 5 categories (in pre-Kunark Norrath the blunts in particular were lacking). All weapon skills are available for use at level 1.
  • Throwing: Of limited usefulness to warriors, throwing is your skill with thrown weapons, such as throwing daggers, shuriken, and throwing boulders. Thrown weapons typically have much shorter ranges than arrows, but shorter delays as well. Available at level 1.
  • Hand to Hand: This is your ability to use your fists as a weapon. Even with very high hand to hand skill, a warrior cannot duplicate a monk's fists. This skill is of very limited use to warriors. Available at level 1.
  • Archery: This is your skill with a bow and arrows. This is a highly useful skill to possess for pulling purposes, though technically your ability to hit doesn't matter, because a monster will attack you whether you damaged it or not. On PvP servers, it's much more valuable to have a high archery skill. Available at level 1.
  • Offense: This is your general ability to attack and do damage, which is applied in conjunction with your weapon skill and strength to determine your ATK rating.
  • Dodge: This is your ability to dodge an attack. It is not affected by your AC. Dodge is gained at level 6.
  • Parry: This is your ability to parry an attack with a weapon. It is not affected by your AC. Parry is gained at level 10.
  • Riposte: This is your ability to riposte an attack (parry an opponents attack and get a free attack in on him/her/it). It is not affected by your AC. Riposte is gained at level 25.
  • Defense: This is your general ability to avoid attacks and mitigate damage. It is applied in conjunction with your agility and your armor to determine your AC.
  • Dual Wield: This is your ability to use two weapons at the same time. The formula is (LVL + Skill/600), so a 20th level warrior with 105 dual wield skill has a 20.8% chance of swinging his second weapon each time its delay cycles. The delay of the primary weapon does not affect the secondary weapon. Dual wield is gained at level 13.
  • Double Attack: This is your ability to attack twice with a weapon per delay cycle. It is slightly easier to double attack than to dual wield. The formula is (LVL + Skill/500), so a 20th level warrior with 105 double attack skill has a 25% chance of double attacking on each successful attack. Double attacks with your offhand are almost immeasurably rare until sometime around 150 skill. Double attack is gained at level 15.
  • Bind Wounds: This is your ability to bind wounds. It requires one bandage per attempt, and the bandage is consumed. Bind wounds never fails unless you are interrupted during the process. You heal yourself (or your target) Skill/4 HP, rounded down, per bandage. Warriors begin with the ability to bind wounds.
  • Taunt: This is your ability to taunt a monster to cause it to switch targets to you. Taunt is an extremely valuable skill. If you successfully taunt, the monster switches to you regardless of the damage or taunting of others. If you fail, nothing happens. Taunting a monster that is already attacking you is useless, whether it succeeds or not. Warriors begin with the ability to taunt.
  • Disarm: This is your ability to disarm your target's weapon. This is of virtually no use except when dueling or on PvP servers. Even with very high skill it is very difficult to disarm, and you can't disarm magic weapons (on monsters). Disarm is gained at level 35.

1.11 What are the skill caps?
Before level 51, all skills for warriors rise at five points per level, with an additional five to start. The formula to determine max skill is (LVL * 5 + 5). (LVL + 1 * 5) also works. After level 51 when the skill caps are increased, some skills gain more than 5 points per level and some gain less. (Detailed post-50 skill increase information may be added later.)

1.12 Which skills do I need to train before I can use them?
You can use any weapon skill by just equipping a weapon of the desired type and going into battle. To gain weapon skills you need to be fighting something from which you can still get experience (i.e., no low greens).

Some skills require you to first invest at least one training point in order to be able to use and/or advance them. Go to any warrior trainer and right click on them, scroll down the list to find the skill, and train as desired (you get 5 training points to start with and 5 more each time you level). You cannot Dodge, Parry, Riposte, Double Attack, Dual Wield, or Bash unless you have trained them at least once. Note that large races can slam from the beginning, but with a zero skill. When they gain bash the slam ability starts using the bash skill. You can use the taunt skill without training it first but you will be unable to advance the skill, so you should definitely place at least one point in taunt at a trainer.

Section 2 - Creating a warrior

2.1 Which race is the best for a warrior?
The answer depends on your goals and motives. If you are primarily interested in roleplaying purposes or have a warrior from a different game you'd like to re-create, pick whichever race you like best or think is coolest. For powergaming purposes consider choosing Barbarian, because they have a good combination of stats, can wear medium sized armor, aren't too horribly large, have good faction issues, and can slam. If you want the absolute highest strength and stamina combination, choose Ogre. If you want regeneration, choose Troll or Iksar. All races that can be warriors make perfectly viable warriors and people have made it to 50th level and beyond with them all.

2.2 What are the bonuses and penalties to each warrior race?
Each race has a different feature set. The stat listings are in Strength/Stamina/Agility/Dexterity format, unmodified. All warriors have an additional 25 points they can assign as desired at creation. Neutral faction denotes that the race can pick evil or good gods and their faction depends on what they choose.

Barbarian
Stats: Good (113/105/87/70, Total: 375)
Armor Restrictions: Large & Medium
Extra Weapons: Misc large-race-only weapons (such as throwing boulders and Weighted Axes)
Faction: Neutral
Night Vision: None
Size: Large
Other Abilities: Slam
Exp Penalty: Minor
Summary: Barbarian warriors are a good mix of stats and abilities, with access to medium armor (some better pieces as well as lighter weight) and large weapons. They are large, but not nearly as large as trolls or ogres, and thus have less difficulty navigating through smaller areas (though they still cannot get through many doors and other narrow spaces in dungeons).

Dark Elf
Stats: Lower than average (70/75/95/75, Total: 315)
Armor Restrictions: Small & Medium
Extra Weapons: None
Faction: Bad
Night Vision: Ultravision
Size: Medium/Small
Other Abilities: Hide
Exp Penalty: None
Summary: Dark Elves can wear medium or (preferably) small armor, which is good because they aren't very strong. They have bad faction due to their race and god selection. The ability to hide is useful (though at a limited and capped skill level), and ultravision is the best night-sight available.

Dwarf
Stats: Good (100/100/75/90, Total: 370)
Armor Restrictions: Small only

Faction: Neutral

Summary: Dwarves have good warrior stats and infravision. They miss out on a few good medium-size armor pieces, but have several good small-only weapons available.

Gnome

Regeneration and infravision are also very useful but add up to a large exp penalty.

Summary: The ability to forage was, until Iksar arrived, unique among warriors. Wood Elves possess average stats. They can wear medium or small armor, have infravision, and can hide, all useful abilities.

2.3 Which of the racial abilities are important?
That depends on your personal preferences. Being a large race IS a problem, because Trolls are a lot bigger than Barbarians, are Ogres are even bigger. Size will cause problems getting into tight spaces as well as getting out of them, and will often cause your party members to have to work around your bulk. With size comes great strength and stamina, however, and the ability to slam. Having poor faction is a problem, but the size of the problem depends on just how bad your faction is. Iksar start with the worst faction (they are kill-on-sight to everyone, even other evil races) and it causes problems with banking, selling loot, restocking supplies, and of course, travel.

2.4 Where should I put my extra points when assigning stats?
This is yet another question that depends on personal preference. The honest answer is that it doesn't matter as long as you put them into strength, stamina, dexterity, or agility. Raising your intelligence or wisdom is pointless as a warrior, and a few points of charisma may not even change the prices you get in stores. Increasing strength is a good idea for non-large races in particular due to the weight of warrior armor. If your agility is less than 75, it's highly recommended that you raise it to 75 at least. Having an agility lower than that incurs a fairly hefty AC penalty. Raising your base stamina increases your hit points, but not by a whole lot, and you won't really see the difference it makes until high level.

2.5 Should I pick a god or go agnostic?
Going agnostic helps a lot with faction issues. If you choose a god you are pretty much guaranteed to be KOS somewhere you wouldn't be if you were agnostic. However, faction problems can be worked around. There are deity-specific items that can only be worn if you follow a certain deity, but currently there are only a few and they are cleric/shaman neck items. It is unknown at this time whether deity-specific warrior items will ever appear.

2.6 Which weapon skills should I use?
All of them, with the possible exception of throwing and hand to hand. If you want to work those, go right ahead, it won't hurt anything and could be fun (particularly if you're a large race and get some throwing boulders). There are good warrior weapons in all the categories, so it's worth knowing them all. Get a weapon as soon as possible and use it to learn the skill each level. It's difficult to raise skills from zero at high levels, so getting a head start is highly recommended.

Section 3 - Weapons, Armor, and other stuff

3.1 Should I use a shield?
Until you get dual wield, sure, the extra AC and ability to bash are nice. After dual wield you should never use a shield again in combat except in special circumstances or when ghetto/ruatha bashing (explained later). Using a shield cuts warrior damage output too much to be worth it in most situations.

3.2 Should I use dual wield or a two-handed weapon?
This depends on your available weapons and your level. Until the mid-30's dual wield and double attack percentages are too low to go off very often and two-handed is generally more effective, unless you have superb 1H weapons. Once the percentages start to get higher and you are dual wielding and double attacking more often, duel wield starts to get better than 2H, unless you have a very good 2-hander. You should keep dual wield maxed each level even if you are primarily using 2H, though, and you should experiment each level with your weapons to see which is more effective. Dual wielding vs. monsters with damage shields may cause you a lot of extra damage, but allows you to ghetto/Ruatha bash. (Ruatha is the Stratics name of a poster who first popularized this technique; ghetto bash is an alternate term.) Your weapon choice can also be affected by the monsters you are fighting or your group makeup, so you should always be flexible and ready to adjust your weaponry as needed.

3.3 Should I bash/slam?
By all means yes, bashing a monster to interrupt spellcasting or stun it is very valuable. Large races can slam, but small races should use the ghetto/Ruatha bash method. To do this, set up a hotkey bank with your secondary melee slot and one of your inventory slots, next to each other or one on top of the other, along with the Bash button. Put your shield in the inventory slot and go into combat while dual wielding. Click on the shield to pick it up and move the cursor over the secondary melee slot, and leave it there. When bash is ready, click the mouse to switch items, press bash (using the number keys, not the mouse), then click to switch back. Practice with a cheap weapon and shield in case you drop them, until you feel confident that you won't drop your items. It is also possible to bash while using a two-handed weapon, but to do this you would need to click on the weapon, then click on the shield inventory slot (to swap them), then click on the secondary melee slot, bash, and switch it all back. With practice, it can be done very quickly, but the risk of dropping items is increased by the required mouse movement. Use at your own risk.

3.4 Should I bother with kick if bash is so useful?
 Yes, at level 55 kick gains the ability to stun. Plus, there will be plenty of time each level to work both skills, and there may be times when you do not have your shield ready and need to kick, or when you are working two-handed skills. Kick also caps pre-50 at 149 skill, which is not very hard to get to (that's 29th level).

3.5 What weapons should I use?
The best you have available to you. It does no good to say, "Use weapon X" if you do not have that weapon or a way to get it. Just be sure to always be on the lookout for weapon upgrade opportunities.

3.6 What armor should I use?
Again, the best you have available to you. It's not possible to make a list of items you should have by a specific level because the game doesn't work that way. You'll find yourself occupied with many things and some of the time it will be attempts to get better weapons and armor, some of the time it will be pure experience, sometimes you'll just want to mess around or explore, etc. The common upgrade path is leather armor, banded armor, bronze armor, and then crafted armor. From there you have individual slot upgrades available, then Indicolite and cobalt. See the various EQ websites for how to get the various pieces of armor.

3.7 Is it better to focus on high AC, or on high stats?
This has been debated many times. Common consensus is that focusing on AC is the way to go, since stats have minor effects at best.

3.8 Should I choose AC items or HP items?
This one is more difficult. AC items are the recommended path but there are instances when the trade-offs are worth it for one reason or another. Items which grant large numbers of hit points are generally worth it, if they also grant AC.

3.9 How many HP do I regenerate per tick?
Ticks are every 6 seconds.

  • Trolls: initially regenerate 1 standing, 4 sitting, then move to 2 standing, 6 sitting, and finally, after level 50, 4 standing, 12 sitting.
  • Iksar: It is believed that Iksars regenerate at the same rate as Trolls, but this has not yet been confirmed at all levels.
  • Everybody else: At first it's 1 standing, 2 sitting. At 20th level it goes to 1 standing, 3 sitting, at 50th level it goes to 1 standing, 4 sitting, and at 51st level it goes to 2 standing, 5 sitting.
3.10 How do haste items and haste spells work?
 Haste is the generic term for any item or spell that decreases your weapon delay. There are numerous haste items and haste spells, each with different levels of haste, usually expressed by players in percent. You can have one haste item and one haste spell and they will stack, but two haste spells will not, nor will two haste items. They work by multiplying the haste modifier of the item or spell by the delay of your weapon, then subtracting that much from the delay. For example, a 20% haste effect will reduce a 50 delay weapon to 40 delay. It is unknown whether the result is rounded up or down, and it's unclear whether a stacked haste item and spell both modify the base weapon delay or if the second one applied modifies the already-modified delay.

3.11 How do critical hits and crippling blows work?
Critical hits and crippling blows, both gained at level 12, are randomly applied to successful hits, increasing the damage of that hit beyond what it would normally have been. Minimum damage for a critical hit is the (Weapon_Damage_Rating x 2 + 1), ranging up to roughly twice your normal max damage. Crippling blows occur whenever a critical hit would have happened, but only when you are berserk. When crippling blows were first implemented they were displayed slightly differently in that the damage listed in parentheses was the same are regular crit damage, but the following damage message had the extra modifiers applied to it, so you could easily see the increase. Unless the percentages have changed since then, crippling blows with 1H weapons are calculated as standard crits first, then an additional 90% damage is added. Crippling blows with 2H weapons have an extra 75% damage over the crit damage. Crippling blows have a chance to stun the creature, which you'll see as "XXX staggers." Warriors can crit/cripple with any attack, including hand to hand, thrown, arrows, bash, and kick.

3.12 Which weapon should I put in which hand?
That depends whether or not you see a damage bonus yet (see section 4.2). If you do not have a damage bonus, put your weapon with the best damage/delay ratio in your primary hand. That weapon does not have to pass a dual wield check and will swing every time. The exception to this rule is if you are trying to work a weapon skill, in which case placing a very fast weapon in the primary hand helps, since the frequency of attack increases the rate of skill gain.

If you do have a damage bonus, it becomes more and more important to put a fast weapon in your primary hand to take advantage of that bonus. The faster you are attacking, the more often the damage bonus is applied.

Your primary-hand weapon should also be your primary concern, since it will be doing the majority of your combat damage (whether you have a damage bonus or not, though the gap widens as your damage bonus increases). After you have selected an appropriate weapon for the primary hand, you should select a weapon with a solid damage/delay ratio for the offhand. The offhand weapon's ratio (and damage proc, if it has one) are all that matter since there is no damage bonus for the offhand.

3.13 What do resistances do, and which ones are important?
Resistances (or save vs.) are your ability to resist that particular type of magic or effect. Warrior magic resistance increases with level and ends up quite high. However, magic resistance only helps vs. spells/effects that are magic-based. Fortunately there are many nasty spells that are magic-based including Root, Mesmerize, Stun, Flash of Light, and Charm. Basically, any harmful spell that is not fire-, cold-, disease-, or poison-based is magic based.

While having high magic resist is a good thing, it's wise to not neglect your other resistances. Wizards and Magicians (both players and monsters alike) specialize in elemental attack spells, and possess many powerful fire and cold spells. Shamans and Necromancers have powerful disease and poison damage spells.

The importance of resists rises slowly with level then takes a dramatic jump at the high end. Fighting Lord Nagafen, a dragon which uses powerful fire spells, with low fire resist is an open invitation to death (or forcing your cleric to heal you a lot more). It is commonly recommended to try to have 100 magic resist and 100 in the appropriate resist type for the creature you are fighting, just from your items, before you go to fight them.

3.14 What is /assist?
/Assist is a typed command which will do two useful things. If you have a player or monster targeted, it will switch your target to their target, and turn on autoattack. This is a highly useful command as it allows the group to concentrate on one monster at a time, killing it as quickly as possible. If you have an enchanter who is trying to mesmerize the extra monsters, having everyone /assist your primary tank will ensure nobody accidentally breaks mez on one of the other monsters prematurely. As a warrior, you will usually be the primary tank so it's important that you ask your group to /assist you. Fortunately, /assist can be placed on a hotkey and placed in one of your hotkey banks to be easily used. Additionally, you can /assist a person without targeting them. To do this, use /assist milena. You can place the name in the hotkey itself to allow you to press a single key and instantly /assist that person. This is a very useful tool any time you will be assisting the same person for a period of time.

4 High level warrioring

4.1 What is different about being a warrior at high levels?
 You have far more HP than before (thanks to the various HP boost levels) and a much higher AC and ATK. However, monsters will have increased their abilities faster than you, and are significantly stronger. Your role in a group is unchanged though. You are still the tank/damage shield and moderate damage dealer. Soloing is much less profitable (bordering on useless) because of the difficulty in finding creatures that won't kill you, and the large down time after a fight. Starting at level 51, your skill caps go up again, which is very nice.

4.2 Is there a damage bonus?
Yes, it's (Level-25/3), rounded down. This is only applied to hits with your primary hand. The bonus is easily recognizable when you land a crit/crip because it'll say a certain number in parentheses and the modified damage in the next line. The bonus raises your max hit as well as your min hit, making your primary hand much more effective. Because the bonus is added to every hit, it's in your best interest to put your fastest weapon in your primary hand when you start getting the damage bonus, to get the extra damage as often as possible. The bonus for two-hand weapons was recently changed due to the disparity between dual wielding and using a two-hand weapon at high levels. It's now (Level-25/2).

4.3 What are disciplines?
 Disciplines are new skills warriors (and rogues and monks, though they have different disciplines) get starting at level 52 and continuing every level until 60th. Each discipline has a reuse time associated with it, and you cannot use any discipline until the timer for the last-used discipline has expired. The timer starts upon activation of the discipline, not completion. To use disciplines, type /discipline. The disciplines are:

  • EVASIVE (Level 52): The warrior is hit by his opponent less often, but also will hit his target less often. This discipline lasts for 3 minutes and has a base reuse time of 15 minutes. The reuse time for this discipline will decrease as the warrior gains additional levels.
  • CHARGE (Level 53): When using this discipline, the warrior will automatically land all attacks against his opponent. In other words, he will not miss. This discipline lasts for 14 seconds, and has a base reuse time of 30 minutes. The reuse time for this discipline will decrease as the warrior gains additional levels.
  • MIGHTY STRIKE (Level 54): When using this discipline, all landed attacks will automatically be either Critical Hits or Crippling Blows. This discipline lasts for 10 seconds, and has a base reuse time of 60 minutes. The reuse time for this discipline will decrease as the warrior gains additional levels.
  • DEFENSIVE (Level 55): The warrior takes less melee damage, but also deals less. This discipline lasts for 3 minutes, and has a base reuse time of 15 minutes. The reuse time for this discipline will decrease as the warrior gains additional levels.
  • FURIOUS (Level 56): When using this discipline, the warrior will automatically riposte every attack landed on him by the opponent he is facing. This discipline lasts for 9 seconds, and has a base reuse time of 60 minutes. The reuse time for this discipline will decrease as the warrior gains additional levels.
  • PRECISION (Level 57): The warrior will have a greater chance to hit his target, but will also have a greater chance of being hit. This discipline lasts for 3 minutes, and has a base reuse time of 30 minutes. The reuse time for this discipline will decrease as the warrior gains additional levels.
  • FELL STRIKE (Level 58): When using this discipline, all hits landed by the warrior will be for at least four times the base weapons damage. This discipline lasts for 12 seconds, and has a base reuse time of 30 minutes. The reuse time for this discipline will decrease as the warrior gains additional levels.
  • FORTITUDE (Level 59): When using this discipline, the warrior cannot be hit in melee combat. This discipline lasts for 8 seconds, and has a base reuse time of 60 minutes. The reuse time for this discipline will decrease as the warrior gains additional levels.
  • AGGRESSIVE (Level 60): The warrior will do more damage with every blow landed, but will also take more damage as well. This discipline lasts for 3 minutes, and has a base reuse time of 27 minutes.
Verant has revealed the specific info on Evasive as an example but has already stated they won't reveal more info than that. The evasive discipline lowers the enemy's hit percentage by 35% but also lowers your hit percentage by 15%.

4.4 What high level armor sets are there for warriors?
There is a full suit of Indicolite available in the plane of Hate. It is no drop and warrior-only, and has fantastic AC and stat bonuses. The pieces are rare drops from Kirakuei, which are giant monk mummies. It can take months to acquire all 8 pieces (helm, breastplate, arms, two bracers, gauntlets, legs, and boots). Indicolite is a brownish color, shiny on some races, dull on others.

Cobalt armor is new with the Ruins of Kunark expansion. It is a dark blue color and carries very high AC per piece, along with spell effects. The legs and (presumably) the breastplate have stat bonuses as well. It comes from Karnor's Castle and Sebilis. The boots have the effect Strength of the Kunzer, which is a 25 pt self-only strength buff. The legs have Invisibility, the gauntlets have Reckless Strength, the bracers have Shrink, the arms have Lesser Shielding, the helm reportedly has Serpent Sight, and the breastplate's effect is unknown at this time.
