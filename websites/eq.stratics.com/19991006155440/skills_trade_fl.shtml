Guide to Fletching

 Why Fletch?   Things to Know   How do I Start?   Skill Factors   Financial Factors   What to Do 

Why Fletch?
Will I make tons of money and become one of the few Norrathian fletching barons? No.
Will the store equivalent products I make be far superior in quality due to the tender loving care I put into my art? No.
Will the time I spend making arrows and bows become highly entertaining and fruitful to the point where killing creatures is a mundane past time? No.
Will I save myself cash versus buying arrows and bows the store? In the long run, Yes.
Will I be able to create dangerous weapons not available in any store? Yes.

Things to Know Before Reading Further
I urge you to buy the books and read them. The purpose of this lesson is not to save you a couple gold pieces and bypass the intent of the game. Instead I offer my experiences. In order to understand what you read below, I will clarify some factors. Bone arrows are better than wood arrows and provide more damage. Therefore they are more costly. The higher the class an arrow is, the more expensive/complex the components. A class two arrow is better in some way than a class one arrow.

I'm sure at some point we can provide charts showing which components produce which arrows but that spoils some of the learning of the art. This guide is meant to get you started on the right foot and help you decide if you want to pursue fletching.

 top


How Do I Get Started?
The easy question to answer is what equipment do you need. Well, you’ll want a fletching kit, the book on fletching, and your arrow components. When beginning, I recommend buying a few of the cheapest knocks, fletches, points and shafts in equal quantities. Arrows are easier to make than bows so stick with them in the early stages.

Train your skill up to somewhere in the twenties before seriously spending time in the art or you will find fletching frustrating. Many failures will also gain you skill if you have the funds and patience. Hold off on making bows until your skills improve significantly. I could not make more than the simplest bows and my skill is in the high thirties.

Skill Factors Worth Considering
I did not have consistent luck (seven out of twelve successes) in making class two wood point arrows until my skill was close to the mid twenties. Making class two bone arrows was even harder and not worth pursuing until my skill was in the low thirties. Even with my skill close to forty, I was not able to make a bow using an elm shaft and silk (level two and three components).

The lesson here is that advanced components require much more skill to combine.

On the flip side of this coin, making more complex components seems to make your skill progression move faster. If you are not progressing and such is your desire, use a better component or two.

The Financial Factors of Fletching
As a fletcher I tried to sell my wares quite often. I had little luck doing this since the price seems high to the consumer. I think the fletchers of Norrath need to make sure they consider all factors when offering their goods for sale.

Here are the factors I weigh in setting a price for class two wood point arrows:

Cost of components (12 coppers) = medium knock (4 coppers) + field point (1 copper) + wood shaft (1 copper) + level two fletch (6 coppers)

Now, when your skill is in the twenties, you succeed seven out of twelve times on average. The cost for these seven arrows is 144 copper pieces. This comes out to a little over two silvers per arrow. If you want to sell these arrows at no profit sure, sell them for two silvers. I think you are doing all fletchers a disservice selling your handcrafted goods for this cheap.

Here are a couple of other factors I consider before setting my price. The store will buy your class 2 wood points for nine copper. If you sell for this price, you take quite a loss. The store then has the nerve to sell your arrows to the consumer for six silver pieces.

The ranger's guild in Kelethin sells flight arrows for two gold. Flight arrows do one damage and have a range of 150. Class 2 wood point arrows do one damage and have a range of 125.

Based on all the factors I list above, a class two wood point price of four silver is a significant discount for the consumer, pays you for all of the clicking you endure and provides incentive to continue in the profession.

When pricing other arrows I recommend you consider these factors and sell them for between 1/3 to 1/2 the difference between your assembly price and the merchant's sell to consumer price. For example, class two bone arrows, failures included, cost about 4 silver to make. The merchant sells these arrows to the public for 2 gold. I price these arrows at eight silver pieces each.

Tell Me What To Do
Fletchers will find one major drawback in this art. You can only make arrows one at a time (hopefully this is changed soon). After making hundreds of arrows, here is the most efficient technique I found.

First you want to put a container with four free slots to the left and your fletching kit on the right as shown here. Align the four free slots close to the four slots in your fletching kit. Fill these slots up with your arrow components. In this example I'm making class 2 bone arrows (field point, bone shaft, medium knock and second level fletch).

Now click on the first component and click on the "Done" button. Notice how your pointer is now positioned conveniently by the destination slot. Drop the component into the slot in the fletching kit.

Now click on the component on the fletching kit. Move your pointer down and click on the left arrow. Click done and move the component back into your pack. Continue this for all four components. This method is easily repeatable and does not change based on the number of components you use. I find the use of the slider frustrating and avoid it with this technique.

Once you have one of each component in your kit you are ready to click combine. Hopefully your skill is at its peak and you produce a fine arrow capable of drawing the desired enemy to you or helping you take down your cowardly foe as they flee.
