Quick Earth & Beyond Primer!
Stratics_Network_Announcement [ 4:25 PM PST ] - Sparrowhawk

Earth and Beyond--the upcoming Massively Multiplayer Online Game (MMOG) under development at Westwood Studios, the same kind folk who brought you the Command and Conquer series--is now undergoing Beta testing!

Let's browse through a summary of the game's storyline, which should help provide a further understanding of Earth and Beyond's interstellar backdrop of war, trade and exploration:

The age of the Universe has been placed at more than fifteen billion years. When the first Star Gate was discovered on the fringes of Sol we had no idea that it was one of the countless Gates that linked the oldest and most distant of the galaxy's planets. The scientist-monks of the Jenquai had tried to keep their discovery of the Star Gate a secret, fearing the other two factions incapable of grasping the significance of their discovery. But the Terrans, watching from the boardrooms of their star-spanning corporations, had spies everywhere. Seeking to prevent a Jenquai monopoly of this discovery that allows near instantaneous travel across vast distances of space, the Terrans forged an alliance with the aggressive Progen, an ordered and warlike clone culture.

So it came to pass - the Gate War. Humankind fell upon each other in an epic war, fought for the same reasons wars were fought since the beginning of civilization - territory. With the weapons of mass destruction that technology has wrought, we almost destroyed ourselves completely this time.

That was well over a century ago. Now, with the 23rd century nearing its end, a tenuous state of Cold War exists between the Three Factions of humankind. Each of the factions has claimed a portion of the Human Sphere as its own, linked now by the same Star Gates that broke them apart.

The Peace is maintained - only just - by fragile treaties and the creation of neutral peacekeepers, a byproduct of the War that nearly plunged the human race into extinction. But the Universe is large. Danger stalks the darkness between the stars. There exists countless worlds, countless discoveries, all drifting through the cold vacuum that spreads outward from Earth and Beyond.

In this case we need the GM switch to correct this naming issue (though I suspect it only affects a single or a very small number of players), so that such players are not inconvenienced by the introduction of these titles. Allowing players to turn off their titles doesn't solve the problem, it actually would make it worse. That means that even if we had both a player and a GM toggle for titles, the GM toggle would overwrite the player one. A GM could toggle off Bahter's title (Master) and the player would not be able to turn it back on.

So, with all of the other stuff we're working on right now, the GM one would have to come first.

Site_News [ 10:32 PM PST ] - Lord_Of_Dreams
There was a small bit of a debate on the forums about company names and where some of them had come from. Brad McQuaid (Aradune) thankfully stopped by and was able to clear up the questions and illusions that had been presented for us.

Hi all,

Here's the scoop on all the names/companies/etc:

Steve Clover and I were hired by a company called Sony Interactive Studios in March, 1996 by John Smedley (who was then a Director of Devlopment for that company). And Milo (a good friend of ours, who helped us on a previous project years before) was indeed already employed there and working on sports titles. Steve and I quickly started work on the game and wrote the original design document.

SISA was focussed mostly on Playstation games, but Smedley wanted to do online PC games, so we ended up forming a department within SISA devoted to online games. In fact, he'd already started an online game before EQ called Tanarus (the EQ engine and client-server code was originally based on Tanarus').

I became Producer on EQ soon after joining and the team began to grow as we needed more resources. The hiring process began in earnest, and we were lucky enough to be able to hire people like Bill Trost and Kevin Burns who had also worked with us on previous projects. And eventually Milo was able to join the EQ team as well.

Sony Interactive Studios then later changed their name to 989 Studios (which was indeed based on their Foster City address). And then in mid-late 1998, with the Playstation 2 on the horizon, decided to completely focus on console games and so gave Smedley the opportunity to spin the online PC department off into its own company. So Smedley approached me, invited me to be his partner, and Verant was formed (although we did briefly call it RedEye, but there was a name conflict).

989 agreed to keep funding EQ, but we needed a partner for the other games we'd begun (Planetside, Sovereign, and the game that turned eventually into SW:G). So we found a completely seperate Sony entity called Sony Online Entertainment who was focusing mostly on ad-revenue based games like Wheel of Fortune, Jeoprady, etc. They were interested in exploring subscription revenue based games like EQ, and so agreed to be Verant's publisher and funder.

When EQ launched, it turned out to be a much bigger success than even the most optimistic of us ever imagined. So not long afterwards, Sony Online and its parent companies began to show interest in acquiring Verant, and that is eventually what happened. So, at this time, Verant and SOE are really the same company, for all intents and purposes.

989 is now pretty much devoted to sports games, with their other titles being part of their parent company, SCEA (Sony Computer Entertainment, America). Yeah, Sony has as LOT of divisions.

It is also interesting to note that Kelly Flock was CEO of 989 and then also eventually became CEO of SOE, and was very instrumental in the success of EQ & Verant (as, of course, was John Smedley, without whom there would be no EQ).

Normally this isn't something I'd do. But, well, I'm going to outline the changes we're making to this quest in rather close detail. But first, let me try to explain the concern that players had with this quest.
With arguably the most popular Epic Quest item, the Cleric trying to gain his Epic item usually finds plenty of people to help in the process. Clerics are commonly the first in line with guilds to help with their epic quests. So Clerics tend to get further in their epic quest faster than many others. This has lead to a very nasty 'bottleneck' for Clerics trying to complete the quest. The Ragefire portion of the quest has been creating a lot of static among players.

So we have decided to make a change to the end portion of this quest. To reduce confusion and to help people understand why we're making this change, I'm going to give out a few specific details. These changes will go Live with the patch tonight.

For those that are not interested in quest details, consider the rest of this post SPOILER INFORMATION:


First, the 'merchant' version of Zordak will now appear at a different location in SoldungB. He will appear once each day (except after a server comes down, then he will spawn in one to three days). The appearance of this NPC will no longer require the absence of Lord Nagafen.

The cleric still turns in his Shimmering Pearl to Zordak. But now the merchant Zordak will return a Swirling Pearl to the Cleric and disappear.

At that point the Cleric will need to head out and look for him. A killable version of Zordak will appear independent of the pearl turn-in every few days (except after a server goes down, then he will appear a little faster). The new dragon Zordak will drop a heart that is a container that requires the Swirling Pearl to be combined in it to create the Heart that is turned in to Omat.

The rest of the quest remains the same.


The goal of these changes is to reduce the problems with the bottleneck that existed before. They will not remove that bottleneck, but hopefully will remove the nasty competition for a single spawn with so many people willing to be… rude to each other.

why were/are the textures loaded over the network in the first place? No textures are loaded over the Internet. It's all on your hard drive. They are, however, loaded into RAM, and that's what takes time. The process is something like this (and I might be wrong on some of the fine points...)

The data for the textures is stuffed onto your hard drive when you install the game, but they are installed in a compressed format (it's more complicated than just compression, but that's the basic idea).
When you load the game, these files are decompressed (again, not the right term, but close enough) and put into RAM.
It takes time to decompress these files. It also takes time to copy them into RAM.
The texture cache just decompresses these files and stores them in a format that can be copied right into RAM. But putting all that data into RAM still takes time.

So you're right, we are trying to use as little hard drive space as we can during installation. But all the textures on your hard drive, and they are indeed updated with the patcher and stored on your hard drive.

Sudden Changes?
GeneralNews [ 2:51 PM PST ] - Abombpk
A poster on The Station boards asked if there are any new changes occurring, and if so, why? Absor had this to say in response to the inquiry:
Well, there really are two considerations involved here: team dynamics and the health of the game itself.

Probably the most important thing is the team dynamic. We are just like any other group of people. One day we can all agree that the sky is blue. But over time we can be convinced, as individuals or as a group, that the sky is in fact yellow. As the team changes, as the game expands, and as the game itself changes, so do we. Especially when you're talking about the soulbinder thing. I think it's fair to say that we've been on the fence about it as a group for a long time. There really are reasonable arguments for and against doing it. We have debated this issue in several forms since I started here.

Very few changes that we make are sudden. But for those that aren't here, in meetings and sitting around the lunch table, I can see how it might seem like a sudden switch.

I think it's also important to understand that once we add something like this, it's fair to say that we really can't take it back. So when we're on the fence, when arguments on both sides are equal and the net result is hard to determine beforehand, it is probably always better to make the mistake that is reversible.

Focusing on the soulbinders as an example, we don't know how these NPCs will affect the interaction between players. We don't know how it will affect the economy for lower level PCs, who can often make a good living being helpful to others by binding them. On the other hand, adding these NPCs reduces the frustration level for some players that play at off-peak times when finding a bind can be a problem.

You also talked about how these changes are being applied gradually. We never really want to introduce a ton of changes at one time. Despite the seemingly popular desire for soulbinders, I'm already getting plenty of mail from people that don't like it (those are the people that made money selling binds). And I, for one, am convinced that this is one change will affect the social dynamics of the game, perhaps in a negative way. Before making any other changes that might affect the social nature of the game, it seems like a wise idea to examine the effects of any similar changes made previously.

So we make changes one at a time when we can. It makes it easier to judge the overall effect on the game world.

I read plenty of message boards. I think I'm fairly well aware of the desires of those that post about EverQuest (though I am also aware that there are many people that just play the game and never come to message boards at all, either to post or read, and we have to try to be aware of those people too). There have been a few times since I took this job that we've implemented a change based on what seemed like the desire of the majority of players, only to receive an overwhelming amount of email from people that didn't like the change. It is very hard to claim that any suggestion has the near unanimous support of the player base. So we try to be careful making changes. EverQuest is a great game right now. It's hard to say what changes might make it better and what changes might only seem to make it better. We have to use our judgement on these things as best we can.

Anyway, those are my thoughts on the issue. :)

Alan
