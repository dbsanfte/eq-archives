Beginner's Guide to EQ

 Introduction   Character Generation   First Flight   Training   Trade Skills   Death   Miscellanious Pointers   Conclusion 

Introduction
In an effort to help all those players out there that are computer and EverQuest illiterate, I have decided to put together a quick and dirty tutorial of what can be expected when first installing EverQuest and creating your brand-spanking-new character.

I realize that many of you out there are relatively new to Online Computer Gaming, and as such are not versed in the ways of a massive multi-player environment. This guide will hopefully prepare you for what to expect and allow you to not make the mistakes that are so common to newbies.

This guide will start from the moment the user installs EverQuest and leave off after that user has attained a sufficient proficiency with the EverQuest world.

Character Generation
Starting out, I know many of you out there during the character creation are asking the question "What am I going to play?" Well, this question, while seemingly innocent, realistically has the most impact on not only how you play the game, but also where you can go in the game. For example, those human guards just aren't going to like you if you choose a Troll, Ogre or Dark Elf. Think about what you want to accomplish, what you want to do and how you are going to (Yes, I am gonna say it) role-play your character in the game. All these variables will greatly impact the Class/Race combination you will want to create.

Once you have selected the Race/Class of your character, the next step is to create a name. Now I know all you unimaginative KeWlDoOdZ out there are gonna just love the name generator that 989 has provided with the game. Simply click the create name button and a name will appear for you. Clicking again will change the name. Repeating the process will continue generating different names, until one you approve of shows up. Now, for you people out there that are determined to have a name like Superflykewljoe or something like that, there is also a name screener that tries to weed out names that aren't "fanciful." If your name gets rejected, odds are it is either in use by someone on that server already, (989 implemented code to prevent duplicate names in phase 3.0) or it just isn't creative. Please, please try to create a name that is fanciful, one thing the GM's actively do is change the name of characters that don't fall within their definition of "fanciful." I knew a monk with the name, believe it or not, Brucelee, and one day he logged on to find that the GM's had changed his name.

First Flight
Ok, now you have created a Character, and have clicked the "Enter World" button. Anxiously awaiting your arrival in Norrath you grab a Coke, and settle down for some hard core gaming. Once to splash screen fades out and the screen brightens, your eyes are met with a beautiful 3D world full of color, sounds, and movement. Not unlike the hustle and bustle of everyday life, NPC's & PC's will be wandering around the city. The first thing I always do is set up my hotkeys. The hotkeys are located in the lower left of the screen and number 1-6. You can ascribe actions, skills, sayings, and personal buttons to these hot buttons, creating shortcuts that make life in EQ much easier. There are 6 banks of hot buttons, each of which will hold 6 hot buttons each. Assigning hot buttons is a simple matter of patience. Every button that you have for your character can be assigned a hot button. To assign a hot button, left click and hold the button you want to make a hot button. After about a second the button will come off and actually take place of your mouse pointer. (The upper left hand of the floating button will act as the tip of the arrow for purposes of clicking on things if you need to move around to get back to your hot button bank.) Take your floating button and left click in the hot button box you want it to go into. This should drop the button into the hot button box and can now be accessed through the hotkeys 1-6. For example, lets say you have the Bash skill. Drop the Bash skill button in hot button box # 4, and every time you press the 4 key, if you have an NPC targeted, your character will bash. **Important to note, the game does not differentiate between a monster NPC and a merchant NPC…..bashing or attacking will work on both without discrimination** You can access the different banks but holding the shift key and pressing 1-6. Shift+2 will take you to the second bank of hotkeys. An alternate method of reaching the different banks is provided for those less dexterous, simply click the left/right arrow buttons above the hotkey buttons and you can scroll through the hotkey banks. A good button that is used A LOT is the "Personal" button, it essentially opens up your inventory and allows you to take a look at all that gold you have, or all those nifty items you have collected, I suggest making that one a hotkey.

Now that you have assigned the buttons that you want to hot keys, its time to take a look at your inventory. If you assigned the personal button a hot key, hit that hotkey now, and you will bring up a framed windowed view that has a picture of your character in the upper right hand side. You can still see what is going on out there, but it also allows you to see what you have in your inventory. From here you can click on the button just above your picture, and open up the inventory slots screen. This screen allows you to see what you are currently wearing and using as a weapon. At the bottom of this screen, it also lists the funds you currently have on hand.

In your inventory you will have differing things depending on your Race/Class. However, all characters start with the same basic items. 1 newbie weapon, 2 notes, 5 Food & 5 Water. Of the 2 notes, one is a PK note and one is your guild note, right click once on either of the notes and you can read the note to find out which is which. Right click and hold over any object in your inventory will give you a brief description of the item. Single left click on the item will pick the item up and replace your mouse pointer with that item. There is also a button above your inventory that says "Destroy" picking an inventory item up and clicking this button will irrevocably destroy that item. The only item you may want to destroy at this time would be the PK note. Keep the rest. Try playing around with the different items and the functionality of mouse usage in EverQuest. Once you have had enough of that, go ahead and left click on your newbie weapon and drop it into the inventory slot for your right hand. Guards in this land don't mind crazy psychos running around brandishing weapons!!!

Once you have your nifty weapon in your hand, read the guild note, (remember to single right click on the note to read it). The guild note details who you need to give the note to in order to join your guild, be it Necromancer or Paladin. The city you started in will contain your guild, and guildmaster, so no need to journey outside just yet, unless you absolutely want to. If you do decide to leave without giving the guild note to the guildmaster, remember that if you meet an untimely demise your guild note will disappear and be lost forever. Finding the guildmaster may be a bit of a problem. Some of the cities in Norrath are just plain huge. Freeport for example comprises 3 zones all its own. Simple trial and error, and aid from other players online will soon get you to your guild. Once there left click on the guild note, and drag the note over the body of the guildmaster. Left clicking on the guildmaster with the note in hand will bring up the trade window. Click the Trade button located at the bottom left of the screen and your guildmaster will welcome you to the guild. You will also get a small amount of experience points for completing this relatively easy quest.

Training
Since you are already at your guild, your guildmaster is a great source for training. Simply right click once on your guildmaster. If you are close enough a training window will come up. Listed on the left are the skills your class/race can have. If the word "practice" appears under any of the skills, that means you can currently practice that skill and increase it. If a number appears under that skill, then you have to achieve that level before you can train in that skill. You get a certain number of practice points per level, 5 to be exact, and you start out the game with 5 practice points. You can technically put these points anywhere you want, but initially I usually give them to one of my weapon skills. (one handed blunt or one handed slashing) depending on the newbie weapon I have. It makes an extreme difference when you finally decide to tackle the wilds of Norrath. Skills at low levels will increase rapidly, and each level has a "skill cap." Not gonna see many 2nd level warriors out there with a 100 one handed slashing. A combat skill, by which I mean defense, offense, piercing, one handed slashing, etc, can increase by 5 points per level. A good way to judge how close you are to the max for your level is to take your level and multiply by 5. For example a max one handed slashing for a 10th level warrior is 50. As you progress in level skills increase slower, and as such, the skills that you acquire at higher levels (riposte, dual wield, parry, disarm, etc) will take more effort on your part to increase. Most people ascribe to saving practice points from the lower levels and applying them to skills you get at higher levels since these skills raise slower. Good practice if I do say so myself.

Trade Skills
EverQuest has recently had a major addition to the playability of the game, and basically has added a completely now aspect of the world, Trade skills. Some minor skills were available prior to this huge installment (tailoring & fishing for example) but now you can do most anything, from attempting to create that most expensive metal armor, to backing cookies and bread and everything in between. I have toyed around with the smithing skill for the past couple days, and let me tell you, it is just down right hard. I don't think that we have to worry much about seeing newbies running around in plate mail for a while yet. I have been saving all my practice points for my new charcter I created in phase 4 for this very experiment, I have a level 14 rogue now, and all but about 10 points were sacrificed for the sake of smithing. (Basically I have about a 60 smithing skill). Out of 5 attempts I was able to create 1 banded tunic, and 1 set of banded arms. The rest went down the toilet. At about 5-6 plat a pop for the try it can get real expensive. Well, to give you some general information about trade skills:

Every trade skill has a book that you can read that will teach you how to use the skill, what materials you will need, what templates you need, and in what quantities.

All trade skills require to have a template of some sort, which is destroyed when you attempt to create whatever it is you want to make, regardless if you succeed or not

The materials you are using will also be destroyed whether successful or not.

Trade skills generally go up on there own very slowly (My tailoring skill has maybe gone from a 20 to a 23 the entire time I have played, and I have made bunches of leather armor for people, my smithing skill has not moved at all on its own)

I cant speak personally about the other skills, but they all seem to work pretty much the same way, give em a try and see what you can create!!

After training in what you want, now is the time to find your way outside the city and practice what you learned!! Find the exit to your city, or in the instance you are in Kelethin, find a lift, and journey into the wilds of Norrath. You will be visited with wide-open plains, dense forests, and steamy deserts. You can go anywhere you like, however, it is probably not a good idea to journey too far from home until you gain some real life experience in Norrath. There will be plenty in your home zone that you can use to gain experience from until you feel comfortable to journey farther.

Death
While an unfortunate aspect of adventuring, chances are that at some point in time during your career you will face the uncertainty of your ultimate demise. Never fear, death while unwanted, is not permanent in the land of Norrath. However, it can be extremely disheartening and simply a pain. Upon your resurrection you will find yourself back at either your starting city or a point where you have had your affinity bound to. It is an either/or situation, you can only have your affinity bound to one location at a time, good sense tells you to get bound to wherever in Norrath you are going to be doing your adventuring. Bind affinity is a sell only castable by the magical classes. Wizards, Enchanters, Magicians and Necromancers can all learn the Bind Affinity spell at 12th level. Clerics, Druids and Shaman can learn the spell at 14th level. Problems arise when you are in a Greater Feydark and meet and untimely demise and find yourself in Halas. (Unfortunately a true scenario in my case J) Death at low levels (1st through 3rd) can actually have beneficial side effects. Your food/water is restored, and it allows you to instantly regain all your hitpoints, and at this low level there are absolutely no penalties for dying. Problems arise after reaching 4th level and are detailed below:

4th level:

At about 25% of your health, you cannot run as fast, and at about 15% you can no longer run. If you die at 4th level your newbie weapon and food does not spawn with you when you resurrect.
5Th and above:
If you die you lose experience points, and can lose levels if the experience point loss drops you to under the experience requirement for the current level you are at. Experience loss is conditional depending on what level you are at, the higher level you get the higher loss as a percentage of your experience you lose when you die. For example, a level 10 warrior would lose more exp as a total of his exp. Bar than a level 5 warrior. (Realistically, at 10th level if you die you lose about 10% of your current exp. Bar, or about ½ a bubble, at 5th level you can hardly notice a difference in your experience points from dying)

Single Right Clicking on NPC's will give you an idea of the toughness of the monsters, called /con, anything red pretty much stay away from, white (or black if using the windowed mode) is pretty much an even battle, and blue is lower level than you, green is pretty much an easy kill.

By now you have gained a pretty good understanding of the basics of mouse and keyboard functionality of the game. I will leave you here to continue your EQ education by saying you now have all the knowledge you need to develop yourself in the lands of Norath. Have patience, and your rewards will be great. Don't try to rush headlong into situations, when time permits analyze and adjust to the situation, when not do your best, and if things go badly do not be afraid to run.

Miscellanious Pointers
Opening doors - One Left Click, the "U" key or whatever key you have bound to use center.

Picking items off the ground - One Left Click

Selecting "targets" - One Left Click

Opening bags - One right Click

Talking to those in the immediate area - Hit Enter, type the message, hit enter to "send" it

Private message to an individual - type /tell or /t or hit the "t"key, type type the name of the person, hit space, type the message, hit enter. It should look like this: /tell Andy Where are you? Andy will receive a message: Jomar tells you: Where are you?

Talking or Saying something to the entire zone: 2 methods

  1. /OOC (Stands for Out of Character) message, hit enter
  2. /SHOUT (self explanatory) message, hit enter.
    /OOC messages will appear in green and /shout messages appear in Red. If someone uses the /tell command to tell you something it will appear in your chat box in purple.
    ** Please do not misuse these forms of communication, if you know someone's name and want to ask them a question use /tell, do not /shout or /ooc the message to that person as it is really annoying and spams the entire zone you are in. It not only is annoying, but you will most likely get flamed for it. I have been in zones where 2 people were carrying on conversations through /ooc, why oh why would anyone want to do that?
