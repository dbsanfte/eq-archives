Can't We All Just Get Along?

A Proposed PK System For EverQuest
I love the community-aspect of EverQuest. Grouping with other players, casting spells of healing and protection on random countryman as you pass by. Cooperation is encouraged and wonderfully woven into the game, and being able to learn and experience the world of Norrath without fear of getting whacked by another player on a random killing spree is a comforting luxury. But once you've had a chance to acclimate yourself to the game, EverQuest is missing something. It's missing the player vs. player environment, promised to those of us who enjoy hunting intelligent prey, and to those of us who simply enjoy the dangers of an unpredictable world.

Right now EverQuest employs a PK-switch method to support player vs. player. Upon entering the game, you are immune to the attacks of other players. By activating the PK mode (accomplished by completing a simple quest), you are permanently-and irrevocably-allowed to attack and be attacked by other players who have activated the option. Sounds good, right? Unfortunately, it doesn't work. In three months as a phase 3 tester, I ran across exactly THREE people who had taken the time to visit their local Priest of Discord, permanently activating the PK-option. Clearly player vs. player was not a popular choice, and I soon learned why.

One day I was looking for a small adventuring party to hunt in the icy plains of Everfrost. An 8th-level shaman at the time, I was pleased when the "who" command turned up a 7th-level ranger…a perfect duo for battling mid-level monsters. I contacted the ranger via the "tell" command and invited him to join me. When he arrived, I was startled to see the name above his head burned in red, denoting that he had activated the PK-option. It was like a brand-I immediately reacted with trepidation, even though I knew he couldn't harm me. He couldn't attack me, but would he abandon me in the middle of a fight? The ranger assured me he wasn't evil, and I reluctantly grouped with him as we embarked to find prey.

We quickly located an orc-trooper, and I began to cast spells of protection and strength on my partner-only to discover that they were ineffectual. And for the first time, I was made aware of the full consequences of choosing the "dark" path of PK-mode. The ranger explained that my spells would have no effect on him. We still won the fight, but I was beginning to question the worth of continuing the hunt. I was much better as a healer than as a warrior, but with this tainted partner my defensive skills were of little use. Our partnership ended quickly when a pair of orc-shamans ambushed us, but it was for the better since we then went our separate ways. How could I effectively team with someone whom I could not help? And a selfish part of me also thought, "Like I really want to sit around and wait while this guy heals!"

At that point, I realized I would probably never group with a PK player again, and I would certainly never choose the option myself. It was just too limiting. All things being equal, I might have considered activating the PK- option once I felt I was experienced enough to be able to get away from would- be attackers. The sense of danger from intelligent threats would make the game more exciting, more unpredictable. But all things are not equal. The drawbacks of the PK-switch are simply too prohibitive

By activating the PK-option I would be forced to abandon some of my favorite aspects of the game. As a shaman I would be useless to most adventuring parties, and I would be left to grouping ONLY with players that had chosen the same path as I had-players who could attack me on a whim if they felt like it. Also, many players would never look at me the same. Good intentions or not, the red-colored name you get when you activate the PK-mode is like a scarlet letter, branding you from the majority of society.

There's a second problem I have, although this is more from a roleplaying standpoint. I'll never forget the first time I encountered a player of an enemy race. I was wandering my homeland of Everfrost, seeking the standard prey, when I saw an ogre march into view. My initial reaction was a mix of fear and anger, rage that this vile creature had tainted my home with his presence. Even after using the "consider" command to determine that he was far my superior, I wanted to take my aggression out on him anyway, if for no other reason than to act on my feelings. But instead I was forced to merely stand and watch as he lumbered right past me. I knew he couldn't harm me, and he knew the same, and that quickly took the passion out of the moment.

Some might argue that EverQuest isn't about killing other players, and I'd be hard-pressed to disagree. RedEye has made it clear that they want EverQuest to be a cooperative game first, and I applaud that. Not every online game has to be about "killing the other guy." However, they have also chosen to try and satisfy the audience that IS interested in PK'ing, and this decision isn't something that should be taken lightly. If you're going to market your product to a secondary market, you better make sure your product delivers to that market. Simply put: If you make PK'ing so restrictive that it's not worth it, you might as well not even bother.

Fortunately, this rant is leading to a point. I think existing EverQuest mechanics can set the foundation for a solid PK-system, but it needs to be more flexible. The other major consideration that needs to be taken into account is that EverQuest is a "roleplaying" game. As such, any acceptable PK system would have to somehow fit into that aspect of the game. Here is a possible solution:

1. Two lifestyles, one community. There's nothing wrong with having a PK- switch option, but why can't a PK-player and a non-PK-player interact? This seems like the easiest way to make a positive change. Allow positive spells to be cast on ANY player, regardless of their PK-status. 2. Take it one step further. Eliminate the "red-flag" from the view of non- PK's. Someone who's activated PK mode still can't harm non-PK's, so why should he automatically be treated like an outcast? Instead, only players with the PK-option activated should be able to recognize other PK's. This way they can interact normally with the non-PK's while still being fully exposed to the dangers of playing PK.

3. This part is more of a radical idea, but it's one that I think would benefit the roleplaying aspect of the game tremendously. Use the faction system to allow player-killing between enemy races. Why shouldn't a high-elf player be able to attack a dark-elf player on sight? This is one instance where the "red flag" would work perfectly. The red name has the effect of causing a negative first-impression-exactly what you want between the first meeting of two races that are taught to hate each other. However, that doesn't mean those two players couldn't work together. The dark-elf player might plead with the high-elf to not attack, explaining that he is not evil like his brethren. At this point, it's up to the high-elf to decide if she wants to take the risk of trusting someone who could turn on her at any second. Voila! Instant roleplaying.

Another plus is that the faction system already allows for improved standings. A dark-elf who works hard to improve his standing with the humans or elves may eventually become immune to attacks from players of his once-opposition race. By the same token, a human who spends enough time doing vile acts (such as killing human NPCs) could eventually attack human players. It would make it harder for players to play the evil races (dark-elves, ogres and trolls), but doesn't the character creation process already warn you that choosing an evil character has its drawbacks?

There are, of course, problems with this solution. What's to stop a 15th level dark-elf from journeying to Faydark and preying upon low-level characters that are just starting their adventuring careers in their home territory? It might take awhile, but eventually the game would degenerate into an Ultima Online- style bandit simulation. You could impose level-limits, saying that 15th-level character could only attack levels 12 or higher, but that causes other problems. Afterall, a third-level dark-elf who journeys as far as Everfrost is taking the same risk as if he had wandered into a bandit camp. Go where you're hated and hopelessly outmatched by enemies and you probably deserve to die, no matter what your level. It's hard to maintain both balance and realism.

So which one goes? Well "realism" can only take you so far. We're talking about a game where you instantly know the name of EVERY person you meet. A game where a computer-controlled character will have the exact same conversation with you every time you engage him. EverQuest simulates many real-word aspects, like night and day and the effects of hunger and thirst, but it is NOT a real-world simulation.

So the ultimate question is this: "What is the best, most balanced PK system that supports the play styles of both types of players?" The answer must be based on game mechanics, and not governed by an effort at realism. In that case a system based on faction standings and level-limitations is the best way to go. Clearly there is no balance in a system that allows a 10th-level character to attack a 2nd-level character, so such an option shouldn't even be considered. (Unless, of course, the 2nd-level character consents to the risk. For example, I once accepted a duel from a character 13 levels my superior, and promptly ran to the guards. When he attacked me they chased him away. A dirty trick indeed, but HE was the one who initiated the challenge.)

What about the race restriction on PK'ing? I think it has several benefits. First, and foremost, it protects newbies. By the time a character reaches sufficient level to safely travel to an enemy land, he'll be long past the range of being able to attack low-level characters. Or, if he does manage to make the journey and is still low-enough level to attack, he'll likely get whacked by other, more experienced players in the area-players who will be all too eager to rid their home of a hated enemy.

Second, things will start to get dangerous when you venture into neutral areas, since you'll have to be wary of players from enemy races. No race has safe haven in the open plains of Southern Karana, so if an ogre bumps into a human-well, you better believe the lower-level character might want to turn tail and run. Or maybe the player will decide to take a chance and see if the ogre player is friendly. So much the better-as mentioned before, such interactions only work to support roleplaying.

One could raise the argument that this overall system would still present the problem of groups of PK's ganging up on players who aren't interested in PK'ing, but who can honestly claim that a band of trolls waiting to ambush a lone halfling ISN'T roleplaying?

The bottom line is this: I believe the producers of EverQuest have a golden opportunity to cash-in on a unique player vs. player environment without damaging the roleplaying aspect of the game. A player vs. player environment does not have to be littered with power-gamers and homicidal maniacs. In EverQuest the foundation is firmly set for an environment shaped by an excellent racial hatred system that is already a deeply rooted aspect of the game. Player-killing can work in a roleplaying game-especially in EverQuest. It just needs to be balanced and flexible, and given the effort, I believe both can be accomplished.
