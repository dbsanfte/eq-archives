The Lady

Leithreas Peregrine


The Lady
By Leithreas Peregrine of Xegony 

In the dim twilight, Leithreas admired her.

She walked gracefully down the sandy path, the long shadows playing through her hair, the last light of the day flickering through the trees and clinging to her as if reluctant to leave, as if somehow the daylight should shine just a little longer whenever she passed beneath it.

Leithreas sat cross-legged against a tree stump along the trail, absentmindedly twisting an arrowhead between his fingers, interrupted briefly from his task of repairing arrows.  He paused his crafting for just a moment to watch the lovely lady walk down the path.  She smiled at him as she passed; he smiled up, and tipped his head politely. 

"Good day, m'lady."

"And good day to you."  She smiled down upon him as she passed.

Leithreas sat back against the tree and continued to admire her as she walked past, watching the flow of her cloak as it swept behind her, watching her long blonde hair cascading down her back, watching her slender hand holding an ornately carved staff before her.  Leithreas watched her pass out of sight, listening to the slow cadence of the base of her staff on the sandy trail, and reflected on her warm smile.

"Lovely," he remarked to himself, as he turned back to the arrowhead in his hand.  He pulled an arrow shaft out of his backpack, and turned it over under his eyes, inspecting it closely.

A sudden scream jolted him out of his concentration, and he flew to his feet, leaves and pebbles kicked into the air in his haste.  He pulled his rapier from its scabbard and stood ready.  A leaf floated back down to rest on his boot as he listened intently in the eerie silence.

The silence was broken by the sounds of a struggle, of rustling armor and muffled screams.  Guided by the sounds, Leithreas crept silently but swiftly through the trees, his rapier held ready, his heart racing.

Crouched behind a tree, he peered into a clearing.  His trained eye and keen senses spotted clues quickly; trampled grass, broken twigs, drag marks in the soil; signs of a struggle.  His heart skipped a beat as he caught the final clue.

A richly carved staff laid silently where it had fallen in the grass.

Indecision lasted only a moment as the lady's radiant smile flashed through Leithreas's mind.

Shouldering his backpack higher onto his back, Leithreas followed the footsteps through the clearing.  As the sun's rays vanished from the sky, Leithreas silently disappeared into the dark forest.

- * -

Leithreas stared up at the sky, catching his bearings, not to mention his breath.

Well into the night, Leithreas had pursued the trail left by his mysterious adversaries.  He had expected to have intercepted them by now; instead, the trail only seemed to get older, as if his prey was fast outpacing him.

And the forest was only getting darker and more forbidding, the further he traveled.

His blood chilled as he heard the distant sound of running water, churning and tumbling over rocks.  He knew where he was; whatever he was following, it was heading north, into the thick and humid forest of Nektulos, ancient home of the cold and evil Dark Elves.  Whoever, whatever Leithreas was pursuing, whether it was aware or not, it was taking Leithreas into these evil woods with it.

Leithreas did not care to waste time imagining his fate if he became the hunted, tracked down by the Dark Elves that inhabit these woods.  Moving more cautiously, he continued to follow his quarry.

- * -

In the damp, cold darkness, Lisette sat brooding.

Her eyes wandered over the dim gray stone and smooth, dull iron bars that had become her new home.  Her slender hands had explored these cold surfaces a dozen times and found no way of egress; resigned for the moment, she sat against the damp stone wall, huddling her knees against her chest for warmth.

In the darkness she heard labored breathing, grunts and snarls that could scarcely be imagined.  Perhaps her situation could be worse, she thought dryly to herself.  Meaning, of course, that any moment it could be expected to become so.

Feeling a chill, she looked up to catch a pair of dark eyes staring impassively at her for a moment before disappearing, leaving her once again alone in the darkness.

- * -

The sound of a twig snapping made Leithreas freeze in his tracks.

He crouched slowly and silently, tensed like a cat ready to spring, taking in every sound.  A single shadow moved before him in the mist, and was gone.

It was alone; Leithreas padded silently in pursuit.  The shadow paused for a moment, and Leithreas sprang.  In a split second Leithreas was upon the figure, his smooth hunting knife held firmly against its dark blue throat.  Leithreas swallowed hard on recognizing the Dark Elven features.

"Be silent," he whispered, "or you will surely die."

The eyes, almost black in the darkness, looked up at him silently, unimpressed, and almost somewhat amused.  "You've given me no reason to believe," the Dark Elf hissed, "that I won't anyway."

Leithreas considered the situation.  He did not relish the idea of killing this man in cold blood; nor did he want the risk of leaving him alive.

"They know you're coming," the Dark Elf hissed.  Leithreas ignored him.

"I know where she is," the Dark Elf whispered again, taunting gleefully.

Knife pressed to his throat again, Leithreas whispered, "Talk."

The Dark Elf grinned, pleased with himself, enjoying his strange advantage immensely.  "Perhaps you are the one who will die," he whispered playfully, "if *you* do not do as *I* ask."

"I'm listening."

"I am called Leachreas," the Dark Elf began.  "I have been waiting for you… brother."

It was hours until Leithreas could finally believe the entire story.

- * -

Leith and Leach crouched in the shadow of a boulder, peering at the guards standing vigilantly at the entrance to the Dark Elven city.

“Are you sure this will work?” Leithreas asked.

“Trust me,” Leach replied mischievously as he emerged from his hiding place.  Nonchalantly, he walked up to the guard station and nodded politely to the officer as he passed; they looked him over and allowed him to pass by, ignoring him once he was beyond the gate.

Once inside, he turned, gave Leithreas a wink, and quietly began to whisper.  The guards did not hear the arcane syllables that Leach uttered, nor were they aware of the warm glow that spread from his outstretched hands.  Then suddenly they were aware of nothing at all, as a cascade of mystical forces fell over their eyes and ears and minds, easing them into a deep trance.

Moments later, Leith padded quietly into the tunnel and stood uneasily at Leach’s side.

“We’re in,” Leach whispered.  “Follow me.”

As they snuck past the first gate and down a winding cavern, Leith’s mind wandered.  To discover a lost brother was strange enough – to discover that brother as a Dark Elf was beyond imagining.  And yet here he was, following this strange man into a stronghold of evil powerful enough to snuff out his life like a candle.  If this was a trap, Leith pondered, it was a mighty strange one.

Yet Leach’s story seemed possible enough…

Leith’s mother, Torrey Peregrine, was – according to Leach – not killed by Orcs as Leith and his younger sister Letrea had always believed – the same Orc tribe that had killed their father earlier that year.  She was captured, yes – but kept as a slave, where she was discovered by a particularly cunning Dark Elf man named D’Sloc.  He had a weakness for Wood Elves, a charming personality, and a general disrespect for established conventions; she had a bleak future and no other chance for survival.  They ran away together – Torrey cooperating because at first she had little choice, but later because she fell in love.  They had a child together; a most unholy mix by the standards of either race.

Shortly after that child was born, their secret filtered back to Neriak, where the Dark Elves were most displeased.  They kidnapped the entire family – D’Sloc was swiftly executed, while Torrey and her “abomination” were imprisoned.  With herself and her child doomed to execution – or worse – Torrey was without hope until she was secretly approached by D’Sloc’s sister Enmity, who offered only to rescue the child – nothing more.  Having no choice, Torrey agreed.

Torrey was never seen again; the child, however, was raised as a Dark Elf in Neriak society, its heritage a secret.

The child’s name was Leachreas.

- * -

Leith was woken from his reverie by a cautioning gesture from Leach.  “Shh,” Leach whispered.  “We’re here.”  Creeping silently along the side of an ancient stone building, they ducked down to a tiny window into the dungeon below them.  They slowly and carefully peered through the bars, into the damp prison cell beyond.

It was empty.

- * -

Lisette struggled against her bonds, the chains around her wrists digging uncomfortably into her.  That would not be the worst discomfort by far she would feel today, she told herself, the pit of her stomach growing cold.  Rough voices shouted around her, and she felt a tug at her chains, pulling her ever onward. They traveled the winding streets, amid jeers from Dark Elves who glared at her out windows and doors and from the street, threats and obscenities shouted in a language she did not understand.  She knew their meaning clear enough; she was a light Elf; she was the enemy; she was to be sacrificed to please their evil god Innoruuk.  The words themselves were not important.

Not all of the onlookers were Dark Elves; there were a handful of Trolls and Ogres enjoying the spectacle, grunting and shouting coarse and suggestive remarks.  One Troll in particular approached close enough to gaze at her with a look that gave her chills – a look that somehow struck her as oddly familiar – before vanishing back into the crowd quickly.

The Troll was in fact quick – ducking back into the crowd purposefully, stooping into a building, sneaking out another door, and into a dark alley.  He glanced around to make sure he was not followed, then closed his eyes for a moment.

His image shimmered, as if he was a walking mirage, and suddenly he refocused into the image of a Dark Elf.

“Did you find her?” Leith asked.

“Yes,” Leach replied.  “Amazing how much you can see when you’re that tall.  I found her.  On her way to her execution.  When they pass into the temple there will be no crowd – we make our move there. Follow me.”

As he started to leave, Leith stopped him.  “Why are you doing this for me?”

“I honor our mother,” Leach replied softly, with an uncharacteristically gentle tone.  “I could not change her fate.  But for your lady, I can.”  Without waiting for a response, Leach hurried down the alley.

- * -

Lisette looked around furtively, searching for some final means of escape and reaching the bitter realization that there was none.  She felt strangely detached as the High Priest approached her, gesturing over her and speaking words of evil, calling upon the vile powers of Innoruuk to bind her onto the altar where she stood with magical force.  From the corner of her eye she saw a dark robed figure, raising a wicked-looking axe, drawing nearer.

As the executioner approached Lisette, a seedy looking human – as were most humans with enough poverty of spirit to be found associating here – a human, clad in dusty robes, drew away from the crowd and approached the altar, muttering quietly to himself.  The crowd, wrapped up in the excitement and the thought of blood, paid the pathetic form no heed.

The executioner drew closer and stopped.  For a moment she thought she saw a strange glow, almost an aura, settle over him.  As if in a trance, he stared blankly at Lisette.  As he lifted his axe, Lisette closed her eyes.  She heard the axe whistling through the air – and she opened her eyes in surprise as she heard the priest collapse at her feet, cleaved in two with a mighty blow.  A hush fell over the crowd, and then cries of anger as the crowd fell upon the dazed executioner.

As the executioner fell under the might and rage of the furious crowd, the strange human cast off his dirty robe and pointed defiantly at Lisette, intoning strange words.  Her confusion gave way to dim understanding as she felt the bonds of force around her falling away in response to his command.  She stumbled off the altar, staggering down the steps, where she fell – and collapsed into Leithreas’s waiting arms.

The crowd, frenzied from murdering the executioner, took a moment to spot the Wood Elf in their midst – and then the air was full of shrill, murderous cries of hate.  The human once again raised his hands, gesturing at the crowd – rearing back, he flung a wave of mystical forces into the heart of the crowd, and the shouting was replaced by panicked sounds of confusion.

“Leith!  Run!” the strange figure shouted.  With Lisette in his arms, Leith wasted no time in fleeing the scene.

- * -

“I’ll hold them off.  I can buy you time, and it’s me they’re looking for.  You get her to safety.  Protect her at all costs.  I’ll…”  Leith paused.  “I’ll do what I can to slow them.”

“There’s still another way, Leith.”

Leithreas paced the room, agitated.  “We’re cornered.  They know we’re in here.  A Wood Elf is kind of hard to hide around here, and we’ve got half the city outside that door looking for us.  Just what did you have in mind?”

Leach smiled, a sad fraction-of-a-second smile.  “Don’t worry about me.  Just run when I give the word.  Run out of here and don’t look back.  Do you understand?”

Leith scowled, but was silent.

Leach walked slowly to the door, and smiled at Leith one last time.  “I will find you again, brother,” he whispered, and gave a mirthful little wink.  With those words, he closed his eyes, and his image shimmered.  Leith found himself staring, for a wordless moment, at a mirror image of himself.  It was as if they stared at each other for a lifetime – and then Leach was out the door, Leith crying “No!” – but a moment too late.

He heard only the sound of Leach running, and the screaming of a furious crowd in pursuit.

A moment later the street was empty.

- * -

“Dear Leachreas,

     For a year we’ve searched for you now, but I refuse to give up hope that somehow, through your resourcefulness, you made it out.  There’s so much I still want to ask you, so much to tell you, so much we want to thank you for.

Warmest regards,

  Your brother Leithreas Peregrine”

Leithreas looked up from his writing to see Lisette placing a hot cider before him.  “Thank you, my lovely,” he said.

“I hope you find him, sweetie,” she said gently, placing her hand on his.  “I really do.”

“I hope so too…” Leithreas whispered back.  “I hope so too.”

- * -

“I need this delivered,” Leithreas instructed, “care of the Enchanter’s Guild of Neriak, to Leachreas Afterglow.  Can you send this?”

“Of course,” the young bard replied, shifting the weight of his lute on his shoulder.  “Trust me… he will get this letter.”  As the bard walked away, he winked slyly at Leithreas, and departed swiftly around the corner.

Leithreas pondered the strangely familiar wink for a moment.  “Wait a minute,” he said to himself as he turned to examine the young human more closely, but the man had already disappeared.  All Leithreas could see was the man’s shadow around the corner, a shape that was drawn long in the evening light.

A shape that shimmered and changed for a moment before it disappeared into the distance.
