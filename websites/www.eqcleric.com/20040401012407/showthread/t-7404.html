First in regards to the rogue consent hotkey...the reason pause is used in this hotkey is because a five line macro with /consent on it will not work. For whatever reason, the game mechanics will not allow you to consent 5 rogues that fast. /pause is used to put a delay between each consent, thus allowing the game to properly grant consent to each rogue.

Now, aside from the fact that the /pause time will yield inconsistant results for many reasons already mentioned, the real reason why I hate pause so much is the fact that /pause locks you in and you cannot do anything else while the /pause timer is running. Say, for example we have 3 clerics and we decide we are going to run a rotation with /pause 40. Your healing away, all is working fine, and the MT takes a nasty round of melee and the second you hit your macro the MT goes down. I for one would like the freedom to be able to /duck the heal, /target the second tank, and throw a fast heal at him, while the other clerics can /duck their CH, /target the second tank, and re-establish a CH rotation.

I know some may argue that's why you have druids and other clerics spamming fast heals during the rotation, and that is fine. But I want the freedom to be able to /duck out of a spell and cast something else if something goes wrong or needs adjusting.

Another issue is if you set up a rotation with a set /pause. 2 rounds into it you realize the MT is taking more damage then anticipated and the lead cleric tells the cleric channel to pick up the pace. Every cleric now either must open the macro and make an adjustment, or go to ignoring the "NOW" message and start counting on their own.

Once you hit elementals this all goes away anyway. We no longer count in our guild. When you see the CH message of the cleric before you, it's your turn to cast. Restandre
Pause is widely-used, and also widely-refuted. The people that don't like it claim that it allows for less adjustment, and that it creates more trouble when there is lag. There are also two ways of doing pause - cast your CH, and then after a pause give a message for next cleric to cast. OR, simply pause when you see the previous cleric cast, and then have your macro cast after the pause and call your heal then. I highly recommend the latter if you do decide to use pause.

For the general answer you wanted, pause is measure in tenths of seconds, and just how much pause you should use is completely dependent on the situation, but can not be less than 12 seconds divided by the number of healers on the chain. (With 5 clerics, you can't do a 2 second chain.) Best thing to do if you don't know is to start fast, and adjust. Someone who realizes the healing power is being wasted calls to SLOW IT TO FOUR (spammed) and you count a little longer. Ofc, this is trickier using pause, but you can quickly edit your macro while your cast is going, so it isn't impossible.

Just to interrupt the flow of the discussion for a moment in order to answer the original question...

In theory, /pause is measured in tenths of a second. In practise, you need to adjust it to take account of client/server lag.

The adjustment which is appropriate varies from zone to zone and from player to player, and sometimes from day to day.

However, if you're of the "let's use /pause" school of thought and you want a simple answer, here's how a lot of people do it: If you need a pause of "n" seconds, then the pause you'd use is (n*10)-5.

So, for example, for a three second CH chain use /pause 25; for a five second use /pause 45; for a six second use /pause 55. Then do a practise rotation and adjust these numbers downwards if you have connection issues or a lot of lag.

Important facts about /pause and connections

First: it doesn't matter what you see on your screen.

No indeed - the only things that matter about CH rings are what the server sees and what your tank sees. What your client sees is irrelevant: Your tank will die if either (a) the server thinks he's dead or (b) his client thinks he's dead.

This means that a tank with a so-so connection will die more often than a tank with the best connection going. Your heal might have landed in time, but if his client doesn't know that he'll die.

So when you rehearse your CH chain before the actual encounter, get your tank to tell you how far apart the heals are landing.

Second: A very good way of timing CH casts is to use the ping counter at the top left of your EQ screen. If your connection is good enough for you to be in the CH ring, that ping counter refreshes almost exactly every second, and you can count the ping refreshes to time your heals.

Edited to add:

EQ gives a very good illusion that everything is happening without lag. You move around smoothly, the monsters animate smoothly, chat seems to arrive quickly. That's in contrast to some other MMORGS, where you get things like lag hotspots where character movement slows to a standstill.

The reason for this is that EQ delegates a lot of information handling and processing to the client. If your client tells the server that you've done this and that and moved to this location, then in most cases the server simply believes you.

(In the past this fact was used to enable certain exploits. I believe that what happened was that some people went into their eq zone files and edited them to add a tunnel leading - for example - from Sebilis zone in directly to Trakanon's lair, enabling them to skip huge amounts of content. Nowadays there's a little more checking as to what's actually going on so this kind of exploit is no longer possible.)

However, it's important to understand that the illusion is precisely that - an illusion caused by clever delegation of processing and excellent net code. In reality lag affects most things that you do. The difference between EQ and other games is that EQ conceals it from you very effectively.

But it's still there, and with things like /pause, you come to see how much lag there really is.
