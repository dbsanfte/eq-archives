BattleAxe
The problem with today's MMO games is the presence of all the information about the game online that isn't available in-game. There is so much information available about quests, items, tactics, etc. that anyone not reading outside the game looks like a n00b. Anyone that wades through all of the information has intimate details about how the 'engine' works and how to best use the system.

Way back in the day, before there was an eqatlas.com or its equivalents (due to Beta NDA), just wandering through kithicor or greater fay was an adventure in itself. It was a big deal when 989 put in the path textures. Every game since has had some kind of in-game map showing you exactly where you are in the world, and Sony caved. Not that this is a horrible thing, but it takes away the "OMG, where the hell am I" aspect. The beastiaries and hunting guides that help you maximize your experience and loot gathering in order to defeat the treadmill remove the "what will I run into now" aspect.

Gone are the days where your group actually adventures into a mythical part of the world based on a rumor of treasure or foul deeds being done. Your guild does a quick check of camped characters, sees which raid target is up, and everyone rushes to kill it. You head to POV or BOT, throw on LFG, and grind some experience.

I don't know if it's up to a game to "make me forget I'm on a leveling treadmill". It's only there because *I* put it there. When I started EQ, I said frequently "oh, I'll never be level 50!" -- I wasn't worried about getting the super amazing gear. I didn't care about getting the most levels in the fastest time. My goals were small and close to home. I wanted a magic weapon so I could kill wisps because they made that annoying mosquito noise and must be destroyed. I wanted to improve my tailoring so I could make AMAZING handmade backpacks.

There are people still in the game for those small goals. It's just... once you achieve all the small ones, you have to go somewhere from there.

The important lesson of EQ for me, I think, is going to be that I don't ACTUALLY want to get the highest levels and the best gear IN THE SHORTEST POSSIBLE TIME. That's not fun. It's like having a great book from your favorite author. You feel the desire to sit down and read the whole thing in a 12 hour sitting, but you won't get the most enjoyment out of it that way. Same as forcing down an entire chocolate cake.

Just my take on it, but I think that keeping myself from trying to get into the "uber" crap is what'll keep WoW fresh for me. That and blizzard is just a helluva better game maker than the parties involved in EQ over its span. Considering the time and attention that Blizzard puts into patching and rebalancing games that have been released for years... I can't IMAGINE what they're going to do for one that's raking in a monthly fee.

A tip for those who hope to recapture the 'first MMOG' experience:

Stay out of the beta. Probably the single biggest thing you can do to help recapture the magic in the next release. Of course, you then have to find out if its a stinker while paying for it.

Also, this whole thread draws some paralells between online gaming and drug addiction/substance abuse, mainly the 'first high', struggling to recapture that feeling, and failing to do so. Looked at from that angle, no, you can never truly recreate that first high. The 'why' of why you can't recreate isn't all that different either, with drugs your body builds a tolerance and mentally you know what to expect, with MMOGs you've built a basis of expectation (the UI should do/look like this, monsters should react like so, equipment should be thus etc.) which functions much the same. Obviously the paralells don't apply to everyone's experience, but I'd guess it does apply to quite a few, which isn't news at all. =p
