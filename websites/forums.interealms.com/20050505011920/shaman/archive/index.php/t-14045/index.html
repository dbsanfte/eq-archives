Hodur
When they made the Matrix, they had no idea that it would be so popular and would be able to make the other two parts of the trilogy. They made sure the first Matrix could stand alone and didn't introduce necessary plot points that would finish in the final movie. Therefore, they were forced to introduce a lot of new information in the second movie. I really don't think we can judge how good or bad it was until we see Revolutions, which is essentially the second half of a 5 hour long movie.

A lot of people are going on about the Rave scene. It was a 10 minute scene, yet people complain like it was 90 minutes long. If you haven't had to sit through 10 minutes of tediousness in a movie before, then you must rarely go. That said, I think a lot of people are missing the point of the scene. While not necessary, it does help develop the storyline.

Minor spoilers:




First of all, the city of Zion knows that the bad guys are on the way and will likely kill them all. This is one last big celebration of humanity before it is wiped out.

Second, you have the religious symbolism. You have naked people, in their bare feet, dancing on a dirt floor, in a temple. This is the real world. People are in contact with the Earth itself, and are even surrounded by pools of lava, the building blocks of the Earth. The people of Zion live underground, below the people still plugged into the Maxtrix, are can be viewed as the fallen people, or those cast out of Heaven. The Matrix, on the other hand, is heaven. It is "perfect," or at least as perfect as the machines made it. The people of Zion have rejected this perfection and would rather fight to survive than follow the path chosen for them by others (the machines).

Thirdly, you see a compare and contrast between the behavior of the machines and that of the humans. The machines are only doing what they can to survive. The humans threaten their existence, so they work only for the destruction of Zion. On the other hand, the people of Zion are relishing their humanity as long as they are allowed.

I'm sure I've missed some symbolism, but I think I've understood at least some of it. Maybe this all requires too much thinking for a movie, but I enjoyed it. Suggesting that it was added for no reason, as some have, is kind of silly. I'm sure they had to cut another scene or two to add this one, so you can be sure there is a reason behind it.
