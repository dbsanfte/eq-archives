Everything depends on if its free mana (you cann to match the cost) and if tanks have a chance of hitting the buff slot limit. If you have 1 and especially 2 other buffers in your group you are in high danger of hitting the cap.

Acumen is nearly never worth it. Agility is nearly useless (adds <10ac) and diaku is not for high end melee (most str/dex capped).

Most melee are braindead and will not keep enough buff slots open, meaning there is a risk of hitting the cap and blocking something WAY better than those 3 buffs (like quiescence, elixir, etc). In alot of groups, hybrids have 3-4 self buffs, virt (or po9/symb), focus, stamina, haste, elixir/quiesc slot, debuff slot (mob malo/tash/curse/etc), brells/SoE/SD, sow, kei, DS, regen buff, resist buffs, spell haste, etc. Trust me it's very easy to hit the 15 buff cap.

So if you have hybrid and 1-2 other buffers I wouldn't bother (except for diaku which is situational). And if it's even a minimal drain on mana, I wouldn't bother with acumen/agi.

Calabar
I’ve noticed from the huge volumes of posts I’ve read that there are many different ways to play a shaman. We each tend to have our own priorities and many of those are based upon how we play. I’ve been lucky enough to befriend some exceptional players. And as I loathe pickup groups and will do almost anything to avoid them, I always group with at least one or two of my more than competent friends. Because of this I haven’t had to worry about many of the little things that others seem to. Crowd control, for example, is something that I rarely worry about as I am almost always grouped with a ranger or necromancer that excel at snaring/stunning.

The reason I bring all that up is because what spells you use will be greatly determined by your play style and by whom you group with. My view of being a shaman in a group is that I’m there to buff and to slow. And to toss in heals when needed. So that’s what I’ve always done. Even from the beginning I cast just about every buff I thought could be helpful to my group mates in a fight. And I slowed every mob that entered my range unless it resisted until it was almost dead. This meant using a lot of manna, which meant I had very little left for damage spells. It also meant that if I wasn’t casting a buff, a slow or a heal I was canni dancing, always.

As you get higher level this becomes somewhat easier. Once you get HoS (Harnessing of Spirit) at level 49 you can cast one spell instead of three. And thank the powers that be for group buffs. These make your life as a shaman sooooo much easier. But even at level 61 I still find myself canni dancing most of the time. I do a lot of LDoN adventures and they are very fast paced. I have to use manna slowing every mob, which means running around a lot in order to target them and often holding aggro to keep it off the cleric or other casters. This doesn’t leave a lot of time to canni dance, much less sit and med. I cast all my group buffs just before entering the adventure and canni back to full manna, but I still have to haste all the melee classes and keep buffs like regeneration going.

To sum up a bit after rambling over half a page, yes I think keeping buffs up on group members is very much worth it if you can manage it with the manna and hit points you have at hand. If you can’t keep all the buffs up that you would like then you’ll have to pick and choose, depending on the situation. Slow and haste are your two most powerful tools. These are the reason most people want you in their group and they will allow your group to take on mobs that may have wiped them out otherwise. But stat buffs are extremely useful as well. My best advice is to learn what effects your spells have and to use your best judgment as to which to use in any given group. As you play your shaman you’ll get a better feel for what needs to be done.

Soloing is completely different. The only person you have to please is yourself. When going face to face with a mob always have SoW up, and gate just to be safe, and keep yourself hasted. Try out different self buffs and see how much difference they make. Before I started grouping I always pulled a mob to a safe spot with slow and rooted it when I got it back to camp. Then I would DoT it and wait for it to charge. I did the same when I got my first puppy. I would give him two rusty daggers so he could duel wield, cast SoW and Haste on him and let him bite the mob in the butt while I tanked in front. It usually worked out very well. I was amazed how much difference even our first slow spell made in the amount of damage I didn’t take. I think that’s one of the things that sold me on playing a shaman.

As you gain levels and your spells start getting more powerful, playing the part of a shaman becomes much easier. It still takes a lot of effort, but the same job becomes much easier when you have the right tools.

Zhahz
At lower levels it's tougher to do all the buffs on yourself because you don't have canni+regen yet for quicker mana recovery, you fizzle more often and it hurts more, and unless you've invested in +wis stuff your mana pool is fairly small so the dent from buffing seems larger. If you're low level and soloing, you might want every buff you can do and just deal with the downtime.

Another thing that can help is to stagger your buffs a bit. Many of our buffs have the same or similar durations so if you buff them all at once they all drop at about the same time. If you do a few buffs, then do a few more buffs 10 minutes later, and so on, you can orchestrate it so that you don't have to completely rebuff all the time. The net effect is the same (you still use the same mana) but spreading it out can keep your more active (medding for shorter times more often than the one big med).

Just wait til you have a pet to buff too :P On the few times I intended to solo when I first got my pet I'd usually try to bum a clarity in PoK - then dash to wherever I intended to hunt and get myself and the puppy buffed before clarity ran out for a little less initial setup time.

For LDoN I usually buff the hell out of tanks and only put talisman and agility on casters (and occasionally +cha on enchanters or the person that will be doing sooth/pac). I figure, I'm a shaman, we have the time, and buffing is one of the things we exist for.

To prevent frustrating the hell out of my groups I will buff myself, casters, and healers as soon as it looks like the group is nearly formed and/or finding an adventure - that way I only have to do critical buffs on tanks at the zone in. The agility and talisman for the casters and healers I consider bonus buffs that I won't refresh inside and that I do before the real buffing (as last people join the group and we're getting the adventure) so they're not a timesink in getting going. If I know I'm in an efficient group I might also do the more mana intensive tank buffs like talisman just as we're getting the adventure just to save a tiny bit more time. Usually I only worry about ac and/or hp buffs on myself for LDoN - because if I'm taking a lot of hits the group has problems in general and usually I'm gonna be slowing, possibly hasting, backup healing/chloro, etc - and not meleeing. I do buff agility because it's cheap to cast and boosts ac a little - and every little advantage can help in LDoN.

One thing I've found for LDoN is that many groups start a little rough but things smooth out as the the group gels a bit. So, I think it's good to have ridiculous amounts of buffs to start just in case things start a bit rough. Once you get going and things are in a good rhythm and buffs start dropping, it's not as critical to maintain every buff on everyone in the group. I will usually only rebuff the main tank to excess and other melees with critical buffs and keep health buffs on myself once things are rolling.
