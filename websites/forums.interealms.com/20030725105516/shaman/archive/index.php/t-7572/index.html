I've never started a game that didn't have terrible lag, at least at the beginning, due to one of two things and usually both -- unoptimized code because they're trying to push stuff out the door in beta stage and only patch it if it makes money, and nothing remotely like the server capacity necessary to launch a game that sells strongly.

For all Blizzards vaunted testing and pushing back release dates, they were just as terrible as anyone -- maybe worse than anyone, with Diablo 2. It was virtually unplayable when it hit the streets. Blizzard said they were expecting to build up to the number of people that hit their servers in the first three days over a period of weeks. Yet, they put on the biggest, most prolonged advertising campaign I've ever seen for any game, and had the experience of the first Diablo also to let them know that many people do NOT play the single player game to start with, and a great many do not even play it at ALL, ever. A huge number will not even read the manual! They just hop on the servers as soon as they install the game.

How much worse will it be with a game that has a persistent world, like WOW? I expect Blizzard will bite it hard, like everyone always does, with horrible server capacity and massive lag for those who do make it on the servers. I expect a LOT of excuse making, and glossing over it all by all the gaming magazines that probably get half their revenue from advertising Blizzard products.

That said -- I have been looking forward to WOW more than any other game since I first heard about it quite some time ago.

Blizzard has something extremely rare in gaming -- a sense of humor, and some personality. I expect their world not to be so deadly dull as Verant's. Verant has some cool monsters, but overall, the NPC's are very lifeless and dull. There aren't really any "funny" pay-offs to quests, or generally even any very interesting ones besides a piece of loot or something. I expect that when Blizzard gets over their initial fiasco when they release it on too few and too buggy servers, WOW will be a game with a LOT of personality and things designed to be genuinely fun. The tedious, depressingly dull quests and key camps in EQ will probably have some parallel in WOW and any RPG for that matter, but I have little doubt that WOW will be just more fun all the way through.

How deep will it be? Blizzard has never gone for deep, and never achieved it. It may seem TOO simple and repetitive to people used to thinking of byzantine quests, and EQ customers may wind up forgetting how much they hated a lot of the EQ butt rot camps and start mythologizing them instead as having more "character" and giving more of a sense of "accomplishment."

So I think Blizzard could give us a very entertaining but limited product. One thing I'll note. EQ has expansion after expansion now -- a crazy number of zones to be in and monsters to fight, etc. WOW, if it's a small world, like EQ originally was, is going to wind up driving a lot of people nuts. They're going to need to have content to burn, because that's what we're used to now, and because they will probably have five million people buy the game the first week.

I wish them luck, and I wouldn't be surprised if I find myself buying WOW even if everybody says it sucks, just to see for myself.

How is death handled in the game?
A character will remain where he or she died for a reasonable amount of time (to allow for possible resurrection) or until the player chooses to "release" the character. How does combat work? What makes it different from combat in other MMORPGs?
In general, combat in World of Warcraft is faster paced than it is in other MMORPGs, without being a "clickfest." When a character attacks a creature, he or she enters into combat mode. In combat mode, the character automatically and repeatedly attacks the target creature, but the player is responsible for timing the use of spells and special abilities to be maximally effective. Warriors and other melee classes will have a host of combat abilities that they can employ while fighting, whereas magic users will be able to cast a plethora of spells.

How will you prevent camping and kill stealing?
We limit camping and kill-stealing issues by "instancing" certain areas of the game. An instance is a section of dungeon or outdoor area that is private. No other players, unless they are part of your group, can enter your instance of the dungeon/outdoor area. There are also plenty of non-instanced common areas where players can meet and adventure together.

How do you prevent camping and kill stealing in non-instanced areas?
Competition over rare item drops and quest items is the primary cause of camping and kill stealing. In World of Warcraft, many rare item drops are not exclusive to any one creature. Several creatures will have a chance of dropping the same item. If the required item is a quest item, the player can join a group of other players on the same quest and seek the item together with them. Every player on the quest will receive a copy of the quest item from the monster or location where it was found.
