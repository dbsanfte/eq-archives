The Coming of Age’s End

Chapter 1: The Sound of Thunder

It didn’t slowly spring to life like ordinary thunder; no, the report that shook the calm, lazy afternoon silence of Faydark Forest sounded suddenly and sharply. It was unlike any thunderclap those within earshot had ever heard. After several moments had passed, a few of the more curious Faydark inhabitants emerged from their huts and tree homes to investigate.

Vornia Weywynd, a Feir’Dal seamstress, was the first to arrive in the area where the odd thunder was heard. An almost over-powering stench of something burning lingered heavy in the air, along with a slowly retreating cloud of white smoke. Within a moment, Vornia let out a short scream of horror.

Before her, lay the body of another Feir’Dal woman, face down in a patch of wild flowers. Slowly and cautiously, the seamstress approached the body. The slain Wood Elf was still warm, although her naturally copper-hued skin already began to pale. Vornia swallowed the growing lump in her throat and rolled the body over. Staring back up at her, was the lifeless face of Wendalyn Morninglight.

Wendalyn was well known for being an absolute lover of nature. She went indoors only to sleep, and, while extremely well liked and friendly, preferred the company of animals, and tree, and babbling brooks to that of other Elves.

Vornia looked down at the gaping hole in Wendalyn’s chest, right at heart-level; the wound went completely through her body.

“Vornia?” Her husband’s voice called on the approach behind her. “What’s going on? I heard –“ But he could only stop and gasp at the sight.

“Who . . . what on Norrath could have done this?”

“I don’t know, my love.” Vornia ran her hand lightly over the bed of blood-spattered wild flowers, once white as fresh snow. “But we need to tell someone in Kelethin. What or whoever did this may still be out there.”

“Yes . . . yes, of course.” Vordia’s husband suddenly wrinkled his nose. “And what of that horrid burning smell? It hit me like a wave as soon as I saw you.”

“Again, I know not.” Vordia slowly stood, eyes still locked on Wendalyn’s body. “Make haste to Kelethin, my love, and summon the Captain. I will bring a sheet to cover poor Wendalyn.”



Saranaea Enothie was stuck in a conundrum – she was an extraordinary young Wood Elf trapped in a mind-numbingly ordinary Wood Elf’s life. She was only 1000 years old, and yet she felt as though she was already ancient. Her whole life, she had been raised as an adult – adult sensibilities, adult interests, adult education. She never got to play or associate with others her own age, because there weren’t and others her own age. To make matters worse, she suffered from the dreamer’s disease. Her mind was always active, racing with questions and daydreams, and no outlet for them, except one – Aelwyl Azuremoon, her godmother. Saranaea would often unload her myriad of thoughts and questions onto Aelwyl. The elder hierophant would listen, of course, and answer the questions given to her as best she could. But at Aelwyl’s age of over 5000 years, she found it harder and harder to keep up with the near daily barrage.

It just so happened that this is where the young Feir’Dal could be found – in the company of Kelethin’s most revered druid. The two lounged on a great patio, listening to the song of the wind as it raced through the immense evergreens, upon which the city of Kelethin rested. Spring had long since chased the cold grip of winter into the coming year; summer was fast approaching, and the last vestiges of the dead season were being warmed away.

“I love this place,” Saranaea mused, watching a single dead leaf being picked up by the wind and gently carried off the edge of the patio with a faint scraping sound. “You can almost reach up and touch the ceiling of the world.” The young Feir’Dal reached her hand up as far as it would stretch and imagined touching a warm, fluffy cloud before slowly moving it across the sky with her finger.

“Many times I wish I could, as well,” Aelwyl calmly agreed. Even in the twilight of her life, the hierophant’s voice still carried an air of authority, mixed in with her normal warmth, as if every person she spoke to was a friend she was meeting for the first time.

“I wonder if we will ever be able to truly touch the sky one day?”

Aelwyl laughed quietly, remembering back to an age long past when she, while riding on the deck of a ship during a clear night, posed a similar question. “Oh, I think so. We are learning new things every moment. You are too young to realize it, but Norrath has changed ten-fold from when I was your age. Many new lands and races of men have been discovered. Travel by ship has all but disappeared, since the lost wizard gates have been re-opened. New technologies, which were almost unfathomable in my time, are making everyday life so much easier. But I think we should slow down. If we lose touch with nature and the primal magic forces, we risk losing ourselves.”

“My whole life, I’ve never left Kelethin. I’ve never seen anyone besides another Feir’Dal. All I’ve ever known is right here.”

“Yes, your father was always overly-protective of you.”

“My father . . .” Saranaea grew immediately silent and let her mind wander. Ioemir Enothie was a good, proud man. As a father, you could find none more loving and protective than he. But therein, in his daughter’s view, lay the problem. Every skinned knee, every cold, every time she didn’t come home when she said she would after going out to play – Ioemir would shake with worry until he knew his daughter was completely safe. He didn’t want her traveling, either. There are dangerous, immoral people everywhere out there. he’d always say to her.

“But you know why he was that way.” Aelwyl broke the silence, as if she could read Saranaea’s very thoughts.

The young Wood Elf was taken aback by this insight, staring blankly at Aelwyl for a moment. “Y-yes, I know. You’ve told me the story many times over. It’s just . . . I feel like I was cheated out of a normal childhood. My father might as well of kept me on a leash –“

“But did you still love him?” Aelwyl interrupted.

Again, Saranaea was taken by surprise, then anger. “Of course I did. How can you even ask that?”

Aelwyl remained silent.

“I’m sorry, Aelwyl.” Saranaea knelt down and rested the side of her head in Aelwyl’s lap. The hierophant placed a slightly shaking hand gently on her goddaughter’s hair. “You’ve been like a mother to me. Everything I’ve learned about being a woman, I’ve learned from you. I just . . . I just wish I wasn’t bound to this place anymore. Everything that I am is screaming to go out and have an adventure; to see new lands and make new friends – make any friends.”

“And you will one day,” Aelwyl consoled. “But you are still very young. Don’t be in such a hurry to grow up. If you keep looking at what’s ahead, you’ll never have time to stop and see what’s happening around you; and what’s happening around you is called ‘life’.”

Saranaea looked up at her godmother and opened her mouth to say something. But before she could get a word out, someone suddenly walked briskly onto the patio.

“M’Lady, Aelwyl,” a brightly-decorated Champion of Faydark addressed with a bow. “Your presence at the main lift is requested. I’m afraid there’s been a murder.”

Chapter 2: The Illusion Shattered

“A murder?” Saranaea leapt to her feet in disbelief. Murder was a virtually non-existent term in Feir’Dal society. To the Wood Elves, life was sacred. To take another’s life through an act of violence was unthinkable. The last such action took place nearly a thousand years ago, when the necromancer, Meklozanth Xolasus slaughtered the entire village of Nithmiel. Before that, any murderers were long lost in the mists of time.

“Oh, by Tunare,” Aelwyl whispered in a concerned tone. With some difficulty, the elder druidess raised herself from her chair and, accompanied by an anxious Saranaea, made her way to the main lift.

Once there, they met with Captain Swiftblade and a man named Menai Weywynd. He explained to the Captain about how his wife had found the body with a mysterious wound in its chest. Immediately intrigued, Swiftblade gathered a small company of his men and Aelwyl for a personal inspection.

“Well,” Saranaea began in the most official-sounding voice she could muster, “as a personal attendant to Lady Aelwyl, I think it is my duty to join this investigation.”

“Erm, well . . .” Captain Swiftblade looked down at the girl and then at his fellow Champions.

“Saranaea, please,” Aelwyl said in that no-way tone, “I know you are eager to get out and explore, but this is a murder – hardly an occasion for your first step out into the world.”

“But Aelwyl . . .” Saranaea began to plead.

“No, my child, please go home. I will tell you of it when I return. Saranaea –“ Aelwyl’s voice suddenly became very motherly. “Look at me.”

As if commanded by a force beyond her power, the young Wood Elf looked up into Aelwyl’s face, serenely illuminated by the sun’s rays.

“Your time for adventure will come. Just please, trust me now, and wait here for my return. I will be back before the sun has even begun to set.”

Saranaea sighed in resignation. “Alright.” Head hung low, she skulked off the lift, turning back to watch it lower slowly to the ground.

Moments later, Saranaea found herself pacing furiously around the treetop patio. She knew that Aelwyl was only trying to look out for her, but curiosity was eating her alive. Finally, when all of it reached its boiling point, the young Woof Elf made up her mind – she was going to sneak out of Kelethin. <>

The Feir’Dal capital city had several emergency hatches that dropped a long length of rope, leading all the way down to forest floor. These hatches were to be used only in dire emergencies, such as in war or a forest fire. Thankfully, they have never been used in recent memory. The only problem was that each hatch was eternally guarded.

Pretending to take an afternoon stroll, Saranaea made her way across Kelethin to the nearest hatch. As soon as she came within eyeshot, the hatch guard began to watch her. She just smiled at him and walked slowly around the area, pretending to window shop at the nearby grocer’s stand. Out of the corner of her eye, Saranaea watched the guard. When he finally averted his gaze elsewhere, she quickly formulated a plan – grapefruit . . . slightly declined floor . . . distracted guard . . . hatch.

“How much for these grapefruits?” Saranaea asked the grocer.

“Those are really fresh, so I have to ask a silver for each. But you won’t find any juicier than mine.”

“Well they certainly look it, but can it pass the roll test?”

“The what?” Asked the puzzled grocer.

“The roll test. See –“ Saranaea plucked a large grapefruit from the stand and gingerly tossed it onto the ground, where the fruit quickly began to roll its way to the edge of the city.

“Hey! Guard! Get that grapefruit!”

The hatch guard’s head darted up at the commotion, and watched (quite disbelieving) as a large grapefruit rolled past him; hesitantly, he ran after the escaping fruit. The moment he did, Saranaea made her move, flung open the hatch, and dropped the emergency rope. The guard, hearing the sound of the hatch opening, turned back to his post just in time to see a young woman wave goodbye and slide down the rope. The grapefruit could not be saved – it fell to the forest floor with a loud splat!

Once her feet touched the soft, grassy earth, Saranaea broke into a run toward the forest line. When she finally calmed down, the realization that she was finally outside of Kelethin hit her. Taking a few moments to look around, Saranaea breathed in the fresh forest air and ran her feet through the shade-cooled grass beneath her. Once her curiosity with this had faded, she refocused on her original mission – to follow Aelwyl and company to the murder site. Swinging around the forest to the main lift path, Saranaea hunted for tracks. It was a trick a friend of her father’s taught her. This friend was a ranger named Talis, who had aided Ioemir on the quest to save her life almost a thousand years earlier. While she only met Talis on a few occasions, the time they spent together was irreplaceable. Talis taught her how to track, how to hunt, and the basics of archery (honed later by her father).

Several yards from the main path, the fledgling ranger picked up the tracks of a small group of people – four were wearing heavy boots, one left only light prints, most likely made by sandals. It had to be them.

After what seemed like endless hours of hiking, Saranaea finally caught sight of a group of people a couple hundred feet ahead. It was they all right. She could see Aelwyl’s flowing silver hair even from this great of distance. Moving as silently as a gently breeze behind the investigative party, Saranaea could see a clearing coming into view. She waited until the party disappeared from the trees, and then observed them from the safety of the forest shadow.

Words spoken could almost be understood, but the distance between the eavesdropping Wood Elf and the rest of the party was just a tad too great. Creeping around the forest perimeter, past the Weywynd’s hut, and behind a tree practically within a stone’s throw of the sheet-covered body, Saranaea finally got a clear view of everything.

“Is this exactly where you found her?” Captain asked a Wood Elf woman, whom Saranaea assumed was Menai’s wife.

“Yes, lying face down. I rolled her over to see who it was.”

Swiftblade squatted down and removed the bloodstained sheet. Saranaea silently gasped at what the sheet uncovered – a pale, stiff face, glossy eyes staring blankly ahead, mouth frozen open as if it were just beginning to speak, and the wound – large and encrusted with dark blood. The young Wood Elf turned and sat against the tree trunk. She’d never seen death before; she wasn’t even with her father when he breathed his last. Too young they told her. The night Ioemir died, Aelwyl ordered his daughter into the care of the hierophant’s priestess.

“Hmm,” Swiftblade examined the wound for several moments. “That is a very curious wound, indeed.”

“An arrow, perhaps?” One of the Captain’s men suggested.

“No, not likely. Only arrow that could have done something like this would be one that had extremely powerful enhancements placed onto it. And even then, look – this wound is too round and neat to have been an arrow.

“And you said there was a strange cloud of smoke and a burning smell, as well?”

“Yes, my husband smelled it, too. I can’t describe it. It wasn’t like anything I’ve ever smelled before.”

“Can you describe the cloud of smoke to me?”

“It was oddly white. Almost like a cloud from the sky came down and then quickly started to vanish once it saw me.”

Swiftblade rubbed his chin for a moment before standing again and shaking his head. “In the 2000 years I’ve held my position, I’ve never seen anything like this. M’Lady Aelwyl, are there any insights you can shed on this?”

Aelwyl turned her head slightly skyward, closed her eyes, and took in a deep breath.

Wind, be my ears; the hierophant called out with her soul to Nature itself. Daylight, be my eyes; Father Faydark, pass your memories into me.

Aelwyl suddenly felt as though the person looking through her eyes was no longer she; she was the forest, she saw what it saw. In a breath, the present scene was replaced by one that had taken place hours earlier. Wendalyn was kneeling before the patch of white wild flowers, holding a freshly picked one to her nose. Aelwyl turned her mind’s eye a full 360-degrees, looking for anything unusual – but all was normal. When her gaze returned to Wendalyn, she was now standing, twirling a tiny flower between the tips of her fingers. Suddenly, from somewhere in the distance, there was an audible click. Scanning desperately, Aelwyl finally spotted the source of the sound. From behind a tree some distance off, there was a dark figure, as if obscured totally by shadow. In his hands, he held out a long tube-like device made of wood and metal with an opening at its end; it was pointed directly at the back of Wendalyn. With a brief, blinding flash, the tube let out a loud report, which sounded very much like an odd, localized thunder. Without hesitation, Aelwyl flashed back to Wendalyn, whose back and chest exploded in a spray of blood, which also issued forth from her mouth in a great cough. Wendalyn stood shocked for a moment, looking at the gore-covered hand she just placed on the fresh, smoking wound. Moments later, her knees gave way, and she fell lifeless into the patch of flowers. Aelwyl looked back at where the shot had come from, but there was no one there. Or at least, if there was, they were obscured by a large, circular cloud of white smoke.

“M’Lady?” Captain Swiftblade was shaking the hierophant back into consciousness.

“Yes, yes, I am here.” Aelwyl was visibly shaken, but still coherent and focused.

“What did you see?”

Aelwyl recounted her vision to everyone, Saranaea listening along in horror. When the hierophant had finished, Swiftblade recovered the body and announced that he would dispatch some more of his men to return with a stretcher, so that Wendalyn’s body could be brought back to Kelethin for inspection by the city’s most trusted healers.

After she was sure they had all left, Saranaea stood and numbly emerged from behind the tree. Just a pace before the body, however, a familiar voice announced, “So you finally came out of hiding?”

The young Wood Elf turned in shock to behold Aelwyl, standing serenely behind her.

“You . . . you knew I was there?”

“Of course, my dear,” Aelwyl shot her a calm smile. “You really are not Norrath’s most quiet tracker.” The hierophant walked up to beside her goddaughter and looked down at the covered body. “So . . . was it what you had imagined it to be like?”

“Imagined what to be like?”

“Death.”

Saranaea’s eyes filled with small, warm tears. “No . . . it was . . . was.” She then started to cry loudly. “Oh, Aelwyl, I hated it! It was awful!”

Aelwyl took her goddaughter in her arms and stroked the back of her head. “Shhh. Now now, Sara, don’t cry. Death is as natural as life itself. The tide rises and recedes; trees grow tall and fall to the earth. The means by which we die are sometimes unpleasant and unfortunate, but it just the way of things. That is why we must live life with each step we take, each breath, each time we look into the eyes of someone we love.”

Sara felt comforted by Aelwyl’s words, as she almost always had. Her mind was still reeling to cope with everything that had just happened.

“Sara, let’s go back home, now. Some warm soup and fruit juice will do you good.”

Slowly, Sara nodded and turned to go. Just before she completed her turn, however, something out of the corner of her eye briefly glittered in the sun’s descending rays. Quietly, Sara sidestepped to investigate the shine coming from the trunk of a small tree, a few paces from Wendalyn’s body. There, embedded a short ways into the tree, was a small, metallic object. Digging it out with her fingers, Sara discovered the object to be an odd, heavy ball of some sort.

“Sara?” Aelwyl suddenly called a short distance off, “are you coming?”

“Yes, Aelwyl,” she called back, dropping the ball into the side pocket of her trousers.

Chapter 3: Lord Korus deMuin and His Associates

The Dwarfish foundries of Dornanthal under the stone city of Kaladim were sites of legend. The Dwarves believe that the foundries were dug out by hand by the first of their kind to arrive in the land now known as Butcherblock. For as long as Dwarfish culture has existed, the great fires of Dornanthal have never been extinguished.

Toiling deep within the Threshold Foundry, Bron Hammerfall had just put the finishing touches on his latest shield. To Bron, something he has forged is not just a simple creation of metal, it is a creation of life; he coddles each piece he forges as a mother would her child. Perfection was his philosophy. If, after rigorous inspection, he found an uncorrectable dent or scuff of any kind, the piece was useless and discarded. The newborn shield would live; it was flawless in Bron’s eyes.

“Bron,” the voice of his friend, Hammick, called behind him. “Erm, he’s back.”

Bron turned to behold Hammick, motioning toward the top of the long stair that leads to the cave networks that is Kaladim. There, descending at a calm but determined pace, were three, definitely non-Dwarfish figures.

“Lord deMuin,” Bron bowed slightly to the man in the center, as all three approached the Dwarf. “Pleased ta meetcha again.”

“Bron Hammerfall,” deMuin smiled and ever so slightly lowered his head, “likewise.” Lord Korus deMuin was the epitome of nobility. Young, sophisticated, handsome, and very, very wealthy. His father was a shipbuilder, the best on Antonica; and when he died, Korus got everything. He had always had a passion for ships and the sea, but his taste for money overshadowed all. Now, the actual work in his father’s company is actually run from inside of itself, while deMuin collects the income, making appearances only when necessary, or when his fascination with seagoing gets the better of him.

“So, erm, did ye like it?”

“Oh it is most acceptable.” Korus smiled, running his hand along the length of his long, black hair, fastened into a ponytail.

“Ehm, if ye don’t me askin’,” Bron began while cautiously eyeing his benefactor’s two companions, “who are they?”

“Oh, of course, how rude of me. These are my . . . ‘associates’.” Lord deMuin smiled wider. “Fen Tzu to my left.” A somewhat shorter man, draped head to toe in a black hood and robes, bowed to the Dwarf. Bron craned his neck to try to catch a glimpse of inside Fen Tzu’s hooded face, but he simply turned away. “And to my right is Magzug.” The other man, much taller than Korus, and dressed in ritualistic armor, raised his covered head slightly and cleared his throat. “Oh oh, forgive me – Magzug, Elder of the Swamp.”

“Dat’s better,” said Magzug in a deep, growling voice.

“A Troll?” Bron gasped.

“Yes, well, you know see the need for the hood. After all, Trolls are not the most welcome race here in Kaladim.”

“Aye, yeah, well, please ta meetcha.”

Lord deMuin waved his hand dismissively and continued. “I don’t have much time, unfortunately, so I will come straight to the point. Your prior . . . ‘artwork’ has far exceeded my expectations, and I would very much like to procure more. I’m sure you’ll find my offer more than generous, of course. Unless 1000 platinum pieces does not suit your fancy”

“Oh!” The Dwarf fidgeted in sudden desire. “Well, I’m sure I could whip up another one o’ those fer ya by tomorrow, and –“

“No no,” deMuin laughed quietly, “you see, the order I am putting in needs to be a little larger this time, say . . . fifty.”

Born almost choked as his eyes flew open wider. “Fifty?”

“You are up to the job, aren’t you?”

“Well, aye, but fifty of those . . . erm . . . things are gonna take some time, laddie. Can’t just whip that many out in a day.”

“Oh take your time, take your time. No rush. Well . . . not much of one, at least. Say, a fortnight from now?”

“Aye,” the Dwarfish craftsman said, scratching his head and staring at his forge, “a fortnight’d be enough time. Course, ummm . . .” Bron leaned in toward Korus and said in a lowered voice, “throw in a barrel o’ that fine whiskey ye shared with me before, and I’ll have em ready a few days early.”

Lord deMuin leaned down to the Dwarf and said with a wink and a smile, “It’s a deal.”

“Glad ta hear it, laddie. I’ll start on it right away!”

“Excellent! Now if you will excuse me, my associates and I need to be going. There’s much preparation to be done.”

Korus and his companions took their leave and started back up the long stairway. Hammick approached Bron again and said in a disdainful voice, “I don’t trust that daMooin’ guy any farther than I can toss him. He’s up to no good, Bron, you mark my words. Besides, you see the company he keeps, a stinkin’ Troll and that – that . . . whatever he is guy, all dressed in black robes.”

“Eh, maybe,” Bron shrugged. “But he could be Cazic-Thule Himself as long he makes me rich!”

Chapter 4: Metallurgy 101

In the morning’s light, Sara turned the small metal ball over and over in her fingers, fixated, as she lay sideways in her bed. But what is this? was the question that remained. There were dark red splotches on the ball’s surface, and what looked like tiny, almost miniscule scratches, partially obscured by one of the red spots.

Dropping the ball back into her pocket, Sara lifted herself out of bed and walked outside into the cool morning air. This time of day had always been her favorite – healed by the night, all was quiet and fresh. Walking through town, Sara watched as the rest of Kelethin awoke. Eventually, she found herself at the Healer’s Guild. Wendalyn’s body was no doubt inside now, being examined like a piece of meat under the watchful eyes of the city’s most revered physicians. Captain Swiftblade suddenly stepped outside, rubbing the exhaustion from his eyes.

“Oh,” Swiftblade perked up a little upon seeing Sara, “hello again, Saranaea. You’re up early.”

“Yeah, I just wanted to take a little walk.”

“You seem kind of glum. Is something wrong?”

“No,” she lied. “Just . . . thinking.”

“Ahh,” the Captain said quietly through a long yawn.

“So ummm, is Wendalyn in there?”

Swiftblade stopped suddenly and eyed Sara. “Yes she is . . . why?”

“Oh, no reason. Just curious is all.”

“Well, that’s no place for you. After Dr. Heim is finished, we’ll bury her properly.”

“Dr. who?”

Swiftblade silently admonished himself for his hasty words. “Erm, just someone from Antonica we called in to help. Look, we really shouldn’t be discussing this. I’m going to go get some sleep, I suggest you do the same. Good day.” The captain walked toward his house, looking back over his shoulder to make sure that Sara was not loitering around the guild’s entrance. What he did not see was the fact that, in the instant he turned his back to leave, Sara had ducked around the side of the building, watching through a tiny separation in the structure’s supports. After Swiftblade had finally disappeared out of sight, Sara darted into the guild entrance.

The Healer’s Guild was nothing like she expected. Sara expected high-arch ceilings, with elaborate candelabras and silken tapestries. Instead, the interior of the guild was quite similar to that of a general store. There were shelves upon shelves of containers and jars of unknown contents – no doubt a myriad of healing salves and potions. Several long, wooden tables were set along the shelves, which Sara guessed were used for the examination of patients. At the end of this large room was set of heavy double doors, guarded by two Champions. Curiosity getting the better of her, Sara sashayed toward the doors, but was predictably stopped by one of the sentries.

“Hold there, child. This chamber is off limits to everyone except guild officials. If you need medical aid, a healer will be with you shortly.”

Knowing it useless to argue, Sara wandered back into the main room and examined the mystery jars. Moments later, the double doors swung open and out stepped a Feir’Dal healer, followed by a middle-aged Human man with short, brown hair, interrupted by a few gray streaks. Sara stared in amazement, as this stranger was the first non-Wood Elf she had ever seen.

“Yes, I agree zat ze wound is definitely not zomething that ve have encountered before.” The Human was addressing the healer in a quite, very casual tone. “But I veel most assuredly be doing further research into zees poor girl’s death.” His accent was fascinating; Sara wondered if all Humans talked like this.

“Oh, why hello zere, little one,” the man smiled and bent down to Sara’s eye level. “And vut brings you here?”

“I – er, I’m just looking around.”

“Oh, vell, zees is a good place to zatisfy one's curiosity. So many fascinating things here. And vut is your name, may I ask?”

“Saranaea Enothie,” she replied, feeling immediately more comfortable with the stranger.

“Ah, pleased to meet you, Saranaea. Zuch a pretty name for a pretty face. I am Doctor Karl Zweiduschletsberg Heimelstratsberger. Erm, but you may just call me Dr. Heim.”

“I . . . don’t think I could say the whole thing anyway.”

Dr. Heim laughed and smiled at Sara again. With an expression of sudden remembrance, he reached into his pocket and pulled out a small, brightly wrapped object, and gave it to Sara. “Here. Zees is zome candy from back veer I live. I like it so much I bring zome vith me, but you may have it.”

“Thanks!” Sara’s eyes brightened as she looked over the vibrant pink wrapper excitedly – it was cylindrical, with strange, yet vaguely familiar writing on it. Antonica’s Finest were the only words she could make out. Opening the wrapper, Sara discovered a pale pink piece of candy inside, which she popped into her mouth and chewed.

“So how is it? Do you like it?”

“Ith goof.”

Dr. Heim snickered. “Yes, zey are a little chewy, eh? Vell, I vould very much like to ztay and talk vith you zome more, but I must go back to my hut. It vas very nice meeting you, Saranaea. Hopefully ve talk again before I go back home.” The doctor smiled again and took his leave. Sara swallowed the rest of the candy, which tasted remarkably like highly sweet strawberries, and darted after Dr. Heim.

“Wait! Mr. Heim! I mean, Doctor Heim!”

The doctor turned to see Sara running up to him. “Oh yes? Did I forget zomething?”

“No. I just . . . here!” Sara produced the metal ball from her pocket and showed it to the doctor.

“Vhy, hello. Vut have ve here?”

“I –“ her face grew flush with embarrassment, “I found it at the murder site.”

Dr. Heim raised a concerned eyebrow at her. “Oh no, my child. Zey made you go there and zee zat horrible thing?”

“No, I went myself. I snuck out of Kelethin and followed the Captain and Aelwyl.”

“And I suppose you’re not really Dr. Valdin?” asked Sara, turning to Ordon’s friend. “Markus, right?”

“That’s right,” he responded with a solemn nod. “Markus Runecraft.”

Sara took in a deep breath and calmed herself slightly. “Just, please . . . tell me the truth. What’s going on?”

Ordon sat down across from his young guest and began:

“Many years ago, Sara – almost a hundred – Markus’s and my grandfathers help to found an organization – The Everwatch Society. It was somewhat of a joke at first, a social club, if you will. Our two grandfathers were boyhood friends, both trained scholars in the arcane arts, and as such, they befriended other like-minded scholars. The closest of these friends were initiated and sworn, again in jest, to oaths of secrecy.

“For years, their little ‘secret society’ thrived. While still in good fun, our grandfathers and the other founding members solidified the rules until they became unbreakable decree: only hand-selected persons may become members; only ten members may be allowed to take active part in the Society per term, for a term of five years; once you were a member, you were one for life, and you were still held by all Society rules, even after your term had ended; you were never to mention the existence of the Society, nor were you to identify yourself or a fellow member as being part of the Society to outsiders.

“Anyway, our grandfathers grew up, married, and even had children. Various friends came and went over the years, and some of the rules were bent from time to time. But through it all, the founding members stayed true to each other and to the Society.

“One day, when my father was about your age in human years, Sara, great danger threatened the very existence of Freeport. From across the seas, a horrible new race of the continent of Kunark, landed on the shores of Norrath. Of course, no one knew the extent of their invasion at the time, but later we learned that they had brought war to Faydwer as well. But no, we Antonicans bore the main brunt of their aggression.”

“Who were they?” Sara asked, now listening intently.

“They called themselves the Iksar – a reptilian race of half man, half lizard. Covered head to toe in hard scales and armor made of metals unknown to us, normal arrows and blades had little affect on them. The Iksar also brought with them exotic forms of magic and psionic powers. They even seemed to have the ability to control vicious beats as though they were common pets.

“For over a month, the city of Freeport held off the Iksar invasion, but with each passing day their grip on the city weakened. It was only a matter of time before the city fell. This is where The Everwatch Society came in.

“Having a vast resource of arcane knowledge at their disposal, the Society members frantically poured over ancient tomes and scrolls for anything that may help. For sleepless days, this research continued, until finally, literally within the last moments of Freeport’s defense, a possible answer was discovered.

“It was Markus’s and my fathers whom discovered the ancient spell. Centuries ago, a nameless Erudite mage discovered the art of assimilation. By practicing this magic, one could absorb the force of their attacker’s spell and redirect it at their will.”

“So they basically they gave the Iksar a taste of their own medicine?” Sara asked.

“Exactly. Our fathers spread the word of this to every arcane practitioner defending the city that they could. Desperate for some kind of weapon that could be used against the Iksar, the arcane warriors began to use the lost Erudite magic.

“The effects were immediate and overwhelming. Horrified, the Iksar saw their ranks being decimated by their very own, cruel magic. When it became clear that the tides had turned completely in Freeport’s favor, the panicked Iksar fled back in their ships. Days later, the smaller assault force on Faydwer followed suit – no doubt in fear that word of our new weapon would spread there, as well. Vigil was kept for many days afterwards, but the Iksar had fled entirely, with no signs of return.

“Our fathers were hailed as heroes. But through it all, they (perhaps taking the Society even more seriously than their fathers) upheld the Society’s secrecy.”

“So if it was all supposed to be a big joke,” Sara began, still somewhat confused, “why all the secrecy still?”

“Well,” Markus picked up the story, as Ordon stood to stretch his legs, “after the invasion, the city of Freeport more or less went back about its normal routine . . . but The Everwatch Society went through more drastic changes. It was no longer just a social club meant for its founding members’ amusement – it was now an order sworn to the protection of Freeport and its allies from any threat, be it internal or external. Furthermore, professions other than the arcane were allowed to join, as were those not born from the founding members’ blood – to truly defend one’s home, one must call upon all resources available. But paramount to all, the oaths of secrecy were strictly enforced. To divulge the secrets of The Watch to the wrong ears may very well spell disaster for the defense of Freeport.

“Besides, some of Freeport’s most powerful and influential citizens are members of The Watch, and I assure you they are in no hurry to lose their status or power.”

“And that,” concluded Ordon, “is what has brought us all here at this table.” By now, the morning sun’s rays had illuminated almost the entire room. “I’m sorry we had to lie to you this whole time, Sara, but I hope you can see from what I’ve just told you that it was necessary.”

Sara said nothing at first, prompting Ordon to continue, “If you wish to leave now, I understand. I’m sure you feel betrayed, and you have every right to be – I just wanted you to know the reasons for that betrayal.”

“I don’t,” the young Wood Elf hesitated on her words, “I don’t feel betrayed anymore. I’m just glad to know the truth.”

A look of instant relief brightened the faces of Ordon and Markus. “That’s good to hear, Sara. I was afraid our friendship would end, and we would part ways forever.”

“How could I? You’ve been nothing but kind to me ever since I’ve met you. All of you have been. You’ve shown me people and places I would probably never have seen otherwise. But . . . I do have a few questions.”

“Of course,” Ordon motioned Sara to begin.

“First of all, Markus, you said that people other than those born to the founders were allowed into The Watch. Does that mean that Cygna is a member, too?”

“Yes she is,” nodded Markus. “And her real name is Cygnatia. I told her to pick something further from her true name, but hey . . . women.”

“Yeah, why did you guys change your names, anyway? I mean, doesn’t everyone in Freeport know who you are?”

“Yes,” answered Ordon, “but we felt it best to do when in the company of outsiders. That’s something that Cygnatia, Markus and I started. No one else in the history of The Watch has ever done that.”

“And what’s with that weird feeling I get whenever I’m around you guys?” Sara asked, barely skipping a beat.

“Weird feeling?” Ordon and Markus looked at each other quizzically.

“Yeah, it’s like whenever I’m near any of you, I feel really peaceful and drawn to you, like you can do no wrong. Aelwyl felt it, too.”

“Ooooh, well,” the two Watch members laughed uncomfortably at each other, “that’s just a little extra insurance spell we keep on ourselves. It’s something we conjured up to help us keep others less aggressive and curious around us. Glad to see it’s working . . . well, except for the curiosity part, which seems to have little affect on you, Sara.”

“Wow, you guys really went through a lot of trouble for all this.”

“If you knew what was a stake,” Markus said seriously, “you’d understand.”

“And beyond that, it . . .” Ordon searched for words, “it just feels refreshing to know you’re part of something larger than yourself. Something you care for and believe in so much that you are willing to give your life for, if it means that another or many others can live.”

“Hmm, something larger than yourself . . .” Sara trailed off into introspection.

“What’s that, Sara?”

“Oh nothing,” Sara returned to the conversation, “it’s just, I imagine that’s a nice feeling to have.”

“It is,” responded Ordon, eyeing the Wood Elf curiously and nodding. “Very much so.”

The morning sun had now completely shown its soft, warm face. Ordon and Markus let Sara sit undisturbed for a short time while everything they told her sank in.

Finally, Ordon broke the silence by asking, “Well, I for one would like some breakfast, who else would?”

Sara’s gurgling stomach answered the question immediately thereafter. Eggs, pork sausages, and potatoes were served with chilled milk from the Gnomish cooling device. Part way through the meal, Ordon asked Sara, “So, I assume this means that you will be staying the remainder of your visit, as planned?”

Sara looked at both Ordon and Markus, then took each of their hands and smiled. “You’ve all been like family to me, including Cygna. So it turns out you had different names – that doesn’t really change anything, you’re still the same people. Of course I’ll stay.”

Chapter 12: Homecoming

The few remaining days of Sara’s Freeport stay had extinguished like the flame of a candle snuffed out for the morning sun. Now, Ordon, Cygna, Markus and she all stood before the Freeport portal, gently shimmering a clear, sky blue in the late morning sun.

“Well, Sara,” said Ordon, putting a hand on his young guest’s shoulder, “looks like this is goodbye.”

Sara looked up misty eyed at the man who had been like a father to her for over a month and shook her head. “No, no don’t say that. Not goodbye, more like . . . so long, until next time.”

Ordon chuckled. “Of course, of course. I suppose we do plan to have you back sometime. You’ve been a joy to all of us.”

Sara momentarily left Ordon’s grasp and stepped up to Cygna, who knelt down and tightly embraced the young Wood Elf. “Cygna, I’ve always thought of you as my older sister. I hope you and Markus have a long life together. I promise I’ll visit you every chance I get.”

“And I’ll hold you to that, Sara,” Cygnatia’s voice began to crack. “Ordon’s right – you’ve been a joy.”

Sara smiled up at her “big sister” for a moment before moving to Markus.

“I’m sorry I wasn’t as close to you as I suppose I should have been during our time together, Sara.”

“It’s okay,” reassured Sara as she embraced Markus’s waist, “it’s like I told you that one night, you’re all family to me. You better take care of my big sister there, Markus.”

“I will,” Markus replied, desperately fighting back a tear. “And you take care of yourself, Sara. I want to see you safe and healthy when you come back to see us again.”

Sara rejoined Ordon, who said, “I’ll accompany you back into Kelethin – that will give us a little more time together. Besides, I’d very much like to bid Aelwyl farewell.”

“Okay,” Sara beamed through her tears of parting. Just as she and Ordon began to turn to the portal, the familiar, melodic twang of a lute. “Oh no . . .” Sara muttered under her breath.

“Well well,” chuckled Markus sarcastically, “it looks like there’s one more friend you have yet to say goodbye to.”

“Ugh,” groaned Sara to Ordon, “Come on, let’s go, quick!”

“So it’s true,” sighed Redd Bluetone. “You really are leaving.”

“Yeah,” Sara blushed, responding somewhat meekly. “Umm . . . sorry.”

“Without seeking me out to say good-bye?” Redd dramatically clutched his chest. “Like an arrow to my heart.”

“Sorry. I uhh, I – I didn’t know where you were.”

“I am wherever the winds of your heart take me, M’Lady.”

Markus choked back a laugh, with Cygna having to give him a sharp jab in the ribs to keep him quiet. Redd plucked a short, resonating tune while humming softly along. When finished, he held out an open palm, into which a small, golden light coalesced. Closing his hand, he took Sara’s and turned it upward. Gently setting down whatever had collected in his hand, Redd closed Sara’s fingers into a fist and leaned in for a kiss on her cheek so quickly, that the young Wood Elf had no time to shy away. Without another word, Redd made his trademark exit.

Sara, still flush with embarrassment and flattery, opened her hand and looked down into it. Still warm as the moist kiss on her cheek, a small, golden rose-shaped charm lay sparkling on her copper-hued skin.

* * *

The Faydark wizard spires rushed to life like a sudden summer wind. Through it, stepped Sara and Ordon with large, hand-woven packs slung over their shoulders. Through the dozen or so people going to and fro from the spire’s base, Sara spotted her godmother, approaching with a smile. Sara set her pack down and embraced Aelwyl tightly.

“Welcome back, Saranaea. Did you have a good visit?”

“It was wonderful! I’ll have to tell you all about it when we get back home.”

Ordon picked up the bag Sara had set down and handed it back to her. “It vas quite a charm having Sara vith us. She is zuch an exceptional young voman.”

“Still speaking in that accent, Ordon?” Aelwyl said with a raised eyebrow.

Sara and Ordon froze.

“How did you know?” Ordon asked, absolutely aghast.

“I’ve known this life for many ages of Man, Ordon. I have many friends in many corners of Norrath. But come – we’ll talk about it more back in Kelethin.”

Aelwyl called upon the Great Winds of Tunare, and a tunnel of air enveloped the three, carrying them back to the city high in the treetops.

“So, how did you find out?” Ordon asked Aelwyl almost the moment they stepped inside her home.

Aelwyl gently waved her hand, prompting a pot of water on her stove to begin to boil. “When Sara left with you, I must confess, I was still somewhat curious as to what it is I felt you were hiding. So, I began to inquire at the Healer’s Guild. There, they all only knew you as Dr. Heim, expert in metallurgy and the arts of crime investigation.” The hierophant sat down at her table with a slight, pained exhale.

“Are you ok, Aelwyl?” Sara asked, approaching her godmother with concern.

“Yes,” responded Aelwyl with a smile, “just the years catching up to me.” With another wave of the hand, the druidess summoned the boiling pot off of her stove and commanded it to pour three cups of warm tea. Offering a cup to Ordon, which he took graciously, Aelwyl continued. “Anyway, one of the guild leaders said he knew someone in Freeport that may be able to help me more. So, I went quietly into Northern Freeport one day and spoke with an arcane scholar named Fedrus Scriber.”

“Hmm, ah yes,” muttered Ordon with a tone of disappointment, “Fickle Fedrus – should have known he’d be the first to cave.”

“Cave?” Aelwyl asked with a quizzical look. “About what? All he told me was that he knew you as Ordon Kanner, one of the local arcane masters.”

Ordon sat back in his chair with the dreadful feeling one gets when a deep secret may have been accidentally revealed. “And did he happen to mention anything else? Any suspicions or rumors about my character?”

“No,” the elder Wood Elf eyed her guest with a most peculiar look, “why? Are you wanted by the authorities?”

“Oh, no no,” Ordon laughed. “Nothing like that.” He looked over at Sara and then back to Aelwyl, “Well, I suppose there’s no sense in keeping from you, as I’m sure you’ll just find out eventually. Heh, I now see where Sara gets it from.”

“But Ord!” Sara interjected. “I thought you said –“

“Please, it’s okay, Sara. It’ll be fine.”

Ordon began to tell Aelwyl a similar story to what he had told Sara, except this time leaving out as many specific details and names as possible. Three cups of warm tea later, he finished, ready for the doubtless onslaught of questions Aelwyl would undoubtedly ask. But unlike her goddaughter, Aelwyl just sat with a contemplative smile and a nodding of her head.

“So, this is why you changed your speech and your name?”

“Yes, I’m sorry I had to be so evasive and untruthful, but, like I told Sara, I felt it necessary."

Aelwyl’s gentle smile broaden. “I hold no ill will toward you, Ordon. I do thank you for being truthful, though. And I too must ask your forgiveness for sneaking around inquiring about you, but I only hope you understand that it was all for Sara. As you know, she is like a daughter to me, and I have no desire to see any harm come to her."

“Hey,” Ordon flicked a dismissive hand, “don’t worry about it, we all have things we are ashamed of, but that doesn’t mean we should let them ruin our lives."



The tea had run dry, and the afternoon sun was beginning to climb higher. Ordon felt it was time for him to take his leave.

“Well, Sara,” Ordon knelt down, embracing the young Wood Elf, “again I thank you for the time you’ve spent with me, but we all have our lives to lead. Of course you are welcome back anytime.”

Ordon stood and faced Aelwyl, “as are you, M’Lady.”

The hierophant embraced him and kissed him softly on the cheek. “Yes, I would very much like to one day return to Freeport. Perhaps Sara and I can come visit together.”

With the aid of the Great Winds, Aelwyl and Ordon traveled back to the Faydark Spires, leaving Sara to reflect on her time with Ordon, and the exciting prospect of returning, forming new memories.
