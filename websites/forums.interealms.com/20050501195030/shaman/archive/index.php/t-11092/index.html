mormegil
Last night, I crawled out of the "kos to skyshrine" hole yet again. It's amazing how forgiving those dragons are considering how often I've flayed their boss. Really, tho, it dawned on me just how overly simplistic EQ faction and how good it could have been.

I have long maintained a love-hate relationship with EQ's rather innovative faction system. But, as much as it defined what faction is in online games, it needs a lot of refining. It's too late for EQ,
but perhaps in a future game.

The central problem is that there's not enough repercussion for your actions -- it's just too easy to swing factions. I've killed Dain like 30 times. Should I ever be able to make amends? Probably
not, let alone by killing a bunch of worker-level giants. econdarily, since there is no logical communication between zones, how are my faciton actions conveyed. How do those guards in Firiona Vie know I killed pirates in OOT? Sorry, I just don't like it.

Here's a few things I would like to see ("Team EQ2" take notes :) )

1) Illusions should work like SoS in most cases -- unless the mobs can see through the illusion. From an RP perspective, it
makes sense. How can you know that somebody killed 10,000 Crushbone orcs if you don't recognize who they are? In return, one person I know suggested you take away the physical benefits conferred by illusions. Does it make sense that someone can fly because somebody thinks they are an elemental?

2) An extended attack should represent a faciton hit. If you beat a mob to within an inch of death, zone out, zone back in he/she really shouldn't like you. I understand the need to create an "out" in case someone accidentally attacks an NPC. But, if you worked it as a percentage of total HP, it wouldn't be an issue.

3) If you engage in a sustained attack or kill a mob with a faction, you become temporarily kos to all mobs on that faction in sight or earshot in that zone. In some cases, if they are a higher race, the area may be wider, representing a regional or zonewide alert. If you're max ally to Thurgardin but kill 10 guards at the zonein, but you're technically (numerically) just dubious, you still should be kos if you're walking around covered in dwarf blood. "He's a
legendary giant killer, so what if he killed a dozen of us, he's a OK guy still." Wrong.

4) If you attack and kill a mob you do NOT suffer a permanent faction hit. You become regionally kos, as explained. Kill all those mobs that saw or heard of the deed and you have no faction loss, because there's no one to tell the tale. But, if you zone out while some mobs still have you flagged kos, you suffer a permanent hit. They just told everyone. This forces you to err on the side of murder and mayhem.

5) Different attack types have a preset "noise" level. A knife in the back or a wasting plague is not going to alert nearly as many dwarfs as a comet of ice falling out of the sky. This rewards those who do it with stealth.

6) While you don't lose faction for killing a mob -- you just suffer the temporary kos syndrome, as defined above -- you don't get full exp either. You can also get permanent faction hits for basic kills by turning in an item dropped off those faction mobs, plus you get exp. Turn in 20 dwarf ears to a troll NPC, for example, get a bit of exp (the incentive to take the hit) and the faction hits. The exp is the carrot. This way, as you're now renowned as a dwarf killer, there's some reason for you to take action to be kos. Again, of course, you're already likely to be kos anyway since some faction mobs are bound to see your actions.

7) All mobs that run near death now run to a zone. If they make it to the zone, they poof and you get a faction hit. They just told their friends and the whole world you're a dwarf killer.

8) If you list in bazaar certain items dropped of specific factioned mobs or items only from one specific facitoned mob, you take
a faction hit. Selling a crown of the froglok kings? Guess what, the secret froglok network will find out, be none too happy and you will take a hit. This is your reward for peddling in blood!

9) Certain items from high-level boss-type mobs, no matter what your reputation is, confer kos status both temporarily and permanently when you wear them. You wanna wear a belt of the dwarf slaying? Don't visit Thurgardin. If you zone into Thurg wearing the WWF-like belt, are seen and word is spread, and then zone out, guess what? You're now known for
your regicide. Welcome to kos. So, wearing the belt makes you temporarily kos to Thurg faction; if you're seen wearing it and you allow word to get it out, you're permanently kos.

Anyway, just my ramblings. Fassade on Fennin contributed directly and indirectly to my thoughts here. So, if you hate this, hate her too!

weissblau
In the Roleplay vs Gameplay debate I'm solidly on the Gameplay side. A more "realistic" factioning scheme would throw up barriers, barriers that do exist in real life but are no fun in a game.

For example, you'd have to choose a side and stick with it. Want to do a quest on the other side? Level up a new character, or just quit and give up on that content.

Under the current system, you'd spend a week grinding faction (woohoo SOE makes another $3) and then a couple of months exploring the new opportunities now open to them (another $30) and perhaps grind back ($3). Instead of a dead end, you've squeezed another 3 months out of EQ and experienced more of the content.

In reality, if the faction system was more complex then there would be factions that EVERYONE would have to keep and other factions that NOBODY would need/want to keep. i.e. the mobs you kill and the mobs you don't kill. The developers wouldn't put the effort into decent quests on mobs on both sides of the faction since half the player base wouldn't have access to those quests.
