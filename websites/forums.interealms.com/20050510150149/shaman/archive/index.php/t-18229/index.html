Sounds to me like they are sacred bundles from Native American religious practices (use is paticularly prevalent among Northwestern tribes). These are generally filled with artifacts (feathers, pipes, etc. of some significance) and wrapped tightly. They are believed to provide spiritual power, which often manifests itself in household protection, healing, etc. As such, it's not terribly uncommon, it's my understanding, to see some of the minor bundles used in the manner mentioned (er...doctoral student specializing in American History-- mainly religious movements-- by the way). Now it's unclear to me wheter these belonged to a Native American family, were adopted by a Neo-pagan group (You'll see New Age groups like Wiccan commonly adopt practices and rituals of Native American groups. I'm not sure if bundles are among them, for varying groups), were considered decorative (many Native American religious artifacts have been turned into commodities to be consumed by American culture) or whether I'm misreading the situation entirely. Medicine bundles tend to be transferred between family or tribe members until they are believed to have lost their power. How to dispose of them is a trickier matter. I would imagine the best way is to burn them or bury them. The means vary among tribes, it's my understanding. Not sure if this was helpful or not. Kanati
Actually, I think that saying it's all about "communion with spirits" is the right sentiment, but not precisely the way I'd like to see it put. Native American tribes, traditionally-as far as we understand anyway, tried to live in a sort of balance or harmony with nature. This did not always mean they were "friendly" with Nature, a fact that often gets lost in New Age romanticism of Native American religions. Several tribes, take for example the southeastern Cherokee and Creek, would say a prayer upon a creature such as a bear after they killed it. It was not done as an act of communion, but rather out of fear that the bear's spirit would haunt them and cause harm to them. Similarly, when we think of things such as medicine bundles (I'm not sure spirit pouch is the right word), we're really talking about things that were put together for purposes of granting the bearer power of various sorts. There are bundles aimed at granting the bearer hunting power, healing power, fertility, protection, etc. You'll see the same phenomenon in regard to dreams and visions. They are sought after because they provide power, which equals status in the tribe, power over the enemies, etc.

Kal bring's up a good point in saying that these bundles are not necessarilly Native American in origin. I alluded to this, but focused on the former thought mainly because the indigenous origins is what occurred to me. Still, it's possible you'll see these things used among Asian or Eastern European family (though I would think the latter far far less likely). Still, you have shamanic groups in these regions that construct similar bundles. As Kal said, some Asian religions (often most prevalent among syncretic or "hybrid groups" like Yao or other Daoist types) rely on household protection types of things relatively extensively. I'm really not all that well versed on these groups, so I won't comment a lot further. Still, I'm not entirely convinced that looking at their contents, unless you just happen to have a real keen eye for this type of thing, is going to reveal too terribly much to you. I suppose a feather or a wilted lotus leaf might reveal something to you. Regardless, she shouldn't be too worried about taboo. Minor bundles can generally be opened from time to time, if done with reverence, or so it's my understanding.

Anyway, enough of this nonsense... I'm truly shocked that someone would e-mail these type of questions to you.
