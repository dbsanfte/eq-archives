Personally I think some of the stigma that ID5 is suffering is because of the way it works, not because it's not working well enough.

With LR5 you get missed completely. This is EXTREMELY noticable. You look at the swings when you get aggro and laugh to yourself as the mob misses you a ton more than they used to. It's a great ability, and I still definitely recommend picking it up before ID5.

ID5, however, is still very worthwhile imho. I've noticed a huge difference since picking it up. Here's the problem though, you have to look closely to really see the difference. This is because where with LR5 you get hit less per round, with ID5 you get hit FOR less per round. ID5 cuts ~20% off of each swing from a mob, but the mob still hits you.

I know for myself, at least, it's sometimes hard to keep up with the red text scrolling by from mobs beating on me. Trust me though, this skill works, and works well.

I clearly remember being in High Risk LDoN missions before, and after getting this ability (already had LR5 before ID5). When drawing aggro without ID5, I would get misses sometimes thanks to LR5, but the majority of the attacks still landed, and when they did, they hit HARD. A shaman's AC and mitigation aren't high enough to stop the mob from hitting for max very often. These things were smacking me for 998 at least once out of their double/tripple attack when they hit me. After picking up ID5, the average hit is NOTICABLY lower. They hit me for minimum damage a lot more often, and almost never hit for anything above 800 (max of 998).

Basically, in a long fight, LR5 and ID5 prevent identical amounts of damage. The reason LR5 is so much better for us is that we aren't usually supposed to be tanking a mob for a long period of time. What we're concerned about is that 10 seconds that you steal aggro and the tank takes it back. For that, LR5 is immensely better, because if you get missed twice for a 1330 point swing, that's 2660 damage you've avoided. While if you reduce 20% off of the, say 4, swings that would have come in, you are only mitigating 1040 damage. You can see, though, that these numbers would come closer and closer together the longer you held aggro during a fight.

Oh, and for the comment about our mitigation not being so great, and a 20% gain wouldn't do much: Remember, it's mitigating 20% of the Mobs' damage, not adding a 20% boost to our mitigation ability. Just like LR5 means you avoid 20% of mob attacks, you don't gain a 20% boost to our avoidance skill.

I don't have any parses, and it's hard to explain the effectiveness of the abillity, but I swear by it, personally. I tank mobs in Sol Ro when the mood strikes, and I watch their average hit wind up at about 300ish(max 680), and I know the ability is working for me. I get aggro in high risk LDoN and watch them miss and hit for 250 half the time. It's improved my survivability a lot.

Now...to finish SCS so I can stop getting aggro in the first place. :cool:

Romidar
LR & ID do not decrease damage by the same amount or in the same way.

ID increases your "chance to mitigate" a hit to be lower than it was going to be. That number still has to be somewhere between the minimum and maximum hit of the mob. What this means is that the effect of Innate Defense and Combat Stability will vary by the mob you're fighting. That is, for mobs with low Damage Intervals and high Damage Bonuses, ID and CS are almost completely worthless. For mobs with high DI and low DB, the skills will be much more effective.

You cannot mitigate away the damage bonus of a mob, but you can avoid it. That's one of the reasons that the avoidance skills (CA & LR) are better than the mitigation skills (CS & ID).

The two skills do not have an equal effect even in the long run, as has been parsed on multiple boards. It's still certainly nice to mitigate more often, but I'd consider the skills a very low priority for non-melee classes. For melee classes, getting an increase to mitigation will reduce the number of max damage hits and therefore reduces the chance of being one rounded. If you can still be one or two rounded when the mob is hitting for minimum or near minimum damage, then the mitigation skills are not very helpful at all.

As for your second comment - the case you describe is EXACTLY where the avoidance skill would prove MUCH more beneficial than the mitigation skill.

Imagine a case where a mob hits for 1500 minimum and that the target has only 5000 HP.

If we assume a base "miss" rate of 40%, that person will one rounded at least 12.96% of the time. That is, even if the mob hit ONLY for minimum damage, that person would die 12.96% of the time in a single round (that's how often all 4 blows would land).

If we assume a 15% increase to avoidance (CA3 + LR5), that's a 46% miss rate instead of 40% and that person will die 8.5% of the time in a single round to that mob (that's how often all 4 blows would land).

Imagine the somewhat silly case where a SINGLE MINIMUM HIT from a mob will kill you - in that case, your chances of survival when the mob aims one hit at you go from 40% to 46% as a result of having the skill. The mitigation skills would do nothing in this case.

For mobs with very large DB's and lower DI's, mitigation skills just don't help that much. It's the same story of how AC in Luclin didn't help much - monks were great tanks because avoidance was much more important than mitigation. PoP and GoD shift that so that AC (and the mitigation skills) matters. Whether you get more benefit out of one or the other depends very much on what mob you are fighting.

I believe others have already mentioned it, but one of the issues also is that even if the damage reduction were the same between the two skills, it is still much better to avoid being hit than to be hit for less.
