There are some distinct differences between Intel and AMD chips. Short version: The Intel chips are superior for intensive processing, but significantly more expensive. The AMD chipset is usualy easier to tweak/overclock.

Ungerargh
In order of importance (all of these are important, however):

Memory
Graphics Card
Motherboard
CPU

For memory, just get tons of it, preferably the DDR SDRAM that takes full advantage of 400 MHz FSB. 1 GB minimum. If you ever want to run two accounts on one machine, get at least 1.5 GB. Get single sticks of larger size when possible, as that usually is more reliable.

Graphics Card: I agree with what another poster said on the $200-$300 range. There's no point in buying a $400 graphics card (unless it has some special features that you need for something else). Likewise, be careful going below $200. For reference, I'm relatively sure my Ti4600 is not the bottleneck in my system (I only have 512 MB RAM).

Motherboard: Do not scrimp on a motherboard. You may see a $75 motherboard and a $150 motherboard with the same specs, and wonder what the difference is. The difference is, the $150 motherboard, assuming it comes from a reputable company, is likely made with a better process. Generally, what this amounts to is more layers of silicon in the board, means the traces on the board have more z-axis to work with, so they will tend to be shorter, meaning a more reliable supply of power to key components (especially memory). I didn't know this when I built my last computer, and ended up with a motherboard that uses a 4-layer process when the design spec for the chipset calls for a 6-layer process; although it has four memory slots, I can only use two of them because otherwise it cannot supply enough power (and yes, I've verified that it's not a power supply issue).

CPU: Anything within reason. Generally 2 GHz and above should be plenty. Your hard drive, memory, and graphics card have far more potential to bog down your system than your CPU.
