This is always a very touchy subject. On the one hand, the spirit of the law is based on a practical principle. On the other hand, the letter of the law allows broad-based application far outside the intended scope of the legislation. It becomes a matter of do nothing and allow for the possibility of a major catastrophy in which millions could die or do something and allow for the possibility of thousands, perhaps millions being detained or otherwise negatively impacted under the guise of "the greater good."

One thing to keep in mind is that almost any position taken "for the greater good" can be reasonably justified. One could give compelling arguments for "unlawful search and seizure" under certain scenarios. Indeed, some legislation to that effect has already been passed in wake of September 11. It is also important to realize that these types of bills are typically written by someone who is very passionate about the topic at hand. As such, often they are blinded to the possible negative implications of what they perceive as addressing a just cause.

The real question is not how the law would be applied today, but how it will be interpreted and applied 50 years from now by people who aren't products of the socio-political climate of today. "Framers' Intent" is a very difficult thing to judge after sufficient time has passed.

Any law can be abused. That is why we have three branches of government. The legislature to make laws, the judciary to evaluate laws and the executive branch to enforce the laws.

There are many circumstances where a person can be held without charges: material witnesses, sequestered jurors, commitment to a mental institution, and in many states there are already forced quarantene laws.

What you are apparently proposing, Kullaas, is that the government not be given power that they might abuse. What about the power to declare war or the power to launch a nuclear weapon, might they not abuse those powers? The entire text of this bill is not on the website, but read what's there. This does not give one person the right to take over the state of Maine. What it does is give the Department of Health and Human Services the necessary authoriy to contain a disease when the Governor has declared a state of public health emergnecy. It hasn't even been passed yet, and it is going through review by the judiciary committee, fiscal committes and others to be certain that if it does pass, it will meet all constitututional requirements, and that all information necessary during the emergency will be available to the public and that health professionals have a clear line of communication.

If you don't give the government the necessary authority they need to respond to a major health crisis, what are they to do? What if pneumonic plague broke out in the US, do you think all citizens would voluntarily stay in their homes? If you do, you're wrong. Without laws allowing for special circumstances, the government would have two choices 1) allow the epidemic to spread naturally, or 2) take illeagal measures 3) hold an emergency session to pass new, hurried laws.

I prefer to make plans ahead of time for who will have the authority and how long it will last. That way we're not trying to rush through anything. Bad laws get made in panic times, and THAT'S what brings dictators to power.
