One of the things that this reminds me of, was the whole Ebonics question. If it's too hard to learn American English, then let's just make up a grammar without rules or structure. Forget that this is the language that gave us Hemingway, Steinbeck, Melvile, Thoreau and Twain. Twain you say? Wasn't everything Huckleberry Finn saying colloquial? Yes, but almost all novels of the period were full of flowery speech and prose, where Mark Twain broke through that to show that there was a beauty in the language of the common man. But even after creating the first truly American novel, Twain didn't abandon structure.

Abraham Lincoln's most famous speech was such a breakthrough because of the same principle. All speeches made in his time tended to be very long affairs and flowery. When he gave The Gettrysberg Address, people were stunned. It was incredibly short and to the point, without all the fluff they were used to. We still feel the impact of that speech in oral communication today. But Lincoln never abandoned grammar for expediency or effect.

My personal pet peeve...

acronym - A word formed from the initial letters of a name, such as "NASA" meaning National Aeronautics and Space Administration, or by combining initial letters or parts of a series of words, such as "radar" for radio detecting and ranging. Scuba, snafu and fubar are also all acronyms.

abbreviation - A shortened form of a word or phrase used chiefly in writing to represent the complete form, such as MA or Mass. for Massachusetts or USMC for United States Marine Corps.

Simply put, if it makes a pronounceable word, it should be refered to as an acronym. If it doesn't, it's an abbreviation. Acronyms are abbreviations, but not all abbreviations are acronyms.

SoW, CoM and HoT are EQ acronyms. JBB, REIF, DL, and VT are abbreviations. "Lammy" is a slang term (or "C: none of the above").

I see EQ as being a lot like the military or the computer industry. It's so different then "real life" that it develops its own vernacular and/or slang. Words, phrases and ideas are often used out of context to describe things. Examples of this are "training", "bunny-jumping" or "c**k blocking". The terms themselves would be almost unknown outside the context of EQ, but are still totally out of context within the game. Training has nothing (or little) to do with instruction or locomitives, rabbits or actually jumping has nothing to do with bunny-jumping and the male member itself, while it may be a penis thing, is not actually used in c**k blocking.

If the idea is properly conveyed, I don't really see it as improper. Why? Because it would be too cumbersome to describe it in "proper" terms, so it's given one of its own. And in some cases, there is no "proper" term for it.

What it comes down to is that English, or any modern language for that matter, changes. There are new words added to dictionaries every year, and old words removed. Who calls a "Q-tip" a cotton swab or a "bandaid" an adheasive bandage? Dictionaries in the 1950's make no mention of a "walkman".
