At 52nd lvl in PoP, odds are you're gonna get smooshed often, it's a level thing. Slow will get resisted a lot, root will break early, your heal is pitiful for the damage that the mobs do. It can be fun but you aren't gonna be pulling off any miracles. Your best friend in that kind of a situation is probably mal-x (or an enchanter that isn't scared to tash early). Once malo lands, slowing isn't a problem, root will last longer, you'll basically own the mob. If you have too much trouble with resists on that, odds are you couldn't have slowed it anyway and would just have caught agro and gotten eaten. In a really tough spot, you might even want to load your best 2 malo spells and alternate em till one sticks. Go after the mobs one at a time; malo, slow to pull it off to the side, root it, and start on the next one. Also, depending on gear, maybe see about swapping out some wisdom gear for ac/hitpoints. 900 buffed ac should probably be a minimum for Tier 1 planes.

If you want to do some fun crowd control at that level, take a group to Sebilis, Chardok, or the Hole. It's a lot easier when mobs are hitting for 140 max instead of 300 :-). I remember Protectors of Zek in Kael Arena being my person nemesis around your level. 3 or 4 deaths per trip was normal back then, and I still enjoy heading back there for payback! Make a list and get revenge once you get to 60.

shaman CC just takes practice (i use instill or immobilize, btw). go group up with a monk somewhere and have them pull 2 or 3 so you can get the feel for it.

- If you're in an open area with few roamers, root one, have monk back up, root the next, back up monk, slow mob still beating on monk. slow other 2 and get back to medding.

- in a dungeon, can do the same as above if there's room, otherwise monk should /disc stonestance, you aggro 1 with slow, pull it away, root it, slow the one the monk is fighting, aggro other with a sup heal (he'll probably need it by now), root then slow and get back to medding.

the number of mobs a shaman can CC is basically only limited by the amount of space you have and the duration of your roots.
