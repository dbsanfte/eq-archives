I agree with Land, Enkebesh, and many others in the game that you shouldn't have to be in the best guild on the server to get your spells -- or even close.

The game has been run and designed for the benefit of those who already have the most and can bust down every door the quickest for most of its duration; sometimes startlingly so. However, those folks have actually always been the tiny minority of people in the game. That bespeaks imbalance in game design and a pretty flawed idea of designing customer satisfaction into the product -- how you make people keep buying expansions, playing, and recommending the game to others. It will be increasingly quite necessary to make the majority, not merely the minority, feel the game offers adequate reward in terms of accomplishment and enjoyment.

At this point, the game is long enough in the tooth that a great number of people are already 60 or over, sometimes with two or three characters that level. Eventually, there will be relatively few people who aren't that high. If all these people must be in the very best guilds to get their spells, many will quit. And they will certainly tell their friends not to bother with the game. It's not like there aren't a lot of other entertainment options out there, many directly competitive with the game already. If all those people have to fight with both the best-equipped, most raid-oriented guilds to get the mobs that drop their spells(and weapons, armor, what have you), the game world will be a very angry and dissatisfied place. And the developers will not be doing their jobs.

Eventually, everyone will want to be eating the uber's lunches. The people in the strongest guilds would rather have everyone else remain as weak as possible, usually -- that mentality often comes naturally with the kind of commitment(perhaps near craziness) required to be in those kinds of guilds. That was somewhat manageable when 60's, and then 60's with good gear, and then 60's with decent gear and good AA, were still not in overwhelming supply.

But eventually ALL of us are going to be in that same place. At that point, exactly like today, all of us are NOT going to want to be in the world's best raiding guild. Just like all of us don't want to be now. People aren't going to fundamentally change just because designers say so -- not even just because people in uber guilds wanting to protect their privileges say so.

What then...retire? Smart azz punks will happily say, "Yes, of course -- losers!"

However, Sony needs money from more than just the "best of the best." It could use the cash from the other 95% of people who comprise their customer base, too.

When you have a game divided into two tracks: 1) uber and 2) absolutely nowhere, you have a problem.

Because of that, you MUST have ways of people having a chance at making some progress in the game even without being in an uber raiding guild and having every flag known to man. Sony realized this to a certain extent with the alternate methods to get flagged to zones like BOTH, COD, etc.

That understanding of what the broadest section of their customer base needs in order to feel like they're not just stopped dead in their tracks in the game must extend to things like spells, too. If eventually 85% of your customers have a level 60 or above(or even more than one) and 90% of them and more are missing important spells appropriate to their level, with little hope of ever seeing them, you have a recipe for disaster -- tremendous dissatisfaction in almost everyone, leading to mass customer migration out of the game to other competitors, or wherever it is that people go. With a good chance that they'll never come back to another one of your products again. (EQ2, anyone?)

Sony must keep alive alternatives to the "crash and burn" syndrome that will eventually hit the majority of their customer base if it finds out that once their character hits a certain level, without even having experience much of the game, he has hit an immovable wall, and come to the point where there IS no point in playing further.

Spell drops available outside of major uber raids and decent alternative ways of getting flagged that don't frustrate people too much are necessary and will become even more necessary as the crushing bulk of the customer base begins inhabiting the same "space" in the the storyline of their own individual characters. Can you envision a successful game where 700 people are LFG in POS and 800 in POV -- caves preferred? I can't.

These alternatives cannot be or remain based on rare spawn mobs or mobs that can easily be killed off by uber guilds to keep 95% of the game's players permanently or interminably fixed at certain stages of progress, or situations that the strongest builds can likewise manipulate to the same end -- The Sleeper comes to mind a bit here.

There can't be a flood into the new, highest level content, perhaps, nor should everyone have nearly automatic access to all the very best spells and equipment, but if the river runs dry, so does will the players' thirst to log in, and pay for the privilege of doing so. A trickle will perhaps be too little as well; we are talking about virtually the ENTIRE customer base, after all, wanting to improve their character avatars.

Where and how to draw the lines is something we can all argue about or have opinions on, but the fact remains that players must have achievable, enjoyable goals -- all of them. If all but a few are locked out of their spells -- or improvements in equipment -- and are locked out of experience in more than a handful of nice enough but fairly simplistic outdoor zones, the majority of players will not have achievable, enjoyable goals.

Becoming a full-time EQ player is NOT an option for most people, so is no solution.

Therefore, the game will continue to need things like spells dropping in ways that people in the strongest guilds find objectionable, just as people in the strongest guilds will at one point have to learn to live with others invading "their" zones and competing for "their" mobs and "their" loot.

Otherwise, they'll have to hope that EQ doesn't become yesterday's news. DAOC didn't do that to EQ and probably couldn't, because players still had plenty they could do, and plenty of people nowhere near doing all of it that mattered; giving players nothing to accomplish or look forward to can very easily do it.

A lifetime in POV/POS or, for some, the other lowest tiers without spells or flags should suffice to accomplish that handily. How far away is the majority of the customer base from that scenario?

And no, after being burned like that, the average customer WON'T be picking up a copy of EQ2.

Timesink key camps and ways of locking out content artificially -- and spells and items -- are not really good game design.

POP prepares people with extra levels for the big time, then denies most the big time and also denies them the ability to prosper from where they were at, as experience no longer exists there. Difficulty does not automatically equal fun, especially when things are artificially made difficult. It's no more fun to do something difficult than something easy, unless the difficulty gives you a sense of accomplishment by requiring something of you that is challenging and has a point.

As to everything being single group or raid-oriented, I think both ways are ridiculously extreme. But there has to be something reasonable in between.

I think Sony agrees by making some of these zones possible to get into with quests, and some items available without a raid. To the game's success or to the vast majority of its players. Quite the contrary. I think that's a stupid goal, and one dangerous for the game. If that's enough to make you quit, you were going to quit anyway.
