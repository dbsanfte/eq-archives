Macnair
Information is your friend. Do your best to get info on the mob before the actual raid. There are other things you'll need to know during the raid itself. Find someone on the raid that you can turn to and ask questions: a senior shaman, a guild officer, preferably not the raid leader who will be pretty busy. But: if there is no one else you can ask, speak up and ask the raid leader. Any good raid leader wants everyone on the team to know what to do. If you don't know what to do, you need to ask questions.

When it comes to buffing, figure out who you need to buff. If the MA is successfully going to hold aggro on the boss mob, and the healers are successfully going to keep the MA healed, the you need to buff the MA and a few additional tanks with all your hp buffs. And if you don't have the time / mana to buff everyone else, that's not a problem to worry about.

Suppose instead that your raid is marginal for dps and that the mob has a nasty AoE that could kill a lot of people pretty quick. Then you just need to suck it up and put all your best hp and resist buffs on everyone. And the rogue and monk that are complaining that prep is taking too long just need to wait patiently.

In terms of resists: you need to swap in the equipment you need for THIS FIGHT. If the mob has a nasty disease-based AoE spell, then you want to be wearing some nice DR stuff: but you don't have to put on stuff that is fire or cold resist gear.

If you are the only shaman and need to buff everyone, speak up in /raidsay to tell people what you are doing. Tell everyone to be in a tight group with their group leader. Then use the raid window and click on group leaders to target and cast your buffs. You'll go oom before you're done: you'll need some necro twitches.

If the raid is marginal for dps, and if you can buff Avatar, do so. You probably can't unless there are a couple other shamans on the raid. It could mean that the raid leader needs to buy you a couple stacks of emeralds. Buff the rogues first, then monks and rangers. Depending on your aa, you can maybe keep a buff rotation running on 7 to 9 high dps melees.

I almost never use a pet on raids. The problems with pet aggro, pet push, and pet control make the risk of causing problems too high. Yes, you can spam /pet back off to keep a tight leash on Gobaner. But most of the time I am too busy to want to do that.

As for root: it does have a place, for rudimentary crowd control on adds, in some rare situations. If you are skilled at ghetto crowd control you will frequently save your groups from a wipe: in a critical raid situation root may be the edge that can bring three chaotic adds back into control. But: that would be a very rare situation. Most of the time if it goes that bad, it's going to be a wipe and there won't be anything you can do about it.
Valsuvious
Well, first off congratz on having attended your first raid. Some of the posts here are pretty good telling you what to expect and what to do, but here are a few other pointers as well as my experiences.

First off, it doesn't matter who you are with, people are going to want their buffs even if you may think that they don't need them. Personally, I'll give almost everyone whatever they want, since I'm here to buff and debuff things, if they want said agility, then I'll give them that.

Second, if you are the only shaman around, then I suggest you doing a few things. First off, make sure that you are in the raid, and tell everyone that you are buffing people as soon as you can and hold off tells until then you ask for them. Since you are doing said pre-elementals raiding, I'm assuming that most of the people that you are raiding with do not have max stamina. So, what I would suggest is be sure to have at least the group version of Khura's Focusing and Talisman of Brute until you can get the better group versions of stamina and focus. Then hit every group with both of those spells using canni4 as often as you can (and even better canni5 if you have it). After that, I would personally go in the raid and hit the tanks only with your best focus and stamina (if you indeed do have single target better version of it, if not, then don't worry). Then in the raid after that, ask for any tells for any other buffs that someone may want.

Pay attention to what mobs you are doing and ask questions like has been said other posts. If you are doing mobs in PoDisease or CoDecay, then give everyone your best group poison/disease buff. Ask ahead if you need to. We have the best disease/poison resist buffs, chanters have the best magic one, and druids have the best cold/fire one (note: necros can also buff PR really good as well if you need the extra PR with DMF as well as rangers have a decently nice cold resist buff that stacks with druid buffs).

Now, as for actual raiding, I personally tend to keep up focus and sometimes stamina buffs. I don't normally do any real damaging of mobs during raids, until the named and that is depending on what my role in each fight is. I do a lot of debuffing (with malos if needed) and slowing during all trash clearing. Some guilds handle it different, but I've tended to use any shaman available to slow anything that they see (use a debuff channel for best results. We have a malos casting on this mob message, and then a slow on this mob message that goes in there, and helps everyone know who and what is debuffed). I don't usually let the raid know what is slowed unless it's the first time that we are doing a certain named and if it's someone where slow makes a huge difference on cleric rotation (solro and Quarm are good examples of that). I am not a fan of using someone to malos all mobs, and then someone else to slow them, mostly because I'm very quick at targeting something and I think most people I'm around are very slow in doing that, so if I'm slowing, I hate waiting on someone else to get their ass in gear and malos it.

As far as damaging mobs, trash mobs tend to die pretty quickly. Using cold nukes are normally too slow, sometimes I'll stick in a poison one, or use rains depending on location and also depending on if mobs are getting mezzed or just tank mezzed. On named, once I'm done debuffing, I'll usually go into dps role, unless it's a fight where we have massive AEs on us and I may need to cure, or if I have to heal some people for whatever reason. We can be very quick on agro so sometimes if there are adds and my tanks are slow at picking them up, I'll use my quick agro spells (meaning, balance of nihil and turgors) and agro them usually getting it off of the clerics.

I wouldn't honestly use a pet ever on a raid, unless it's against a named that you know he will last through an AE or six, and you know that his push won't be something that ends up messing up the raid somehow. We use spirit pets sometimes to help push a mob in the direction that we want him in. That's fine, but can't use it all the time. Our pet just isn't something that's going to be a life saver in a raid and it's typically something that does more harm than good.

As far as maxing out your dps in a named fight in a raid, I'd say the best thing to do is use one of your magic dots, and then use your rains after that. All depends on the mobs that you are killing of course and if there are adds, but typically sticking a magic dot and then rains seem to be my best dps parses. As long as the mob isn't that PR.

Dont' even bother giving out any form of Avatar until you Feral Avatar. It's not worth it. Do use that once you get it, but make sure that whoever you are raiding with knows that you are only going to cast it on the people that would benefit the most from it (meaning: rogues, rangers, monks, maybe beastlords). You can keep it on warr/pal/sk if you want, but basically their dps is low for the most part compared to the above classes that they wouldn't get the best out of it.

Oh, in raids, typically we use the shaman doing most of the debuffing (malos and slowing). The beastlords are also in that channel and will help slow if needed, but we rarely use the chanters to do that. Chanters and shaman both have the same cripple type spells, so it doesn't really matter who does it, so I tend to do it so that I know that it at least gets done. We use our mages to do malosinia.

It's been mentioned before about resist gear, but honestly if you have to swap in resist gear (and I'm talking about the stuff like the old player made JC stuff) then you might as well keep your real gear on and use it. With buffs on, you shouldn't be that bad off, and if you really need the resists, some bard should be playing AE resists anyways. I have some resist gear that I swap in and out sometimes, but most of that gear that isn't that much of a downgrade from what i'm wearing.

Last thing I'd say is that.. I think that in my few years of raiding, I've honestly used root maybe a handful of times in a raid, and all of those times were because I ran ahead and pulled a mob and just rooted it in front of raid, or i used it cause slacker tanks wouldn't get the mob off of me so I rooted it and backed off hoping someone didn't break it automatically. You will almost never use it, and you shouldn't honestly ever have to.

Hope this helps you or anyone else just a little bit.

As far as debuffs go the more the better. I've always believed every shaman on the raid should be on malos/slow. So what if the mob got slowed four times, at least you know it got slowed. If using multiple split tanks have each shaman assist one tank. If I'm the only shaman I usually slow the off tanks mob when doing trash first because more healers are likely to be watching the MA. If its a named I always slow it first.

Know your mobs, use the slow lookup table for the zone your are raiding. If the named you're about to do can't be slowed make sure the healers know about it because they'll have to adjust.

Once the mob is debuffed do what is needed for your raid. If you have enough healers dish out some damage, for named bane (or athema in your case) and rains can generally be a pretty safe bet as long as there are no lockdown mobs right next to the target. If you need to help heal HoTT is your best friend, either the raid leader or group leaders should have this ability. You might always want to keep an eye out on the rampage tank if there is one. On ae mobs you'll probably be curing as well, this is one of the things we're really damn good at so get used to it.

Don't ever get your pet out on raids unless you have pet hold and more importantly remember to use it. Wiping a raid because your pet chased down a mob that ae'd isn't going to help you one bit.

Root is only used in certain cituations, if your raid leader tells you that you're on CC then have it ready, otherwise don't root anything.

As far as your buff question, the only thing you should be worrying about at your level is Focus, STA, and tribunal when needed. If you have IoS offer to int caster/cleric types, especially when MR is important because it stacks with their shielding line.

OK, here's my responses based on my experience raiding with my current guild on Stromm.

1) How do I figure out who is going to be main shaman (i.e. who decides who is slowing)

We usually have a "head" shaman that determines who is responsible for what. If she is not there, then we usually just determine amongst ourselves who does what. Our guild uses class specific channels for each class so we can coordinate on what needs to be done.


2) If I am only shaman, ok, I do all that I can, with resist debuffs/slows on the highest priority after buffing.

This is a painful situation to be in when you have a raid of any size. My priority is usually get the key raid members what they need first, I.E. fully buffing the main/secondary tanks. Then start working on others as I can. Debuffing/Slowing is more important than getting str to that druid just because he thinks he's a melee.


3) What is the best way to raid buff if I am the only shaman, and I don't have mgb. How can I find out who are the group leaders, for instance. (Hmmm.. I suppose its in raid window.. )

With our guild, when it comes time to do group buffs at the start of the raid, the raid leader calls for them to be done. Otherwise, we have a buffs channel that group leaders use to call for all buffs. Usually, I use the raid window to target group leaders. This can be painful as a sole shaman and people are just going to have to be patient as you do your job. Getting with a necro to feed you mana while you buff an entire raid can be a life saver.


4) Apart from Stam, FoS and resist buffs, and assuming chanters are there for haste, are there other normal/obvious buffs? FA don't apply to my level, and Avatar is ridiculous.

Generally we keep Focus, stam, str and agi up on all, replenishment on the necros, and any other buffs are dependant on the encounter.


5) Should I always swap to resist gear for raiding?

As mentioned by others, resist gear usually isn't too important for trash mobs. Main event mobs, resist gear can mean the difference between success and a wipe. I carry mine with me on every raid in a WR bag.


6) What are typical roles (leaving buffing aside) if I am not slowing? Is healing/curing the obvious area?

Most often if I'm not slowing/debuffing I'm healing my group and patch healing the main tank/secondary tank/rampage tank.


7) Do I keep pet up at all times? Have seen one guide suggetion no pet by default... didn't understand why. I don't have pet control aa points though...

With our guild, if you don't have pet hold, you don't have a pet summoned. We've had easy encounters wipe because someone broke the rule and their pet aggro'd too early on an AE. Besides, dogdog is just another thing to keep track of.


8) Is there ever a time to use dots? If not, and if manna is ok, should I nuke?

Depends on what I'm doing, how much mana I have, and if I have the spell slot. I usually try to have a dot or nuke loaded just in case I get the chance to drop one. Some mobs have to have a controlled burn on them, i.e. Terris Thule in PoNB, and a dot is not appropriate.


9) Do I ever attemp root in raid context?

Sometimes if things have gone sideways I'll use root on trash mobs. Usually tho, our off tanks and chanters do a pretty good job of keeping things under control.


Like I say, that's from my experience with my current guild on Stromm. I've been raiding with them for about four months now. This is the first raiding guild I've been a member of, but I had done some raiding before joining them. It can be a bit of a learning curve getting into the groove at first and it will all depend a lot on the group you're raiding with. Try not to get too stressed and you can have a lot of fun.

1) How do I figure out who is going to be main shaman (i.e. who decides who is slowing)


There are basically 3 ways to work this.

1) Have each shaman pick a job, so 1 might be the malo/malos guy, another might be the slow guy, another might be the rebuff guy. etc. Issues with this method: shms die, ppl cry if they do the same job every raid. So rotate jobs, have shms ready to slide into the spot above if someone gets smoked.

2) Work assists. One shm takes the MA, and will handle all mobs off that assist. Another shm takes the SA and will handle all the mobs off that assist. Next Sham takes the overflow assists (ie - omg the bard/monk/necro pulled more than 2). Let each shm have 1 rebuff also. Makes most sense to give the MA shm the smallest mana rebuff (you dont want this guy casting 7th and going oom during a pull). Issues with this method: You need a core of shams. 3-4 work wonders here and you need shams that are paying attention. Again, rotate the jobs nightly/weekly to avoid burnout. And have shms ready to handle a death, the SA shm dies, the 3rd sham (cc) slides into the 2nd spot till SA shm is back on his feet and with mana.

3) Chaos. Shms just do what they want. Alot of family guilds or guilds with new players end up in this method. Good luck if you are in this mess. There are so many issues with this I can't even begin to list the problems with it.


IMHO: Working assists is the most efficient and the preferred method. BUT you need the right core of shms acting as a team to make this appear seemless.

IF you are training alot of new shms or attendance is constantly changing each night so you can never get a flow going then you might be stuck with method 1.

Heaven help you if method 3 is your only option.

You don't say whether you are doing pick-up raids or real ones. Pick-up ones are hard to say. Best I can say is join/create a channel and talk to each other and spread the work and info. Also find out who is a real shammy and who is boxed. Boxed shammies are there to be abused -- do so and make them buff till they think they will never see blue on their mana bar again. They can also be used for suicide debuffing on Boss mobs. :-)

Organized raiding with a good group of people who are used to each other is some of the most fun you will ever have. The tips above are great and give you a flavor of styles and personalities you can come across while raiding. You need to find a group that meets your style. Some additional tips that came to mind while reading are:

1. Get used to dying. You will a lot. Partly you need to to find your timing for debuffs and how early you can do it for your tanks, healers, mezzers. You also need a healer watching you. You can do a lot more if you are being healed than if you have to do it yourself.

2. When you get rezzed DON'T LOOT! Look around. If the raid is moving drag your corpse to the raid then loot.

3. Do no damage unless you are completely certain you should. Don't hit, nuke, or dot anything. Malxx and slow do not break mez so debuff mezzed mobs.

4. /join every raid channel you can handle. Info and what's coming is real important.

5. Keep an eye on the pullers. You want to target their mobs as early on incoming as possible. But be very careful not to cast when they are still trying to split the mobs to get just 1 or 2 to the raid. Be ready to duck out. It's real hard for a shammy to use a mount in a raid. You need to keep jockying for position to be near the MT and to be able to see the pullers.

6. Make people send you /tells for buffs. Use /rt to target the person who sent the last tell.

7. Targeting -- use /assist, F8, and have Q or END keys (or any key you want) set up to "cycle NPC". Always make sure your target is one you want to cast on before you cast. F8 and cycle NPC can target other mobs nearby. Also set up a key that does /hidecorpse all. It can get real hard to target with bodies around. [do /hidecorpse none if you need to see the bodies again.]

8. Buff dead peeps as soon as rezzed. They get focus and stam only. Use a hotkey /1 Buffing %T to let the shammies know you are buffing.

9. Team debuffing works very well for fast slowing and spreading the agro. Shammy_01 hotkeys /1 %T malXX in n secs. The n is the time it takes your spell to cast. Right click on it to see the casting time. Shammy_02 times slow to land just after malxx lands (hopefully).

10. Learn to say NO. Poeple are going to ask you for buffs just as the raid is about to pull a Boss mob, you just got rezzed, you're oom, any number of times you just can't do it. You have to tell them "Sorry not now".

11. Keep acumen or any other see invis up. You can't buff them if you can't see them and you need to keep an eye on the sneaky rogues and bards running to the raid with a few mobs following.

12. If you're made a group leader just invite anyone who is LFG. Just trying to get everyone in raid at that point. The raid leader should switch people around later to get proper groups. If you are a group leader ask your group who has the most leader AAs and make them leader.

Most of all have fun. )
