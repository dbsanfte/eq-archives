I think they're going to have to be very careful with this one. In both UO and DAoC, item decay is a huge hassle. Actually, because of item decay, I never even used my best stuff in UO; I was always waiting for that 'perfect opportunity' to bust my massive silver heavy crossbow of vanquishing out of my bank.

In DAoC, the problem is that use of items is extremely limited because the time between when you become able to get reasonable use out of an item and when it gets too low for you is extraordinarily short. I don't think Mythic understands how attached some people are to gear. Part of what has made EQ so tremendously popular has been pride of ownership. The chance at extremely rare items is a powerful motivating factor in playing EQ. I doubt anyone ever quit a game because their items never wore out, but many have because their items did.

That being said, the EQ economy could be in much better shape. I think that a nice system could be designed that had some aspects of the old (like no item decay) and still have some outlets to keep the economy from deflating so rapidly.

Overall, I think this combination of factors would work well:

1. Have vendors buy items at prices that reflect the level of the mob that dropped the item and its rarity. If an item is the rare drop from a level 55 mob, it should sell to vendors for an amount commensurate with those facts. This puts a lower limit on how much that item would ever sell to players for.

2. Put in a large number of money-sinks, to suck up all the extra platinum. There should be tons of purchaseable, expendable items in the game. Allow people to trade platinum for time, which is the very basis of economics. If you give people heal potions of varying qualities that cost 5 times as much as the average value of loot for their level, in exchange for enough healing to take them to full, and make it take 20 seconds to use, you let people trade their time for their in-game money. These potions can be both vendor sold and player sold, with the vendor type being moderately more expensive, as long as at some point currency leaves the game.

3. Allow magic items to be salvaged for their components for use in tradeskills. Instead of selling my 10 centi shortswords to vendors or lower level players, it migth be worthwhile for me to give them to a skilled dwarven smith, who would melt them down to produce 10 units of moderately high-quality enchanted metal. He could then craft those units of metal into, say, a two-handed axe. The quality of that axe would be based upon the skill of the smith and the quality of the metal he was working with. Reaching the highest levels as a smith would require the destruction of a significant number of magic items.

4. No item decay. =)

If item decay doesn't lead to item loss, it serves no purpose in terms of keeping the economy from deflating. It just takes a bit of time to have your guild smith sharpen all your weapons, and money for repair materials, which is a money sink, but not an item sink. With that said, I hate the idea of item decay for EQ. EQ's strongest point compared to any other game out there is the opportunies it presents to assemble massive raids to go after items of unparallelled quality. You can't add item decay to the current system; it takes way too long to get high-end items for decay to be feasible, and you can't make the items more common, because their rarity is what keeps people coming back night after night. In other words, you can't add item decay without changing the loot system, and the loot system is what makes Everquest work. You need ubers taking out the highest end mobs and posting their accomplishments on the boards to motivate the just-sub-ubers to take it up a notch, and you need the just-sub-ubers posting their stories to motivate middle-levels, and so on. Without those huge goals to strive for, the hack and slash will wear out very fast. Other games use PvP as the endgame, because there is only so much PvE content to experience. Everquest does not; it is a PvE game from the ground up, with an ever-advancing set of goals that require larger and larger numbers of people. This formula makes it work. No one in the game has all the best loot, and it seems we will continue playing until we do.

To make an analogy relating item decay to current Everquest using a rhetorical question: why do people hate dying so much and losing their level? I mean, after all, they did get the level; they accomplished the goal, it's just that later events took that away from them. Well, that is exactly what item decay is like. You assembled the people, you killed the impossible mob, you got the reward, and now, some time later, you no longer have that reward, and have to do it again. If you love dying and losing your level because it gives you the opportunity to regain it again, then you will probably love item decay as well.

"it's basically a 'time spent camping mob = plat' equation. "

Restated: (Time = Money). You are correct, that is the basis of all economics. There is no commodity in this world except time, and currency was invented as a tradeable medium for it. If you want to create a balanced economic system in a computer game, you start first with this very basic tenet. Efficiency is another thing altogether, and creates the niches for people to be good at things, but what you want in an economic simulation is for people to be able to turn their time into virtual money, and spend their virtual money to save time.

This is already the way the EQ system works, it's just poorly distributed. In a perfect system, the risk/reward would be evenly distributed, so that better items never drop from easier encounters. But, we know it doesn't work out that way, in part because it's an incredibly difficult job to keep populating the world with new items that all have utility to the people who would be likely to be challenged by that encounter. The solutions I posted above let you always gain a minimum amount of utility from anything you loot, and create entire new economies for the materials you can get from destroying items and for the items you can buy from selling them.

A few additional comments on this thread:

I must agree with dlmcpaul and Kiriani on this: item decay is necessary to stop "item inflation." Why? Because I know several people who tried to start playing EQ, but quit in disgust because these days it's "be twinked or be instantly obsolete."

And think of the side-benefit: reduced camping! The better item decay (and related money-sink ideas) reduce the inflation problem, the faster new items can drop.

Imho, the single-most important idea DAoC uses is that the rate of item decay depends very heavily on the relative level of the item to its user. Twinking is possible, but expensive in terms of decay and repair cost/time. And the highest-level characters using the highest-level items notice an extremely slow rate of decay.
As a result, new players to DAoC aren't finding all the other level 5's twinked like Liberace.

Aizle's idea is very innovative, though: have high-level item-repairing npc's located in places which only high-level characters can brave. That increases the time cost of repairing those items with whiched you twinked a newbie-- you'd have to take the item back and go get it repaired for the newbie, too.

Actually, one of the biggest problems with deflation is simply because the gear becomes so commonplace. The people who aren't part of a large guild that is ahead of the curve never get the gear to sell until it is nigh on obsolescence.

I also don't think twinking the living crap out of a newbie character should be possible. I think learning the nuances of the class should be done the hard way, regardless of the time/effort gear that drops from 50th level mobs could save you (I feel the same about powerlevelling - a few buffs here and there aren't bad, it's the 60 cleric and druid sitting near your character in a zone nobody your level should be in that's too much). You should have to work for your levels and equipment whether you are just starting out or playing your 5th new character.

As for the "working class", I work 8-5 everyday. I have a good 4-6 hours a night that are viable to play in, provided I have nothing else to do. I have never had a problem with spending one hour or so per week maintaining my gear (in DAoC or UO). We're not talking about a decay system that you would have to spend an hour or more each day on dealing with upkeep, but something to make things:1)more interesting, 2)more economically minded, 3)making practicing a trade more than just a means to blow platinum and accomplish quests, and 4)adding a whole new appreciation for what you've got.

I wouldn't neccessarily say that magic items are eternal because of being magic. More durable, yes, because usually magic items of only the highest possible (some would say impossible) quality are enchanted with their abilities. I would not say invincible though.

TO use an AD&D example: the Retributive Strike ability of the Staff of the Magi. You destroy the staff to release the power in one massive attack.

Magic items in various tales have not always been in the best of condition, and many have been destroyed through various means. ANYTHING with a physical form can be destroyed, although some may be harder than others.

For that reason, I say go ahead and apply the decay to magic items as well. Because of their magic (and inherent high quality) they will decay/take wear and tear slower than a typical item of that type, but they will decay non the less.

Example: Short Sword of Ykesha vs. Fine Steel Short Sword. THe FIne Steel Sword may require repair/upkeep once per day of average EQ use (meaning 6 hours of non-stop fighting realtime) whereas the Ykesha might only require the same upkeep after 4 days of this same level of use. Sure, it's magic, it's higher quality, but nonetheless it takes a beating.
