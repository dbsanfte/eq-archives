Fortunately, a roommate, who was a networking engineer by day, was able to get around these problems by using WinRoute (a routing/NAT utility) to force the EQ packets to be routed via the modem for both directions. I imagine the same thing could be better accomplished today with a linux system.

It wasn't that hard to get everything working (he spent about 30 minutes on it), and it was fairly stable and effective once it was working. We could both play EQ at the same time over the same dialup connection even while downloading massive binaries from usenet groups, although it took forever to zone. We did have to dedicate a 3rd PC to routing/firewall duties, but we had an old Pentium II box laying around anyway.

If you want the satellite connection for other non-EQ stuff as well, this may be the way to go, although it obviously won't be functionally any different than a plain old dial-up connection for purposes of game play.

Also, don't expect Hughes (the DirectPC company) to be at all helpful in getting this working; their customer service is even worse than SOE's. Or at least it was 3 years ago.

Integreated Services Digital Network (run a google search).

In a nutshell, its a digital phone line that relies on the same networking technology that businesses and ISPs use (e.g. T1s, etc.). While I'm simplifying greatly, ISDN is sort of like a fractional T1 (a very small faction). The basic connection is 64k both directions, but usually 2 lines are paired together for a total speed of 128k (about 5 times faster than dialup). I had a ISDN setup years ago in grad school, and it was fairly stable and speedy enough for my needs at the time.

ISDN service is sold by the phone company and is generally available anywhere that phone service is available. Like DSL, it requires high-quality digital-capable phone lines. However, unlike DSL, it does not require that specialized DSL routing equipment be installed at the local telephone switching station or office, which is why it is often available in places that DSL is not (it also has a longer range than some flavors or DSL). I have no idea what current rates would be; the connection that I had years ago was about $100 per month. You'll probably also have to buy a special ISDN modem, for about $200. Lastly, phone companies are usually pushing DSL for high-speed connections today, so you may have to talk to the salesperson that deals with business accounts to get any real info from them about it.

On the phone line issue: in most places, you can call the phone company and get them to come out and test the line for free. You ISP may be telling the truth on this one. Phone lines are generally required to provide a certain degree of bandwith capability by law (depends on the state probably, but this is the case in California). Typical phone lines provide a much better connection than they are legally required to and it is this extra bandwidth capability that 56k modems rely on. At any rate, once you get the line tested, you can then go back to the ISP and hassle them much more effectively.

Assuming the line is crap, one way around that is to order several new lines beyond those already supported in your house (homes are usually wired for 4 lines). This will force the phone company to come out and install new lines to support your new service (usually at no additional cost to you). Then you use these lines and cancel the old ones.

Finally, assuming you have no options beyond dial-up, there is yet another way to get more bandwidth out of that, called channel-bonding. In essence, multiple modem connections are multi-plexed together to form one virtual higher-speed connection. This does require specialized configuration and hardware at the ISP, but this feature is commonly supported by all the major-vendor modem bank hardware that ISPs use, so there is an overwhelming chance that your ISP can provide the feature, if they want to.

I ran a quad setup for about a year when I was living in a remote area around Santa Cruz, and it was reasonably effective. We had crap phone lines that could only typically support 26k, and ISDN wasn't available (let alone cable or DSL). We had to get a special 4-port serial card for the routing PC to support all four modems, but the software setup wasn't too bad; both Linux and BSD support channel-bonding out of the box. The ISP (got.net) had to make a one-line config change in our account, and that was it. Kinda kludgy, but it worked when nothing else did and was good for a fairly solid 100k connection.

Lastly, if it were me in your shoes, I would start by looking at the wireless angle.

Groovdog, is the wireless you talk about CDMA-1000? It's probably not. It's probably a 802.11a/b/g or one of the comercially licensed frequencies that are available to such businesses (these require equipment that is similar to, but operates on a differenct frequency than 802.11a/b/g but regardless the company has to deploy an antenna network in an area to support thier customers). Those technologies can support very low ping times, and when coupled with a very fast backbone could yield the results you describe. I do not bellieve that CDMA-1000 can support such low ping times regardless of the backbone it rides on. The main advantage of CDMA-1000 for a ISP like verizon is that it uses the same hardware that their cellular phone technologies work on, so they don't have to install new equipment in an area to support their clients. Anywhere they have a tower, they have a potential client (like me and maybe howler).

I too looked for a close neighbor that has broadband to set up a wireless network. Sadly there are 3 such individuals within a mile and half (well within range of 802.11a/b/g) from my home that could get DSL but the trees in my area prevent a line of sight link. CDMA-1000, because it works off the same technology as cellular (and the tower is high on a hill) blasts right through the trees and I'm in business.
