eximius
There are lots of good guides on how to maximize what you bring to the group. If you are solo, you will find out soon enough with only risk to yourself.

I would suggest considering the following in a group: (1) pre-buff; (2) most important buffs are usually haste and hit points; (3) control aggro and crowds--your tool for this is root, which you should be ready to cast if cleric or druid gets aggro; (4) use your heal to keep other healers alive; (5) slow is very powerful over the long haul, but dont let everything fall apart to get slow in on mob; and remember (6) you are versatile and can fill many roles for a group of your level. Multiple shaman can be in the same group and fill different roles at your level, and in fact any time before the end game. Practice different roles as long as group understands what you are doing.

FInally, have fun and be fun for the group. Its a game.

Congratulations, you're past the hard part. Level 24 is where the Shaman class starts to rock. Levels 14-23 were tough.

Read up on Cannibalize and Cannidance in the medicine cabinet. Sure, Cannidance has become less useful now that mind buffs and horses are so easy to get, but if you don't know about it you'll suffer the ridicule of your peers here on the board :-) and it does have some uses. Right now you NEED to do some soloing to get your defense up. Getting defense maxed at 200 is one of your most important tasks in the 20's and 30's. However, once you've maxed defense for a particular level, go ahead and group, it's faster XP. Root rot is a viable option for you at your level, but it doesn't raise defense, so get used to mixing it up in there personally.
Wilcas
When grouping, keep in mind that while you can do alot, you can't do it all. This was my biggest problem at lower levels. You need to negotiate with your group what you can do for the group, given limited mana.

If slowing is your main job for the group, you will have less mana available for buffs - and everyone wants buffs. But if you slow and buff, there will be little mana to heal.

For most of your career your main job will be to buff the team and slow the mobs. Healing is always up but as the backup/emergency healer. Remember that both Clerics and Druids can heal better than you, and Paladins can heal as well as you can. But keep the regen line up on the team unless a druid already is handling this.

When you are main healer, you may need to control the pull rate so you have enough mana to cope if something bad happens. One trick mentioned to me was holding off healing until you teammates get to 50% when mana is tight. That tends to keep them from being overactive in the pulling department.

Get stoicism as soon as you can - it redefines the game for shamans. A HoT to recover from Canniing is liberateing.

Grafsha McDaughts
Besides reading the many sections on these boards that provide great bits of information on playing a shaman (where, what, solo, grouped, why this item or that one) go out and try stuff.

Push your abilities while you are still at low levels to learn what your strengths as well as limitations are. Key is not to be afraid of "not doing something right." You will make mistakes and you will find little tricks that work for you that would not have happened if you didn't try different things.

My leveling days were spent primarily soloing due to my schedule at the time (and in some cases - preference.) I did have a personally imposed rule, which was to group for ~1 level every new spell cycle. Group dynamics alter as you level up. Spending time in a few groups learning how others change along with yourself only helps you find your balance and usefullness as a group player.

Bottomline, as always, is enjoy playing. Hopefully that fun can be found with your shaman. If not, there are other classes to try out until you find the one that works best for your playstyle.
