I think one of the problems in learning the details in experience is that you need so much of it to level, but each kill gives you typically less than 1/100th of what you need. Now this is a good thing (TM) overall, because it would be lame if you just leveled every 100 kills or so. However, it makes doing any sort of study of exp difficult, because you can't accurately gauge changes in exp gains. So what's the solution? ShowEQ, or any similiar packet-watching program. Now I don't use ShowEQ, since I don't have an extra Linux computer lying around, but from what I understand about it if you use it you can see exactly how many exp points you get after you kill something. If anyone out there who has this program could do some tests PLing lowbies, it'd probably be a lot easier to determine where the cap is and how it works. Until then, or until someone comes up with an alternative method to study experience, everyone's data will be more or less anecdotal.

No, it's just that those particular mobs give ASSLOADS of exp per kill! Exp is not measured by how far it makes your bar move. It's measured by the amount assigned to a particular mob, which varies according to numerous conditions, subject to bonuses or penalties.
Let's say a lvl 47 chardok Reanimated Partisan gives 10000 exp points as its base. A level 46 iksar shaman manages to solo this beast, and exp is awarded. He is awarded a 10% bonus for hunting in a dangerous zone. He is fined a 20% penalty for being a stinky leezard. He is awarded a large bonus of 50%(unsure of this number) for killing something higher level than he is.
So:
10000
+5000
-2000
+1000
--------
14000 exp points, which in lvl 44 he needs 1000000 exp points to "ding" and therefore he recieves 1.4% exp in that little blue bar.

Now that same lizard shaman goes to PoN and fights a bird who is level 47 and gives 20000 exp points as his base. Do the same math and you get 28000 exp points awarded, or 28000/1000000 x 100% = 2.8% exp to his little blue bar.
