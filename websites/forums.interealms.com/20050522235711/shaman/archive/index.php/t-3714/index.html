In the US, the difference between a college and a univeristy is this.

Univeristies contain several colleges into one big school. For example, I went to Kansas State University which had the college of arts and sciences, college of engineering, college of agriculture, etc. Within each of those colleges, are different departments that then have different majors.

My sister went to Mcpherson college, which is a small private school that has several degree programs, but they are all under one college, just different departments.

As far as what you should pick, that depends on what you would like to do in life. Right now, with the economy the way it is, getting a job right out of high school probably isn't your best bet.

I'm not sure how the canadian armed forces are either so I really can't comment on that.

My suggestion is to look at the different universities or colleges close to where you live, see what major you might be interested in and go from there. Visit them next year, see what campus life is like. I wouldn't trade my time at KSU for anything.

Actually, in the US, the difference between college and university is becoming less clear. Traditionally, a college is a four year institution that results in a bachelor degree. A university offers advanced degrees--masters and/or doctorates in some field, I believe. However, I seem to recall reading about a legal case where a college wanted to call itself a university and sued some government agency. Go figure.

At any rate, yes, go to college. The military may be a good choice for training, or financial aid, or just a chance to find out what you want to do, but get a degree before, during, or after. Even in some militaries, it's difficult to advance past certain ranks without a degree.

You can get a job with no degree, but unless you are extremely lucky, you will eventually hit a wall past which you can't really advance without a degree. This is becoming more and more true (there are occasional exceptions) with the huge number of students that are graduating every year. School doesn't necessarily make you more mature and certainly not any more experienced for a job, but it does help with communication skills, analytical thinking, and some basic skills that employers look for. 2 or 3 degrees make even more of an impression if you have the patience.
