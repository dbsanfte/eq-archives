Part 1 - Versatility vs Power

One of the shaman's biggest strengths is supposed to be his versatility. We have a great number of abilities we can use. Most other classes have far fewer options. The questions is, "How versatile does one have to be to make up for lost power?" or conversely, "How powerful do my extra options have to be for my versatility to be useful?".

There are a few different types of abilities - some abiltities you either have or have not. Spells like Levitation or Ressurection or abilities like Invisibility fall into this category.
Other abilities are innate, like tanking ability or a necromancer's mana regeneration.
And yet other abilities are mutually exclusive, meaning using one of them makes using other such abilities impossible. Healing, doing damage and shaman mana regenration fall into this category.

The first category is pretty easy to evaluate - any extra ability will be just as useful to a class as the first ability.

The second category is similar, though additionally these abilities have a degree to their usefulness, like warriors tanking better then knights. However extra abilities are still as useful as the first ability and a more powerful ability is more useful.

The third category is radically different. Adding new such abilities to a class will provide more options but every such ability after the first is less useful then the previous. In game terms, being able to heal is very good. Being able to heal and nuke is a decent bit better. Being able to heal and nuke and mez is a tiny amount better. Being able to heal and nuke and mez and stun is still better but by such a small amount as to not matter.

Thus a class that can nuke for x amount and heal for y amount is (all other abilities being equal) strictly worse then then a class that can nuke for 2x or a class that can heal for 2y. Basically, for versitility to be useful any exclusive abilities have to be quite close to the benchmark (usually the best ability).
Let me show a few (simplified) examples:
- A cleric's CH heals for 7500, a druid's for 4680 and a shaman's for 1950. It is generally agreed that a druid CH is acceptable as a substitution for a cleric CH in most situations. Thus the druid's versatility is useful in this case. A shaman CH however is nowhere close to the cleric CH and thus, while not useless per se, is not worth losing other abilities for.
- A shaman's best strength buff is Ferine Avatar at 140 strength. A ranger's strength buff adds 35 strength. Even though this is not an exclusive ability, the ranger buff is so far behind the shaman buff that it's loss would hardly be noticed.

The above examples are of course oversimplified in the extreme. They do however illustrate one of my main points:
An exclusive ability should be within 30% of the top ability in question to be a viable alternative. A nonexclusive ability should be within 50% of the top ability in question to be a viable alternative. Else the ability will be better left to others.

In game terms:
Druids can in many situations act as a main healer instead of a cleric (main healer being defined as having a large CH-type spell within 30% of a cleric's).

Shaman are not able to do this in a large part of the content (because their CH-type spell is not in this range).

Paladin buffs are an acceptable substitute for cleric buffs as the stats are within 50% of cleric buffs.

That being clarified let me explain a few key problems of the shaman class.

A shaman is to a certain extent a jack of all trades. Shaman have a large variety of spells for a lot of situations. Many of these spells used to be pretty close to the best spells in their category. Expansion by expansion many of these spells have been scaled back compared to other classes' spells until today shaman spells are often the worst in their category.
All the versatility in the world is useless when all the options are third class or worth in their category.

- Shaman HP buffs are the weakest of any class (tied with rangers).
- Shaman healing is tied for third place with paladins (only beastlords and rangers heal worse) and is solidly past the 30% difference from cleric healing to be considered a viable alternative.
- Shaman nuking is the weakest of all casters.
- Shaman DoTs are solidly behind necromancer DoTs and (due to resist modifiers) behind druid DoTs.

That leaves a shaman with stat buffing, slowing and haste as tier 1 / 2 abilties.
- Stat buffs are largely useless due to caps.
- A very large part of this (and the last 2) expansion mitigates slow. Even a minor slow mitigation easily halves the effectiveness of slow.
- Haste is still pretty useful.

This is part one of the shaman problem. There is two things shaman do really well - buff stats and slow, both of which is getting more useless every expansion.
As an example for versatility done right consider the bard. A bard is among the best at
- hasting (first)
- mana regeneration (first)
- crowd control (close second)
- pulling (first)
- resist buffs (first)
- damage shields (first)
- slows (4th but within 30% of the top slower)
This is why bards are a great class. They are actually good at what they can do.

Part 2 - Active mana regeneration

Just about anything a shaman can do seems to have been designed with cannibalize in mind. Whenever a shaman complains about any of his abilities one of the arguments invariably is "but you can cannibalize" (the other being "but you have slow").

Cannibalize does have a major problem though. As noted above cannibalize os one of our mutually exclusive abilities. While we are cannibalizing we are not doing anything else. This used to be no problem at all back in Kunark - back then med breaks were common, every caster conserved their mana and cast spells sparingly, giving shaman the time to cannibalize without falling behind.

Apparently this was deemed to powerful and every shaman spell since Kunark is, compared to the upgrades other casters got, more expensive as well as slower casting and has a lesser effect.

Fast forward to today. Whereas a healer used to be expected to cast a heal or two every minute they are now required to spend half of their time casting. Similar things are expected of the damage classes.
Now, given slower casting times and lesser effects a shaman will easily spend not 50% but 80% of his time casting these spells and still pay more mana for them. He now has little time left to cannibalize the additional mana back.

This not only puts the shaman on par or below over long durations, it also means that shaman burst healing or damage capabilities are much less then other classes' abilities - and burst healing and burst DPS are becoming more and more critical in the last expansions.

This is part two of the shaman problem. We simply do not have enough time to use our active mana regeneration if we want to match or even come close to other classes' capabilities.

At first glance shaman mana regeneration seems impressive.
Cannibalize IV will bring back 82 mana every 5.5 seconds.
In addition the cannibalisation skill will generate 1066 mana every 3 minutes.
This adds up to a total 3712 mana every 3 minutes, or 124 mana per tick

Druids on the other hand only regain 12 mana per tick (Blessing of the 9 + Mask of the Forest).

Now let me show you a scenario - we will compare sustained shaman nuking to a druid's. I chose nukes for my comparison because the druid DoT spells have a resist modifier, which is hard to account for.

Both the shaman and the druid will have a mana preservation item (10% average), an improved damage item (10% average) , a spell haste item (10% fixed), flowing thought 15, and mental clarity 3. Both will use a horse. In addition the shaman has the cannibalisation AA.
Both will be buffed with Blessing of the 9, Voice of Quellious and Spiritual Dominion. In addition the druid will be buffed with Mask of the Forest.
I will assume assume neither shaman nor druid are specialized in evocation (thus a 3% mana saving on all spells).

To account for the nature of cannibalizing I will first compute the shaman mana regeneration as if he was doing nothing but generate mana and then add to the cost of each spell he casts the mana he could have generated in that time.

Shaman mana regeneration:
One cast of breath of Trushar costs 285 mana and heals 4*360 =1440 damage.
Focused mana cost is 214.
That is enough damage healed to offset 9.7 casts of cannibalize IV.
One of these cycles generates (9.7*82)-214=583 mana and takes (9.7*4.75)+7.95=54.2 seconds.
Shaman mana regeneration from cannibalize IV is thus 10.8 mana per second or 65 mana per tick.

The cannibalisation AA will bring back 1066 mana at the cost of 1924 damage. Healing this damage with Breath of Trushar takes 1.34 casts, which costs 287 mana. The total mana generated by one cycle of cannibalisation is 779 every 3 minutes, or 26 mana per tick.

The shaman's mana regeneration over 3 minutes is thus 779 (from cannibalisation) + 1909 (from 177.5/54.2=3.27 cycles of cannibalize IV) = 2688 mana every 3 minutes, or 90 mana per tick (14.93 mana per second).

Now, this was the mana from cannibalizing - added to this is the mana from the above sources (same for druid and shaman).

28 (meditation mana at lvl 65) + 8 (Blessing of the 9) + 18 (Voice of Quellious) + 9 (Spiritual Dominion) + 15 (Flowing thought) + 3 (Mental Clarity) + 4 (Mask of the Forest; druid only).

This brings total mana regeneration per tick to
171 (shaman)
85 (druid)

Now let us compare the nuke spells available to both classes.

The shaman will be using Velium Strike.
Velium Strike does 925 damage every 10.05 seconds. Accounting for focuses and specialisation brings this to 925*1.1=1018 damage every (7.8*0.9)+2.25=9.3 seconds for 300*0.87=261 mana. We do also lose 9.3 seconds worth of cannibalize mana, which amounts to a total mana cost of 400.
A shaman can thus cast Velium Strike every 400/171=2.3 ticks, or every 14 seconds, which amounts to 1018/14=72.9 DPS.
The shaman's burst DPS is 1018/9.3=109 DPS.

The druid will be casting Sylvan Fire.
Sylvan Fire does 1705 damage for 430 mana every 9 seconds. The numbers adjusted for focuses are 1705*1.1=1876 damage for 430*0.87=374 mana every (6.75*0.9)+2.25=8.3 seconds.
A druid can thus cast Sylvan Fire every 374/85=4.4 ticks, or every 26 seconds, which amounts to 1876/26=72.2 DPS.
The druid's burst DPS is 1876/8.3=226 DPS.

Now, this is over an infinite timespan. If we instead allow for a med break every 20 minutes both the druid and the shaman can also empty their mana pool.
A mana pool of 6000 mana emptied over 20 minutes amount to another 30 mana per tick.

With this extra mana the shaman damage rises to 1018 every 400/191=2.1 ticks for a DPS of 1018/(2.1*6)=81.3 DPS.
The druid damage rises to 1876 every 374/115=3.3 ticks for a DPS of 1876/(3.3*6)=96.1 DPS.

So even chainnuking for 20 minutes straight the shaman does not match druid nuking damage. Chainnuking for literally an eternity the shaman pulls ahead of the druid by less then a percent.

Note that this shaman has no lag at all, does not stop casting for even a quarter of a second, never overheals his cannibalize damage and never fizzles. He also magically finds something to cast in the 0.75 seconds between casting cannibalize IV and the cannibalize IV gem refreshing and he never moves.
The druid on the other hand is only casting 30% of the time and thus has ample time to move or make up for lag or fizzles.

Neither character is using resists debuffs - this would skew the results further in favor of the druid due to their cheap and fast fire debuff line.

Part 3 - Mana regeneration for everyone

Mana regeneration is supposedly the area where shaman excel and we do have the biggest potential mana regeneration. The ratio of shaman to non-shaman mana regeneration has shifted a lot though.
Every casting class now has some mana regeneration buff now, many of them castable on others and many of them stacking. Flowing thought gear is easy to get nowadays. Horses add a large amount of mana regeneration.

Now, all of these are usable by shaman too. But every time a fixed amount of mana regeneration is added to everyone the shaman suffers. We were balanced to regenerate not a fixed amount more mana then other classes but to regen a multiple of it, and this multiple is shrinking with every expansion since Kunark.

At the same time this ontributes to problem two - the more mana everyone regenerates the more a shaman is expected to do, further reducing the time to cannibalize and thus reducing the advantage in mana regeneration even further.

This is part three of the shaman problem. Our mana regeneration, while impressive on paper, is not nearly enough to sustain our ineffective, inefficient, slow spells to a degree where we can pull our weight in groups. This is getting worse every time more flowing thought gear enters the world, every time bigger mana regeneration buffs are created and every time another horse is bought.

Part 4 - Casting times & Impact

The obvious general spell design for a class with active mana regeneration would have been expensive but fast casting spells so as to leave time to make up for the mana. Shaman spells however are slow, inefficient and have a lesser effect then comparable spells of other classes.

Shaman are thus simply unable to match the short term damage or healing output of other classes. These days "short term" might as well be defined as "anything under 5 minutes". That is the point where other classes might start to go out of mana. It does not mean a shaman starts to overtake a cleric's healing or a necromancer's damage. This is where a shaman starts to catch up. With the inefficient and low impact spells shaman get catching up to either over any length of time is simply impossible.

This is part four of the shaman problem. Slow casting times and low impact spells prevent us from matching anyone at all in the short term and from ever catching up to the top classes in the long term.
At best we can hope to catch the secondary healers or secondary damage dealers in the long term - however these long term fights do not exist anymore.

Part 5 - Raid stacking

Some spells, noticeably buffs, change their value on raids - for example noone is going to use a ranger's natureskin as it will be overwritten by the better druid spell. These are the spells a shaman might use on raids:
- Focus
- stat buffs
- Slow
- Malos
- Malosinia
- Nukes (Velium Strike)
- DoTs (Bane / Blood of Saryrn)
- Heals (Tnarg's / Quiescence)
- Ferine Avatar

Focus + the few needed stat buffs can easily be done by a single shaman. Another major part of this list is debuffing (Malos / Malosinia / slow ). Debuffing does not stack however - you can only slow a given MOB once after all. However due to resists and the spawned adds on today's raids there is usually work for two, sometimes even three shaman.

DoT usage on raids is tricky - unless there a multiple MOBs to spread the aggro on a shaman will easily pull aggro of even the best equipped tanks when stacking DoTs. Multiple MOBs are generally killed or mezzed though - meaning DoTs are at best used to augment nuke damage.

So the stacking roles for a shaman on a raid are healing, nuking and casting Ferine Avatar. Shaman nuke damage is the worst of any caster and shaman healing is the weakest of the priests (and arguably worse then a paladin's). Shaman mana regeneration also does not come into play on raids - I can not remember the last time that people actually ran out of mana ona given encounter.

This is part five of the shaman problem. The stacking abilities of a shaman are the weakest parts of the class. Every shaman after the second will feel like a second rate druid (and be about as useful).

Part 6 - Fast fights & constant movement

Planes of Power introduced MOBs with huge damage output and comparatively very low HPs. Lost Dungeons introduced the concept of constant movement.

Neither of these concepts are bad per se. Both do however reduce the effectiveness of the shaman.

Short fights mean that the contribution of slow is lessened. If a MOB spends 90% of the fight slowed the overall slow percentage will be very close to the maximum value of 75%. If however the MOB spends a third of the fight unslowed this overall slow percentage drops to 50%. As the corresponding MOB damage output is 25% and 50% this means that the effectiveness of slow is halved.

Constant movement simply means less time for a shaman to cannibalize. Everyone looses mana by moving, but buffs still provide their mana regeneration while the extra mana from cannibalize that the shaman was balanced around is lessened.

This is part six of the shaman problem. The short brutal fights and constant movement of GoD (and to a lesser extent the previous expansions), while not a bad thing by itself, indirectly nerf key shaman abilities.

Part 7 - Slow mitigation

Slow is also adversely affected by another thing - slow mitigation. Slow mitigation lessens the percentage by which a MOB is slowed. Due to the way slow works the scaling is a little curious however.
Reducing a slow by a third from 75% to 50% halves its effectiveness.
Reducing a slow by two thirds from 75% to 25% quarters its effectiveness.
Reducing a slow by half from 50% to 25% halves its effectiveness.

Generally speaking, those last couple percent of slow (from 50 to 75 %) are the most effective and are the first to go when slow mitigation is applied.

Even a small mitigation in slow effectiveness of a quarter of the original value gets very close to halving the effectiveness of shaman slow. Just reducing the slow percentage of a shaman by a tenth reduces the slowing power by 30%.

Bards, beastlords and enchanters, though similarly affected, experience a lesser reduction in the effectiveness of their slows.

This is part seven of the shaman problem. Slow mitigation, even the least variations of it, have a huge impact on the effectiveness of slow. As slow is the only contribution to a group that shaman are clearly best at this greatly reduces the overall shaman contribution.

Part 8 - Shaman healing & slow

Shaman healing seems to have been designed with slow in mind. That is, a shaman would be able to keep his group alive after he slowed the MOB.

Aside from the obvious problems - MOBs generally do not arrive slowed, adds have to be slowed separately, spell and riposte damage is not lessened by slow - this might well have been true during the Luclin era.

Nowadays however, even on non-mitigating MOBs, the short fight durations reduce the effectiveness of slow a lot. On the other hand the comparatively greater increases to cleric and druid mana regeneration compared the tiny increases shaman got mean that we have more healing to do then we used to so we can match a cleric's or druid's overall healing.
However we have to do this with spells that cast slower, heal less and take more mana.

In GoD another problem has become apparent. It is very possible that a shaman can just barely keep up with an unslowed MOB by chaincasting heal. That means that in the case of a resisted slow the tank will need chain healing, leaving no time for a new try at slowing.

Druid or cleric healing does not have this problem, as their heals were not designed with a resistable spell in mind.

This situation of course does not get better when you take into account slow mitigation or flat out slow immune MOBs.

This is part eight of the shaman problem. Our healing was designed with slow and our superior mana regeneration in mind. Neither of these applies to the degree it used to and this is if we even get a chance to use any of it. Even if we can slow a MOB decently we barely break even with other healers and are much much worse when emergency healing is needed (such as in the case of an add).
