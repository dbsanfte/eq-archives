One of the other things that I've noticed about teammates and higher rated players from other teams is that they tend to make things more complicated than they have to be. It's almost as if they feel that being a higher rated player means you have to take a bunch of fancy shots. A little bit of draw here, a little bit of follow there is all you really need, yet they continually try to force break-outs of balls that are either not problem balls or could be broken out much easier on the next shot. Think about your leaves, but don't make the leave the whole point of the shot. Unless it's an intentional defensive shot, the best leave in the world doesn't mean squat if you don't sink the ball. If you're as good as you say you are, then the leaves become less important. The follow up shot doesn't have to be a perfect set. It just has to be reasonable. You can miss your leave by two feet and still have a follow up shot. The best advice I can give is just to relax and have fun with it. If you miss a shot or a leave, work with what you have. Don't look back and don't get upset about it. To quote the great Yogi Berra, "Half of this game is 90% mental."

Are you sure you're a 5 and not on my team? j/k

I have a player on my team with the same problem and, well, the same problem. I had to race to get him a glass of coke during a match. He downed it practically in one gulp, settled down, and was fine for the rest of the match, which he won.

There really are only three things that you can do to get over that problem. First, check your local league newsletter or area bar postings for tournaments. Go to as many tournaments as you can. Second, spend some time touring the local pubs. Play in as many different bars as you can, against as many different people as you can. Third, play as many matches as you can during league play. When you're not playing in a match, go play on a spare table if the establishment has more than one. Ideally you'll want to play against idle members of the other team rather than against your teammates.

The best way to overcome the jitters is to go into the match having already won it in your mind. You're a good shooter and you know it. You have nothing to prove. It's not you against your opponant, it's you against the table and how the balls end up after the break. Your opponant is irrelevant. If he beats you because he's a better player then that's going to happen sometimes. It doesn't matter how good he is, all you need is for him to miss one shot and the table is all yours.

Slumps: I look forward to slumps now. I used to hate them, especially when they lasted for months and months. Now I love them because every single time I've gone into a slump, I've come out of it better than I ever was. When you're shooting well, you're coasting. When you're in a slump, you're learning.

Heh. Sounds like what I do. Once I step up to the table for the lag at the beginning of a match, I do nothing but focus on the pool table. There literally is nothing and nobody else in the room as far as I'm concerned. When I'm shooting, I'm concentrating on the shot and where the balls are on the table. When I'm not shooting, I'm concentrating on the table and planning what I'm going to do if my opponant misses his next shot and I'm anticipating what I would do if I were my opponant. Everything else is completely blocked out. You've heard I'm sure of pro sports players who have a career defining game? They get into what they call "the zone" and no one can touch them. The trick is learning how to put yourself in the zone at will rather than riding it on those rare occasions when it happens spontaneously.

Never been to Tucson, and Arizona is one of the ten states I haven't even been to yet, but just about everywhere I've been, most bars with coin-op tables work on a challenge system. You keep playing until you lose. Then you put your set of quarters up at the end of the line and wait your turn. On league nights in bars with more than one table, usually if I just start shooting by myself, someone will walk up and put quarters up to play a game. From there, there's usually four or five people waiting in line to play all the time. Now, if you play in a pool hall where the tables are pay by the hour, it's a different story.
