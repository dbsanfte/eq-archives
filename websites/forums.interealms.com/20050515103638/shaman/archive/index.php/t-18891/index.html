Originally, Shaman were supposed to be the battle priests in EverQuest. But when Verant was running things, in my opinion, they did a pretty bad job of creating roles for classes and filling them in. They designed classes around what would make the most sense from a role playing stand point. Actual game balance was horrible in practice. Not too mention that they made a lot of skills and abilities completely worthless either due to bugs (Alchemy skill ups *are* possible, we swear!) or lack of implementation (Rogues get to disarm traps, but we didn't really put any in game). There are still some things that don't work well, but SOE (which obviously is not the same people that were at Verant) has done a fairly good job at smoothing things out as best they can, when you take into account the pile of crap balancing that they had to work with. It took the development team almost 3 years before they stopped treating hybrid classes like they were gimped up version of their parent class and started giving them more unique spells and abilities.

I signed on to play a Shaman as a "battle priest". I wanted to melee a little and have some decent buff spells. But I really don't think that's what Shaman are anymore. I'm much more of a support class now than I was pre-Kunark/Velious. When I'm in an exp group, my primary responsibility is to debuff mobs and buff my party members. But aside from that, I also can contribute some healing and a little DPS with nukes/rains and my pet. I'm not the best healer or DPS class, but it's nothing to scoff at. I'm happier with this role than I was with my role as a battle priest, and I never really thought I would be.

I see a lot of hostility towards Beastlords here, and I can't deny that they are the battle priests that we used to be. I've got a Beastlord alt I play from time to time. But what I've found is that I didn't want to be a battle priest as much as I thought I did. If you're hung up on being able to melee, you really should try a Beastlord, because that is the class that SOE has created to fill the battle priest role. And I don't think there is much chance of them transforming Shaman back into this role at this point. I personally don't want them to.

My 2cp,

Grafsha McDaughts
Shaman lack in one significant area of damage dealing: adaptability.

Resist rates on poision/disease based damage spells exist on too many mobs to make our primary lines of damage spells effective. Durations on our most effective dots take too long to cycle thru, thus minimizing effectiveness in short duration battles. In comparison wizards/mages/and druids do not deal with these problems (well, mages .. a little, but pets make up for his.)

Wizard can select from 4 groups of spells to ensure damage against targets: fire, ice, magic, lure. They also get inherent level based and resist based modifiers to easier land spells.

Druid dots are subtle landing (no initial damage) so don't break the root they cast that, when it (the root) landed, did more dmg to the mob than our first strike from dots do. Druid dots are rarely resisted. Did I say rarely? The is giving them the benefit of the doubt that they ever get resisted.

Fast heals, druid has it over shaman ten times over. What should be our class defining HoT spells are not viable in combat until lvl 65 because of the slow/loss of attack hit they 'provide' tanks. No other priest class gets a heal spell that actually reduces the effectiveness of the target it is healing.

Root. Compared to snare and druid root our root line is useless due to the resist/breaking rate. Note I said 'compared to.' Druid snare, once landed it's a done deal. Mobs do not break snare before it runs it course. Druid roots are solid and rarely break. Ours on the other hand are like our invis. The percentage swings more towards breaking than holding for full duration.

Enchanters' tash is a cast and move on spell from the very first level acquired. It lands and lasts forever ... practically. We have to wait 60 levels to get a maloxx spell that is "unresistable." By that time we have to cast that spell every time to get an increased chance to land our class defining spell, turgur's. If it doesn't land we are generally getting beat upon waiting on it to refresh or trying to cast a root that may or may not stick so we can back off and try to slow again. In the same scenario (getting beat upon) chanters can stun via numerous fast casting spells or mez with numerous fast casting spells and then recast their slow in relative safety. Difference is we can take hits better; reality is chanters can react faster and not take the hits because of a larger arsenal and faster recast times.

So, to make my point: I don't want to be druid, but I want my dots to land with the same reliability and my roots to be as dependable on the majority of mobs. I don't want to be a wizard but I want some flexibility in my spell lines to allow me to effectively damage the majority of mobs - any place, any time. I don't want to be an enchanter but I want the same ability to do my job fast and efficiently instead of waiting for a 'recast' time. I don't want to be a cleric but I do want to be efficient and capable as a healer because I am a priest class.

At face value and in many, many situations shaman are a significant powerhouse. Other classes can make the same claim. The downside is this is countered by game walls that minimize, marginalize and even negate everything we bring to the fight. No other class is faced with this type of limitation to the degree shaman are. These limitations have been progressively introduced from expansion to expansion.

The end result is, no matter how you phrase it, nerfage. Let our tools actually work with the same reliability the class specific tools work for other classes.
