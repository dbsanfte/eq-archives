I'm not Mormon. I'm pretty sure you all know that by now. However, when I lived in Boulder, a pair of missionaries tried very hard to convert me and my first husband. One thing I noticed was that the church was more of a cohesive family than any other church I've known. They just expected to take care of each other. If someone was sick it wasn't a
question of whether they needed someone to sit at the hospital with them, or they needed someone to cook meals for their family, the only question was which day was it your turn. They simply took for granted that the church was going to take care of its members.

In all other churches I've seen, there have been only a few people who organized all the assistance, and if you weren't in their radar, or you made them angry somehow, you didn't get mentioned for help. Most churches, the most they really did for the average member in a crisis was to pray for them. The Mormons prayed, and then they got out and did the mundane things that made the crisis a little better - and it didn't seem to matter who you were or how popular your family was. If you were a church member, you were helped.

I couldn't agree with the theology, but the community itself was almost enough to convert me in spite of that. Regardless of popular opinion about LDS church policies, I will always respect them for their caring attitude and loyalty to each other.
