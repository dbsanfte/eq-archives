As you can see, ask 50 computer users how to fix a computer and you’re likely to get 50 different answers. *grins* For what its worth I’ll add my opinion into the mix as well. Ideally you should buy a new system. There have been so many fundamental changes in everything from RAM to the bus system on your motherboard that upgrading your system piece by piece really isn’t worth it in the long run. It’s just the nature of computers to become obsolete within six months of purchase. And the prices for a new system have dropped a lot from what they used to cost. So they are much more affordable.

That having been said, not everyone can afford to upgrade their computer even if prices are relatively “reasonable.” If you can’t afford to rush right out and buy a new system, there are some things you can do to clean up your current system that might help your performance. First, empty out your Temporary Internet files. This not only clears out a bit of space but you could have some nasty files hiding there that can wreak havoc on your system. I’ve worked on systems that couldn’t connect to the internet until the Temporary Internet files were deleted. You can also delete the files in your Temp folder. This used to be a big deal when hard drives were much smaller, but even on today’s larger hard drives it can’t hurt. There is also a free software program I would recommend you download and use regularly. Its called Spybot (http://www.safer-networking.org/index.php?lang=en&page=download). It’s designed to seek out programs that are known to cause users computer problems and remove them. I’ve used it on twenty or so machines at work and my home PC without it causing any problems. And finally you should deferment your hard drive. Depending on how fragmented you PC is, running defrag can often improve your processing speed.

If you do decide to upgrade a part of your current system, I’d suggest starting with your video card. I say this not because of the speed of your video card, but because Voodoo cards are known for having issues with numerous games, including online games. Unfortunately, there’s no way of knowing which part or parts of your system are causing the problem without spending the money to replace them. It could even be a problem with your motherboard, which could be an expensive discovery if you’ve replaced everything else on your system but the motherboard. That’s why I suggest buying a new system if you can save up for one. It’s far easier to buy a fully functional system, I currently own a Dell, and it can even prove to be cheaper in the long run.

Best of luck,

Have you changed any settings recently that may be affecting video performance, update to DX9 by chance, etc.? There is a slight possibility that DX9 is so far removed from the Nov '00 V3 drivers that it's causing these problems.

Since I don't know what changes to your system may have occured from the last time it functioned properly until you started having this problem, I'll just throw some ideas at ya.

1. Run scandisk. Pretty basic and guaranteed to not solve 99.6% of all Windows related problems but hey, it might work.

2. You run fine in full screen but crash in windowed mode - try setting your desktop to 16 bit color (which is what a Voodoo will run any game in) and make sure the desktop is running at least one resolution higher than the windowed game (eg., EQ windowed at 800x600 and Windows at 1024x768).

3. If you have NOT installed DX9a then go to www.microsoft.com/directx and look for the link to the DX8.1 (might be DX8.0a) drivers and reinstall them. If you have installed DX9a try running it's installer again or do a search on google.com for a DX uninstaller, run it, then patch 8.1 back in.

4. Download a utility like regclean.exe or better yet, hit symantec.com and get the trial to Norton's Utilities and run the full spectrum of tests and repairs. Even if it doesn't fix your problem it'll improve your system.

5. Re-install the Voodoo3 drivers from Nov '00. This is the last set of drivers that are approved by SoE to work with Everquest. The Feb '01 drivers and most "aftermarket" drivers do not work with EQ.

6. If anything in your system is overclocked, return the hardware to original operating speeds. Blow the dust out of your computer while you're at it. :)

7. Go into the BIOS and disable Video BIOS shadowing and caching along with Video RAM caching. Disable System BIOS caching as well. Some software/hardware configurations have problems with these being on due to memory address spaces and certain low level system calls.

8. Visit the Windows Update site if you haven't lately to install any critical patches. This is unlikely to resolve your problem but it's a good idea to do it anyway. You can find it under Start - Settings - Windows Update...

9. If you've disabled virtual memory or limited it in size for any reason make sure to return control to Windows. If you've never messed with it then don't sweat it. It doesn't change on it's own. Note: Norton's Utilities may want to set and optimize a permanent swap file. You can go for it but it's safer to just leave it under Window's control for now.

10. Disable any unecessary programs running in the background and in the taskbar prior to playing EQ or any game for that matter.

11. Make sure V-sync is enabled for Direct3D and Glide/OpenGL applications.

12. As a last resort re-install Windows over itself to restore any possible missing files. Make sure you run the setup from a normal boot of Windows and not from DOS or safemode. When the setup ask if you want to preserve newer files say "no", finish the install then visit the windows update site and install all critical patches and reboot. Re-install DX8.1 (or 9a maybe), the Nov '00 Voodoo3 drivers, and sound card drivers... in that order.

13. Shake the monitor and slam your fist on the desk screaming profanities at the system box. I swear to you this works.

14. There's more but I'm too tired to think straight at the moment. By the way, these suggestions aren't listed in the order you should do them. Just pick the easiest ones first and test your games after each one. Doing them all (except for uninstalling DX and installing Windows again) isn't a bad idea though.
