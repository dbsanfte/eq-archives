I kind of treat my mind like a computer hard drive. I file unimportant things away where I would have to work to retrieve them.

Having a photographic memory isn't as great as it sounds sometimes. If you don't train it, and learn to "file" memories, your mind gets too cluttered and you miss what's important. You can remember the page of your high school chemistry textbook that talked about the molcular weight of benzene, but you can't be trusted to pick up the dry cleaning on your way home - not because you "forget" but because it's not filed in the right place.

If I sit and think about it, and really work at it from the right angle, I can remember everything that I've done from about the age of 2 until I was 20 when I crashed on my bike, giving myself a closed head injury. For about 5 years after that, I didn't have the photographic memory anymore. Then at about the age of 25, I realized I had recovered the ability, so I can remember everything I have read, seen or done from that point until now. However, I learned at about the age of 17 to file unimportant details away where they are difficult to recall, just to keep my mind clear for important things. It's kind of like forgeting details, but they're really just remembered in a different way - like a computer archive. If I really want them, I have to restore the archive.

Having actually studied about memory, a lot of experts believe that everyone has a photographic memory. The human mind never really forgets anything - it just files it in a way that most people can't get to. In reality a photographic memory should be called "perfect recall" instead.

The one thing that a photographic memory/perfect recall is really best for is that I have never taken notes in a lecture class. Saves a lot of time studying, and a lot of notebook space.

There are two more very significant moments that I will never forget.

Have you ever had a true epiphany? A moment where you could almost feel the light bulb turning on over your head like you were a cartoon character? I've had that happen twice.


The first time, I was in Advanced Calc I my senior year of college. I had already taken a year and a half of calculus in high school, Calc I, II, III, Differential Equations, Proofs, and several other math and statistics classes. Advanced Calc was basically a theory class, in-depth exploration of the theories and proofs behind all the things we had done in the previous classes.

One day, Dr. Kelley was going through the proof of the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus with us. We had just spent several weeks proving everything up to that point, and we were starting from nothing and proving that calculus works the way we'd all been using it for so long. Up to that point, the Fundamental Theorem had always been assumed. The books would show us the theorem, and say "This will be left for proof later."

About halfway through the proof, that magic light bulb turned on over my head. CALCULUS MADE SENSE. I had always been able to do the problems, but suddenly, like an electric shock, it wasn't just math, it was...beautiful. I almost lost my breath; I was so overcome with that sudden understanding. Dr. Kelley stopped class to ask what was wrong, and I was too embarrassed to explain. Even now, it's hard to make someone understand the difference between merely being able to perform calculations, and truly understanding where they come from, what they mean, and why they work.


Advanced Calc II was actually fractal geometry. We spent the semester making pretty pictures in Mathematica. I got an A, and Dr. Kelley really wanted me to go on studying fractals in graduate school. I ended up having a baby instead. Fractal geometry was hard, but fascinating. I spent hours calculating the fractal dimensions of common objects and geographical features, etc. I learned about fractal encryption, a little about very large number theory, chaos theory, etc. I was hoping to go on and get an MS in applied math and work for the NSA, or something exciting, but life got in the way.

Then, one day last year, I was listening to a radio play on tape. It was John DeLancie and Leonard Nimoy, in a dialogue called "Spock Vs. Q". Something they said in there put all the pieces together for me. It was a simple phrase "Sensitive dependence on initial conditions" but it was the catalyst I needed. I was driving, listening to the tape in my car, and I had to pull over. That light bulb was shining over my head again, and I couldn't watch the road. FRACTALS MADE SENSE.
