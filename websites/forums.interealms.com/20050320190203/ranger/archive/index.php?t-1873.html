I'd recommend every parent (especially parents of girls) to read "Reviving Ophelia" by Mary Pipher.

In her practice she found that girls with parents who set rules and boundaries (ie. something that could be construed as strict and overprotecting) often had a better sense of self and were better prepared for later life.

Obviously this is anecdotal and hardly universal, but it's something to think about.

It needs to be pointed out that good intentions, while necessary for good parenting, do not by themselves make for good parenting. Although my parents had good intentions, my father wasn't good at dealing with the irrationality of a child (he's an engineer), and my mother couldn't handle me being no longer a child, but not yet an adult (she's a social worker, but has only worked with young children since before I was born).

Consequently, during my entire young life, one of my parents was "the mean one." Although still only slightly removed from this time, I know that this wasn't a construct of my imagination.

Running away is NOT a solution. I tried it twice, at about 10 and 12, and it just made things worse. If your parents agree on something, but you think its irrational or unfair, you've got two choices. If they strongly agree there's no way to change their minds. You can either deal with it and be unhappy, or just tell them you're not going to follow their stupid rule, do your thing, and deal with the consequences.

Due to the state of things, I've hardly seen my parents since last June, never feel like I want to talk to them, and doubt this will change. I do get along with my father, except on the issue of my mother, so this is the only reason I consented to come home at all. My little bro is also growing up, and becoming more fun.

Being neglected would have been worse, but not being able to stand the presence of your mother is pretty bad too.
