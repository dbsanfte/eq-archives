Tastian
I've seen a lot of discussion about dot aggro lately and have spent a lot of hours doing a lot of testing the past week or so. I'm going to share some of my results and then offer my thoughts/analysis.

Setup: The tests were doing using an SK and various DoT casters. I used a shaman, a beastlord and even a necro in spots just to test my results across a couple classes and to test different dots. The usual setup was me pulling a mob and then having the SK pull it off me with a 200 hate spell. Then I'd do various things until the mob turned, then hit the mob with 200 hate, wait for turn, cast again, and just ping pong aggro back and forth like this keeping track of exaclty what it took to turn the mob each time. Characters were on level ground, same faction (max KoS hehe), had backs to the mob at all times to avoid ripostes, etc. The tests were as close to identical as I could get them. I'd stay in 3rd person view and keep the distance between chars as close to the same as I could without using /loc.

Case 1: Plauge - Plague is very low initial aggro. Once the SK had taken aggro it took me casting plague and then it doing 2 ticks to turn the mob, then my SK would cast a 200 point hate spell and the mob would turn back, next plague would tick 3 times (55) and then turn away from my SK, after this I'd cast a 200 point hate spell again and the mob would turn back. Then plague would tick 4 times and mob would turn. This is where it stabilized. Plague almost always takes 4 ticks of 55 to turn the mob from a 200 aggro cast by my SK or 4 X 55 for 220 total "tick" damage.

Case 2: Venom of the snake - Venom of the snake is higher initial aggro like most of us know because of the poison counters. The thing to note though is that once aggro was onto the SK it took 4 ticks of Venom (59) to turn the mob. A 200 hate cast and it was back on SK, 4 more ticks, and it turned away. This was tested numerous times and to turn from a 200 hate cast usually took a total of 4 X 59 or 236 total "tick" damage.

Case 3: Curse of spirits - This was nice because curse of spirits is low aggro when cast and it builds up as it runs its course. What would happen with curse of spirits is this...

11, 23, 35, 47, 59, turns away from SK, cast 200 hate mob is back on SK, 71, 83, 95, turns away from SK again, cast 200 hate again mob back on SK, 107, 119, turns away SK again, cast 200 hate again mob back on SK, 131, 143, turns away from SK again.

In other words it was 175 total "tick" damage (+ landing the spell), and mob turned, then 249 total "tick" damage and mob turned, then 226 total "tick" damage and mob turned.

Case 4: Venom of the snake + burning affliction. This was just like the previous tests except once I landed the spell I swapped in a burning affliction item to focus the damage of all the ticks. In the previous tests Venom took 4 ticks to turn a mob. However, once I added in BA the mob was turning in 3 ticks. I'd get things like...

65, 64, 67, TURN (or 196 total "tick" damage)
70, 63, 64, TURN (or 197 total "tick" damage)

I kept hoping for a series of 60s to see if it'd take 4 ticks then to turn, but mob kept turning from 3 ticks instead of 4.

Case 5: Plague + critical affliction. Plague is just extremely predictable and after all the tests I ran it was just really easy to predict when the mob would turn. Like was said before once the initial spell is cast and the first couple ticks happen plague settles into this really predicatable situation of 4 X 55, turn, 4 X 55, turn, etc. However, with critical affliction a tick of 110 meant that mob would turn in 3 ticks instead of 4.

Case 6: Scorpion venom + critical affliction. Scorpion venom does 146 per tick (spell is basically ebolt, but eats an eth to get). What happens with scorpion venom is that it takes 1 or 2 ticks of 146 and the mob turns to bst, cast 200 hate with SK and mob is back on SK, nother 1 or 2 ticks and back on bst, etc. This is how it always goes. However, with critical affliction what happens is that if a crit tick of 292 turns the mob my SK could cast a 200 hate spell and the mob simply wouldn't turn back.

How I think dots work after all this testing:

When a dot is cast works like any other spell and generates aggro. In the case of disease dots this is quite low; well in the case of poison dots the aggro is fairly high. However, what appears to be happening is that each "tick" the dot generates more aggro based upon the amount of damage it does. Now I've also been testing nukes and the aggro they generate seems to be tied to the spell itself. One example was having a spell with base damage of 675 get resisted and generate more hate than a spell with base damage of 450 that was ID focused and crit. Unlike nukes though dots seem to simply do aggro based upon the amount of damage the tick did. The tests with BA and Critical affliction both showed noticeable increases in aggro. Also from the testing this amount of aggro generated by a dot tick seems to be in the area of 1 point of "tick" damage to 1 point of aggro. From my testing I don't think it's quite a 1 to 1 ratio, but there are many unknowns with aggro still, so I just can't say for sure.

From all of the testing (this isn't even a 10th of it) it does appear clear though that dots are generating aggro tick after tick. From the tests though this doesn't seem tied to the counters on the spell, or duration it runs (unless like curse of spirits the damage scales up), but rather just to the amount of damage that tick did. Meaning that even though poison dots are higher initial aggro if you stack a couple disease dots on the mob that total up to more "tick" damage you'll be generating just as much aggro per tick.

Personally, I think this needs to be changed. We've all known that dots did crazy aggro for their damage as it was, but this double aggro seems excessive imo. You pay aggro when the spell is cast and lands and then you pay aggro again tick after tick. It's no wonder people are pulling aggro even when all they do is use bane because each tick they doing about the equivilant of an anger II proc. With BA this appears to go up and on those great crit ticks we paid so many AA for appears to make it go up even higher.

I hope I didn't get too far off track hehe. There are still a lot of unknowns when it comes to aggro, but from all of these tests and others I've done the amount of aggro dots are generating each ticks seems excessive in my opinion and I do think it should be looked into.

One additional factor seems to be something like, How long have you been the thing the mob hates most?

Common situation, I slow a single mob and get aggro, then the tank quickly takes aggro, with no difficulty.

Common situation, I AoE slow two mobs, the tank takes one, I offtank the other. Thirty seconds later the tank turns from the first mob's dead corpse to take the second mob off of me. In those 30 seconds I have done nothing (almost nothing, if I've Quiescenced myself) to further aggravate the mob: just patiently letting it hit me instead of the cleric or the casters. But now, instead of needing 3 or 4 seconds to take aggro, it takes the tank longer, 8 or 10 seconds: extra time in melee, two casts of a hate spell, two procs, two stuns from a paladin.

I theorize from this that there is some time factor built in: the fact that the mob hated me the most for 30 seconds adds some further cumulative hate, so it takes the tank more effort to take aggro off of me.

If this is correct, then as shaman we take aggro:

(1) from the initial cast of a damaging spell, for the spell's damage (same as a wizard);

(2) from every additional tick of damage, for the tick's damage;

(3) from any amount of time that we were the most hated, for an unknown additional factor.

A further test would be this two-part experiment.

A. Shaman puts a low level disease dot on a mob that will take about 30 pct of a mob's hp, bringing mob to about ~70 pct health. Shaman tanks the mob for the two minutes that the dot runs. Tank then attacks the mob with full melee, no taunt or spells.

... A1. If aggro is figured on a per hp of damage basis, the tank would take aggro when he/she has done the same amount of damage as the dot: mob will be at ~40 pct health.

... A2. If dot aggro has also a per tick basis, then the tank will bring the mob to ~10 pct health prior to taking aggro.

... A3. If there is also a time-most-hated factor, then the tank will bring the mob to ~0 pct health prior to taking aggro.


B. Wizard nukes a mob for about 30 pct of the mob's hp, bringing the mob to ~70 pct health. Tank then attacks as before, no taunts or spells, melee only.

... B1. If aggro is figured on a per hp of damage basis, the tank would take aggro when he/she has done the same amount of damage as the nuke: mob will be at ~40 pct health.

... B2. If there is also a time-most-hated factor, then the tank will bring the mob to ~30 pct health prior to taking aggro.

I predict we would find case A3 and B2.

One thing to keep in mind when doing tests of this sort is that agro = hate + "bonus hate", where "bonus hate" is things like sitting etc, and more importantly the position on the rampage list. For example, a successful taunt puts you at the top of the hate table +1. This doesnt necessarily get you agro though since if your position on the ramp list is low, others may still have more agro than you. While hate can be acurately metered, the bonus hate cannot, and as such will allways represent an unknown if you want to try to accurately fix numbers in parses like this. In your case you have pulled, so the agro number the SK generates from one cast of their 200 hate spell is somewhat less than 200.

This is from simple observation my friend. Not only that, there is a very long thread (20+ pages) somewhere on the TSW about this subject. Agro is NOT the same thing as the hate list. Your position on the rampage list (ie the order of agro) does affect agro when things are close.

If you dont believe me, pull a mob with slow and have a warrior make a succesful taunt on it. Taunt sets your hate to the hate of the person at the top of the list + 1. The warrior will not get agro after that taunt until they have hit the mob. If you use a low agro attack, like kick, it might be necessary to hit the mob a few times. You can also see this effect at work on mezzed mobs where someone other than the warrior breaks mez after a successful taunt is made. If the chanter is higher on the rampage list for that particular mob than the warrior, it will go for the chanter even though it was successfully taunted (so if you see melee other than the warrior breaking mez, feel free to scream at them for screwing up).

If you want to help your tank in getting fast agro, make sure you are behind them on inc. Taunting off you is that much harder if you dont.
