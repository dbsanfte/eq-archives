QoS (Quality of Service) is the idea that transmission rates, error rates, and other characteristics can be measured, improved, and, to some extent, guaranteed in advance. QoS is of particular concern for the continuous transmission of high-bandwidth video and multimedia information. The packet scheduler takes the information from the packet classifier, creates queues for each data flow, and then empties the queues at the rate specified by RSVP when the flow was created. This is the definition for QoS. This is from a quick search on QoS. It sounds like it might effect someone on dial-up more than someone on DSL but worth checking.

QOS will re-order the packets going out of your machine based on "priority", in other words, it will allow what it considers high-priority traffic to break in line ahead of lower-priority traffic. I am not sure what priority the Windows QOS scheduler thinks your traffic has.

QOS has to be implemented end-to-end, i.e. if you have a router anywhere between you and the other end that does not support it, you don't get QOS. That includes a Linksys/DLink/whatever in your own home, something at the cable company/DSL company's local head end, or any of your ISP's routers you go through to get to the Internet backbone.

I had crashing problems with the latest ATI drivers; reverting to the previous ATI driver fixed it.
