Xev_Grammar
If you had experience with previous characters already, and know what good playstyle and etiquette is, then this should be easy for you.

I solo'd my shaman for almost his entire life up until maybe the 60's. I always avoided groups because I feared that I would not know how to play correctly. But I tried grouping and it turned out just fine.

I would say try to play your shaman with that twinge of fear that people are watching what you are doing. This will make you want to prove yourself and perform your roles with utmost diligence. Shamans complain about buffing-- learn to enjoy your buffs. Shamans complain about healing-- heal as well as you can without complaint. Learn standard shaman roles-- buffing, healing, slowing (how soon, standing where, etc)-- and then go that extra step-- canni-ing, damaging, debuffing, etc. Never be idle.

Then when you hit 65 you can start getting lazy again ;-P (when stuff gets more natural for you)

A common "newb" identifier for shamans is when they just sit there without canni-ing even though they're full health-- and they're always wanting a KEI. Also, some shamans slow way too slowly. Some shamans slow way too fast or cause the mob to chase them by not slowing next to the mob/main tank. Some shamans just don't know how to play well-- crowd control is very doable by a shamans... root/dog are powerful cc tools. More advanced shamans can tell when a shaman is "newbish" in more subtle ways-- if all they do is slow and nothing else for instance.

Anyway, do your best to prove your worth to people, and ask for feedback on improvement. Eventually it will all come naturally to you.

Well friend, the majority of your problem lies, not in your faults, but in the faults of the class at that level. They are really expensive compared to the typical mana pool of that level, and yet you can't seem to talk anyone out of even the more useless ones.

The problem is, the only way to offset some of these problems and keep ourselves from wasting all our mana on things we shouldn't is to take charge and simply tell everyone what you are doing and why you are doing it... However, never grouping before probably makes you kind of timid in that regard so your more inclined to let other people take charge... This will only lead to you getting more and more frustrated. At that level I usually gave them an option. I said I will only pass out talisman of tnarg, health, and shifting shield (only if no cleric buffs) to the MT. If he's not holding agro and everyone wants hp/ac/sta buffs, then your all going to have to sit and let me med back to full when I'm done casting them.

Strength buffs will be easy to keep up next level when you get tumultuous strength (group str buff). And ask off the bat who is wielding a proccing weapon. Tell them you don't have infinate mana, so your only going to dex buff those that are. At that level, you shouldn't have too many people to dex buff. Make sure to keep telling everyone why you are doing what you are doing, and it will all be fine. Say if your being asked to do something outside the realm of your typical routine, then you will need a little more med time in order to compensate.

Regen actually makes a pretty big difference at these levels, but it costs ALOT of mana, so tell the group it's simply not cost effecient to keep up on everyone. Only keep it up on the person who seems to be taking the most damage. This should be the MT, but at these levels, noone seems to hold agro worth a squat, so be flexible. Also, always keep yourself regened. It'll pay off in the long run by how much more canni-ing it allows you to do.

That handles buffing. As far as slowing goes, I never really started needing my slows untill sometime in my 40's. The mobs just don't hit hard enough before that... But more importanly, until you get torgors at 39, you're not slowing for all too noticeable of a %, yet your still dumping 125 mana. At 39 you get torgors which is 175 mana but will be slowing by another 11% than by what targars was doing at lvl 38. This is when the mana wasted on slow pays off, cause your not having to heal nearly as much.

As for damage, don't worry about it. Just send in dogdog, keep the mobs slowed, and let the melees deal with killing the mobs. If you do what I have said above, you'll really only be down on mana right after buffing, and slowly be able to work up your mana pool in-between slows. Eventually it will get high enough to dump a little mana on dps for a few mobs, but usually after about 3 or 4 your right back buffing... It's just how the game goes at these levels.

The problem is we have too much utility at lower levels, and not enough mana to handle it all. Canni II will help a little, and if you can get your guild to help you get it, that'll solve some of your problems. Stoicism at 44 kinda changes the game in your favor, and then it slowly starts going down-hill from there. You start having the mana pool to keep up with all your allotted jobs, and by your early to mid 50's you have enough to do all your duties and drop some dps on every incomming mob.

Don't fret about being a bad shaman. We all look bad early on unless we are rediculously twinked. Only way to avoid this is to let everyone else know the score, so when mana is low, they understand it is because tnarg, health, and shifting shield aren't cheap, haste falls every time the wind blows, and idiots who ask for agility buffs are basically eating your mana pool for the sake of a pair of pretty boots to look at. When the group realizes your mana limitations, they will start trying hard to streamline the process so you can do what helps them most.

Only other piece of advice I can give you, is if your still having alot of mana trouble try grouping with enchanters, or clerics, or both. Not having to haste is a HUGE mana save, and it'll give you the chance to do alot more. And leaving healing to the clerics really helps till you get the spell arsenal to be able to handle it yourself.

With adds, other classes can do a better job IF they are in the group... rangers and druids can snare and root, chanters can mez... a beastlord will send in pet to hold an added mob for a bit. However, it may be that your group does not have good crowd control, or all hell breaks loose. What do you do?
1) Try and root ONE add, in such a place that it is NOT hitting on your tank and mellee types. If this is not possible, root ONE mob and tell group to pull back out of harms way.
2) slow the original mob and sic dog onto that
3) slow the add (and tell your group it is slowed).
4) keep watching the mob... our root is short, and you may need to root again.

I strongly suggest you look out for comprehensive focus effects on your armour.. buff extenders and spell haste especially, and buff efficiency.

Build your wis through judicious armour selection. At lower levels, this provides bigger manna pool.

Learn when to heal. Our heals are poor in lower levels, but if there is no cleric in the group, you can be an assist healer. Watch the health bars, let the main healer tell you when and who to help.

Lastly learn to use hot buttons... "such and such a mob is slowed", "such and such a mob resisted my spell" would be two good ones to start with.

mem different spell sets of course... buff, self buff, slow/heal, normal fight would be four good sets.

Check out the various on-line lists for accronyms and in game terms... ninja loot, ffa, cawu, ML, MT. I was a dodo even at level 40 and ninja looted an aug 'cause I just didnt understand what a ML (main looter) was and did.

PennyAntics
Contrary to common belief, everquest is a twitch game, and to become a truely excellent shaman you must learn to twitch like a pro.

1) master assist healing and super fast targetting. You should be able to assist heal and patch any character under attack in .5 seconds. If you can't, practice.

2) slow as soon as possible. Always. Your mission as a shaman is to turn mobs into docile and harmless creatures with slow. Until you do that, they are a threat to the raid. Target mobs on inc, begin casting Malos while they are incoming, and once a tank has picked them up, run next to his shoulder and attempt a slow.

that's all you realy need to know besides the obvious, buffing is a duty that doesn't really require any skill.
Kenneth
Just a few more tips...

Slow, Malosinia line, Cripple... these don't break mez. Use them on mezzed mobs.

Your pet can also be a good buff timer (if you don't want to use yourself). Just cast on the pet first (or yourself).

Once you get going, start refreshing buffs in cycles. Example -- Health and Furious Strength both last 54 minutes. Don't cast them both in the same cycle or they'll wear off at the same time. Cast one on everyone (dog or you first), then wait a few minutes and cast the other. That way, you won't blow all your mana when all the buffs start wearing off at the same time, and you everybody won't be complaining that they don't have their buffs.

At higher levels, IoS, Stamina and Tuna (Hp only buff) all stack.

HoS, FoS line does not stack with int caster personal shields. Some will want HoS, some won't (I only have a couple casters that always ask me for FoS -- a few necros and one wizzy that has great gear). When in doubt, when buffing an int caster, ask first because you may be overwriting something they preferred to keep.

And, constantly do something... canni or whatever, there is always something you can be doing as a shammy.

This comment clicked with me. What class is your main? What level? Guessing here, but if your main's rep has resulted in a higher level guild asking you to join with your shaman then that toon's reputation in a group/raid environment must be pretty solid.

Whatever the class of the main, if you have experience grouping there are many attributes that make a good group member that are more about the player than the class. Examples: 1) good wizards can CC adds, pull aggro off chanter/cleric, save groups with evac. 2) enchanters can heal (rune - damage buff) in a pinch to give that warrior a few more seconds to pull mob off cleric (rune the cleric.) The list goes on. It boils down to doing what is possible and necessary to win. As far as flexibility goes in "doing what is possible" the shaman has soooooo many tools available.

At 38 your greater healing is the same as a lvl 38 cleric. They don't get complete heal until 39. From 29-39 you are as effective a healer as a cleric. Reality side of this is the mana cost. With all the other buffs you are dropping constantly and the slows/debuffs in combat it can be very hard to keep mana up with demand. Guess what - it is equally hard for the cleric. Groups have always been notoriously patient with letting clerics buff groups and then sit around waiting for the cleric to med back to FM (or close) before pulling mobs. Shamans (and druids) on the other hand are expected to buff then switch to battle mode as soon as finished and a mob is incoming.

If you are LOM (low on mana) the simple fact is this: you do not have mana to do your battle jobs. That is going to happen if the group wants all the buffs. Tell them at the beginning. Work it out from there. If the group expects/wants full buffs on all members they are going to have to wait. Look for those groups that understand that focused buffs (useful buffs on key group members most likely to need) is the best approach as it minimizes your downtime/maximizes pulling rate.

GET CANNI 2. Beg, borrow, steal, sell first born ... but get CANNI 2. You want to mem that as soon as you can. Same applies to ALL canni series spells. Make a canni hotkey and use it constantly. Always strive to be FM then strive to be FHealth then strive to be FM ... do the canni dance. Love the canni dance. It is the one tool you need to master first to solve the demand/ability problem with doing all you need to do in a group. Though you will never-ever get away from canni it gets better and better as you level. At 65 I can go from rezzed to group fully buffed (FO7, sta, str-diaku, group regen, bih li, agi, hastes and FA) and pet up/buffed and ready for pulls in 6-8 minutes (without KEI) while the cleric is at ~50% and still finishing single Virtue casts. This is thanks to Canni-5 and Canni-4. I mention this not to brag (though personally I think our mana recovery is way cool) but rather to give you some insight into what is ahead of you.

Know your group members. Those mid-30/lo-40 folks you are grouping with are likely twinked. At least some of them. If you have a tank super twinked your heals are just denting his hitpoints. Know what those are and (if main healer) adjust accordingly. If tank has a haste item already it is pointless to toss your haste on top of that. He can swing faster, sure ... but balanced with the mobs you are killing with ease it is more of a waste of time/mana to keep shaman haste up. If tank has a regen item use your regen to complement that. Flip side is if the tank is poorly equipped for their level your job got harder exponentially. If tank is not mitigating damage/inflicting damage well you need a new MA ... you could likely do better tanking if your skills are maxxed and gear is decent.

Others have pointed out a key to being a shaman in a group. That key is knowing your role. In pick up groups my initial focus is to play a traditional shaman: buff, slow, debuff. I do this a few rounds of kills to see how the group is working. Only takes a few mobs and a couple of multiple incoming mobs to know if other folks are playing their toons well in that group. If the cleric has a habit of starting CH when the tank is at 40% regardless of 1 or 2 or 3 mobs beating on him I know I have to toss quick heals on the tank until CH lands. This while also doing my best to get the mobs slowed and aggro pulled away to root or pet tank (if no chanter or unmezzable mobs.)

There are tons of variable in a group situation. First is being a primary shaman = slow/debuffs. Next is what is needed to keep group productive. Quick heals, root parking, off tanking.

As you gain experience in reading your groups and working well with any mix of folks and styles of play there will be often times when a multiple pull looks bad and because of you things settle down, mobs die and everyone takes a deep breath followed by comments of "good work everyone." Take that comment personally. It usually really means, "WOW! Awesome shaman!" To be fair to other classes, the same can be said of well played enchanters and necromancers and bards. These 4 classes have those versatile tools that can turn defeat into victory. Shaman has the best of those tools .. then again I may be prejudicial in that view :)

Good luck on your shaman. Come back and let us know what you think once you get to 49 and beyond. Its a whole new world then.

Number one get to know your spell book intimately. Use your Spell Sets! They are fast, reliable and save the downtime from opening-searching-memming-closing-standing-casting. Lately, I've become very dextrous at on-the-fly spell swaps: Right click a spell to drop it. Right click the empty slot. move cursor down the list to the set you need and then over to the specific spell and click it. auto sit-mem-stand and boom you're casting without having to o-s-c-s. Learn how those spells are sorted and use it over and over to get it down.

Spell line stays like this:
1. Biggest heal
2. next biggest heal/regen
3. haste
4. Canny
5. malo
6. cripple
7. slow
8. root

2, 3, and 6 are the ones I'll swap-on-the-fly or set up spell sets for. hit spell set "buffs" and 2,3, 6 go and 3 buffs come in. buff up and hit spell set "Attack Line" and I'm back to the start.

Set up hotkeys! I got one that allows me to canny 5 times. one casts malo and slow and sics dogdog.

My suggestion for a Shaman who wants to learn to work in a group situation is to start doing all the LDoN's you can. You will learn to play your class well or you will die a lot.

Start by only taking on the "classic" Shaman role of Buffer/Slower/De-Buffer. If a Shaman is unable to take care of this role in a group situation he's useless. Once you become comfortable in this role you will be ready to start handling Crowd Control.

Slow is an AWESOME way to grab aggro. You can easily turn a resisted pacy or bad pull into gravy. Enchanters? Who needs em. Back off from the fray. Slow one of the adds . . . you can bet your @$$ he's coming for you. He whacks you once, you root him. Sick Scooby on him to keep him parked. Repeat for all adds, minus Scooby who will be busy with that first mob for quite some time. Once the MT starts on the mob Scooby has parked, back Scooby off and send him to a different mob. I've juggled up to 4 mobs this way while our MT was polishing off the initial pull.

As you get higher in levels, you'll probably want to learn to pull. Depending on the mobs, you can either pull with Slow or with Mal(x). You'll learn over the course of leveling when to use which. Basically, if my Slow will land 3 out of 4 times without Mal then I pull with Slow. If my Slow is 50% or less, I pull with Mal so I am SURE that my Slow will land next.

As you become better you will find that your presence can turn almost any group into a seemingly well oiled machine. I get a LOT of comments from people I group with often that Adventures go SO much smoother with me there that they can't believe it. Its a good thing.

Good luck to you.
