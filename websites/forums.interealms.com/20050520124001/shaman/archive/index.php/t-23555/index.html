Fudapuda
One translation of Heyokah is, in fact, "holy clown", but you have to understand the context in which that term is used. In some of the southwestern US tribes (IIRC specifically its Hopi, though it could be Zuni, or both) the Heyokah, or holy clown, is a very important part of the religious structure, having the job of reminding the population of their humility, of their place in the order of things. They act in some ways as a trickster, humbling the mighty and keeping everything in context. The provide "balance."

In that light, I think Heyokah in an imminently appropriate title.

Caanus Grey
Just to clear up the Thunder Spirit and Holy Clown translations for Heyokah, you're getting about half of it each.

A Heyokah sees the Thunder Spirits in his vision quest (a ritualistic 3 to 5 days alone in the wilderness with no food or drink. It places one at the very brink of death so they are brought close enough to the spirits that their own personal guardian spirit can manage direct contact. The spirit who appears is used as an indicator of your life path).

If you see the Thunder Spirit during a vision quest, that is the greatest honor. The Thunder Spirit represents life itself (as opposed to seeing the greater spirit of a simple animal form which can mean anything from the Owl's Teacher, Bear's Healer or Wolves Hunter). The Heyokah is the greatest of all teachers for the tribe, and the strongest of healers. The "price" for this power however is that he becomes the Holy Clown, or Opposite Man. All his reactions are the opposite that would be standard.

So if it was a clear cloudless day with the sun beating down on the village, he stumbles about as if in the darkest night. If it is snowing outside he dresses in only a loincloth and grumbles about the heat.


Of course, the spirit quest is only one of the ways that tribes use to select their jobs, in some tribes it is simply your own dreams that determine it and that person simply dreams of thunder. I recall reading about one in which you had to pull the entrails of a dog from boiling water, but a Heyokah would always end up with the heart and (more importantly) his body would not scald from the boiling water.
