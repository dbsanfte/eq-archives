ok, lemme try to give you a short overview of what we do. :)

- Each slot has, for each class, a BASIC item. Ideally an item any recruit coming to your guild should have. This basic item may vary, depending where your guild is at, of course. :)
- All items better are priced using this item as reference value.
- We set a Maximum price for stats only.
- We price the effects and add them to stats price.

The best systems are 0% inflation systems. What this means is you hand out roughly the same amount of DKP per raid as you take out of the system in loot costs. IE. If you have a 40 man raid force for a mob that drops 2 loots, and you decide that mob is worth 20 DKP you are putting 800 DKP into the system. Therefore the cost of the 2 loots should come to a total of that same amount.

Not doing this will result in a % of inflation that will lead to a smaller % of your power raiders earning a larger % of the DKP than they are entitled.

No system will be a perfect one, but the closer you can keep it to 0% inflation, the more fair it will be to all your raiders.

beware of variable points for an item based on who is online or how long it has been dropping.

Variable or lower pricing over time for items can encourage point hoarding and can also mean that the folks who rarely play actually end up ahead (if they spent 20% of the dkp for the same gear, but only played 50% as much they end up ahead).

The 1-hander from emp is a great example. If the first 10 people paid say 20 points, why should the 11th get the same item for free? If an item is no longer worth spending the points for, let it rot, and let rot help the guild learn to stop doing the target.

In a variable system the person who always passes on the item til 3-6 months from the time it started dropping could end up with such a point advantage that they could possibly pick their favorite items from end game mobs and get them all.

Variable systems or systems where the price of an item goes down over time bother me since they can easily encourage behavior that is contrary to the guild's goals. It can also mean that the person who plays rarely and is patient on upgrades ends up with better gear than the hard core every day folks.

Most systems let people go negative. The fixed cost makes it so the go-getters who pay 110 for an item are not left behind the patient people who get the same gear a few months later at a fraction of the cost. If you routinely pay 3x for an item that will only cost x points 12 weeks later, and only raid twice as much as the lowest attendance person, you will routinely lose to that person later.

If items keep getting cheaper and cheaper, the point hoarders who wait and wait for that one dream item spend even fewer of their points as they go on their post dream item spending spree, and are even more able to hoard again in the next expansion.

Pretend you earn 1000 points a year. If you get the good stuff whenever you can, you spend heavily and likely spend all 1000. If the patient person next to you also earns 1000 a year, and is happy to wait to be near the end of the pack for those same items, they get the items almost for free, and probably end up with the same gear and at least 500 more points than you at the end of the year. The system Redchief mentions lets people do that, but the variable cost means if you hoard, you may only get 1-2 items tops before you are beaten down to a zero balance again, so you may as well not bother.

Rot only happens when no one bids on the item. You can be at negative 1400 and still win any item that no one else with more than -1400 points wanted. Having a huge negative balance just means it will take longer before you win an item that several folks wanted.

DKP vs Merit (Officer Vote/Decision) is always a pro-con system.

Basically it comes down to:
Point System vs Human judgement system

Human judgement will always have human fallibility in it. Such things as biases, lack of understanding the value of an item, etc.
Even the best officers are still human, so will be fallible. There is also a problem in that it allows 2nd guessing/perception of favoritism. Also gets into issues on Class based favoritism. in thas case the perception is often as bad or worse than any reality.

Human judgement's pro is that it can put the gear to better use.
Take the time ring with 50AC buff that's all/all. Is that ring better in a tank's fingers than a casters. Sure it is. If your cleric chain is failing from lack of mana pool/ft you can fix it. etc etcetc

thing I like about point systems is it allows people to take responsibility for thier own upgrades and choices. You want item X, save for item X. Period. Gets rid of complaints.

Personally, you would think a point system would be most valuable only based on peoples own intelligence on whats a "good item" for them. But people don't really pass on items that are better suited elsewhere.


Now for the whole inflation vs deflation aspect. Personally I've always assumed items should lower in cost over time. Reason is that usually a guild progresses over time. So what was say a top expensive loot like say FT6 Shield from Ssra is not as valuable compared to FT6 Shield/Torch from VT. If I could design a dream system I'd likely just categorize loots.

Top Tier Loot (Would be subject to posting)
2nd Tier Loot (Very Good level)
3rd Tier Loot (Okay mild upgrades)
4th Tier Loot (basics)
5th Tier Loot (toys)

as a generalization as a time Guild.
Top Tier Loot now (Quarm/Tier5)
2nd Tier Loot (Tier3/4)
3rd Tier Loot (Tier2)
4th Tier Loot (Tier1/Elemental)
5th Tier Loot (rot tells)

now obviously while a guild is learning time. Top Tier might well be Tier3/4. etc. if you want the first Bow of Tempest, you are going to pay more than if you want the 10th. The other key would be once an item stops getting tells at the level its classified at. It's time to drop it down.
