Boinshar
The "soft cap" isn't something hard coded. It's long been suspected, and now confirmed, that AC's usefulness is relative to the mob's ATK. However, the concept of diminishing returns exist, and hence the soft cap exists, because of a statistical anomoly instead of actual hard code rules.

Increasing your AC to 1500 from 1200 hardly does anything for, say, frogs in seb, because 1200 is well above it's ATK. However, it does make a difference for Vindi (who's cap was tested at being 1276 by one set of test on the steel warrior), yet at the same time, it could mean nothing to, say, AoW, who's ATK is most probably higher than any AC a player can achieve at this time.

The theory goes that the higher your AC goes, the less difference it makes because it only is effective against a very small number of mobs. At around 1300 AC you have surpassed the ATK rating of 95% of mobs in game. Therefore, any increase in AC would only make a difference to 5% of mobs in game--if it could make any difference at all, and this is why people experience diminished returns from increasing their AC.

HP should only become important for a shaman when your desired AC level is reached, since it's the only stat that does not decrease in value.

==============================================

Mobs do damage based on the following formula: (found by Joimster)

Damage = Bonus + (Damage_Rating * x)

Where:
Bonus is a number assigned by VI
Damage_Rating is a number assigned by VI
x = integer from 1 to 20

*Warriors Defensive Discipline Modifies x to a range of 1 to 10.

*Monks Stonestance Discipline Modifies x to a range of 1 to 2.

==============================================

Base damage for Characters is calculated from the weapon:
Damage = round/truncate[Bonus + (Damage_Rating * x)]

Where:
Bonus is the Damage bonus listed on the weapon
Damage_Rating is the Damage listed on the weapon divide by 10 (result is non-integer)
x = integer from 1 to 20

*Some irregularities and possible explanations.

1. Effect of High Strength on damage?
(*Test in extreme strength differences of 75 or less versus 200+ to see more obvious differences)

Q: Does extra damage from Strength add simply to the damage bonus of a weapon therefore increasing damage bonus and therefore increasing both max and min hit?
A: Easily tested by using a character with Low strength and parsing min and max values and then buffing strength to very high and parsing min and max values.

Q: Is extra damage from Strength added as an integer?
A: Easily tested by the above method and seeing if the increase to min damage is the same as increase to max damage. If they are the same then damage from high strength is added straight onto the weapon damage bonus. If they are different it is possible that the extra damage from strength is added to the damage rating of the weapon and is therefore multiplied by 20. This would mean that it may add 1 to your min damage and 20 to your max damage although it need not be integer values such as these as they may simply be rounded before damage is displayed on screen.


2. Effect of higher level characters on damage?

Q: Is this phenomena caused by higher ATK ratings, damage tables for higher levels or both?
A: ??? Research and logs at each level required.


3. Calculation for offhand damage?

Q: Is the formula the same as the above with the damage bonus simply defaulted to 0?
A: Put a fire beetle eye, lightsone in main hand and weapon in off hand and parse damage. Put same weapon in main hand and the fire beetle eye, etc in off hand and parse damage. What is the difference in min and max values?

==============================================

On the subject of Avoiding and Mitigating damage taken from a mob. (particularly mitigation)

The raw data listed by Aauminki while tanking Seneschal Aldikar show that hits were indeed mitigated to min damage five times greater than the average and hits were also found at max damage two times greater than average.

Indeed 37% of all hits that landed fell as either max or min damage. Note that the fact that this is a single set of data makes this unreliable but does point at an avenue of exploration.

An initial hypothesis is that x (as used above in the damage by mobs formula) is not initially calculated as an integer between 1 and 20 but is truncated and/or rounded to this in the final steps of the algorithm.

Example: Mobs ATK is compared to your AC and the R.N.G. is also used somewhere and comes up with a number. That number is then truncated/rouded and made to equal 1 for any number lower than 1 (therefore mitigating damage to a min. hit) and made equal to 20 for any number greater than 20(making sure that the damage does not go above max damage).

*Your AC is much higher than the mob's ATK:
More x values are lower and in quite a few cases are negative values and therefore made into an x value of 1. Comparatively few hits are for max damage.

*Your AC is much lower than the mob's ATK:
More x values are higher and in quite a few cases above 20 and therefore made into a value of 20. Comparatively few hits are for max damage.

*Your AC is almost even with the mob's ATK:
The x values have a fairly even spread between most possible values except for the min and max which have more occurences than the others. Reason being that x may still have been in a bigger range than just one to twenty, i.e. -15 to 35.

This snippet describes how these mechanics explain the "soft AC" cap phenom

Then we can infer that if your AC is high enough that the non-random part of hits is mostly BELOW N = 1, you will get the largest spike at minimum damage. This represents a ceiling on mitigation AC, since the random component will always put SOME hits over (N = 1) and reducing hits further below (N = 1) doesn't help. If the random component continues to be reduced even when the modal hit is far under (N = 1), this becomes a soft cap.

Secondly, we can infer that as the modal hit moves through the range (N = 1) to (N = 20), we see the 'sharp dropoff' effect that so many people have reported in the AC 700 to 1200 range.

Thirdly, we can infer that if the modal hit is often over (N = 20) most times, you see the big spike at max damage. This represents a floor on AC, which has been observed but never really commented on. Since at (N = 20) the random component will ALSO be large, we find that the floor is softer than the ceiling.
