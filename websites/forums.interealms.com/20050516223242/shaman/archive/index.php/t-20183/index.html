Mob damage greatly scaling up means healing has to greatly scale up, to keep up, or it will fall behind and change for the worse the contribution of the healer classes and perhaps the balance between them, if not the balance of the entire game. For shamans, healing has not kept up. Their healing contribution has become severely marginalized, and the balance among the alternate healers has changed dramatically.

Encounters with mobs speeding up because their hitpoints are so low and their die relatively quickly, narrowing the window of opportunity to put DPS and debuffs in on them, means you have to adjust player abilities accordingly, or balance again will suffer.

Shamans still are the kings of slow in more ways than one. Almost every spell we have casts very slowly. Adjusting our slow heal probably will have little effect because part of it's problem is simply that it's slow, regardless of how much or little it is.

On the offensive front, we have a few problems as well.

1) With mobs dying quickly, the slow-casting DOTs we have are minor contributors at best, since they often have no chance of lasting for their full duration.

2) Significantly, shamans have so many other things to worry about -- malos, slow, spot healing -- that getting a chance to have a DOT last long enough to do the damage it was designed for is even less that it would appear. Other DOT'ers, with good aggro control, can land DOTs fairly quickly. A shaman has a lot of things to do first. This makes the ability to do damage with DOTs much less than it really might appear on paper.

3) High resist rates on DOTs(on our DD rains too, don't forget), combined with the fact that shamans have to do other things before they can worry about DPS anyway, means failure rates on DOT casts can push the potential DPS that DOTs bring to the shaman class even further away from panning out in actual play. By the time malos and slow and perhaps a spot heal go through, and then a failure to land a DOT go through, a DOT that subsequently lands can have a very small chance of doing anywhere near its full measure of damage. By this time the idea of DOTs doing much damage begins to drift into the very theoretical indeed.

Theoretically, it's nice to have a DOT that does reasonable damage but takes two minutes to do it. Or even ones that are, thank goodness, a little faster.

But in actual play, the theory frays around the edges pretty severely, for the reasons noted above. Think about how long the chain is that can lead to the ability to land the first DOT (malos, slow, perhaps a spot heal and/or perhaps a failure of the DOT to land even a single time). By then, the time that has passed can make old ways of looking at shaman DOTs outdated. Certainly you can forget the disease line entirely.
