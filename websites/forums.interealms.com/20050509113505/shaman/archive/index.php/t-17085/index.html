Kriel Darkbane
In my experience, a lower level shaman's primary role is healer. You're usually the main healer in a group, if not the only one. You just got Healing at 19, and can fill this role very well. Your buffs and slows are secondary, for now. If you actually by some miracle are grouped with a cleric, then you can do more slowing, buffing, nuking, etc... But, really, the best thing you have for a group right now, by far, is the Healing spell. If you're healing, figure out by experiment if it's more efficient for you to slow a mob, and then heal, or just heal. It'll depend on the mob, and on your tank. Your general goal (besides having fun!) should be to keep the melee classes and pets in your group doing damage (and thus earning exp) as much of the time as possible.

At 24 you get some great spells - regeneration, cannibalize, envenomed breath, frost strike. You get major bonuses to your grouping AND solo'ing abilities.

At 29 you get Greater healing, Tagar's Insects (your first slow that makes a major difference in mob damage output), and quickness. You're a really good healer/buffer at this stage. In fact, I think healing and buffing is still your primary role in a group, until L 39, when you get Togor's Insects. That's the first GREAT slow spell, and is also about the time your heal spell really doesn't keep up with monster damage well at all. At that point your primary role becomes debuffing, with healing a secondary (but sometimes you're still the only healer in a group!) role.

I wrote this during lunch and I’m afraid it’s both rambling and long-winded, but I’ll toss it out anyway for whatever its worth.


I soloed my way up to the mid thirties. I’d buff myself, including haste, slow the mob, dot the mob and then beat it to death. When I got my first pup I’d have him guard the camp and pull mobs to him. While I tanked the mob my spirit pup would bite its butt.

About level 36 I started grouping with a ranger and a cleric. With those two in the group, and whoever else joined up with us, my main job was to keep everyone buffed and the mobs slowed. This worked out very well till I hit about 55. Then I started grouping with a warrior, necromancer and cleric on a regular basis. Again my job was to keep everyone buffed and to slow down the mobs that were by now putting out a lot of damage. Thankfully I started getting group buffs, which really helped speed up the buffing process. I’m also thankful I’ve never had to play main healer.

I would still like the option to solo, but I feel I’ve about seen the last of my solo and melee days at 61. I’ve found one spot I can successfully solo. I can get 1% AA xp per kill, which isn’t bad, but I must admit that LDoN adventures have kind of spoiled me. Solo xp seems even slower than it used to be. I can still take a number of hits when meleeing in a group without worrying about it, especially if I can get Torpor off, but I’m so much more effective as a caster I may as well hang up my Witchdoctor’s Cudgel.

In my opinion the strength of a shaman lies in a group. If you really want to solo I’m sure you can manage it, but your skills will really shine in a group. Your ability to slow down a mob’s attacks and speed up the attacks of your group will make a huge difference in what you all can and cannot fight. Playing a shaman, or at least playing a shaman well, is an ongoing learning experience.

You need to learn to canni dance to make the most of your manna regeneration ability. In short, your manna increases every six seconds and you gain more when you’re sitting down. When your medding you want to cast cannibalize during that six second period so you’ll get your natural manna regeneration along with what you get from your canni spell. So you watch your manna bar. As soon as your manna increases you stand, cast canni and sit back down. If you do it right you’ll be sitting when its time for the next manna increase and its as if you never stood up. You can set up a hot button to make this easier. And when you save up enough coins for a mount, which you can only use in outdoor zones, you won’t even have to worry about standing up, just press the canni button over and over again.

You need to learn when to slow and when to DoT so that you get aggro as little as possible. When I was grouped with a ranger I never had to worry about when to slow. I could slow a mob as soon as it got within range. If it came running after me the ranger would snare it and the mob would forget all about me. Without someone to snare or cast some other spell to really tick off the mob you have to be more careful, especially in places like the plains. Those creatures hit really hard so you may have to wait until the tank has dropped the mob to 90% health before slowing it. If you have the manna to spare for DoTs, assuming the mobs live long enough to make casting a DoT worthwhile, don’t do so right after casting slow or you’re very likely to have a mob chewing on various parts of your anatomy. And when you do have a mob chowing down on you, and it will happen, don’t go running around screaming like a little girl. If it’s the mob the tank is beating on, give the tank a chance to pull it off you. They can’t do that if your running up and down the zone. If you have adds attacking you, root can be your best friend.

You’ll need to learn, to some extent, how other classes are played. A good example of this is the necromancer. I cast Torpor on any non-melee that needs healing during combat. But you may not need to do that with a necromancer. They have spells that can suck the life from a mob and heal themselves at the same time. Though at higher levels a necromancer may be doing so many other things with his or her manna that they may still appreciate being healed.

And you’ll need to learn certain tricks to make your job easier. Since buffing is such a big part of being a shaman, it’s handy to know when to refresh those buffs. Group buffs are easy since you see them fading from your own character. But casting something like haste on myself, for example, is a waste of manna since I’ll be sitting on my fuzzy kitty butt. So what you do is cast haste and other such buffs on your pet first. When the buff is dropping from your pet its time to cast it on the melees in your group. With the new pet buff window this will be even easier. It’s amazing how many time’s I’ve gotten compliments on my timing because of this. *grins*

Okay, that’s more than enough rambling from me.

Gwain Buffmonkey
The dance!

Every six seconds, you regenerate mana and HP.

How much hit points you regen depends on your buffs, equipment, race, etc.

How much mana you regen depends also on buffs and equipment.

When you sit, you regenerate more health on those "tics" every 6 seconds, and more importantly, your med skill kicks in and you regenerate way more mana.

Canni dancing is the process of standing and sitting and casting canni in order to both canni and get sitting regen and meditation bonuses.

So....sit down. Wait for your health and mana to go up (you can see the little bar move, of your hp number go up), then get up and cast canni. Sit down again, and you'll get the next meditation tic. Repeat.

doqtir
One thing I didn't see mentioned was 'slow positioning', for lack of a better phrase. While not extremely important (unless grouped with a rogue perhaps) at lower levels, it's something you'll want to practice and ingrain into your routine.

Basically, slow positioning is just keeping the mob's position relative to the main tank the same while you slow. The MT's primary job (besides holding agro) is to position the mob. In group situations this may mean giving the rogue a back to stab at while in raid encounters he/she may need to back the mob into a corner, push it, etc. Our job is to stay within melee range (no running around) AND stand directly behind the MT until they have regain agro from our slow. As you grow more comfortable with different mobs (in different zones), you'll get a feel for how close you need to be. Ideally you want to be behind the MT and at max melee distance. Mob agro is based on a couple of things, but proximity is the one you can control directly. Again, with some practice you'll be able to step in and slow, take a couple of hits and back slowly away allowing the MT to use his proximity to regain agro.

At any rate, that's my 2cp. Probably not very coherent (12 hours of dealing with idiots), but hopefully you get the point.

Most of the basics have been covered. I just want to emphasize what dogtir wrote. Stay near the MT when casting slow so as to not cause the mob to move away from him/her. Mobs running around, ping-ponging aggro is BAD. Avoid it at all costs. Now, if a caster or cleric gets aggro, it's beneficial to cast "slow/cripple/high aggro spell" so that the mob will lose interest in them and come to you. Or if there are adds, use those spells to get the add's attention so that you can run a ways from the group and root-park the mob so it can't damage anyone. To reiterate my main point, though, do NOT ping-pong aggro. Stay NEAR the MT when slowing. It's a good habit to start early. :)


ps. You don't get the spell "cripple" until later levels so insert the level appropriate spell of that line (melee debuff - ac/agi/str debuff) in its place.

LaughingWoman
As a shaman your role is to fill the spots that need filling in your group. Heal, slow, crowd control, even extra damage - no other class is as equipped to round out a group and compensate for their weaknesses as you are. Try new things, learn as much as you can about other classes' abilities, strengths, and weaknesses and you'll get "wow, how did you DO that?!"... all the time.

In an emergency, the things that you do at the beginning of the encounter will turn the tide. Often, people won't notice or realize what you've done. Just try to learn from every dirt nap you take - there's almost always something you can do, as your levels increase and your spellbook rounds out. Get well acquainted with Lucy http://lucy.fnord.net/ and make sure to know what each spell does as you get it.
