I think August Derleth was the guy. He did do a good bit of writing in the Lovecraftian style(thought it seemed pretty inferior in comparison) as have a number of others -- there are even whole books of short stories out.

The rats in the walls! I remember the Stephen King story of that name, taken from his "Night Shift" collection, which was a clear copy of a Lovecraft original, but was great all by itself, not just a cheap paint-by-numbers business. King was very good at that point in his career, I think. He wrote about Lovecraft and horror and fantastic fiction in a really great book called "Danse Macabre," a non-fiction book about how that kind of fiction works -- how it pulls and tugs at our psyches, and appeals to us, and why -- and looks at examples of leading themes and writers in those genres. He pays great tribute to Lovecraft, who he rightly credits as a sort of father of American horror writing. When I originally read that book, I had to go find Lovecraft. And I did, and had a good time. :)

I did see his picture. Poor guy! Not a looker. I remember the phrase from some biographical sketch of him saying that his wife described him as "an adequately excellent lover." Anyway, in response to some of the discussions here: August Derleth and Clark Ashton Smith were two of Lovecraft's closest friends and members of his writing circle. I believe Derleth was the one responsible for making sure Lovecraft's works did not fade into obscurity. Smith was more of the fantasy/sci-fi Lovecraftian, as can be seen in his "Xiccarph" and "Zothique."
