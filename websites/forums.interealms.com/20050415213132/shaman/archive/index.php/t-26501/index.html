Glori I want to debate whether it is insanely powerful.

The spell is powerful. I've done some calculations in some of the other threads that propose a max burst dps on a raid of about 1100 dps over a three minute period with this spell. That's cooperative dps, for a shaman casting this buff on 18 other characters.

That compares to a wizard's max burst dps of about the same 1100 dps, chain nuking their best Ancient spell, using all available aa and focii and buffs. That's direct dps: the one wizard causes it by nuking the mob.

It's not entirely a fair comparison: what damage one character can cause by nuking, up against the damage another character can cause by buffing 18 others.

The fair comparison may be to look at an enchanter casting one mgb buff, and increasing the actual burst dps (not the theoretical max, but the actual dps) of 30 characters in a typical raid.

150 unhasted dps x 30 characters = 4500 dps

4500 dps x .7 haste = 3150 dps

Assuming 75% efficiency during a boss mob fight: 2362 cooperative burst dps created by one buff, cast 40 minutes ago. (We won't even bother to count all the additional damage the enchanter caused during the plow on all the trash mobs along the way.)

So my 1100 max theoretical cooperative burst dps compares to the enchanter's 2362 actual cooperative burst dps.

If I keyboard like a madman and drain my mana bar over these three minutes I may get close to that theoretical maximum: which would mean that a very capable shaman casting and retargetting constantly could provide 47% of the cooperative burst dps of a run-of-the-mill enchanter who cast one buff 40 minutes ago.

Being able to chain buff people as fast as I can go, to create that much damage: that is powerful. But it's not insanely powerful. It's still less than half of what an enchanter routinely does, with one spell, once an hour.
