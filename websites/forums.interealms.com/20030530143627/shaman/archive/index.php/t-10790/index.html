This is his firewall detecting, and blocking, a Trojan Horse virus being sent to his computer

First off firewalls don't detect trojans. This is his firewall seeing UDP packets coming from EQIM servers and, because of a default rule, alerting as POSSIBLE trojan activity. Going by the above traceroute, there is nothing suspicious about the packet in question as it is originating from a sony ip.

Most untweaked intrusion detection systems or firewalls will alert/block UDP packets in the reserved port ranges. Especially for ports like the above, not registered as being used by a legitimate program. EQ itself communicates on ports in this range also.

There are really only two realistic ways to have your account compromised. 1. Give out your account info to someone else or 2. Leave your machine unprotected without an antivirus program and allow a trojan horse to be installed. While there are other ways than these 2 I don't think you willl see anyone go to the effort the other options entail just to gain access to an EQ account.

> What does that all mean Panamashammy?

It merely means nothing SOE writes takes into account firewalling at all. EQIM uses exactly the same network protocols as EQ itself does, and is thus no more or less secure than EQ is.

[ This has many drawbacks, among them being that it is one of the least firewall-friendly software packages imaginable and it has none of the features you might reasonably expect from a messenger program. On the plus side, lots of IM software has too many features and many of them are so ill-conceived that they constitute substantial security risks. Overall, it's a wash. ]

In this case, the firewall is pointing out that the source address on the Sony server happens to use a port number that is the same as that used by a well-known trojan horse program. The same thing happens when you run EQ, and it's perfectly benign; it happens because the EQ server code and network protocols were not designed with the existence of firewalls in mind and thus they generate all kinds of false alarms.

Sony could change the EQ network protocols so that they don't generate traffic which can be mistaken for threats, but clearly they don't see doing so as being sufficiently worthwhile to them.

In the specific case of personal firewalls (such as the one the above log comes from) you can get around this by configuring it to recognise the EQ or EQIM program itself and give it specific permission to bypass the firewalling. Alternatively (and for firewalls running on separate boxes) you have to configure the firewall to treat any traffic going to the specific IP addresses Sony uses as being "trusted".

> If you wanna really be safe setup a *nix box as your gateway and configure your own firewall.

EQ is so firewall-hostile this would be an exercise in pure frustration; the same problem will occur, namely that the port numbers used by both the client and server ends of the connection are chosen by the host OS, and so virtually any combination of port numbers can occur. That means you have to let any traffic to sony's network be unfiltered, and you will periodically have to reconfigure your firewall when Sony happen to move the IP addresses of the servers around (as they do from time to time).

I have worked in a network admin job for 9 years now, and 'stolen' user passwords are something that we have had to deal with many times. I would guess that upwards of 90% of passwords that are stolen (or in this context accounts that are "hacked") are done so through simple social engineering.

Yes, even if you don't want to hear it, that means that 9 times out of 10, no matter how much the user screams up and down that they didn't, 90% of the time they told their password to someone else. Even if they only told their best friend, or their son or daughter who "wouldn't do anything like that", they never believe you until you show them the connection logs, the date and time to the second of the connections and the IP and IP addresses used. And still, after you show them the logs with the IP address of their friend's computer, they still don't believe you.

Just from these cases that I have investigated, again I would say at least 90% of unauthorized account usage is by someone who has been willingly given the password. About half of the other 10% is owing to poor password selection (picking an easily guessed password, such as your birthday, dog's name, wife's name, something stupid like that), or a password being written down where it can be found by someone else.

I would compare the chances of someone using actual technical computer skullduggery to get your password to those of being struck by lightning. No matter how much you trust whomever-it-is, if you give someone else your password you are a complete and total fool, and are just plain asking for it. No two ways about it.
