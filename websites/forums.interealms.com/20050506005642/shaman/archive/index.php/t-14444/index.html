In my limited experience, if it is an ongoing AD&D campaign, characters don't generally die because you have a party adverturing together and if one person has to roll up a new character that character won't be able to keep up with the rest of the group. The DM has a lot of flexibility in AD&D, also, that a computer just wouldn't have. The DM knows if someone is griefing, and is unlikely to tolerate it (well, if it is a DM I would play with), while a computer can't make that distinction.

I am a member of a local gaming group which runs gaming sessions which only last for one afternoon/a couple of hours. So then you could conceivably die, but it wouldn't be that painful, since you wouldn't have much time to become attached to your character.

SOE makes a lot of money because people *are* attached to their characters - I know a lot of people who don't even play anymore who keep paying SOE money every month because they don't want to let go of their character. Guilds wouldn't work with their current structure - what would happen if the leader died? How many people would want to raid?

There are people who like this type of game (I'm not one of them) as there are permadeath MUDS. They tend to be really small, though, and definitely don't have a population that would support a for-profit MMOG. There might be a market, but I don't think they would get anywhere near the subscription numbers EQ gets.

Just my opinion.
