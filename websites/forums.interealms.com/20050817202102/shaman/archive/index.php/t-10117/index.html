BlargX
One of the best documentaries I've seen in a good while came out and was later repeated on PBS a few months back.

It was about "cool chasers," people who try to both figure out what might sell as cool and design it to sell it back to people who think that by buying products crafted for "coolness," they themselves will somehow be "cool" or have the benefits of fitting in with a "cool" lifestyle.

It showed how a band, Korn, was marketed as rebellious and "cool" and "outsider chic" when in fact it was guided from early stages through MTV marketing plans to its heights; it was reminiscent of the "corporate rock" of 70's bands like Journey and Foreigner.

It's very interesting to see those people I work around who are uneducated devoting a staggering percentage of their paychecks and their emotional and even sexual energy to every ramification of whatever new trend comes along, thinking it's somehow rebellious or, most perversely of all, "genuine" or whatever, when in fact it's merely prefab pop culture dreamed up by Madison Avenue suits and spooned out to dullards like oatmeal slop slamming onto a tin cafeteria tray.

Seriously, every star that gets a tattoo is huge news, and god forbid J-Lo wears a new belt buckle -- suddenly everyone HAS to have it or they completely suck and are totally laughable. Of course, next year that belt buckle sucks and you suck for wearing it -- even if it actually is not a bad belt buckle.

A marketer in that special noted something along the lines of -- the guy who says, No way, I never buy into advertising and all that...I'm a total individual and I'm not suckered in by that stuff, I just brush it off -- is ALWAYS the guy with the Oakland Raiders t-shirt, the porsche sunglasses, and the budweiser beer cap -- a complete walking billboard in complete denial.

It's very funny, but sometimes sad and even outright disturbing to see a good chunk of the people I work with open a People Magazine and go apesh*t over who is supposedly dating who and their clothes and then do everything in their power to dress up in similar clothes and whisper about the shoes and belt buckles of those who don't. They're completely convinced that they're out there and on the edge, defining a new way of living or something, but they're like open floppy disk slots waiting for a program to be slid into their gaping slots. These folks aren't all kids, either. Some of them are well over 30 or 35. Sometimes I look into their eyes and I'm scared for all of us.
