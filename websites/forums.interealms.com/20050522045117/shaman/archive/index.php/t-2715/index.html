Something that's struck me funny is how on so many other servers the prices on Shaman spells is so ridiculously out of hand. I'm curious if Shaman on any other servers have tried what many of the shaman on Tribunal did.

Anyway, from what I remember Myzr took all the excess shaman spells their guild got and would sell them for guild funds, of course after equipping all his own guild with said spells and keeping spares as needed I assume. The interesting part is that Myzr was not greedy, despite the implications of a name sounding terribly similar to 'miser':eek: and capped his prices at 1k/5k. 1k for non leet spells, and 5k for the biggies such as Malo, Avatar, Bane, Pox...

The net effect was that this trend caught on and in a large way still is in effect among many of the upper level Shaman on Trib. I paid 5k each for Malo, Torpor, and Avatar from a shaman in another guild as recently as a month ago. :D As far as I know, Torpor typically sells for around 10k on Tribunal even now that the demand has gone up much more than the supply. At least, I know several shaman that cap their price at that if/when they have one to sell. /shrug

Now Myzr kicked this trend off, or at least that's what I hear since I was too low level to be effected back when this started up, but it continues on to this day. I talk to shaman often who will simply refuse to pay the 20k+ prices some spell farmers ask for high level spells, and the result is that after enough tells about how their price is too high, even these farmers start to bring their prices down to more reasonable levels.

So, get together on your server with the other guilds and work out an inter-shaman trading community. It pays to work together, or rather it at least costs less. ;)

It is certainly the choice I would make again, especially with the drop rates being increased. (edit: I did actually buy Pox and was gifted a copy of VoB)

I don't see how what we did was anything other than a win/win situation. Lots of people who cannot or are unwilling to go farm Kunark spells got Kunark spells from us. I got a copy of Torpor. My guildmates and friends made money for buying other spells we needed and for really nice equipment upgrades.

Just as a note, I've never sold a copy of Malo or Torpor. I've never seen Malo drop, and Torpor only once, which is my copy. I was deathly anxious I would never get either of those spells. The "lower prices mean lower supply" theory is not really a very significant problem here. It is generally true in the real world economy, because producers choose what specific things to produce based mainly on the marginal cost of production and the demand for them.

Shaman spells don't really fit this model because people rarely choose to kill spell-dropping mobs primarily to get these spells to sell in the marketplace. The main source of these spells are the Kunark dragons, Chardok royalty, and VP. These targets have a wide variety of other very desirable drops. A decrease in the price of torpor will not lead to a significant decrease in the killing of these mobs, and will therefore not significantly constrict their supply. The same is true, albeit to a slightly lesser extent, of Chottal. So, you may see a very slight decrease in availability, but only a very slight one.

A real-world analogy is something like selling restaurant grease. Just because the price of grease goes down does not mean that less grease is produced, because the grease is generated largely as a by-product of cooking food. If I own a restaurant, I will sell my grease to the grease man anyway, since no matter what I have the grease. The exception, of course, is if the price of grease falls below my costs to collect and store it until the grease man shows up. Then I'll just throw it away. This caveat is not relevant to this situation unless the price of the spells was set very very low by the cartel (less than 3k I'd say).


This is pretty much an example of people who think they understand economics mindlessly applying a basic model without thinking about whether the model's underlying assumptions apply to a particular situation. It is the kind of thing that gives economics a bad name, but it plays well on talk radio and the WSJ editorial page ;)

We keep tossing around economic principles but you folks left some important ones

The marginal utility of money: Each dollar's (or plat) value is relative to that of the dollar next to it. $100 is alot if you are starving, nothing to Bill Gates.

The common law has a definition of reasonable value borrowed from economics (used in every US state to help courts determine what something should sell for): What a reasonable buyer would pay a reasonable seller, with neither under undue pressure or influence?

The economies on servers are a reflection on the fact that Verant did nothing to address a year or more of (benign?) neglect regarding Kunark drop rates and placement. I fully believe these pricing issues were caused by 1>miscoding of Malosini at Juggs, 2> the decision to put keys spells into Veeshan Peaks at higher drop rates figuring people would raid there enough to balance out the drop rates, 3> and then a few months later to remove all incentive to go there by creating Tov coupled with the key issues. A year of flawed distribution has created a supply problem. The options for guilds to hunt locations of more value before Veeshan Peak was mined out hard created tremendous pressure on the poor supply.

We all have our pet theories on what caused the economies. Not the point in this discussion and there are lots of threads hashing out our favorite conspiracy theories. Regardless, we now have inherited economies where certain single class defining spells (unlike weapons or armor for which there are alternatives) cost more than entire sets of Thurg multiquest armors. Shaman are cursed with more of these spells than any other class.

And to argue that spells should cost a little or alot, or is worth it to wait or pay a premium to get them fast, is ignoring the fact that 40K or 90K is "fair" or not depends on individual circumstance and perspective. If you fell into a guild that gets them easily, or can generate that kind of money, the economy helps you and your friends, and is not easily shrugged off. If you are in a mid range guild, the issue is the ability to raise the money and will they be there when you raise it. Those in small or no guilds due to play hours or real life commitments, or relationships they dont want to sever are in real trouble and have to rely on the "karma" or the "largesse of strangers" method.

Verant can fix this in a minute and effectively set a ceiling for spell costs: Put L60 for under dropped spells on a vendor for a high but set price. Would the market adopt that price automatically? Not necessarily, there are lots of items that vendors buy for 10-100 pp and then try to resell at values that are outlandish. Having a vendor sell all L60 spells for all classes for 10-50,000 pp would not cause all spells to cost that much, but would serve to cap values and stop the preception of price gouging.

Verant is in an awkward position. I dont think they believe they can be seen as directly tampering with player economies in this way, irregardless of how much their actions (or inactions) have caused them. But on the other hand they know they will lose player base over time as people abandon the game in frustration because the guild system that they insist we use can virtually turn into cartels. (And, on some servers, actually have from what I read.)

We all understand that there are items that the most uber and committed of players should get first if at all. The issue really is should class defining spells be among those. Or should they be readily available and Ancient versions or other effects seperate the uber from the ordinary.
