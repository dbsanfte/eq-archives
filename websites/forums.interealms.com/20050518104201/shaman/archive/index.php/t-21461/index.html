Sorry Zuksharn, but you are dead wrong. I'm a VP at a small software development company, 50 employees. We build applications for mostly government clients in the Washington, D.C. area. I can tell you, that even with multiple teams, every business has limited resources. You can only afford to have x number of people on your staff, period. So, if you put a large share of those folks on one project, it naturally leaves less folks for the rest. That is simple business practice.

Even if they could afford to hire more folks and still make their profit margins, there are other problems, finding good developers and the cost of recruiting, equipment and office space for them, additional overhead staff to support them, learning curve and ramp up time issues, etc. No company has unlimited funds, not even Microsoft or SoE. They are expected to work within a budget and an established level of staffing. So, I'm sure that additional folks are hired to work on expansions, but that does not keep them from taking the brightest and the best and putting them on developing new code vs. fixing bugs. I can tell you for a fact that the guys doing bug fixes are the new junior developers that have no clue as to how the code was originally written. Happens that way at every software development company I've been with. A senior and experienced developer does not want to fix code, they want to generate new code. Make them fix stuff, other than in an emergency, and they will leave.

Also, don't forget, working on a new expansion is work that is being done on "overhead", meaning the company does not realize any revenue until well after it is released to the public. Therefore, these types of efforts are investments and financially risky, so companies tend to limit their financial exposure by doing them as cost effectively as possible. That means holding to a release date even if everything is not finished, in order to start generating revenue and recover some of the sunk costs, and using internal resources as much as possible so you don't have the expense of bringing on a lot of new folks.

Whatever the corporate focus is, is what will get the most and best resources. And in this case that is developing new, money making expansions, not fixing the existing game.

That is just the way it is. I just wish they would take a short break from grinding out expansions and focus on existing content, that's all. But being a businessman, I know that will never happen, I guess the gamer in me is hoping for the impossible.
