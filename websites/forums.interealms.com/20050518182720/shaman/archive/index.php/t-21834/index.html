BlargX
Ever since Nixx first posted extensive play by play on his solo dragon kill in Velious, the game has been swamped with shamans. Shamans were really fairly uncommon until then.

Because of the massive influx of shamans into the game over a period of years now, and the ability to trade and sell toons, it really doesn't matter much what Sony does with shamans from this point onward.

Not that they intend to do anything anyway.

But unfortunately, they have no motivation to bother. (No short-term monetary reasons that is -- the only type that appear on their radar screen.)

It takes very few active shaman per guild to support a raiding guild, and much of their tasks can be automated, that is, "botted." The fact that many shaman have quit playing floods the market yet further with buyable or borrowable shamans to use as bots or instantly just pick up and play at high level, fully flagged.

The truth is, the game has reached a state where shamans are so plentiful and at the same time the need for them is so completely trivial that people won't need to make any new ones for a very long time -- perhaps till the end of the game's lifetime. Nor will they need to be much improved or developed; they merely need to be botted to pick up any particular spells that might come along.

Sony won't be impacted by how few shamans start up or by how many of us quit, just like they haven't been by how many have quit already. There are acres of old accounts out there for guilds to farm, and new ones adding to the shaman acreage every week. And by design, a little is already plenty.

So Sony has no pressing reason to make the shaman a character of equal overall utility to the other classes now. There's therefore little chance we can expect fixes or improvements of any significance, because without that utility, what is there to improve or fix that really matters? What falls apart that matters once you trivialize the class?

By design, the shaman "problem" was a problem that neatly solved itself.

I do not understand the notion that shamans are currently well balanced because we can do many things poorly.

This falls in line with my comparison of shamans to farm tractors in a different thread.

Shamans can do many things given enough time. So can a farm tractor. Hitch a wagon to a farm tractor and one can conceivably do anything with it, from get groceries, take the kids to school, go club hopping, or pick up the new sofa. With a farm tractors traction no amount of snow, mud, or almost any other road hazard could be a danger. However the speed at which a farm tractor can operate makes it impractical for day-to-day chores. A large four-wheel drive farm tractor with a wagon is too cumbersome to operate in crowded traffic, and an absolute danger to others on fast highways.

The shaman is a very strong class in the long term. But what good is it to be the one in the party with the most manna left if one's heals are too small, one's dots are too slow, one's dd is too small and slow, ones rains are always resisted, or one's pet is dispatched in a single round? Any long-term advantage is insignificant, if one is unable to contribute meaningfully in the short term.

There was once a time when we were true jack-of-all-trades, in the sense that we could heal in most situations, or when our best dots were considered significant damage. We were strong enough in the short term to pick up the slack in many roles, and pull things out of the fire. Our rapid manna recovery combined with our ability to do things poorly did make us balanced, then. But now, all too often, cannibalization, plus our poor quality skills just means that when the party dies, were the corpses that had the most manna left.
