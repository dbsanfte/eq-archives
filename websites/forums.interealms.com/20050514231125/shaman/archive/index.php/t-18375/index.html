Shiuna
So, I've been reading a lot of rants lately about the price of our spells in Bazaar, mostly along the lines of "it's not fair!" or "I shouldn't have to spend 100k on my lvl 60 spells" or "those vendors are ripping us off!". I haven't heard a single complaint about the cost of Bane at 650 adventure points. The reason is that the currency to buy Bane is different from the currency to buy stuff in Bazaar. LDoN has its own economy separate from EverQuest.

What most people haven't recognized yet is that Bazaar is also a separate economy, and has different currency. Unfortunately this currency has the same name as the currency in EverQuest, so people are confused. To eliminate this confusion I'm going to start calling Bazaar platinum Blat, which is different from EverQuest plat.

All our spells are free for the taking, either by killing the guy thats got 'em or killing the guys that have the research components to make 'em.

But Shiuna, you say, that takes too long, I have to get a group together and go to dangerous places and kill hundreds of monsters to get the right one to spawn and it might not have the spell I want. OK, then just buy it in Bazaar. But Shiuna, you say, I don't have 35,000 Blat to do that.

Ah...In EverQuest, as in Real Life (tm), it's Time Versus Money.

The official exchange rate is 1 Blat = 1 plat, but we can estimate the real exchange rate by looking at some typical transactions. If you go stomp a spiderling and get 2 spiderling silks, you can sell them to a vendor for a few silver each, but if you take them to bazaar you can easily get 5 Blat each, for an exchange rate of about 1 plat = 100 Blat. If you make silk thread from them and combine them with a stinky low quality pelt you get a leather padding, which sells for about 25 Blat, for an exchange rate of about 1 plat = 750 Blat. So, adding labor to raw materials improves the value.....Time versus Money.

Unfortunately you can't just take plat to Bazaar and expect to trade for Blat at 1 to 750, you have to take stuff there, stuff other people want and are too busy (or too lazy) to get for themselves.

But Shiuna, you say, those vendors with all the spells are lvl 1 players who have never been outside the Bazaar, they just sit there day after day buying and selling, they never adventured for a single spell, they're ripping us off!

Ah, they may be lvl 1 EverQuest players, but they're lvl 65 Grandmaster Bazaar players. They know the rules, they know the items, they know the prices, and they know what you, the unsuspecting novice Bazaar player, will pay. So, if you don't want to adventure for your spells, then you'd better learn to play Bazaar.

Well, Shiuna, you might ask, how did you get your spells?

I learned to play Bazaar. I made leather padding, blessed fishing poles, heady kiolas, whatever other people weren't interested in making for themselves, and sold them for Blat. I memorized the list of spell components that I needed for all my 50+ spells and prowled the Bazaar for them. I watched the shaman spell market for months, learning the prices, learning the players, watching their strategies. I bought low and sold high, and, except for 2, I've filled my spellbook from Bazaar at a net cost of zero.

Wow, Shiuna, you might exclaim, that sounds like something I can do. You've really given me some hope that I can eventually get my spells. Will you teach me how to play Bazaar???

Roxxanne
This is an interesting musing on the economics of EQ. Your "plat" currency is really what an NPC vendor would pay, while your "Blat" is the fair market value in the bazaar. Your theory is that the difference is related to adding value with time invested, either in trade skill work or in time waiting for a buyer in the bazaar. But really, the difference is arbitrary in the sense that vendor prices are arbitrary. For example, you have Jaundiced Bone Armor which has retained an inflated "Blat" price of 300-500pp each, because that is a little more than you can get selling to a vendor. Or Blue Diamonds, that sell for about 290pp since you can get 238pp for them at a vendor. In those two examples, the fair market value in Blat is distorted (propped up) by the unusually high vendor price.

In most cases, I would say time and effort is simply factored into supply. For example, a Jade Mace drops off an easy solo mob (Drolvarg Capt in KC), but is a very rare drop in a place most farmers get no exp anymore. So the price is higher than the utility of the weapon would otherwise dictate.

No point to my post really, except that the dynamics and distortions of the economy are kind of interesting to watch.
