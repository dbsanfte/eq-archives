One thing that most people don't know is that there is a verified link between dental problems and many other physical ailments.
I took care of an elderly woman for 13 years (my mother-in-law) until she passed away last year. She had a massive stroke and needed alot of care. During that time, I did alot of reading and found out as much as I could to be as good a care-giver as I could be. One of the things that I found out was this:
Several years ago, it was discovered that elderly people who had recently had any kind of dental work done were at a significantly higher risk of having a heart attack. When this was looked into, it turned out that there was a much higher rate of serious illness within a few weeks following tooth extraction or other oral surgery. Not just for the elderly. For everyone. Apparently, the bacteria which resides in the mouth can compromise other parts of the body when it is introduced into the bloodstream to a much greater extent than was previously thought. In fact, most of the current thinking is to discourage the elderly or infirm from any non-essential dental procedures.
The reason I am posting this is to help you to understand that your dental problem is not just alot of pain and possibly failing a test. Infection in your mouth is dangerous. The danger can lead to infection in the sac which surrounds your heart. There are other possible complications. Take tooth problems seriously. Make sure that the infection is not present when the dentist/oral surgeon removes the damaged root. Generally, if there is still alot of swelling and pain, the tooth is still infected and should not be removed. A good dentist will give you antibiotics to bring the infection under control before he removes the infected tooth. If the antibiotics don't bring the infection down, they will switch to a different antibiotic.
The most important thing is to find a dentist who comes highly recommended. There are alot of incompetent dentists and doctors and your health is important enough to not accept whoever happens to be on duty at a clinic.

Dannin, a few words of advice from a soon-to-be M.D.
1. Hagle has a point. 7 shots is overkill, especially for a single nerve block (your one tooth). Tolerant or not, it still should not take that many shots of Novacaine to block the nerve. And if your dentist is telling you it can't be numbed due to an infection, he/she is mistaken. Basically anything can be numbed with the right anesthetic in the hands of someone competent.
2. About this infection, Laynee has a point as well. Dental infections are nothing to joke about as they can lead very easily to systemic infection (septicemia) and worse, infection of your heart and its valves (endocarditis). However, in most healthly, young individuals, without a history of heart disease, this risk is much lower than that of Laynee's mother-in-law. But nevertheless, it should be looked at and treated appropriately.
3. Your blood pressure (BP) of 174/98 is impressive, but not in a good way. I'm guessing your age to be <25 because of your visit to the school nurse/history exam. That is a significant elevation of BP for someone your age. If your tooth was absolutely killing you at the time, then maybe it can explain the pressure, but just in case, something else you might want to have checked out.
4. No insurance. Ouch. Don't know what else to say. Try for public hospital ERs, but expect to wait.

I feel your pain, my wisdom teeth are still healing, and Tylenol just isn't cutting it. :D

Stress echo = pharmacologic stress test + echocardiogram

In other words, it's usually done by having you lay on an exam table like you would for a routine echo, however they use certain drugs to increase your heart rate for you, so they can run the echo and watch the EKG for changes while your heart rate goes up. It allows them to do two tests at once essentially.
