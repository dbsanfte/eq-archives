Kieran
Part of the problem is that the EQ protocols are designed to be extremely firewall-hostile. That's not deliberate, just that playing nice with firewalls isn't something anyone at VI cares about.

Firstly, the various zone and world servers allocate their UDP ports and match up their local port bindings with remote clients in a way that means that they WILL use port addresses on their severs that are associated with well-known attacks.

Secondly, for most of the sub-protocols in EQ the client releases its ports the instant it's no longer interested in listening to the server, but while their server is still sending to the client (rather than going through a coordinated disconnection). This generates lots of traffic to an unbound port every time you log in, or zone, or whatever, and personal firewall software then has to decide whether this is a probe or not - which, because of the way they use ports, can look like a well-known attack.

Thirdly, they are constantly patching the containing applications, and most personal firewalls check to see if applications change so that they can exclude applications that have been compromised by viruses.

None of these things VI do are strictly necessary; individually they wouldn't be so bad, but all three is the worst-case scenario for personal firewalls. Dealing with over-eagerly unbound ports is something personal firewalls can sort-of cope with gracefully if they remember the most recent application/port associations for a short time; but the rest are really up to VI, and are not going to be fixed ever.

Because of all these things, the easiest - not the most secure, but the easiest - way to make NIS work with EQ is to add global rules to permit particular Sony hosts to send any traffic to your machine. That means eqlogin1.989studios.com, eqworld-XX.989studios.com, and so forth - if you play on a range of servers,you just have to consider opening the entire VI netblocks.
