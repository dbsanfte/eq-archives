Envy
Function: noun
1 : painful or resentful awareness of an advantage enjoyed by another joined with a desire to possess the same advantage

Now that we understand the definition of envy, let's talk about where to draw the line between legitimate comparison across classes and envy. I think really the key word here is "resentful".

When we make a comparison like:
Mob X does 290 dps, after a slow, which is mitigated to partial.
Clerics can heal 3275 / (3.75 + 2.25) = 546 dps
Shamans can heal 1300 / (3.75 + 2.25) = 217 dps

We have something useful here. A clear statement of how one of our abilities is failing - even after a slow (not counting the time at the beginning that a mob is unslowed) we are unable to keep up on healing, let alone catch up. There is still a comparison here, yet there is no class envy.

The problem with class envy is that half of the argument is likely to be erroneous. Statements are being made based on a shaman's powers and the *perceived* powers of other classes. It's exactly the same with SOE looking at the shaman DOT's and assuming that shaman have great damage potential. They do, in theory, but in practice they don't. And you have to be a shaman in play to see why. The same tends to occur when one class looks at another.

What this means is when a class comparison is used as the basis of an argument you have to be very sure you are being incredibly fair, complete and accurate. Otherwise it allows people to find weaknesses in the comparison and on that basis invalidate the argument.

The variety of a shaman's duties, and the fact that he must do certain things first before doing either a heal or a DD or DOT, greatly factors in to reducing the efficacy of any shaman heal or DOT or DD from what it might easily be perceived to be.

Having to malos and slow, sometimes having to slow multiple times, or add in a malosinia too, means that shamans won't be able to apply heals, DOTs, or DD's for a long time. It's quite clear that this makes better DOT's pretty irrelevant, but it's also fair to keep sight of the fact that it tends to put in perspective -- or it should put in perspective -- the fears of other classes that with better nukes, shamans would really compete with and even threaten the desirability of mages or druids, and with better heals, they would be competing with or threatening the desirability of druids and clerics. With all the time shaman have to spend slowing and debuffing mobs, and then actively using canni(rendering them unable to do anything else, even move to follow their group!), shamans simply spend a lot of time all tied up. Better heals and nukes look far less majestic when you're too busy debuffing/slow first off and then too busy canni'ing back mana afterwards to do very much of either. Sneak in a heal or DPS, or sneak in a canni?

Shamans constantly have to make that decision on how to spend their generally slow casting spells. No matter what improvements we get, the window of opportunity to even use those improvements is substantially smaller than the windows of opportunity that classes that are not forced to debuff up front and canni afterward are forced to deal with.

Before other classes add up our "massive" potential to do DPS or heal even if we get great spell upgrades, and then conclude we have it impossibly, unforgivably good, they would do well to remember how infrequently we're really able to use them.

Sure, some classes can chain nuke or heal...but if the mob is over half dead by the time it's slowed and shamans need to spend some time canni'ing to be ready for the next one, how are shamans going to find the time to do chain-anything in the first place.

I can foresee improvements in the shaman class being leaped upon in a panic by other classes as if they turn the whole world upside down -- so it's important that they understand that all the abilities in the world don't really mean much if you only have the time to use them here and there.

If I can get a mob malosed and slowed and then get back mana in time, sure, maybe I can fire off another spell here and there. But only after I do my own duties, by which time mobs these days are starting to get to perilously low health and the encounter is rapidly nearing its end. Considering I have to actively canni back mana, the very design of a shaman's duties and abilities structurally limit his healing and DPS no matter how good his heals and DPS are. Kind of like leaving your wallet at home -- the waiter doesn't want to hear about how much you COULD have paid for the meal. It's what you've got right in your hands that you can actively put to good use that matters. Time will limit a shaman's contributions enough that even remarkably better heals or DPS potential from a shaman on paper will rarely work out to be terribly impressive in actual gameplay. Healers and DPS will be hard pressed to be threatened much by a shaman's ability to do either.
