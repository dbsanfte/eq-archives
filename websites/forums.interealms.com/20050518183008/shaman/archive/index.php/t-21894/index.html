Blazz
One of the biggest problems we face right now is that if you ask 10 Shamans what needs to change you may get 12 variations of 17 differnt answers. Shamans are probalby the most "Jack of all, Master of none"class in the game, and as a result, there are a lot of different playstyles and ideas on whats "best" to fix us. Murtak wants to heal. I want to melee and nuke. Others want bigger buffs. Some want better debuffs. Obviously, there is NO POSSIBLE WAY to keep everyone happy unless . . .

. . . we find an alternate solution. Here is what I propose.

AA. Expensive ones. Expensive with big pre-requisites. Here are some examples:

Healing: Cost = 10, 20, 30, pre-requ = MAXXED all healing AAs, + maybe SCR and SCRM, and a couple Gates ones. Result = +200, +300, +500 on Daluda's Mending, or the new OoW heal (pre-focus, pre-crit, pre-aa, straight addition to the original spell).

DPS: Cost = 10, 20, 30, pre-requ = MAXXED FoM, Gates FoM, SCR, etc. Result = +200, +300, +500 to Spear of Torment (or the new OoW poison fast-nuke). Again, pre-aa, pre-focus, pre-crit.

Buffing: Cost 10, 20, 30, pre-requ = SCR, SCRM, couple Gates ones, whatever, lots. Result = +100 hps, 2% melee damage, 100 attack; +200 hps, 4% melee damage, 150 attack; +350 hps, 6% melee damage, +200 attack to Focus (or the OoW upgrade).

Debuffing: Cost and pre-requisites = you get the idea, Result = an additional -10 resist modifier and -50 attack, -15 resist modifier, -75 attack, -20 avoidance (mobs), -30 resist modifier, -100 attack, -40 avoidance, (whatever, GOOD MEANINGFUL debuffs, not jsut -100 STR and AGI, thats lame) to Balance of Nihil (or the OoW slow if there is going to be one).

Here is my reasoning behind this. This game used to have the player base seperated by levels. Leveling form 45 to 60 wasn't a simple matter, took time and patience. Now, getting to 65 is a relative joke. Instead of levels seperating the player base, we need another alternative. Zones is one way, but it sucks to have HALF the zones unavailable to the players who CHOOSE not to raid. The answer, IMHO, lies in AA.

The difference between a lvl 65 with 300 aa and a lvl 65 with 25 aa is staggering, even more pronounced than the difference between lvl 60 and 65. This applies across the board. The lvl 65 with 25 aa is LIKELY fairly balanced in his current play enviroment. However, the 65 with 300 aa in a Time + guild is struggling. This doesnt only apply to Shamans, its fairly universal across a number of classes. So my solution is to make tools available to the more "advanced" characters, the ones who need it NOW, but not make them completely impossible for the more casual non-raiders to even achieve.

Granted, a Shaman who went and did the 700 aa needed for ALL these powerful upgrades would certainly be goddly. But honestly, do you care? At that point, that Shaman is LIKELY to be at the very tip-top of the games content, the Final End Game of the current expansion. So what if he can solo Grummus. Seriously. I can solo bigol former "Raid level" mobs now, but I usually don't bother because I am busy with other things that actually matter.

Make it expensive to choose these "trees". Make it hard. But make it doable and accessible by anyone who wants to dedicate THAT kind fo time into it. I mean, we are talking about MONTHS and MONTHS with some of those examples I proposed.

Anyway, thats my idea. Thoughts, comments and flames welcome.

The idea of advancement being tied to time is a terrible one.

The chance that anyone would buy any game if they knew that was its premise up front is next to nil. Just try to imagine anyone picking up a box saying that the main way to advance in a game is to spend more time in it.

Advancement should come through the application of intelligence and perhaps intelligence unorthodoxly applied. Being smart enough to do things well and also finding out the shortcuts, the way to do things the best way possible in the shortest length of time, is the challenge for me. Speed is an essential ingredient, and directly ties to the intelligence of the player as an objective measure of his ability to accomplish. Merely spending time is no accomplishment at all; it's how you spend it and how little of it you need to spend to achieve your goals. A game is not a longevity contest. We're all going to die anyway, and I don't have to come to a game to do it in real time.

A further challenge comes in some games from putting my knowledge and insight and attempts to be clever and deceptive to work against others in some games, like say poker or chess.

Merely plodding along using up time doesn't figure into my picture of mastery at all, and shouldn't be particularly rewarded. Any fool can just use up all his time.

Indeed, in both games and sports, it isn't merely practicing that makes you accomplished; it's practicing the RIGHT way. Using the right training and drills is not only better than using the wrong ones; using the wrong drills can keep you at the same level forever, or even make you worse the longer you do them.

Yet though merely using up time is neither fun in itself nor challenging in the least, it should be made the major focal point of a game?

What kind of accomplishment is that to celebrate -- that one merely spent more time than the other guy? In every other situation I can think of, the more time you spend getting somewhere, the greater your failure.

In games, I like that you start from an even playing field, where everyone can excel. The rules are the same whether you're young or old, rich or poor. The fun and accomplishment is available to everyone depending on their skill and application.

Starting from that even playing field, I like to work at outsmarting other people and surpassing my own limitations, gaining knowledge and skill as fast as I can. If I'm great at a game, it will go faster. If I'm not, it will go slower. But in no case will it be about time directly; it will be about my abillity to apply myself and achieve over time.

Tying time closely to achievement is a recipe for boredom. I doubt anyone will ever move a single game box off a shelf by telling them that their cleverness and intelligence will rank distantly behind their ability to just put in hours.

That's the opposite of a game, or fun. That's more like a dead-end job.
