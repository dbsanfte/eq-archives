I was thinking today about how culture changes come about in the businesses that I've worked for, and trying to view my desires for changes to the Shaman class through that lense.

And I guess all these thoughts bubbled forth after stopping by the planetside forums. I played that game from beta through live, and then about 6 long months, before I finally gave up in disgust. I checked back at about the 8 month mark, and the same problems remained. Big ones too. For instance:

1. Doors wouldn't open, causing your entire team to get wiped out cause you couldn't take cover in a base.
2. Lag. Big battles, its major selling point, were unfeasible due to server performance. I mean, you'd run forward 30 feet and then warp back 10 feet. Really bad.
And of course 3. Balance issues: X is overpowered, Y is underpowered... a change would happen, now Y is overpowered, X is underpowerd, etc.. standard cycles of power shifts.

So anyways, what I noted when I checked the planetside forums a week ago was this: A) The major problems were fixed, B) the 'class' that I played that used to be underpowered was now considered overpowered.

The whole overall feeling on the forum was more one of gameplay tactics, thoughts about this or that, etc.. What was now missing was "I hate this game I'm cancelling" or "If the vanu are not buffed up I'm leaving", etc..

So basically, a massive amount of change, for the better, occured just past the one year mark. My dates might be off, but thereabouts.

I guess what I realized from this, was that it takes way more time to fix something major, from a business standpoint, than someone immersed in the product is willing to wait.

When I was playing planetside, and the reason I quit, was because the changes didn't occur in 6months.

I'm a programmer. I often work on year long projects. I then oversee those projects in production, and modify them over time. Typically, it takes me over a year to make major changes to system rules and 'balance' within the system. Meeting the needs of marketing without hosing the needs of billing, for instance.

I guess I came to realize that its not that it takes me a year to make the actual coding changes to better my systems. Its that it takes a year for the business cultural to A) Identify the problem B) Come to terms with the real problem (A and B are always different from the customer and programmer standpoint) C) design a plan to address the problem D) work towards consensus on the plan to address the problem E) go about implementing the solution F) deal with new problems that crop up when you implement the first solution.

The whole point of this thread is that it feels so intensely WRONG what the system is doing when you are using it. In my case, it was planetside for 6months. It felt like an eternity. I couldn't fathom why they couldn't fix things. So I quit.

But, I give myself that same time in the real world.

So I guess my point is this: I'm sure SOE has heard us, I'm sure a shift is brewing for shaman, but I'm sure its going to take at least 6 more months, maybe 8 months for it to occur. Thats assuming that the content in Oow is similar to GoD and our problems will remain.

So the bottom line is this: Shaman will get better, but are you prepared to wait 6-8 months for a major shift to occur? I am, now that I started thinking about what SOE is probably doing internally, and seeing that through the eyes of one of my projects.

Now, on the other hand, if I'm completely mistaken, and SOE thinks we're fine then I'd be dumbfounded. I mean... they can't truly think we are ok compared to the other priests, right?

Ungkor, I agree with your analysis. Fundamentally it applies to lots more than just programming/coding. That said, consider this:

A) SOE has not admitted that there is a problem. Having not admitted, we have to presume they do not have a (any) shaman problem(s) identified. So, I would add a pre-A condition to your process: recognizing there is a problem which leads to A (identifying.)

From the shaman player side of the fence, I can't believe they are not aware there is (are) a problem(s). But lets be practical here. Either SOE (1) doesn't recognize there are serious problems with shaman class development paralleling game development, or (2) they realize there is a growing gap between game design and shaman functionality and are dealing with a priority list that shaman are not on, or (3) the fantastical is true and SOE is phasing shaman out of the game.

IMO, I think it is (2): SOE knows shaman functionality has been trivialized. Prior game changes caused this - none of it forseen. Had game development continued along the lines of Velious and even PoP shaman would still sit on the top of the slowing food chain. Shaman would still be the weak link in the healing chain. Shaman would still be minimized in their buffs. I say top slowers, marginal healers and minimal buffers.

But, where was the next expansion going to go? What were other options for GoD (and soon to be OoW) that expanded the game difficulty? Shaman arguments would be longer fights - which SOE has already 'decided" was not what the player base wanted. (Frankly, I disagree. Players will do what ever it takes to progress. Longer fights would not matter - if it was the progression path.)

SOE blew it long ago when shaman were given Turgur's. It overpowered the game so the game had to be balanced around it. Clerics were upgraded to compensate for the additional mob power needed to compensate for slow. Druids were upgraded simply to provide them with viability for groups. This template can only go so far though. Eventually you either have to have encounters where clerics can instantaneously CH in a chain to compensate for massive melee damage or you have to re-think the template. SOE's re-thinking was to minimize (mitigation) and remove slow while still increasing encounter difficulty to the point that clerics need to spam heal to compensate.

Of course this tossed shaman to the side. Tied in with the balancing of melee classes in the last few expansions to provide them with gear that stat maxxes them (marginalizing shaman buffs) and the minimal level of shaman healing abilities and ... well, here we are.

So, when you say "Identify the problem" you have to consider that in SOE it is apparent that shaman were the problem. Other classes have gone through the same situation. Late in Kunark rangers were all but cast aside as a class. Rogues were gimps. In PoP warriors were extremely minimized - as the hey-day of the paly/sk shined on.

Changes came to these classes. Slowly. In some cases, marginally. In some cases at the cost of other class viability (warrior changes and game changes in GoD also slammed paly/sk.)

To wrap this up without further detail, I think, using your process analysis, shaman were the problem. That problem has been fixed. What to do with shaman - what new role to provide shaman - is in the review process. That review process involves letting the game development play out and see how players find the ways to play the game the designers can not anticipate. To let players find the brick walls designers are not aware of. To then find solutions that require specific class abilities and provide them predominantly to shaman, secondarily to others.

While I see the events yet to unfold differently than you, I think we are looking at the same process. In my view the process is stopped at C: design a plan to address the problem (as we are discussing shaman.) The identified problem being: where do shaman fit into the game (without slow.)

For those who will say SOE doesn't think there is a problem, I point out, from a PR standpoint SOE can't officially recognize a problem as extensive as the one shaman are facing. When they say, "shaman are working as intended" they are being honest. That they don't say, "shaman are working as intended, but their abilities are not contributory to the current game direction and we are reviewing how to make them viable again" is simply because they can't say this ... officially.

SOE changed the game which has excluded shaman power from contributing. In time SOE will change shaman to fit back into the game. It will not be as slowers. It will not be as buffers - as we know that role today. It will not be as healers because the game has enough of them already. In the meantime, welcome to being the red-headed stepchild of EQ.
