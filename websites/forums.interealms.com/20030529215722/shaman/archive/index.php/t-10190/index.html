If you get people who honestly want to play better and make themselves and others happy, and don't have ego problems, you can make a fast friend in pick up groups by giving them little hints here and there in tells, answering their questions, etc. Key is to have a positive manner and expect them to be very nervous and self-conscious sometimes, because at first they will think any comment is just criticism designed to be mean or snotty, not designed to help out.

It helps a lot to handle as much as you can in private tells so the person isn't feeling held up to ridicule in public. That take a lot of the ego pressure and confrontation out of things. Like I noted above, casually popping your epic'd higher level character of the same class around to group buff everyone seems to have a -- heheh -- "calming" effect sometimes on people who think that since you're not playing a shaman atm in their group, you couldn't possibly have the slightest clue what you're talking about. And if your advice is very clean, simple, and concrete(not just "do better!"), a little trying out what you say will prove to some of them that you really are helping and your intentions are good.

Some people really like hearing stuff they don't know. Telling someone how to do a hotkey for canni-dancing can be doing them a big favor and really wowing them sometimes -- not only are you telling some people how important canni is and how to canni-dance, but giving them concrete info on how to put it all on a button is really useful stuff that will make them glad they listened to you.

You might even find yourself getting tells on your shaman or other character asking for advice or helpful tips from a guy who used to seem clueless and jerky but now, with a little push in the right direction, seems like he's going to be one of the better additions to the shaman class. (There are a LOT of bad shamans out there giving the rest of us bad names.)

Kinda like when a monk tells and shows another monk how to pull a certain area optimally. You could just grit your teeth or bark at all the people around who screw everything up(and then let them train you for the next two weeks), but sometimes some of them actually would LOVE to learn, even if they're a bit on edge about getting advice at first cuz so much of what they get is smart-ass remarks instead of something clear and helpful.

KEI = Clarity 3 + WIS/INT buff that lasts 2.5 hours or more and has a group version. Chanter invites you to a group, casts on himself, and even a level 1 now has incredible mana regen. For a 75 plat donation, you eliminate virtually ALL downtime for most casters.

KEI is drastically changing the lower levels (under, say 45). Mana regen is no longer an issue, a KEI'd lvl 35 shaman might as well melee because he's not going to need to med or cannibalize. And the plat cost is easy to justify since the kill rate goes up enough to generate the cash even in the mid-30's.

My own story about relatively clueless newbie shaman is from the other point of view. In my low 30's I grouped with a warrior at the FM giant fort. His main was an Oracle, and he gently asked me why I was not slowing the giants (I wasn't). My reply was that they were dying pretty quickly and if I slowed all of them I wouldn't have the mana to keep Quickness up, so I chose to keep Quickness up on all the melees and kill faster.

Now, at the time all I had was Tagar's, which is 35-50% slow, and there were like 4 melees in the group. Looking back on it I was arguably CORRECT in doing it the way I was doing it - after all, Quickness was lasting for 3-4 mobs and slow tends to wear off when the mob dies. I'd have to know more precisely how fast my Quickness was, how long the mobs were living, and the relative mana costs of each. But it definately got me to thinking, and when I got more advanced slows I was much clearer on when to cast them and when not to bother, because I'd done that thinking.

So the point is, the Oracle/warrior gently asked me a question and accepted my answer, but did make me think and helped me out later.
