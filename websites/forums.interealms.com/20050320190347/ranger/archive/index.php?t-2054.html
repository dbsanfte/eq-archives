It does sound like there may be a pattern, and therefore gounds for a discrimination suit. However, unless it can be proven that employees of another race and/or sex were NOT fired under the same circumstances, it can be very hard to win.

It varies by state. In some states, like California, you can only fire someone for cause, and there are very strict regulations as to what you have to do to write them up, etc. In Colorado, where I live, you can fire someone without warning, for cause or not, as long as you are not disciminating by race, sex, religion, handicap, etc. Colorado is an at-will employment state, meaning unless there is a signed contract at the time of employment, there is no presumption of duty prior to termination.

On the moral side of the argument, perhaps you want to take your talents elsewhere. If you feel they are discriminatory, the best thing you can do for yourself is get out and find work elsewhere.

There are several factors to connsider when pondering issues such as this.
One: Union or non union
Two: Right to work laws
Three: Company policy
Four: Company history
Five: employee history
Six: State laws regarding Labor Regulations
Seven: Federal Ragulations on Labor

South Carolina is a Right to work State. Most of the state laws are on the side of the employer, unless superceded by federal law.
Case in point. The store I woprked at until tonight had a policy that we were held responsible for any shortages occuring on our register during our shift. We were also held responsible for drive offs for gas. Most of the time, they would not look for an over ring or a mis-count of the safe drop to find where the shortage occured. I know. Had it happen to me. I was told I had a 118.05 shortage but was not told when it was. I ewas also told I would have to pay it. I refused on the grounds that I didn't take the money, they couldn't PROVE I took it (there is video surveilience) therefore they could pretty much stick it. They TRIED to hold my paycheck. That didn't play either. What they failed to realize was Federal law states that a person CANNOT be paid less than minimum wage. had I paid that shortage. So, to your question "did they do something wrong?" Maybe. depending on the factors I listed. They may have done a MORAL wrong but in all likelyhood they broke no laws. Employers tend to be smart about how far they can push issues
My suggestion is to read your state and local labor laws. Also look up federal laws. Almost every bit of that info is available on the net. Read it. Study it. KNOW your rights because you never know when you may have to enforce them. YOU are the only one that will stand up for you.

How can being a few minutes late in a grocery store be a huge concern?

Typically in a high volume store(this could be true in lesser volume stores as well) a new clerk goes on the clock at a certain time. At the same time another clerk goes to lunch, and the first clerk relieves the second, whether they are checking or stocking. If the first clerk is a few minutes late and the store is busy, the second clerk can't leave until they are relieved by the first clerk. When the second clerk returns from their 30 or 60 minute lunch, then they will return a few minutes after the time when the next clerk is supposed to leave.

The problem compounds itself. If a clerk leaves the checkstand before being relieved you can get unhappy customers, unhappy customers can equal less customers. Less customers means less work needs to be done in the store and too many people on the payroll. Then people get terminated or layed off.

A few minutes may not seem like much, but as the employer where do you draw the line? 3 minutes? 5 minutes? 10 minutes? An example, the express line check-out, i.e. "10 items or less" A customer comes through the lane with 10 items, no problem. A customer comes through with eleven items, what do you do? You let them through, being nice. The next customer comes through with 12 items, what do you do? I was given a warning for not allowing a customer with 14 items go through the express lane, while there were customers with just a few items each waiting behind her. Where do you draw the line? I would say you draw that line at ten items just like the sign says. You draw that line at the time that is written on the schedule.It is far easier for every person to show up and be ready to work at the time they are scheduled.

These may be extreme examples but it illustrates how a few minutes can affect the store.
