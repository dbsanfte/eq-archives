Gloriana
Greetings all,

We wanted to pass along an update on our work on EverQuest classes. We are still gathering data from many of the logs that we have now enabled, however discussions are still occurring and progress is being made. We wanted to answer a couple of questions in the meantime to keep you all up to date.

Why do you need all this new data, don't you log everything anyway?

While many things in EQ have always been logged, others have not. When we decided to begin the process of evaluating class roles in groups, we found that some of the data we wanted was not available, so in our last update we added many new logging features. We found that some of the logs had to be adjusted to get us better information and we have made the necessary changes and plan to push them live with our next scheduled update. At the loggings completion, we will have mountains of accurate, server gathered data that will need to be digested and organized.

The EQ team stated that they wanted to evaluate group roles, what does that mean?

We hear from some classes in the EQ community that in a group situation, they are less desirable than other classes. That's not a fun position to be caught in when you are playing a game that relies upon groups for most encounters. This prompted the team to sit down and took a hard look at what roles there are in a typical group (Tank, DPS, Healing, Debuff, Crowd Control, Pulling, Buffing are some examples but there are others) *Note that these can be broken down into sub groups and debated endlessly, however for the sake of this discussion let's settle on these for now*. We then identified which classes filled which roles and numerically identified how well they filled that particular role. After this exercise, it was plain to see that some classes filled several roles well and others performed in a secondary or tertiary role only. This came as no surprise to us, but we felt it was an important first step to formally identify where we felt classes are today so we could get a better perspective on how much each class would need to change to help bring it in line with the other classes in EverQuest.

Does SOE consider every role to be of equal value to a group?

As the game has changed and evolved, the roles have shifted in their value. For example, the advent of longer buff timers means that a buffer might not be as needed in a pick up group as a DPS class is because the buffs can be acquired by players in a safe zone long in advance of actually reaching the content that requires them. This is another tricky part of this puzzle that we are sorting out. We are looking at the group roles and intend to make sure that each role is necessary.

When do you plan to release your conclusions and make changes?

When we have conclusions, we will release them. The end result of all this may be a few minor tweaks, or it may be some vast sweeping changes. We are far too early in the process to state what will be changing. We will make any changes slowly and with your full awareness. We will put things on test for you to mull over and consider and gather your feedback as we do. This is very much a cooperative effort between our community and our development team.

The EverQuest Team

Brenlo Bixiebopper
Community Relations Manager

It takes a noticeable amount of work to prepare this. The dots selected need to be specific to the situation: which means you have to reconfigure the dots spellset for the specific occasion. To do that correctly, you must de-mem all your spells, load only the two or three dots you want for the situation you are facing, and then save that spellset. Then you need to re-mem the rest of your spells.

Once you put in the time for the preparatory work, though, execution time is amazingly fast. One key and you have memmed the debuffs. One key and you have memmed the dots.

On horseback you draw zero aggro doing this. Grouping indoors when there are adds, or soloing, you need to lock down the mob(s) first, but you would do that anyway. All you need is one tick of successful crowd control, and you hit the hotkey and you have switched to dps.

When you need to recast one buff, it goes into slot 9. After you have cast it, it gets auto-dememmed and replaced with a dot, if you are using four dots; or it gets ignored if you are using just three dots.

When you need to recast three buffs, they go in slots 7, 8, and 9 (with a spellset, of course :)) You cast those buffs, and then hit hotkey 9 or 0, depending on what you need to do next.

Once you are habituated to this pattern, it becomes quite natural to tap the hotkey for debuffs at the beginning of each round, swap in the dots, rebuff as needed, and repeat.

Well, the basic concept is really simple in my opinion. Imagine a zone in the next expansion. It has the usual 10 new MOB models per expansion. In past expansions the basics for all MOBs were pretty much the same for the entire expansion. Sure, DPS and HPs depend on the zone and the level of the MOB but generally speaking most OoW MOBs mitigate slow, deal pretty high DPS and have moderate to low HPs.

Instead of this imagine some golem type MOB actually having 10 times the HPs of your average trash MOB. He deals a lot less damage though and is fully slowable. Then you have, say, some lizardfolk MOB with a flaming sword. He does a lot more DPS then the golem and has a nasty DD proc but he has few HPs. He mitigates slow but can be snared and rooted and does not summon. Then you might have a caster MOB that nukes for 6K a shot every, oh, say 7 seconds, 4 of which are casting time and he can be interrupted with stuns. And finally add a healer MOB that has 4 pets which are not all that tough but are unrootable and of course he heals and cures them. There should be more distinct MOB types but these 4 should suffice to illustrate what I mean.

Each of these MOBs favors certain classes. Each of them can be easily defeated using a specific tactic (or one of several that work) but is a hard fight if you do not or can not use said tactic. The golem favors slowers and endurance classes. The lizard requires burst healing or snare + nukes or root + DoTs. The casters is pretty weak if you can debuff his MR and stun him. And the healer with his pets can be AEd, mezzed or taken out in one shot by a Shadowknight.

Now you mix all these MOBs. The zone would have areas where you find mostly golems or mostly nukers but not exclusively. Say you have a castle in which you found 60% of the nukers, 20% golems and 10% each lizards and healers. The area right outside the castle features 40% golems, 40$ healers and 10% each nukers and lizards.

You could go with a cleric, warrior, enchanter and 3 random DPS classes. You would do fine on the nukers with your 2 stunners, be good against the lizards and healers and possibly suffer somewhat on the golems. Or you could go with a shaman, paladin, druid and again 3 random other classes, say a magician, beastlord and wizard. You would still be able to kill each of the MOB types but you would have some problems with the nukers with only one stunner and each of the lizards would probably cost you a lot of mana for heals. Where group 1 mezzes the 4 pets of healer you just AE all of them so you probably do a little better and you do fine against the golems. Overall though you would probably be better off outside the castle so you get more golems and healers.

If you do this with more and more varied types of MOBs you end up with a lore more variance during a single experience session, you end up actually rewarding groups that have 6 different classes and you also enhance the immersiveness of the game. A golem should have more HPs then an imp. A wizard NPC should do more damage nuking then meleeing. And you should have to do different things in different encounters. Why do only raids get interesting encounters, yet in experience groups you get to kill each MOB in the exact same way?

Now all you have to do is to be consistent in regards to what MOB does what over zone boundaries. Then over the course of an expansion you introduce more and more MOB types. In OoW you could have 90% of the current MOBs in Dranic's Scar. In the castle structures however you could add some of the golems and maybe a single nuker. People would wonder, then figure out a way to beat them efficiently. In Noble's Causeway you also have some of the healers with 4 pets roaming around. And in the Bloodfields you introduce the lizardfolk.

All it takes is a little work and creativeness on the part of SOE. And what they get in return is more immersiveness, more variety, easier class balance and probably more fun for everyone.
