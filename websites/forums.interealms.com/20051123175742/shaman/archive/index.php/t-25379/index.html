Basic description:

A Paladin’s main focus is defense and damage absorption. Able to withstand much punishment, the Paladin’s longevity is marked by the ability to utilize healing spells, access to the best armor type: Plate, and several key spells which sustain their life and the lives of others. A Paladin has access to many types of weapons, most notably usage of the shield, various 1 handed weapons and 2 handed weapons. Paladins have no access to ranged weaponry or offensive spells for that matter (Not considering talent specialization or trade skills).

How a Paladin Operates:

A Paladin is a melee oriented hybrid class primarily wielding three types of spells in their arsenal: Auras, Blessings, and Seals/Judgments. An Aura is a group buff that the Paladin emits up to a certain radius, with him or her operating as the center. A Blessing is a short term, typically 5 minute buff. Seals and Judgments are lumped together because they operate synergistically: A seal is a self only; 30 second beneficial buff that when triggered with a spell called:

Judgement - Retribution, 9 mana, Instant Cast, 15 sec Cooldown, Lvl 4. Unleashes the energy of a Seal spell upon an enemy. Refer to individual Seals for Judgement effect.

is transferred into a debuff on the hostile target. For the most part, only one Judgement, Seal, Blessing and Aura per Paladin can be in effect at any given time. I cannot, for instance, have two different Blessings on me and my group; both cast by me. I can emit one Aura at a time. I can only have one seal upon me and only one Judgement upon a hostile target at any given time. If there are two Paladins in a group, one can emit one Aura, and the other can emit another. They do not stack, That Paladin can cast a Blessing on me, and I can cast a different blessing on myself and him/her. We can both judge a hostile target with different Judgments, the same ones do not stack. Since Seals are self only buffs, we cannot exchanges those upon one another. You can tell when a new aura has gone into effect by the graphic effect occurring under the Paladin’s feet. Seals usually have a graphic effect that floats up above the Paladin’s head. Blessings have the standard casting animation.

As you would suspect, Paladins use the judgement system to achieve a sort of combo benefit in accordance with their attacks, active seals and auras. Here are some Seals and their related Judgement effects that are common to PvP:

Seal of the Crusader (Rank 4) - Retribution, 75 mana, Instant Cast, Lvl 32. Fills the Paladin with the spirit of a crusader for 30 sec, granting 85 attack power. The Paladin also attacks 40% faster, but deals less damage with each attack. Only one Seal can be active on the Paladin at any one time. Unleashing this Seal's energy will judge an enemy for 30 sec, increasing Holy damage taken by up to 80. Only one Judgement per Paladin can be active at any one time.

Seal of Light(Rank 1) - Holy, 95 mana, Instant Cast, Lvl 30. Fills the Paladin with divine light for 30 sec, giving each melee attack a chance to heal the Paladin for 39. Only one Seal can be active on the Paladin at any one time. Unleashing this Seal's energy will judge an enemy for 30 sec, granting melee attacks made against the judged enemy a chance of healing the attacker for 25. Only one Judgement per Paladin can be active at any one time.

Seal of Righteousness(Rank 4) - Holy, 80 mana, Instant Cast, Lvl 26. Fills the Paladin with holy spirit for 30 sec, giving each melee attack a chance to cause 20 additional Holy damage. Only one Seal can be active on the Paladin at any one time. Unleashing this Seal's energy will cause 47 Holy damage to an enemy.

Why Paladins are called "Cats":

Paladins have been nicknamed "Cats" on various PvP servers for their ability to withstand tremendous amounts of punishment and practically reset the fight, as if they had "9 lives". Let's take a look at those particular spells used to achieve this feat.

Notes on the "Time Out" Spells:

Divine Shield and Divine Protection both run off of the same 5 minute Cooldown timer, so the Paladin will have to choose the one that best fits the situation. They both have an orange-yellowish bubble effect around the Paladin when they are in effect. Usually, a Paladin will activate one of the "Time Out" spells to get out of sap, snare-root type effects, polymorph, and to buy them time to heal. While under Divine Protection, a Paladin is most likely running or healing themselves. They cannot attack or stun you. They can however cast spells on you, most likely a Judgement of some sort. They must be within melee range in order to use this ability. If the protection bubble is up and they are attacking/stunning you, then they have activated Divine Shield. They probably feel confident in winning the fight seeing as they used it, or they are attempting to finish you off, hold you off until reinforcements arrive. Blessing of Protection does not have the same effect as Diving Protection/ Divine Shield. If you do not see the orange-yellowish bubble and the Paladin is emitting "immune" signals from attacks, they can be assaulted with spells.

Examining a Paladin’s PvP weaknesses:

Ranged Combat

One of the glaring weaknesses of the Paladin class is the total lack of any sort of ranged combat. A Paladin has no natural means by which he can attack or pull anything that's more then a few steps away. Some Paladins attempt to make up for this deficiency by taking up engineering and using some of the items made in the profession. Other's use other items found in the world. 90% of all Paladin’s are totally unequipped to deal with a ranged enemy. At the very bottom of the Holy Talent Tree is an ability called Holy Shock, which is 320 mana, Instant Cast, 30 second cooldown, and does 205 to 219 Holy damage. This ability requires 30 talent points to have been invested in the Holy Tree before it is purchasable. Obviously, even with heavy talent specialization, ranged combat is not an option for a Paladin.

Running/Kiting

Paladins have very few tools to keep a Player from getting away from him or her. A Paladin has no other option other then staying within melee range to maintain any sort of pressure. Paladins have two spells they rely on to keep an enemy player around for more punishment:

(Again Considering up to level 35)

Hammer of Justice (Rank 2) - Retribution, 50 mana, Instant Cast, 1 min Cooldown, Lvl 24. Stuns the target for 4 sec.

Seal of Justice - Protection, 33 mana, Instant Cast, Lvl 22. Fills the Paladin with the spirit of justice for 30 sec, giving each melee attack a chance to stun for 2 sec. Only one Seal can be active on the Paladin at any one time. Unleashing this Seal's energy will judge an enemy for 30 sec, preventing them from fleeing. Only one Judgement per Paladin can be active at any one time.

Depending on who their up against, some Paladin’s like to use Hammer of Justice early in the fight to get the advantage and make the enemy player panic. Others prefer to save it for the first attempt to heal themselves, or the first attempt for the enemy to heal themselves as a guaranteed interrupt. Still others prefer to try to end the fight with it, preventing the player from fleeing out of melee range. It's important to note as well that the range on Hammer of Justice extends to about melee range and 1/2.

As for Seal of Justice, this seal usually comes out with fights against casters, or anyone the Paladin hopes to interrupt often. If an enemy begins to run at mid to low health and the Paladin can manage to stay within melee distance, you are guaranteed that Seal of Justice will come up in an attempt to thwart your melee range leaving plans. It's good to note that the ability to improve the potential proc rate of this seal is available in the talent trees. The "preventing them from fleeing" portion of the Judgement version of Seal of Justice does nothing to prevent an enemy player from fleeing in my experience.

Shallow Mana Pool

Without getting bogged down by endless comparisons, I can safely assure you from personal experience that a Paladin’s mana bar has no sense of endurance. Roughly speaking, a Paladin’s big heal will eat up about 1/3 of their mana bar. Throw in some seals, aura changes, judgements and a purify here and there, that mana is gone in no time. If Lays on Hands is available however, a Paladin usually won't panic, instead redouble attempts to engage you in melee combat to break your morale. As they approach low life, they activate Lay on hands, draining them of all mana (what mana?) and at later levels even restores a bit of mana. Most people who start off attempting to avoid melee combat with the Paladin fall for this trick. They want to end the fight so they come into melee range "for the kill". This is the biggest mistake. Before you know it the fight has reset and you're struggling to get out of melee range. At this point the pressure is off of the Paladin to heal and protect himself and on you to get out of his melee range, which he or she will not give up without passionate resistance.


Why Paladins win in PvP fights:

Simply put, Paladins are built to last. They managed to keep up with the enemy player and whittle them down to zero life. This isn't done quickly. There is no barrage of attacks or one big blast of death. The Paladin managed to engage the enemy player in prolonged melee combat, the Paladin had ample mana to heal himself and utilize his invulnerability spells as needed. Paladins deal slow, consistent damage in melee combat. You'll never see a Paladin win a PvP fight in which the enemy controlled the distance of engagement. They are not dangerous in melee combat from the damage they deal, but from all the benefits they can reap from their auras, seals, judgements, and blessings. They need an enemy to engage in melee to reap those benefits properly.

Why Paladins lose in PvP fights:

Paladins lose in PvP fights when they cannot utilize their powerful blessings, auras, seals and judgements on an enemy because they are consistently denied close combat and/or mana when it matters. Melee and mana is the catalyst for a winning Paladin. If the enemy player consistently controls the distance of engagement then the Paladin cannot kill them. If the enemy can manipulate the Paladin’s ability to regen, expend, and use their mana, the Paladin cannot endure for very long. A Paladin will always, always, always make strides to engage in close melee combat. A Paladin will never, never, never willingly allow an enemy to leave close melee range. If they do, it's because they're planning to run, or they're planning to close the distance again very soon.

What to do about the "Time Out" Spells:

Most players start to panic when they start to see the Paladin "Time out" spells come out. This is displayed in the amount of tactics and mana used in the next wave of assaults. You can tell in a fight between a Paladin and some other class that the Paladin is on his "2+ life" because the style of engagement gets more in favor of the Paladin. Every class seems to become a close combat specialist out of panic or frustration as time goes on. What most people don't realize is that the tension works both ways. The Paladin wants you dead just as much as the enemy wants them dead. The only difference is that the Paladin knows it will take him some time to whittle down the enemy players life total; the enemy player just believes that if they use more mana, more combo points, more rage, more anything, then the Paladin will take more damage and perhaps, this time, die. They eventually get desperate or frustrated, engage the Paladin on their terms, and usually die.

In the worst case situation, you have three "Time Out" spells to deal with (Presented in the order most commonly used): Blessing of Protection, Divine Shield or Divine Protection and Lay on Hands. Normally, a Paladin will try to melee you and use the "Time out" spells to get the upper hand again in the fight; be it in health or in morale. You can usually force a reaction out of a Paladin by surprising them (Sap anyone?) or by making them think that they absolutely must close the distance of engagement. If the Paladin senses that you're going to exploit their range weakness, they will most definitely sacrifice a "Time Out" simply to reach you and desperately keep you in melee combat.

Most people don't realize that when a Paladin calls "Time Out" it's a good opportunity to change the situation. If Blessing of Protection is up, they are susceptible to spells. If they use Divine Protection, they are most likely going to heal themselves up and fight on. If they use Divine Shield (You'll know because they're trying to attack you if this is the case) then you know they want to make you panic and use your mana and tricks to recover. If you see a chance to create a distance between you and the Paladin, do so, even if you intend to melee them, especially if they throw up the shields. Why? In effect you are giving them a choice: heal up while invulnerable and then try to close the gap, or maintain the close combat and sacrifice the chance to heal up. Most Paladins are used to getting in one heal while invulnerable while the enemy spends a few seconds determining how invulnerable the Paladin is. By that time the Paladin is most likely loading up a blessing, a seal and a judgement to toss on you for being kind enough to stick around and wait for him to come back to melee combat. Abusing the range deficiency will eventually force the Paladin through all of his shields. The most difficult one to get will be the Lay on Hands if it's available.


Only use ranged combat?:

No, what I'm saying is that running up and attacking the Paladin dead on is what they expect, what they crave, what they're designed to shrug off. By exploiting the Paladin’s lack of range and dependency on mana you force them to sweat, and they will become more desperate to get you back into melee combat on their terms through using up their mana and their "Time Out" spells. Most people's sense of tactics center around running up to the Paladin and turning on attack. They mash some buttons, watch the life totals go up and down, begin to panic and try to get out of melee combat, which the Paladin won't allow to happen at this point. They then die and wonder why they lost. Rinse and repeat a few times and it is easy to see how misperceptions began.


The most effective class against a Paladin:

Understand that the most difficult match up for a Paladin is not from any particular class, but from someone who is willing to be patient, to exploit the Paladin’s weakness of range and systematically deny them close combat and mana. By denying them melee combat at crucial times, you deny the Paladin nearly all of the benefits of his Auras, Seals, Judgments, and Blessings. Those types of people are the most lethal Paladin killers in PvP.
