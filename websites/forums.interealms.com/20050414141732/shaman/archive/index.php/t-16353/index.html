Looks to be your wireless setup. Most mainstream wireless gear is 802.11a/b which runs on the non regulated 2.4gigahertz bands. Here's the most common reasons why you will have trouble with your wireless setup. Work these out and you should be golden.

1. Configs- make sure your setup, configs, antennas and gain are configured correctly.

2. Interference- This is the biggest problem with 802.11. Things that are the most common sources of interference: Microwaves (approx 15-20ft radius when in operation), 2.4gighertz phones, certain electrical devices, certain EMI emitting devices will get in the way, neighbors devices (mostly applys in aparment complexes) and building construction depending on the materials used in your building it might be very tough for 802.11 signal to pass through your first and second story.

3. defective units/cables- the other problem is things like defective units, loose antenna connections, bad CAT5/5e/6 cables or jacks and loose connections.

Good luck.

Are all wireless routers like this?


For the most part, yep. They all suffer from the same vulnerabilities to EMI (electromagnetic interference) due to the industry standards (that 802.11a/b the other guy referred to) and generally use the same configuration schemes.

I honestly am not overly familiar with wireless networking, so i can't offer any advice, but if they're anything like ethernet routers, sounds like one of your ports may be configured incorrectly. Either that or it's just interference problems.

In general, the basic rule about troubleshooting is: it's almost always a physical problem. That is, the problem 90% of the time boils down to wiring or user error. Not being rude, just a simple fact of life =)

yikes. all internet traffic conforms to the same size MTU (maximum transmissioni size), which isn't anything more than the allowed size that a single IP packet is allowed to go up to, which at every single internet provider is 1500 bytes. By them having you tweak it lower its telling me a couple things here:

1. they don't know how to troubleshoot all the possible EMI that could be interferring with your connection, so they.....

2. .....assume you are shredding packets (a term used for when packets of internet data get warped, broken, or fragmented by degraded connections) and so they suggest.........

3. turning down the largest size that your router will fragment packets at, so that you wont shred packets as badly.

As you can see its a poor solution, and you will always be running at a 'degraded state'.

My suggestion? If you live in a apartment complex, or anything like it with close neighbors and know you dont have any EMI interferring devices yourself (microwaves, ovens, some appliances, phones) and you still have problems, then drop a little money and have a wiring guy come and install ethernet in your house. Get a quote- it may be cheaper than you think to get just a couple runs from your upstairs to a central location (where your router is).
