Actually, you couldn't. Sorry, but the law student in me just wouldn't let me pass this up. Since SOE retains posession of all characters and merely licenses the use of the software to each of us, we have no legal right to sell them. Since the original player of the character has no legal claim to the characters, a breach of contract suit (such as false advertising as indicated above) against him would be tossed out at the complaint level before even making it to a courtroom. This just goes to reinforce the age old, but still very true phrase, "caveat emptor," buyer beware.

hmm, yeah this is the stance that Microsoft and other large cooperations would like everyone to internalize. these sorts of one-sided arrangements stand primarily because large corporations have more money to enforce them than small time users have to contest it. once some fancy stepper figures out a means to a nice profit with a nice class action suit on behalf of users, these sorts of agreements will go the way of the dodo... this isn't even adressing the enforceabilty of these end-user licenses in other countries

if you can't legally semantically "sell" your character, you certainly have the right to transfer the fruits of your time invested in the character, sublease "access" to the character etc.

in any case, assuming a seller and buyer agree to a transaction (however it's semantically structured) you'd assume the same sort of consumer protections that you have for any transaction assuming of course the terms of the agreement can be confirmed. it's not like you would sue someone for ****ing your faction and not telling you but widespread fraud on the part of yantis or eisgiest would certainly be actionable

it's important to remember that intellectual property laws are a relatively new (and distinctly American) addition, go back 100 years and the concept doesn't even exist. i think the general dork on the street assumes too great a leaning in principle of intellectual property statues towards protecting the rights of the creator, but really intellectual property is (like all laws) tuned to the general good of society. the buyer/licensee has definite rights also.

it's a GOOD thing that soe retains the legal ownership of the characters, otherwise the burden of proof involving banning a cheater or idiot would be almost insurmountable and soe would almost be precluded from banning anyone at all, but I think any good american would in the interest of fair play stand by the principle that a person has a right to do with their own character as they wish. that's certainly where I net out... legal semantics of the EULA aside
