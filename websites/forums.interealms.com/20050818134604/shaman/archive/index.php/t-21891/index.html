The following is posted here for the elucidation of the Shaman community, and has been sent directly to Brenlo as well, as per his wishes.

I've begun a series of reasonably in-depth parses and statistical analysis concerning shaman issues. I started with strength, because its generally been considered a fairly well-understood game mechanic (thanks to the folks at Steel Warrior for your years of work!) and because its one that is reasonably easy to test the properties of in a small span of time. I've found some interesting - and unexpected - results.

First ... the testing conditions. All data in this parse was collected using my level 65 shaman as a test subject. I wielded the Mace of Confusion the entire time (21/22 1hb). My 1hb and Offense skills are at the shaman maxima of 200 each. In order to ensure that mob defensive properties were not variable, all this testing was not only done against the same type of mob, but the physically exact same instance of that mob (in this case Guard Fintran in North Ro, who conveniently spawned as the amazingly-high-regen version, allowing him to survive the testing). Some might question my choice in the use of a shaman melee combattant (rather than a more "functional" melee class) and my selection of a green-con combat dummy. Selecting myself as the subject was done for testing purposes - because shaman cannot double attack or dual wield, and do not receive a damage bonus, there were less complicating factors that might obscure the effects of strength on damage output. In short, if strength works at all, it should work for me. Likewise, I felt that the choice of a green target was beneficial, as it ensured a higher ratio of successful attacks, and, thus, more parsable data - after all, this is not a parse of potential DPS, but instead an examination of the properties of combat on the per-hit level.

In order to acquire a suitably large spread of strength values, I was unable to hold completely stable all other possible conditions. Notably, dexterity was not held constant during the parsing exercise. It is largely presumed (and there has been no indication from Verant or SOE to believe otherwise) that dexterity does not alter melee weapon damage (its effects on bows, for rangers, are suitably documented at their forum). However, ATK was held constant --to the extent that it was not modified by strength--. That is to say, no items granting a raw +ATK bonus were added or removed during the exercise, and no buffs (such as FA) that provide a raw ATK bonus were applied.

Approximately 15 minutes of data was collected at each of 5 strength values : 95, 170, 205, 280, 305 - meant to provide a varied, but intentionally uneven, spread of tests. This provided between 377 and 647 swings per test (case 3 is admittedly a weak sample size, but fortunately its results are not out of line with the other data).

Analysis 1: Attack Accuracy

I first attempted to discern if strength had a demonstrable attempt on accuracy - the percentage of attacks that are successful, dealing at least 1 point of damage. I theorized before examining the data that it would not; I believe ATK (like AC) consists of 2 components - one related to accuracy, and the other to damage - with the displayed value being some form of compromise or hybrid.

Initial examination of the data appeared to lend credance to this theory. Accuracy percentages at (95, 170, 205, 280, 305) strength were (45.1, 49.1, 50.1, 47.3, 46.9) - providing no statistically significant evidence of corrolation. Concerned that the (presumably) mob-skill-based dodge/parry/riposte numbers were skewing the data and obfuscating a trend, I altered the percentages to remove those instances, yielding (50.9, 56.3, 58.0, 53.2, 55.9), again providing no convincing evidence of corrolation.

Analysis 2: Attack Damage

Here is where the bread and butter of strength should shine, I believed. Or in all honesty, where I expected it to fizzle weekly. My faith in the effects of any stats in this game has been traditionally poor. As the numbers would demonstrate, however, the effect of strength is measurable ... but likewise flawed.

I began with the easiest statistical measurement, the simple mean. Averaging the values of the successful strikes in each attempt, I quickly discovered a visible trend. The average damage from each set was (26.7, 29.3, 35.0, 38.8, 40.3). Some quick manipulation with a regression function revealed a clear trend using an already available value as the constant intercept. D(ave) = STR/16 + 21 (the constant in this case being the damage value of the weapon used, a trend I expect would continue for any weapon without a damage bonus - a hypothesis I may parse later when I have time). The R^2 value (a measure of how accurately a regression line fits the data) for this function is a startling 0.95 (1.0 is perfect - meaning no data noise or flaws at all). What divergence there is from this formula can clearly be attributed to the rounding and truncation inherent in Everquest.

Thus, every 16 points of strength adds 1 point of damage to the average melee attack.

But ... I was unsatisfied. Clearly there was more at work here than a simple linear increase, or there would not be nearly the confusion about strength that there has been in the past. I decided to examine the consistency of these average values - that is to say, how likely it was that any given hit would be an average hit, rather than an extreme value. I did this by computing the bias-adjusted standard deviation for each of the data sets. I expected a reasonably small value for the standard deviation, indicating a fairly bell-shaped damage curve centered around the average values. I was wrong. The standard deviations were (15.7, 17.8, 19.9, 21.8, 20.1). One of the important pieces of information standard deviation provides is the assurance that roughly 68% of data points lie within one standard deviation of the mean (above or below). But - especially on the scale of damage numbers to begin with - these standard deviations were enormous! This means that either the distribution of values was very diffuse (hits near the mean were not much more likely than extreme values) or perhaps even that a reverse bell-curve was in effect (where extreme values are more common, and hits near the mean relatively rare). I re-examined the frequency of each damage value, looking for what was occuring. I believe that what I found is the first flaw in the strength-to-damage system.

Independant of strength, the damage distribution in all five tests has a huge frequency spike at 42 damage.

My first explanation for this value is that it is twice the weapon's damage rating. Substantially more parsing would be needed to confirm this, however. However, the reason for its existence is not as important (at the player end) as the simple fact of its existence. Put in plain English, I am more likely to hit for 42 than for any other specific number on any given swing, regardless of my strength at the time. I cannot conclude from this data set if my strength alters the likelyhood of hitting for 42. I will require substantially longer parses before I can make any determination of that claim. But the sheer magnitude of this frequency spike is shocking - in two data sets, over 17% of my hits were for this value. This value is completely independant of strength, and is hugely distorting. Because this modal value is greater than the mean damage in all of these cases, it is currently working in the players' favor. If the strength scale were expanded, and the mean value were to increase beyond 42, then this artificially frequent value would work against the player, mitigating some of the effects of that strength increase.

Having found one flaw in the strength-to-damage relationship, I began to search for another. And, indeed, there was one there to find. Searching for other unusual data points, like the huge frequency spike at 42, I began to examine the extreme points - those at the very high and very low end of the scale. Naturally, looking at the damage maxima followed shortly thereafter. The idea of the damage maximum is easy to understand, and one that we are all used to in Everquest - it is the biggest number you can hit for under your current conditions. Charting the damage maxima from the first four data sets, I was beginning to see a very nice, clean, linear relationship. Indeed, I was able to fit a regression line to those four data points almost exactly. D(max) = 0.137*strength + 50, for the conditions I was testing under. Roughly every 7 points of strength increase the maximum damage by 1. Although I had no in-game values to support these numbers (50? 0.137??), the fit was astoudingly exact, with an R^2 value of 0.998. In short, that function IS the determination for damage maxima over those four data points. Oh ... but I did five tests, you say? And therein lies the second problem in the strength-to-damage relationship.

Damage is capped.

We're all used to hearing about damage caps, like the one that is lifted at level 20. This damage cap is similar, but it is based on strength. Simply put, the maximum damage parsed at strength 280 is 88 ... and the maximum damage parsed at strength 305 is 88, when the regression line tells us that it "should" be 92. There could be any number of reasons for this problem, from a hard cap in the code set before the stat caps were fully lifted, to an improper implementation of Planar Power, to any number of other code-based concerns. But none of that escapes the fact that the cap is there, in the parsed data.

Conclusion 1: What does this mean for strength buffs now?

Strength makes a measurable - but not impressive - difference. Every 7 points of strength up to 280 raises the maximum hit by 1 point, and every 16 points of strength raises the average damage per hit by 1. Its not a big difference, though. Basically, if you're getting the strength while in the process of getting something else (like gear, Focus, or FA), then strength is a good thing to have. But (assuming DW/DA and damage bonuses do not substantially alter these formulae) if a player attacked twice per second (possible with dual wield/double attack), the difference between a 95 strength and a 305 strength is 26 dps - or roughly the same as adding a shaman's dog pet to the combat. And nothing affects the modal damage.

Conclusion 2: What does this mean for cap-exceeding strength buffs later?

The news here is grim. The maximum damage cap will prevent the curve from growing in the manner it did previous to 280. Additionally, as the mean damage reaches, and then exceeds the modal value (at approximately strength 333), the effect of that strange modal behavior will further retard the damage-per-strength exchange rate. These two flaws are synergistic - and their effects would be increasingly visible as strength increased beyond 333. In fact, I believe that these two factors are sufficient to prevent average damage from ever reaching substantially beyond the modal spike value. If in fact the frequency of the modal value is dependant on strength as I have personally come to believe (that is, if higher strength causes a higher percentage of hits to be modal hits), then being able to push the average above the mode is even more unlikely.

By means of final conclusion ... this data does not bode well for stat buffs, a conclusion that will doubtless not surprise many shaman. The conversion rate of strength to damage is poor. The game's internal math appears badly flawed. Unless both of these were to drastically change - requiring a major code overall, I am certian, and likely altering NPC mechanics greatly - I could not in good conscience recommend a strength buff to any melee character with reasonably high unbuffed strength (~200 or more), regardless of how far above the cap it might be allowed to go.

The problem with this is that the ATK display lies. This is no secret, really - a lot of people have always at least suspected that it does, because the AC display lies.

Okay, to catch up the folks who haven't been following game statistics for four years... =) There are (admitted by Verant, even!) 2 different defensive values in Everquest. One is "mitigation AC", and the other is "avoidance AC". They are used to compute, obviously, mitigation and avoidance, respectively. A lot of stuff - like actual gear - probably adds to both values. Regardless, we have only one value displayed. We have no particular way to know how the displayed AC related to either mAC or aAC - I have substantial reason to suggest that it is NOT just a simple average.

Likewise, I've taken the first step to demonstrating that ATK is also a composite value. Strength did not, to any statistically significant degree, improve accuracy. If anything, it should have been MORE visible in my test than with a melee had it done so, since shaman melee accuracy is poor. But strength did affect the damage numbers (albeit in an minimal, quirky way). While there are several possible explanations for this observation, the most likely would be that there are 2 server-side ATK values. Strength alters dATK, but not aATK, as it were - contributing to damage but not accuracy.

Which means that we can't just compare strength buffs to vengeance increases on even footing ... they aren't doing the same things.

The problem with this is that the ATK display lies. This is no secret, really - a lot of people have always at least suspected that it does, because the AC display lies.

Okay, to catch up the folks who haven't been following game statistics for four years... =) There are (admitted by Verant, even!) 2 different defensive values in Everquest. One is "mitigation AC", and the other is "avoidance AC". They are used to compute, obviously, mitigation and avoidance, respectively. A lot of stuff - like actual gear - probably adds to both values. Regardless, we have only one value displayed. We have no particular way to know how the displayed AC related to either mAC or aAC - I have substantial reason to suggest that it is NOT just a simple average.

Likewise, I've taken the first step to demonstrating that ATK is also a composite value. Strength did not, to any statistically significant degree, improve accuracy. If anything, it should have been MORE visible in my test than with a melee had it done so, since shaman melee accuracy is poor. But strength did affect the damage numbers (albeit in an minimal, quirky way). While there are several possible explanations for this observation, the most likely would be that there are 2 server-side ATK values. Strength alters dATK, but not aATK, as it were - contributing to damage but not accuracy.

Which means that we can't just compare strength buffs to vengeance increases on even footing ... they aren't doing the same things.

I wonder, for stuff like AC, would it go something like [reported AC] = [base mitigation * level modifier] + [base evasion * level modifier] + [raw AC * mitigation modifier] + [raw A *evasion modifier] + [AGI effect] + [AC effect], where AGI spells adjust base evasion, and AC spells affect base mitigation. Equipment AC would then adjust the two values based on factors in class tables.

So what would affect offense? if it was based on the same idea then it would look something like [reported atk] = [base damage * level modifier] + [base accuracy * level modifier] + [raw atk * damage modifier] + [raw atk * accuracy modifier] + [STR effect] + [DEX effect], where STR affects base damage and DEX affects accuracy? Damage and accuracy modifiers being determined by class tables of course.

If the equations do look something like that I would think that AGI and DEX buffs would have difficult to spot effects, while AC and STR might be more apparent, particularly if damage given/taken is constrained by DB/DI restrictions.

It would take a lot of data to separate all of that, it would be much easier if Sony would just hand over the algorithims :D
