Kinedorf
In my opinion, the most specialized classes are the easiest to play. Clerics heal. Warriors hit autoattack and taunt. Wizzies wait until the mob is at x% health then nuke. I'm not saying that these classes cannot be challenging or enjoyable just that it is clearer how these classes can contribue well to a group. I've found that the less specialized a class is, the contribution to a group is more heavily influenced by player skill. The examples given above (e.g. bard, necro etc) are classes that have a wide breadth of abilitieslots of utility and they can influence the group immensely by the careful use of an ability. With regards bards, I have utmost respect for several people that I know who play bards but remember that being busy does not necessarily mean you are good at what you do. Anyone can sit there and mash keys. The question is, are they mashing the right ones at the right times?

Rather than pick a class that is hard to play, I'd recommend picking one you think you will enjoy. Remember that, if a class is hard to play, chances are you will do a bad job initially and this may not be fun, particularly if the group suffers as a result of your mistake. Also take a look at your play style. If you like to solo often, for example, that removes a lot of classes from consideration. If you want to get a feel of different classes without investing the time to create a character and level it up, have a look at the other class sites and guides then have a scan of lucy to see what spells/disciplines they have. This should give you a bit of an idea. Do keep in mind that many classes don't get "class defining" abilities until well in to their progression and this may radically alter a classes play style at that level and beyond.

Good luck!

I have a 65 chanter, so I can speak with experience here. ;)

Charm soloing is actually very easy. Charm mob, guard, pull a victim, root it, do 1 point of damage, then back WAY away and let your pet kill it. Lather, rinse, repeat. Charm soloing is actually kinda boring, and is made even more so with Total Domination AA, which makes it harder for charmed pets to break charm. A wise charm soloing enchanter never allows herself to get into a position where the pet can kill her, usually by ensuring a quick escape route if things go sour. This isn't always possible, but veterans always try to set things up so they have as much an "out" as possible.

Charming in GROUPS is where charming suddenly becomes very complicated. A chanter charming in group has to worry about the pet as well as performing all the normal tasks a chanter performs. He can't devote his attention on his pet completely, and that's bad. Pets have this nasty habit of breaking charm the instant you start slowing the mob in camp. They have this "radar sense" that lets them know when it's the absolute most inconvenient time to break charm, and they go for it with gusto.

Charming enchanters like my alt Auli are paranoids. We believe that everyone is out to get us, including our own groups...because they ARE. ;)

Seriously, being a paranoid is the only way you'll ever get good at charming in groups. Grizzled veteran charmers buff the pet out the wazoo and arm it, and this is where you get the monstrous charm DPS. Vallon's Quickening on a charmed pet with mage proc weapons equipped is just brutal. You have to respect your pet, and also respect the idea that your group isn't respecting your pet. They're gonna do stupid #%#& like break mezzes or just stand there like dorks when charm breaks and you spam the "CHARM BROKEN" hotkey to warn them that you're now dealing with a runaway pet. The worst of them actually complain: "Why aren't you slowing the mob, you slacker? Why aren't you mezzing?"

"Because I'm trying to keep the pet from killing YOU by virtue of trying to keep it from killing ME, dorkwad!"

But not everyone's like this. Many people in high-end guilds have experience with charming enchanters, and when I play Auli I much prefer having these people in my groups, lol. A single well-timed snare or stun pulls agro off me and lets me recapture the pet.

Unfortunately, people who don't have much exposure to charming often don't let me do it. Be it LDoN or grinding in PoP, more often than not I'm told no when I ask to charm. I've even had people disband the group when I asked.

You can't really blame them for being gun shy, though. Charming in a group is one of those things that separates the good enchanters from the great enchanters...and there are WAY too many buff-bot-to-be Enchanters running around on Vazaelle, people way too high level for the skills they possess.

Anyway...

If you're looking for a challenging class to play, then I highly suggest sticking with your Shaman, or playing an Enchanter. Shamans do a LOT with a LITTLE, pulling stunts that most other classes can't come close to duplicating because of the fact that we're "kinda good" at several different things, and our slow is very powerful. Shamans are the toughest of all the casting classes, and bring to the table a varied array of spells that help them. I have to agree with the others...there are shamans, then there are SHAMANS. Those who are very good at playing the class find it the most challenging, because they push the envelope as it were, taking on things that others won't try, soloing in places few others dare to go. The better a shaman you are, the more exciting and challenging the class becomes.

But if shamans don't seem exciting enough, then try an Enchanter. You get your first charm at level 12, and trust me, at level 12 charm is a spell that really pushes your adrenalin levels, because it's rather unreliable, lol. Enchanters thrive in high-pressure situations, and a well played chanter can get groups through things that would kill them otherwise.

But as it was pointed out, Enchanters can be boring if everything's going well. It's only when you're in a group that pulls half the zone because there's an Enchanter in the group that makes them an utter blast to play. Few things are as exciting as seeing 10 mobs come screaming into your camp and knowing that if your skills as an Enchanter aren't excellent, that train's gonna wipe your group.

Anyway, to go back to the original question, a lot of things in EQ are situational. If you are playing, say, a Cleric in a controlled environment, then yes, it can be as exciting as hitting the HoT button and the CH button once in a while. But if your group is trying to do something non-trivial, then you may find yourself juggling 5 different heals, root parking, stuns (if the mobs are stunnable), DA/DB (and when to click them off) and sometimes even off-tanking on low-damage unrootable mobs. Same deal with Wizards in non-trivial environemnts. They are easy to play as long as you know every mob's resists in every zone and can guess your MT's ability to hold agro 100% accurately - and if you are wrong, you get to tank a 1K hitter while wearing wet napkins for armor :)

Generally, I would advise against picking a class based solely on the perceived difficulty of playing it. There are other considerations that may prove to be more important in the long run. For example, if you don't like playing a support class, then don't -- it just won't be fun.

As a shaman become bard, I have to disagree with the people saying bards take the most 'skill'. They are intensive, and due to the way songs work, you're often left with quite hard decisions as to what extra songs to have memmed, (aside from your core 4-5 songs) because, even more so than a shaman, your versatility is both a blessing and a curse.

I've been in groups where I've been puller, CCer, slower, snarer, and haster. (thankfully, these are few and far between) Shaman don't quite have the same utility, and the advantage is that most shaman spells last longer than only 18 seconds, giving you more breathing room. In all honesty though, if you're not pulling, and not in charge of CC/slows, things can be quite boring and require very little skill. Sing your 4 songs. Melee the mob. Keep singing. Repeat ad nauseum.

It's really the situations, and how you handle them, that make a class difficult. (as people already stated) It's much easier to say which are the least hard classes to play, because you can simply look at the more one-dimensional classes, where they don't have as many tools to deal with out-of-the ordinary situations. (wizards, warriors, mages, clerics?) Despite the many problems with shaman at the moment, they can still save the day when everything goes ass-up, and it all comes down to how 'good' you are at playing your class.

A good shaman/enchanter/bard/druid/etc can save the day, because you've just pushed your class to the limit. A mediocre player might not even understand where to start in order to save the day, and this is where the 'skill' is. It's all about knowing your class.

P.S. Most bards don't use gamepad macros when it counts; macros are restrictive if you're in a fluid situation where you need to be constantly adjusting what you're doing. This is the same reason I never actually use my horse on my shaman; it's a PITA to interrupt a spell. On the other hand, I have a gamepad macro designed specifically for Rathe if I'm on keeper duty, or if I'm the 'clerics bitch' bard for a raid. :D
