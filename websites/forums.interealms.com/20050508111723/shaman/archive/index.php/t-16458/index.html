Hail,

Lots of people have given you good information so far.
I have been an officer in two excellent guilds, and I'm very much enjoying a brief respite right now completely untagged.

There are good officers and bad officers, just like players. Bad officers can hurt a guild in SO many ways & yet the guild keeps them as such for just as many unfathomable reasons, much like many organizations IRL.

To directly answer your question, I would also ask in what ways you could improve & seek the advice of others who know you better than we do here. I would also ask if your leader thought promotion would be a possibility in a few months if you did show enough improvement. Definitely ask yourself why being an officer is important to you. You don't need the rank to be a leader. You can do more for your guild with your actions than any title will give you. People ambitiously aspiring for promotion generally weren't the type I was eager to promote most of the time, though there are always exceptions.

Things I specifically looked for when considering promotion candidates.
1) Seflessness.
2) Dedication.
3) Playtime (both total hours per week and specific to the time of day).
4) Diplomacy.
5) Level-headedness
6) Demonstrated raid Leadership ability/knowledge.
7) Player Skill/Knowledge.

I've voted against officer candidates for any of the first 6 reasons on more than one occasion. I have advocated promoting someone who did not have #6 but was obviously making strides & had terrific qualities that the guild needed in other areas. Not every officer has to be a good raid leader, but it certainly helps. This isn't as important in a family/social guild, where traits 1 & 4 may be more important.

Take a good hard look at yourself. Are you looking for an officer position to allow you to do more to help the guild, to become part of the 'inner circle' (which can sometimes just be a smaller bitchfest circle :p ), to make loot decisions, influence policy, or something else entirely? Why was it important enough to you to directly ask your leader (which sort of put him on the spot). I didn't care for the answer he gave as you have relayed it btw.

I would also echo that not every skilled player needs to be an officer. Otherwise you would have too many Chiefs and not enough Indians, and raids can be stalled while 40 members wait for 20 officers to make some basic decisions.

Being a guild officer is ALOT of extra work, usually involves a HUGE amount of your time (preventing you from getting exp or pursuing personal goals whatsoever most of the time) both in and out of game, and oftentimes isn't very rewarding by any usual measurable standard. If the intrinsic enjoyment value of leading a group of people is something you are after though, a guild officer position can fulfill that when other opportunities IRL may not be available to you. Most of the time though, being an officer can just be an exercise in herding cats. :D

Take care,

Hail,
As a follow-up, trust as Steelbear mentioned is also very important, which I would definitely include in my list. As an officer I generally had full access to guild vaults and numerous player accounts (so many I had to have a special notebook for them).

You CAN influence the guild. Your ideas CAN get implemented. Use private messages (out of game) or e-mails to existing officers. Find out IG who would really be receptive to such stuff, because not everyone is. The leader's inbox is probably overflowing w/ all sorts of stuff so he might not be your best 'suggestion sounding board'. There are plenty of people who are ready to complain when things go wrong. Be one of the few who instead of whining makes good suggestions. Be part of the solution! Saving your suggestions for e-mail or Private messages prevents it from being lost in /raid spam or from getting ignored by officers who are busy w/ IG stuff.

I had a few regular members in my last guild that I actively sought out their counsel, and I was their conduit to the Officer forums. They'd give me a suggestion via Private Message, I'd examine it and if I thought it wasn't worth pursuing I'd thank them for their input and tell them why I didn't think it would work. Sometimes that would lead to a back-and-forth PM discussion that ended up with a more workable idea. Either way, GOOD ideas would make it to the officer forum for discussion. On several occasions this ended up changing guild policy on certain matters - and it would have never happened w/o that member's PM'd suggestion. In a few other cases it saved a potentially huge fiasco from getting out of control & prevented drama (drama=bad). By the way, a few of them ended up becoming officers eventually - it was just a matter of spots opening up & their inputs over time almost automatically put them on short-list.

Raid Leadership ability is important, as is the ability to take criticism w/o getting hostile about it. But you can still make a huge difference in your guild even without leading raids. I can sympathize with your wanting to be "in the loop" for how decisions are made. Your raid leader is obviously sensitive to how busy a shaman can be, which isn't always the case. When I am leading raids, I can be an awesome shaman or a decent raidleader, but rarely can I ever manage to do both at the same time. Usually I will put myself in a backup slowing position & just do MGB Fot7 when I'm leading more than 5 groups. If I try to do everything as the lead shaman while also leading the raid, the raid progress generally slows WAY down.

If it makes you feel any better, I purposely steered several excellent players AWAY from being officers when they showed interest. Mainly because the inner circle politics itself can be very exhausting in addition to all the other work involved. I didn't want these people who I depended on during raids to get wrapped up in officer things and eventually leave the guild over a matter non-officers had no clue about. Kind of a "what you don't know can't hurt you" thing... The irony here is that's exactly what happened to me recently. I left over a dispute over moral issues in leadership that had nothing to do with (or was visible to) any of the regular members. Integrity>guild.
