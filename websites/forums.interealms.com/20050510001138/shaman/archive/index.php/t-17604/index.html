Do not worry about root-rotting just yet. Pull with slow, let the mob hit you, then start casting your dots. All shaman dots stack, every one of them, even the upgrades to previous dots. For instance, tainted breath will stack with all other poison spells. I'd say stay in Kunarc for solo exp at level 25..or perhaps old world like rathe mnts (cyclopse's) or the karana's (aviaks).

Make sure you have enough mana/hp to kill said mob w/o medding then
Slow him, let the first round of melee go off, then cast all the dots you can stack on him. Then start poking or bashing him. Reaply dot's as needed for quicker effect. Canni down, regen up (you do have fungi, right? :) ) and repeat. I still use this tactic in non-PoP zone's at level 58.

Now..once you get 34, you get dogdog, who is, and always will be, your best friend, and your best DOT :) Learn how to pet kite, believe me, you'll love it. I was solo'ing red HG's in rathe mnts at level 34 w/o kei, and with crappy gear, when i got Dogdog. :)

Jinheim
Playing a shaman is pretty simple. First you debuff mobs, then apply heals where needed. If no heals are needed, nuke and dot your little heart out. Keep regen on yourself at all times and use cannibalize all the time. Keeping your health around 90% and cannibalizing whenever it gets higher than that works fine. Just don't let yourself sit at full health, otherwise you're not getting maximum mana regen.

Other than that, there's nothing major to know about playing a shaman. It is just little nuances that you pick up along the way, and tehcniques that you master. If you already played a high level character (you said you had a druid, which is a priest and really pretty similar to a shaman), then you shouldn't have any trouble at all picking it up. Knowing general concepts about gameplay is more important than being a complete master of a class. I've boxed every class there is and done a decent job of it, even though my shaman is the only high level character I've ever had. This is because I have (or at least I like to believe I do) a massive knowledge of game mechanics and such.

If you really want to improve your skills as a shaman, do this: Group. Soloing is easy, because the only person you have to worry about is yourself. There is really no manual dexterity involved, like there is with a kiting class. It is just knowing what spells to cast and what to do when stuff goes wrong. In a group, though, you have to be able to respond to the actions of your groupmates. There is a nearly infinite number of combinations for groups and they all function differently, even if the differences are subtle. The more you group, the more tricks you'll learn for keeping your group alive and running smoothly. Experience is everything. A lot of people will tell you to avoid the kunark cycle (LOIO -> FM -> OT -> DL -> KC) but I don't really buy that. The low to mid levels are really more about teaching new players how the game works. If you already know this, then there's no reason not to level through these boring levels as quickly as possible. A nice dungeon romp every now and then is definately good for you, but you shouldn't feel that just because you aren't in a dungeon, you aren't really playing EQ.

Basically, I'm pretty sure that things will turn out just fine. If you are aware enough of your own performance to come here and make this post, then obviously you aren't an idiot who automatically believes that just because his group isn't wiping, he's doing a good job. If you are that self-analytical, then you will undoubtably grow up to be a good shaman. Try and ask yourself questions along the way, like: "Why did that go wrong? Is there a way for us to tackle this situation that we haven't thought of, given the tools that I have? Is there a more efficient way to do this?" Basically, just try a variety of things and make observations whenever you can, and try to group whenever possible. Don't be in too big of a rush, but don't feel guilty for wanting to shoot through the lower levels. Good luck.

My experience playing a shaman seems to be a little different than many. I never grouped until I was about level 36. And after that I hardly soloed. I’ve never owned a JBB. At 62 I don’t have my epic, or even my SoW boots from the epic quest. And most of the time I had little better than K-Mart quality gear. But I still managed to reach my 60s and I had a lot of fun doing it.

I created my kitty shaman when Luclin first came out. I played him to about 15th level or so and then shelved him for several months while I played my ranger and druid to 51st level. So I don’t remember much of his early days. When I started playing him again I hunted bugs in North Karana and then moved to gorge hounds in East Karana. From there I killed Netherbians in Netherbian Lair and when they started turning light blue I killed Greyhoppers and Stonegrabbers in Marus Seru. After experience started coming too slowly there I returned to my old druid hunting grounds and killed Ulthorks in Eastern Wastes. I was still hunting there when I started grouping with a friend and my solo days came, more or less, to an end.

My method for soloing remained basically the same throughout all my levels. I would find a safe spot where nothing wondered or spawned, buff myself up, med or canni my manna back and head out for a victim. I’d double check for adds and then slow or DoT the mob. I usually used slow unless it was a long pull and I was afraid slow might wear off and I figured the Dot would do some damage on the run back to my safe spot. Once I got to my safe spot I’d wait for the mob to catch up, god I love SoW, and root it. Then I would slow it if I hadn’t already and pile on my DoTs. If rood still held I’d med and wait for it to break. I never had to wait long. Then I’d beat it about the head and shoulders while the DoTs did their work.

Your first slow may not be overly useful in a full group, but it makes a huge difference when soloing. I remember being amazed at how much slower the mobs hit me. And once you get your pet the fights will go even faster. Be sure to give your new puppy his fangs before using him in combat. A couple of rusty daggers will do, allowing him to duel wield. I always had my puppy guard my camp and positioned myself so that he would bit the mob’s butt when the mob attacked me. That way the mob couldn’t dodge any of his attacks. Puppy can also distract the mob while you back up and heal yourself, but don’t let him tank very long. The poor pup just isn’t up to it yet.

This is the best time to work on offence, defense and to practice your spell skills. Offence isn’t all that important later on, but defense is. As others have already told you, slowing a mob tends to tick it off and it often wants to express its displeasure in up close and painful ways. With a high defense and good armor we can take a fair amount of hits before we have to worry. Well, that’s true until you start fighting in PoP or going on major raids where the mobs start hitting a lot harder. And I can’t stress enough the importance of practicing your spell skills. It can be boring, but at least you don’t need to cast on a mob. Just pick a low manna spell for each school of magic and cast it over and over and over again until you max out your skills. But since you played a druid I’d guess you’re already familiar with how that works.

Once I started grouping everything changed. I became much more like the druid I used to play. I went from up front tanking to sitting in the back buffing and slowing. At first buffing can be a pain because there are so many different buffs to keep up. And of course they drop at the worst times, like right after a bad pull when you have little or no manna left. But unlike my druid I never just sat and sat, waiting for my manna to come back. I was always doing something. If I wasn’t at full manna, which I rarely ever was, I was canni dancing during every spare moment. When a mob was pulled I slowed it. If I had enough manna I would also cast a direct damage spell or a DoT, depending on how long the fights lasted.

As you gain levels your job will became easier. You’ll get group spells so you don’t have to cast on everyone separately and at 49th level you’ll get Harnessing of Spirit that’s three buffs in one. And as you get better canni spells you can regenerate your manna quicker. I just recently earned enough AAs to buy canni five and between it and Torpor my life as a shaman has become infinitely easier and a lot more fun. The only time I run low on manna now is just after a really bad pull.

There are a lot of tools you can buy to make life easier. If you can afford a JBB then by all means buy one. I watched a friend solo his shaman with one and it really does make a difference. I think I’d still be willing to camp for one to wear, if just because I’ve wanted one for so very long. If you find yourself grouping in outdoor zones I would highly recommend buying a mount. I spent 10k on a little green lizard, Pokey, and I’ve never regretted it. It makes a huge difference in manna regen and being able to sit without inviting every mob around you to attack can come in very handy. I would also recommend buying KEI once you hit 45. You don’t have to have it, but it make life much easier. If you can get any kind of manna regen at your level it can’t hurt. One of the big things about KEI is that it lasts so long. But if you can get clarity then it will certainly be a help for however long it lasts.

How you play your shaman is not set in stone. We each have our strengths and weaknesses, depending on our experiences as we leveled. I spent the vast majority of grouping time with a very capable ranger who rooted adds, a necromancer who stunned adds or a bard who mezed adds. Thus my crowd control skills leave much to be desired. However, I’m very good at what I do. I keep everyone buffed, I slow everything that attacks us as quickly as possible and I make sure I ALWAYS have more than enough manna to deal with the unexpected. I’m also good at keeping my head during bad pulls. I make sure everything is slowed and I heal where needed. If I can’t keep adds rooted I’ll let them beat on me and keep myself healed until someone has the chance to deal with them.

If you pay attention to what’s going on around you and play your character to the beast of your ability I’m sure you’ll make a great shaman. It’s the lazy shaman or those who just don’t give a damn about their other group members that tend to give us a bad name.

As a druid I had to learn kiting, quad kiting, med kiting, root/rot, and all that jazz (and then threw it all away when I realized I like grouping ALOT more than I like solo). Are there any skills like this that, as a shammy I need to know how to do?

Most of these things are based around soloing. Shaman have 3 key soloing methods:
1) IN TEH FACE. Debuff mob, You tank, throw dots, dog eats away at em from behind. Keep yourself healed.
2) Root/Rot. Really, this is only for PoP tier 2+ stuff where it hits so hard it becomes inefficient to keep yourself healed.
3) Kiting. Quite effective when you get your new pet. Throw dots and low level cripple to hold aggro, run around whilst dots do their job and pet nibbles at its heals. Not so viable at higher levels esp. when mobs summon :)

My overwhelming preference is for the first method, particularly as you have the gear for it and at your level its very important to keep your defense skill up. That'd probably be my no. 1 tip - don't outlevel your defense too fast.

For grouping, the hardest thing to learn as a shaman is what to do when the **** hits the fan. Problem is you have so many options its not always obvious what is best. Your top 3 options are usually:
a) root park some adds out the way
b) slow
c) heal

Sometimes I think that a slow just won't be enough to save someone and a quick heal is needed. But then you're soon back at square one again, so this only works if someone else iin the group is gonna save the day. E.g. Emergency heal on the chanter, save them from low health aggro, chanter lives and thus mezzes everything.

Its something that comes with experience more than anything else. Root parking is a key ability of shaman for a few reasons:
1) You can get aggro and thus pull the mob out from the group. Slow, cripple and poison dots are all massive aggro.
2) You can take the hits better than a druid or wizard whilst trying to channel that root through
3) At high levels, the VP AA is an awesome rootparking tool as it can knock mobs right out of melee range, and is also a fast cast.
4) Ornate legs have clicky root, so its always ready :)

The other key thing to learn is how to manage your aggro vs the tank. At low levels this isn't a big deal, but when you start going on raids sometimes you have to land slow on incoming and take the beats (and maybe die), other times mobs are quite resistant and its important you survive in case slow fails - so you have to time the slow so you don't steal aggro, but still get it landing asap. Again, this is just a case of experience - of the mobs, of the tanks, and of your own ability to take the hits.

I learnt timing slow mainly in Kael on Vindi's guards. In the low 50s with average gear I went down pretty fast if I got aggro, and they were quite hard to slow at that level. Obviously at 65 its a different story, but the lessons learnt there are invaluable.

At around 25, my slow pretty much sucks, and in a group that is killing quickly, It just seems like a waste of mana to put casting slow as a MUST. not to say that there aren't circumstances (i.e. someone pulled a red con that is just not dropping fast enough, or the group is getting low on health and mana and we got another add before having time to med, so slow just to keep things a bit safer), but in general, it seems only really useful as a means of pulling agro (which lately I have had to do alot. Not many low level tanks where I am at).

DD spells are useless unless I have extra mana. my dps comes mainly from melee and dots.

Buffing the tanks is my main mana suck, and learning to time my buffs (and keep enough mana around when buffs fall) is a must. Putting them on yourself first is a good way to time your buffs if you are taking some hits. But if your tank is keeping agro well, and you barely ever get touched, it is hard to justify wasting a ton of mana on buffing an whole other player (i.e. you) just to make timing a little easier. Try to learn the length buffs last, and how to refresh them before they wear.

If my health is above 85%, i am getting lazy. If my mana is above 70%, I am getting lazy. If my mana runs dry and I have a decent amount of hit points left, I better have had a heart attack or turned suddenly catatonic. If my mana is dry and my health is too low canni, either the whole group is in a rather @!#%ty state of affairs, or I am doing something terribly wrong.

Watch the health bars like a hawk. usually when your needed for that spot heal is right when you have 30 other things to do. If your getting busy, chances are, everyone else is too. This is when your heals are most needed.

LEARN TO ROOT PARK. I can not tell you how many times I have had to do this, even at level 25. Hell, especially at level 25. At these levels, there are tons of people who haven't been playing their class very long, and this includes your puller.

Casting the lower level resist debuffs to help your fellow casters is a waste of mana if no one is getting resisted in the first place. Learn the zone your are in as soon as you get there, and try to figure out if the resist rate warrants such spells. Eventually, I am sure you will always be casting your lvl version of malo, but in the lower levels it is quite often over kill.

Agility buffs blow my left nut. Unless your fighting a dispelling mob, there is little to no point casting this crap during rotation as it is just wasting mana.

Charisma buffs actually have a place in the buffing rotation -- chanters (and maybe bards. Haven't grouped with enough to know if this actually helps)

Stack your dots!!! I know this is obvious, but alot of times people think their lower level dots aren't worth the mana. But really, at level 25, every dot added helps. If for nothing else, then to curb the regen.

Buy a training weapon. recently (I am an idiot) I found out that 2hb was the way to go (despite my maxing out 1hb and piercings). I got 2 great 2hbs that had procs that worked at my level (per a previous poster's advice) and found that training the damn weapons was killing me. So i did this... I went to the bazaar, bought the 2hb with the absolute lowest attack rate (regardless of stats and damage) and used it to train the skill up. Simply pick a mob that cons dark blue, dot him down to 40% while swinging your training weapon, and then chill around that hp range as long as you can. If you are training a weapon from scratch this won't be hard, as your damage won't be more than their regen. Then when your health gets kinda low, dot them to submission, heal up, and do it again. When you get better, just solo them from 100% down to death. I honestly feel it is worth it to waste some time and solo mobs untill all your weapon stats are full. You never know what your going to get later in the game, so might as well be ready for it.

I don’t feel you are wrong on your assessment, but keep in mind that right and wrong do not necessarily apply to everything we do. Many things are situational and many things are preference based, as in some shaman swear by them and other shaman swear at them. Direct damage spells vs. DoTs is a good example. You’ll find many discussions on this board as to which are best to use in different situations. In my opinion, when soloing DoTs are usually best because they usually have time to run their full course. But in a fast paced group I prefer using direct damage spells when I have the manna to use for damage. The mobs are dropping so fast a DoT is going to do very little damage before the mob falls over dead. Thus the direct damage spell actually does more damage. But as I said, that’s my opinion and I’m sure other shaman handle things differently.

I think its great that you are trying to learn all you can about playing your new class. But after a certain point I think you’ll find that the best teacher is experience. And I think you’ll also find that you never stop learning. There are so many different factors that can change how we handle ourselves, from the makeup of our group to the zone we’re fighting in, that every day is a potential learning experience. But then, that’s half the fun.
