I disagree. You are saying that adding security to the computer(s) involved is "not fixing the problem". Use of those security measures are indeed a part of the solution.

Yes, if he disobeys he needs to suffer the consequences. The punishment should start with removal of EQ/Internet/Computer privileges, exactly how long and what restrictions are a judgement call.

However, the parent can't camp out by the computer 24/7, and the child's already demonstrated a willingness to defy the established rules. So the parent has to enforce the rules in a way that the child can't get around. That's the question the original poster really came here with, and that's where the security measures that we've been discussing come in. AFTER the punishment has been established and AFTER you've established that the child can't or won't obey the rules on his own.

Once the punishment period is over, then the parent can start rebuilding the trust relationship by allowing lightly supervised EQ privileges... with the clear message that any abuse of the new privileges will result in harsher punishment. At that point I'd look into detection mechanisms, rather than enforcement. i.e. I'd want to be able to look at some logs and determine what apps had been run on the computer when.

Eventually, someday, the trust relationship might be robust enough again that the parent's detection mechanism can just be his report card, i.e. if he's bringing home good grades, he can do what he likes with his spare time.
