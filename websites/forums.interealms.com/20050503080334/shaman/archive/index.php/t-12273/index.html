Chutoi
To be perfectly honest, Shandrillar, yes, that's a pretty typical raid for the situation you were in. Some of the earlier posters hit the nail on the head. The speed and effectiveness of a raid is dependent on how well you know each other, and how comfortable you are with each other's ability. The cons you mentioned are things you will run into on every single raid until you find a raiding guild of your own that you like and merge into it. The chances of you getting loot on an open raid are very slim. Experience is abysmal, even with the raid feature. It will be long and slow unless you have a core of experienced raiders that can move things along quickly.

As for the lag issue, there are several things you can do. There is a file in the eq folder than I believe is datarate.txt. I forget the default, but I believe it's 4.0. If you have DSL or cable, I believe you can go to 9.0. If you have a good and very stable phoneline modem, you can probably go to 6. If you are at 6 and you keep going LD, you can lower it to 5 or so. I believe that effects the speed of the packets of information sent, so that can SUBSTANTIALLY reduce lag. Some other methods you can use to reduce lag are the following. Turn all new models off except your own (for me, male iksar) and horses (if you have one). If you don't have a horse, turn all the models off for raids. Also, I recommend you use /shownames 1. That reduces everyone to first names only. You can experiment with /shownames 0-4. Zero is no names at all, 4 is full names, titles, guild. If your graphics card doesn't have to move the full name over every character, that will help. As you noted, turning off spell effects will help a lot. Also go into options and turn off particle effects and make them 0% opaque. That helps a lot too. Turn your clip plane to 0. Set up your filters to only show bard songs and spells in group and if an NPC is attacking you. Turn off critical hits and spells. Turn off others hits and misses.

Those options will substantially reduce lag. In some cases nothing you do will help.

One other tip that has proven very effective for me is to reload your user interface. When you are lagging, sometimes reloading will help. I don't know what buffer it is that fills up, but this has worked pretty consistently for me. If you use the default interface, type /load default 1

If you find a guild that you like, you can really improve your raiding experience. I'd have quit long ago without raids. Frankly, I'd argue that you are getting to the stage where EQ will start to lose some of its fun if you don't raid. That's a personal judgement call, of course.

Most of the good loot is off raid mobs. If you like new toys, you have to raid. My personal reason for liking to raid is just the juggling part. Trying to figure out the speed of heal chains, the dps needed, the mana required for the raids, parsing the damage of the mobs, agro management (some mobs stun/mem blur), etc. There are a lot of things to learn in running a raid, especially on new encounters. As you get in a more experienced guild, the heal chains will be set up automatically, and the clerics will often have 2 or 3 hotkeys for different speed chains. The shaman will know who is doing what and when. As you get more experienced, you'll find you spend a lot more time waiting on mobs to path to the right spot, or more time travelling to the spots than you do just sitting around trying to get organized. Higher levels and Mass Group Buff abilities do wonders for speeding up raids.

You're at a fun point. You have lots to learn, and will probably enjoy doing it. Gonna be some frustrations, but don't give up. Being a part of a team and watching those huge mobs fall is a lot of fun!

I hate to say this but for us shamans, that really doesn't change too much, especially not soon for you. In my experience taking out one tough baddy is done by 3 character types. Clerics, Melees, and Nukers.

The objective of a raid is to do immense damage to the target as quickly as possible and have nobody die. Nukers and melees cover the former, Clerics cover the latter. Even as the highest level shaman on a raid, your job is 1) buff everyone before the fight and 2) slow the mob. After that you really dont do much. You try to spend the mana you have left on damage (through dots or nukes) and patch heals (which really do nothing when a good CH wheel is in place. As a shaman of a lower level you basically have no job whatsoever except for covering the lower level buffs and again patch heals and damage.

However when a raid must fight through crowds of mobs to reach their target, then your job becomes a little more defined, especially after level 58. Your Tirgirs insects along with enchanter AE Mez are one of the essential things for fighting through hordes of mobs. Slow becomes more of an active job when action becomes more speedy.

But to be honest although the shaman's job in raids is without a doubt essential, it's kind of boring.
