Well, if someone asks a price you don't want to pay, that's not blackmail.

I think it's kind of outrageous if they ask for an insanely high price, but the only way you set a price on something in the first place is by asking it or refusing to pay it. That's what a free market is all about. And EQ is definitely a free market, in both the best and (often) worst ways.

If I find prices outrageous at a restaurant or clothing store, I just don't spend my money there and immediately understand that I will not be able to enjoy the goods offered. I might be highly annoyed, but that doesn't mean it's time to start cussing out the proprietor. I'm happy to leave without paying way too much for something, and the proprietor is happy I'm not taking up room that could be filled by some sucker or some clown with so much money that he really doesn't care about the real value of what he's buying.

On a side note, 5k seems a piss low price to offer for something valuable and that is perceived as rare. You might have only yourself to blame for not getting the shard. You could possibly have been trying to lowball him as much as you thought you could get away with. I don't know if you're willing to admit to that, but if you don't feel that was the case at the time, you should understand that the seller might easily have felt that way. He might have been willing to sell it for 10k until you told him 5k.

When I get lowballed when trying to sell items by someone, I immediately double the price on them. Screw them -- I'm not interested in being played for a chump. I'm more than happy to walk away from potential deals that just piss me off. Heck, if you don't, immediately after the sale you have Mr. Lowballer crowing about ripping you off to his pals and telling them you'll cave in right away and that they should spread the word never to give you a fair price on anything.

An item's value also has no necessary relationship to what it's worth to the seller. It's a silly dead-end in the discussion to ask what good the shard would do the seller, whether he kept it and never used it or destroyed it(or maybe used it one day). It acquires value when there is a BUYER. Exactly as much value as the buyer and seller are willing to agree it has. Period.

YOU bring the value to the item by virtue of being willing to buy it. If you aren't, it may well have zero value.

It's like the torpor spell. I paid 85k for mine. People occasionally suggest all shamans should band together and refuse to pay more than a few thousand plat for our spells. Well, there's always a buyer who will pay more. We cannot fix what something's real value is just by saying so, unless nobody ever works around us to pay less or more. That's not gonna happen in an open market.

And you're not going to get items -- ANY kind of items, EVER -- by putting your foot down and sticking to a price someone doesn't want to sell at. Wouldn't it be lovely to think so, though?

So what can you do? Dunno, maybe do without. Lots of folks do. Maybe try a bit of charm when negotiating. Maybe do the work and spend the hours camping the item yourself. Lots of folks try that method. Maybe even pay the price the seller wants.

At any rate, I don't think either a fair or intelligent response to a disagreement in price lies in the direction of becoming upset, or cussing out the seller, calling him names, or threatening his reputation, if you're ever tempted to do any or all of those.

By the way, I tend to give away MQs etc all the time, and have no talent for making an EQ buck, nor do I have the patience. But I am well aware of how a market works, and you never see me cursing out store clerks and cashiers in the check-out line.
