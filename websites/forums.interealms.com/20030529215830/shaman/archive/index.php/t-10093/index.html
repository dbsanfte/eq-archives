I've certainly never been anywhere near as disappointed with an expansion as POP. It has changed what I want out of the game and expect out of it, and probably done more than anything I imagined could to make my EQ experience seem to have clear limits; and even to make me not regret reaching those limits anymore as I've seen the product degrade.

A big part of that is not the fault of PoP directly, but the effect of the game design on human nature; yet I think that effect was probably pretty predictable even without the godlike benefit of hindsight.

The first thing PoP did is confuse my guild and start tearing it up. A number of solid players found themselves facing the same situation I did, and, like I did, left the guild at least partially because of it.

We were a guild that was the second from the top guild on my server; the top guild is nothing compared to an Afterlife or FOH, but at least they were in Vex Thal for months and months before PoP came out, and they were well above us. (Yes, we are an unusually gimp server.) Yet their top shamans looked nothing like the top shamans of the real top guilds. Of course, our shamans looked far lesser still, including yours truly.

We never seemed to push to get ready for the Emperor by farming bane weapons intensively, or by camping shards much. We did a lot of Ssra, but it seemed we were mostly at a dead end if we just did it for odd bits of boss loot that might eventually float into the guild year by year. We did a good amount of NTOV. Once in a while we did the Burrower in The Deep or a rare Thought Horror Overfiend. But I held out hope that things were building to a point at which people would get serious about what the top goal of the game at the time was, Vex Thal.

PoP made clear how splintered and undirected we were. Some people got flagged, some didn't. Some people did the trials over and over and over again(after winning) and chortled and bragged about it, while many of the rest of us struggled to find a space in a trials group -- and some waited long, long hours -- when POP was still new. That had the effect of locking out a lot of people. Same thing happened with the Hedge Maze, only it was almost worse, because getting six people through an event is a lot easier to do than getting 18 people together at a time. It seemed the guild was clearly splitting into two tiers--those who were getting all the loot and all the flags and generally ignoring everyone else, and those who were getting neither and feeling more or less like laborers funding the drearily predictable bonuses awarded management.

So we seemed stymied as far as getting into POP content, but weren't really doing a thing to pursue what you really need to be ready for PoP content either -- Vex Thal, and flags for everything for as many people as possible, so everyone can get their levels, their AA, scraps of loot, what have you. And we were separating into tiers of those who belonged and those who were the accessories and tools of those who belonged.

People started drifting off; I found a guild that was trying to advance neither in PoP nor non-PoP a complete waste of time. Many of our members joined the server's uberguild, some quietly, some getting called names in the process. I was interested in neither situation really, so deguilded and have since been on my own.

Unfortunately, PoP kind of encourages this. It's structured to pull people apart -- by virtue of flags and levels, not just the ordinary ugly greed rampaging through the game world, as it does through any world. The difficulty of getting everyone flagged for everything by funneling access to content through the bottleneck of flags (or broken and ridiculously time consuming quests, on the other hand) encourages guilds to break apart as some get flagged and some don't, and some people are deemed "worthy" of going back to get flagged and some aren't. It's programmed to make those sick of how the average guild handles these problems either opt out of their guild and go on their own or jump to an uber guild where they aren't wasting their time, or, if they are wasting their time, at least they have a much better chance at much better loot while doing it.

Worse still, the ability of outsiders to a guild to block a guild's progression by killing off key NPC's necessary for other guilds to kill takes natural internal difficulties and supplements them with a vulture attack to destroy from without what might not be destroyed from within. Add on SOE nerfing alternate quests by mitigating slows so that the early people can sail in while latecomers have more of a struggle, and you have an expansion that seems to bring out the worst of the game in every aspect: guilds are strained to stay together as they splinter into factions of who is flagged and who isn't; one guild can hold another down; guild members become harder and harder to usefully replace, because the prospect of flagging new people is a nightmare.

I separate this kind of thing from what I would think of as mere "features" -- the ports in POK, or the extra bank slots in the new downloadable expansion. These features are often really nice, and do a great deal to improve the game, at least by erasing its limitations, if nothing more, to make the gaming experience less tedious and aggravating.

Yet the basic design of PoP does indeed seem to be very flawed.

People have rushed to 65 without torpor or canni 5 or good gear or AA and found themselves less capable than they think of handling it -- although other people notice it just fine. And most of them find themselves trapped in PoV or PoS -- but mostly PoV.

All the "opportunity" to get AA (in one zone) doesn't make up for that for the majority of players.

I do not hesitate to state that the majority of people are foolish. Very few people will stay 60 and find whole guilds worth of people who want to stay 60 so they can beef up their abilities and experience and gear to take on the post-60 game. That will not be the nature of their friends, their guilds or any guilds they could possibly find, or likely their own nature. Instead, the majority will level themselves up and find themselves in guilds that are still not prepared enough or dedicated enough to really progress in the game, leaving them with nothing really besides PoV, changing guilds, or quitting outright(never to buy another Sony MMORPG) -- none of which is tenable to the game's continuing popularity or profitability. Partly because, frankly, the leap in time spent per day that is necessary for many guilds to make the leap from TOV or NTOV to Vex Thal and tier 3 and above would require too much divorce and unemployment. How are you going to pay child support without a job?

SOE is trying to cram a square peg into a round hole. If you take the average person and tell him he can level but once he gets there he can't do anything, he'll wonder why he's paying money to do nothing.

And, he might even stop doing it. I've seen an awful lot of people stop playing lately, or stop playing as much.

It used to be that the limitations in progressing in EQ were more self-imposed or a matter of the luck of not being in the right guild or on a server with guilds that have your kind of play style. There weren't such heavy stumbling blocks put into the path of the customer by design.

If you wanted to do ToV, what did you? You went and did it. Kael? Same. NTOV? Go for it. You win or lose, but you have access to the content.

PoP gave most of us content that could not be accessed, and worse yet, encouraged the average Joe to sprint ahead as if he could somehow magically access it with levels and AA, when of course there was always a bottleneck of flags to stymie him. And a bottleneck of flags to stymie whoever happened to go linkdead during a fight, die during a fight, get bugged during a fight, or simply not be around online during a fight for one reason or another.

The whole flagging phenomenon wasn't necessary during EQ's early times, and it isn't necessary now. In fact, it is driving and absolutely inevitably will drive people from the game. They won't come back to future Sony products -- once the magic is broken, it's broken. And it will continue to do so, as long as the game designers believe that locking content out is a good idea, or that tedious quests or events(some that can even be easily sabotaged) to get flags or keys can function adequately as some sort of a quick and dirty substitute for providing real creative content.

You know, the real RPG stuff rather than hack and slash, or hack and slash progression that naturally follows from your ability to hack and slash, not the ability to be in just the right guild at just the right time that manages to get the critical numbers necessary through one bottleneck after another and that somehow doesn't get c*ckblocked by some other guilds from progressing.

Personally, I believe that Sony might think there will be a natural progression of the less hardcore nutcases from EQ to SWG no matter how good EQ is, and that the only people who stay with EQ will be the real hardcore nutcases. After all, SWG will have a strong pull even on the hardcore nutcases, and many of them will go too, and even more so on everyone else. Certainly more so on anyone new to online gaming. Who wants to join EQ and see endless numbers of level 65's KS'ing you and calling you a noob and lording it over you when you can go to SWG and be part of something new and fun, something you have an even chance in, and, for almost everyone these days, vastly more interesting than a fantasy universe -- the Star Wars sorta schlocky sci-fi fantasy universe?

So does Sony really have to care or cater to anyone but the highest end people or the people who will mosey around in EQ no matter what, playing their 3 hours a week and considering a dollar an hour for fun a heck of a good bargain?

I don't know that they have to.

But this is the first expansion that seems to me to put inherent limits on things -- for the ambitious, the less ambitious, and the indifferent alike.

Need torpor and old world level advancement? Bottleneck to VP and trying to get unclaimed old world dragons, plus Sontalak and a few others far less likely to help you out. Need Luclin advancement? Hope you have torpor and old world level power. If so, bottleneck through Emperor and AA to another bottleneck in Vex Thal. Need PoP advancement? Hope you beat the old world and Luclin bottlenecks so you can bottleneck it through trials and flags one after the other, but ones you almost always need raid level forces of high level characters to beat -- and each and every bottleneck serves to funnel you into yet another bottleneck. PoP is the first expansion ever that is almost completely about bottlenecks. Success in it is like watching mirrors reflected in each other, ever receding.

The game has become a nearly infinite number of bottlenecks reaching out into infinity(and beyond! ;) ). PoP might have been Sony's effort to make the unbeatable expansion, so difficult and unlikely that they would never need to make another. Kind of a perfect design, except for one thing.

The human factor.

I remember a Gary Larson cartoon from the Far Side strip he used to do. One cow looks up at another one with a sort of stunned realization. "Wait a minute. This is grass! We've been eating grass!"

Unfortunately, most of my friends seem to feel the same way, as they don't play much anymore, if at all. This has never happened at this pace before. And I found it hard to imagine I would play as little as I do lately myself.

Sure, I suck, whatever. Love it or leave it, yadda yadda. Half-baked wiseacre comebacks are expected and the norm, and I expected.

However, potential flames aside, I do think Sony has made a mistake by deciding that bottlenecks are the answer to every problem, and that people will either: a) put up with them forever, or b) leave EQ BUT...be quite willing to invest the hundreds of dollars they might have wound up spending on EQ on another Sony online product in the future if they find the first product falling short. Remember, there's a difference between people leaving while liking a game and leaving while thinking it has insurmountable problems, at least for them in particular. I'm sure that if EQ doesn't make a customer happy, his demand for a supply of Sony online games, present and future, is far less resolute and inflexible than Sony might like to suppose. And I'm absolutely sure that one bad or mediocre recommendation or review passed between friends is worth more than all the advertisements in the world.

Sony really needs to stop mistaking bottlenecks for challenges and timesinks for content. Customer demand is not inelastic.
