DSL is sometimes more reliable than cable. It all comes down to how good the phone lines in your neighborhood are. Consider this, in some places phone wiring was installed before you were born, it is all corroded and nasty, it has enough trouble carrying your voice, let alone DSL signal. DSL is a pain in the ass to install, you either have to have separate wiring installed, or you have to put these lame filters on each phone in your place to keep them from interfering with the signal.

Cable on the other hand is much newer technology. The wiring is protected (phone lines are often part of a bare wire rats nest if you check the junction box outside) the diagnostic options are superior (there is positive feedback from your cable box/cable modem). I found the difference in support between my cable company and phone company (I switched from DSL to cable) to be day and night. The phone company often had to keep me on the phone while they called someone else to see what was up, and then half the time the answer was "we dunno, call us in an hour if you still have problems", or worse, they would say well, it is the weekend and the comm center in your area isn't staffed right now (I live in so cal, that is f'd up). Whereas the cable company would tell me right away, yeah, cable is out, we are working on it right now.

Bottom line: I had DSL with Verizon (by the way, f verizon to hell, can you hear me now you bastards?!?!?) for two years, I had multiple outages that lasted for a day or two, two outages that lasted more than a week, and to add insult to injury, the bastards disconnected me a week and a half early when I cancelled service (not counting that in my original outage count, so technically I had THREE outages longer than a week). I have now been with charter cable for three years, the longest outage I had was for about 6 hours in a really kickass storm. The connection has been far more reliable than my DSL connection ever was, I hardly ever have to think about my internet connection, and that is just plain awesome.

To answer your other questions. You can two box using DSL just as easily as with cable. Patch day will likely take the same amount of time.

Look at usage in your area. As the previous poster mentioned, cable is shared, and that can depend how many people the cable company puts on each node. I'm in an area that just recently got cable (Time Warner), and has a pretty small subscriber base. I have never seen speeds go under 1 mbps, and often reach up to 2 mbps, because there are few people on the network. If you live in a large neighborhood where lots of people are likely to be on cable, then you will probably have lower quality, and will notice spikes in "peaktime", usually about from 4:00 to 12:00 pm.

Best bet though is to ask people using both services in your area, or check out sites that do ratings. www.dslreports.com is a good one, you can find people in your area who use the service and what sort of average results are.

(cable modems terminate back to a router of some sort normally referred to a headend, dsl terminates back to a dslam. Both devices translate the various protocols into other protocols so they can be moved back to switch and data facilities to be sent to their appropiate destinations on the net)

Cable and DSL only get slower when they are oversubscribed heavily. Basically most cable modem headends and dsl dslams are normally not in a telephone companys central switch location. The dslam/headend have to get back hauled to a central data center/switch facility some how, and the *size* of that circuit it the limiting factor on how fast your connection is during peak usage. That is if the connection is not limited in size already (most dsl has caps on it at the dslam).

Asking questions up front of the provider you are interested will take you a long way :) heres a few.

1. what size connections do you backhaul your headends/dslams back to your data facilities with? What percentage is it oversubscribed?

2. what types of bandwidth caps to you configure your connections with?

3. how much oversubscribed is your total available internet transit in my area? whats the utilization percentage of your internet backbone?

4. what type of service commitments do I get with my connection (i.e. how fast they will respond if your connection breaks and when then will respond)


You'd be suprised how much mileage you could get from giving these questions to a sales rep or something. Keep in mind that many of these questions will only be answerable by a engineer.
