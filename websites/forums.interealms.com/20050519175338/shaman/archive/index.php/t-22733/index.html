Tugela
"Increase Chance to Avoid Melee by x%" as I understand it applies a modifier to the probability of being hit. So, if your chance of normally avoiding melee (as determined by the relative values of your avoidance and the mobs accuracy) is 50% for example, a 7% modifier would push it to 53.5%. It all depends on what you are parsing and how you present that data. You might say that it is a 3.5% increase based on swing strikes if you are mathematically superficial, but in this particular case the damage reduction would be 7%.

Obviously the effects of this would be larger on a low accuracy mob than a high accuracy one, so when doing parsing you need to keep that in mind. There would be negligible damage reduction on a mob with enough accuracy to land 90% of its swings (it would change to 89.3% hits with the buff, or a 0.8% damage reduction), and a large effect on mobs that landed 10% of its swings (it would change to 3.7%,or a 63% damage reduction)

I think that (correct me if this is not the case) the combat effects on the linked armor add directly to your avoidance and accuracy values, and these in turn affect the probability of hitting and being hit based on the relative values of your targets accuracy and avoidance. How that probability is calculated is an unknown though, so it is less obvious how those particular modifiers affect performance. "Increase Chance to Avoid Melee by x%" is much easier to understand. It is probably applied after the relative effects of accuracy/avoidance are calculated, whereas the bracer mods are applied before the calculation.

At least, this is my understanding of how it works, I'm sure someone will correct me if it is not so ;)
Jaidin
The original use for slow mitigation was to generate a more even damage output from very high level mobs. For example, a mob that quads for 1100 does it's full damage unslowed but only 25% of it's damage when fully slowed. Basically, that mob is very tough *until* it is slowed.

Enter: slow mitigation. The next mob that quads for 800 but is only slowable to 50%. It's damage is less than the 1100 quadder unslowed, but much higher once slowed, allowing the overall damage of the mob to be much higher.

I remember the reasoning being specifically, to get around the "insanely high dps we had mobs dishing out unslowed."

While this practice was actually a pretty fun new tweak to larger encounters, it shouldn't have been applied to trash mobs nearly as much as it has been.

I don't see an overall act of balancing content within any design template because there's just too large of a player spectrum to cover. For example, GoD mobs hit so hard and mitigate slow so regularly because they are fighting elemental+ geared players mostly. These mobs (and arguably the entire expansion) are not balanced versus a casual pickup group.

If there is a way to balance content overall though, why would you want to limit Shaman to just affecting one dimension of the group by increasing damage generated? I enjoy how Shaman can add direct damage, add indirect damage, cure damage, and prevent or mitigate damage.

Wulfbane
Sudedor
Perception is the key, you're right.

Damage mitigation is, to most players, a very ethereal and cloudy concept. Sure, they "know" that they have increased surviveability, but it doesn't show itself as easily. Different mobs hit for different amounts and so no one really "knows" what they get hit for.

On the other hand, damage given is a very known quantity. Regardless of the mob, players know what their maximum hit is. If they see that number increase, it's a very real and noticeable thing to them. It's not cloudy or feely or anything like that. "I was hitting for 350, now I'm hitting for 400."

Let's say that Class A increased their group damage mitigation by 20%. Class B increased their group max hit (and damage overall) by 15%. I guarantee you that Class B will quickly become the preferred class. Class A is statistically superior, but Class B is something players can measure without a parse.
