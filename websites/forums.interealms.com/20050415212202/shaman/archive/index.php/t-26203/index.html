I believe that Brenlo's generalization here is based on incomplete reasoning.

Consider four cases.

Case 1. The mobs our group is killing are mid-range dark blue or lower; most solo classes could solo these handily; most any duo could kill these quite easily; we have a full group and we are ripping through these very very fast.

Case 2. The mobs are upper range dark blue or white. We are killing fast in an experience grind; the goal is to kill these high experience mobs as quickly as we can.

Case 3. The mobs are white or yellow, and we are farming for names. We can handle this readily most of the time, but there are occasional damage spikes that give an adrenaline rush as we get close to losing people.

Case 4. The mobs are white to red, and we are farming for names. We are pressing close to the line a lot, and the damage spikes mean that someone dies pretty often: but we figure that as long as we only lose one or two in bad fights and don't wipe the whole group, we will just recover and keep on going.

Analysis.

Case 1 is the place where a shaman is set up to outshine a druid. Mobs like these are the ones that are fully slowable; plus a shaman can heal the modest damage that even unslowed mobs inflict with just a HoT. But it doesn't matter. Any healer can provide far more healing than the damage generated by the mobs. If the druid can provide twice as much healing as needed while the shaman can provide three times as much healing as needed, the advantage is irrelevant.

Case 2 will be a situation where most mobs mitigate, and so the difference between a slowed mob and an unslowed mob is less. This is the situation that is closest to all healers being equal and sufficient to the task.

Case 3 is the most problematic. All the mobs mitigate; most of them mitigate a lot; and so shaman healing is already marginal compared to the other two. And the shaman will be busy: in order to provide that marginal healing, the shaman will often need to cast two or three spells at the same time. But of course it is not possible to cast a malos and a slow and a heal all at the same time.

If I am a cleric in this situation, the job is not trivial. Do I cast many fast heals? That will keep the tank alive, but it might run me out of mana. Do I cast Complete Heals? That's very mana efficient, but the tank might die during the 12 seconds between casts.

But if I am a shaman in this situation, it may well be that:

(a) by the time I cast malos and slow successfully the tank is already dead;

(b) after I slow the mob and then start casting heals, the heals are not sufficient to overcome the amount of damage the slowed mob generates;

(c) by the time I have cast malos and then bounced a slow, the tank's health is so low that I can only spam heals as quickly as I can; the tank does not have enough health margin for me to risk taking the time to attempt another slow: and we are going to wipe unless three lucky things happen for us right now: I get a crit heal and the wizard gets a crit nuke and the mob doesn't generate a damage spike.

In Case 3, then, a druid is presently a more reliable healer than a shaman, and a cleric is a much more reliable healer than a shaman.

In Case 4, virtually all groups plan on having a dedicated healer and a dedicated slower. It is rare rare rare that a group will trust a shaman to be able to fulfill both of those roles successfully, consistently, against mobs at this level.

(Please don't bother to explain that you have successfully done this. So have I. I've failed at it a lot, too. And if you are honest, you'll admit that you also have failed plenty of times. The group members know that the chance of failure is high: unacceptably high for most people. So the fact remains: it is not at all typical that a group will trust a shaman to be able to slow and heal consistently versus this level of mob.)

Commentary.

The fear that Brenlo expresses has to do with Case 1. This is where mobs are fully slowable. I would propose that SOE's best reaction to this fear is to laugh at its irrelevance. Suppose that improving shaman healing results in shamans being five times more effective than druids in such situations. So what? Druids are fully capable of dealing (lazily) with all the damage the unslowed mobs dish out in Case 1; the group will gain nothing by dumping the druid and choosing a shaman to deal (five times more lazily) with all the damage the slowed mobs dish out.

The critical area, by contrast, is Case 3. This is the place where the inadequacy of shaman healing must be addressed: in situations where mobs mitigate a lot, resist a lot, and can kill a good tank in just a few seconds.

Presently, SOE's worrying about Case 1 prevents them from augmenting shaman healing to deal with Case 3. This is a mistake.

The proper course is to recognize that no boost to healing can unbalance Case 1, because all healers are already more than adequate to deal with that. And therefore, what SOE needs to do is augment shaman healing so that shamans, like druids and clerics, can heal sufficiently in Case 3.
