The other system they had spot on was the Karma system for PvP. Basically if you whacked someone who was also good, you gained a karma point and you became 'chaotic', if you kept on going you became 'Evil'. The downside was, city NPCs would chase you out of town and try to kill you. In other words you could PvP, but there was no griefing because it wasn't worth the hassle of being banned from the cities. You could of course lose Karma by the victim forgiving you, or going to the confessor ghost.

I think modern versions of these ideas on a grander scale would work well. My favorites games have always been the strategy empire-building games (i.e. Civilization, Romance of the Three Kingdoms, Total War series). Some of these games have a small roleplaying aspect incorporated into them whereas you can recruit personalities who have varying stats to help you decide what role they should fill. It would be interesting to see a game where the players took on the roles of those personalities, and they were assigned to certain duties by their leader, and some players would eventually even become leaders themselves.

For example a space game, where everyone starts out as a rookie pilot, fresh out of training. 'Training' however might be with one of many different militaries, or perhaps a corporate flight training, or even some less legitimate organization. You perhaps would have to serve a tour with whatever organization trained you, and then you had the choice to stay with that group or branch out and do whatever you felt like doing.

You would learn skills (and improve them) based on the things you do in your career, not by some arbitrary design. For example, say during your initial rookie tour you werent the greatest marksman with your ships laser cannons, but you had a very high kill ratio with your missiles - you'd gain more skill in missile targetting (or whatever you'd call it). Then perhaps after you finished your tour of duty and decided to strike out on your own, somebody might recruit you as a missile ordinace officer on their gunboat.

Players would advance in skills, and they could also advance in rank if they belonged to an organization. Players could eventually even form their own organizations that new players could join, be trained by, and serve with. You would earn money to purchase ships and weapons and other upgrades, so if you decided to not be a part of any organization you could still be a trader or bounty hunter or just an explorer.

If you've played Freelancer, it's a very good example of what makes a good space-based game - but it needs to be more fleshed out on the roleplaying side. Pilots should actually have skills and such, and capital ships should require a compliment of crew, so if you were rich enough to buy a battleship - you'd still have to have enough friends (or employees) to actually crew the ship and make it fly. Imagine a guild in this game would be measured by the size of it's fleet. A small guild may just be a few wings of fighters or one medium-sized capital ship. A medium guild perhaps would field a large capital ship and some fighter wings, and an uberguild perhaps could field a couple capital ships, their escorts, and some fighter wings, as well as own a station.

MORE SKILL-BASED CHARACTER DEVELOPMENT

Level based character development is useful because it helps to guage relative power vs enemies. Levels also help regulate power levels and prevent gross exploitation. But pure level based systems tend to breed the same characters. Look at Two 65th level Barbarian Shaman from 2 different uber-guilds on 2 different servers and you'll see 2 characters with roughly the same gear, the same stats, the same AA choices.

-In adventuring classes, I would see a point buy and skill tree system in place for virtually all areas of character development:
Attributes
Pure physical combat, unarmed
Pure physical combat, armed
Pure magic use, arcane
Pure magic use, divine
Knowledge skills
Craft skills
Athletic skills
Rogue skills
Social skills
Special skills

-Players would earn skill points (explained below) that they can spend on skills on the various development areas. Except for Attribute minimums and other skill tree prerequisites, no restrictions on how players spend points, players can spend points on whatever skill areas they like. In addition, in order to mitigate 'poor buying choices' players can opt to rearrange point priorities over time, reflecting a character's change of life's focus. This would allow players to continually evolve their character into the way they want to play it but keep it from happening all at once to prevent exploitation.

-Players would gain skill points based on:
Character in-game aging. ie: A newly created character 1 minute old automatically earns a skill point, 2 minutes later earns another, then another at 3, then 4, then 5, 10, 15, 20, 25, 30, 60, 120, 180, and so forth. Skill points earned from aging could be used on any skill area.

Additional skill points would be earned from experience levels in other categories.

Gaining Experience for Roleplaying. Upon gaining enough experience role-playing, you would earn skill points that could be used on social skills.

Gaining Experience for Exploration. Moving from area to area would net your character experience. Would require a detection system to make sure a person wasn't simply on 'autorun' or some such, or perhaps it really doesn't matter if someone macros movement to earn exploration experience. Regardless, skill points from this category would go towards purchasing knowledge skills.

Gaining Experience for Doing New Non-Repetative Tasks. Each skill area would basically keep track of its own experience totals too. The more you use skills in an area, the more experience you gain, after gaining enough experience, you would earn a skill point in that area to spend on a skill.

To give people an idea of how skills would affect gameplay. The combat trees and magic use skill trees are probably self explanatory, but before a person would question why they would need Knowledge skills....
Knowledge skills
-Mapping. Greater aptitude in this skill would enable greater detail in your automap (sort of how Anarchy Online handled it)
-Animal Lore. Would allow you to gain information on a creature you see in the wilds when you target it.
-Herbology. Enables you to forage.
-Combat Tactics. Enables you a bonus in combat when working in a group.
Social skills
-Barter. Gives you a greater discount when dealing with merchants or allows you to sell at higher prices.
-Seduction. Gives you a greater chance to influence an NPC if you decide to use this skill. Additionally, can give you additional ways of gaining information or items from an NPC. (Much like how Neverwinter Nights worked)
-Intimidation. Same as Seduction. Just that an NPC will react in a different way if you fail. On the other hand, Intimidation is more universal and is more likely to succeed.
Special skills
-Special skills are actually a unique branch of skills, gained through adventuring. Some would be available only through questing, others by meeting special NPC's. In all cases, special skills wouldn't be mandatory for adventuring but would open up new lore, or unique quests.

Also, you would probably have expenses beyond the cost of the software and license, to handle player load if your server became popular. In fact, you may need additional hardware (a server, for example) in the first place if you really wanted to host a world anywhere close to the size of today's MMORPGs.

But, I know myself and probably quite a lot of you would love such a challenge, and the reward would be the fact that your purchase price included a license fee to allow you to be compensated for your work - if your work was good enough to draw in people willing to pay to play in your world.

In my opinion, I think this is the next step in MMORPGs.

Well, that's not exactly what I meant, Seventh. If somebody tried to charge for an Eqemulator server, they'd get their pants sued off by Sony, because they dont have license to use any of Sony's code for their own profit.

What I'm talking about is something like this:

Torque Game Engine (http://www.garagegames.com/pg/product/view.php?id=1)

Except, go beyond just selling the bare-bones engine. Sell an engine (and the license to develop with it) that is also an application for modifying the engine without having to know how to code or create models and animation. Something any of us 'dumb' people could use, if we had the time and determination to build a gameworld bit by bit. And those smert folks who do know how to code and create models and animation could add unique code that the base engine didnt ship with, to make their gameworlds even more unique than users only building with default models, etc.

Imagine; if you were versed in computer model creation, you could create an entire library of your own custom models to give your gameworld it's own unique quality and atomsphere. Dont like the default player character models? Make your own. Dont like the European theme? Create a whole library of Oriental models and create a fantasy game based on Eastern lore. Hate druids? Dont include them in your world. ::snicker::

It was sorta attempted by Neverwinter Nights with their initial release, but it was quite limited in the amount of content included (i.e. character/creature models, terrain textures, etc). Also, they initially hinted that the opportunity was there for private users to actually make money off their customizations of the NWN engine, but eventually reversed their stance on that concept.

With other games I play online (Freelancer, Raven Shield), there are hundreds of mods, custom maps, custom models, etc that the player community creates and distributes for free (since they cannot legally sell any of it). I even created a modified version of Freelancer, and I barely know anything about coding. I just did what I could with the .ini files and .dlls, and got permission to use a couple custom ship models that were created by other modders in the community.

With an engine like I describe, the amount of player-made content would probably be staggering, leading to more and more diverse gameworlds very quickly. I'm sure eventually, you'd have games so totally unlike each other it'd be hard to tell they were all made using the same engine.

And because of this, you would not have anyone making a fortune doing it, because there would be so many worlds being created. Maybe one or two might make a good bit when they established themselves as the premier games through word-of-mouth. But, I'd imagine that would change over time, and the one's doing the best would of course be the professionals who had the knowledge to actually create totally unique features for the players.

Just something nice to think about, ya know.
