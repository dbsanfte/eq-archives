Copy/pasting this from my priest covenant post ("Design Philosphy") as I can't seem to get a single person to actually debate my post instead of getting hung up on peripherals. Maybe I can get some feedback here.

I have seen a lot of talking about how to balance the druid's superior nuking to a cleric's superior healing. In my opinion you can not balance straight healing vs straight nuking at all. Let me explain.

This is going to assume that all classes only have single target heals or nukes - no other spells or abilities at all. Further content is static, that is the encounter never changes.

We will assume that there is a class with the best heals and a certain amount of nuking power. There is also a class with the best nukes and a certain amount of healing power. These secondary abilities may well amount to zero (wizard healing).

How to balance a third class to equally desirable for a group through a combination of heals and nukes?

The EQ approach is to give that class (the druid) a certain amount of healing and nuking - for discussion's sake let us say 80% cleric healing and 80% wizard nuking.

Now, when a group is formed that group needs damage and healing. If they need healing they will look for a cleric, even if the druid can heal 99% as well as a cleric - as long as he is healing he is inferior to a cleric, thus the cleric is chosen. The same happens when the group is looking for a damage dealer - the wizard is chosen.

There is only one reason to ever choose this druids: If the group needs half a healer and half of a damage dealer the druid may be chosen. Even then druid healing has to be very close to cleric healing, because clerics can do damage too.

If a cleric can heal 100% or nuke 60% and a druid can heal 80% or nuke 80% it would seem that the druid is the superior choice for the job of half healer / half nuker. However consider the following situation:

The group needs 60% worth of healing. A cleric can heal 60% and nuke 24%. A druid can heal 60% and nuke 20%. Thus, despite having weaker nukes this hypothetical cleric is a better nuking / healing hybrid.

The percentages required for a druid to be preferable to a cleric or wizard by virtue of straight nuking or healing abilities are staggeringly high, approaching the 90% areas.

My conclusion thus is:
You can not balance straight nuking vs. straight healing in static content. There is always going to be two specialists (best nuker, best healer) and all other classes are left behind.

The only way to balance druids healing and druid damage is by letting them do half cleric healing and half wizard damage at the same time (or any other ratio that adds up to 100%).

Macnair
This is going to assume that ... Further content is static, that is the encounter never changes.I think your argument is correct, Murtak, as long as the encounter never changes.

And there is a lot of reason for assuming that the encounter will not change. With rare exceptions, the model is this: you must kill a mob that produces lots of dps (mostly from melee, sometimes from casting). To do it, you need solid tanking, large quick heals, and lots of fast dps. You get one person in front of the mob who takes all the damage and gets all the healing, and everyone else beats on the mob from behind.

There are lots of interesting ways that mobs could be difficult encounters, apart from this model. Many of these were implemented in older content: wicked AoE damage spells, banishings, curse and mana drain traps, mobs that spawn adds, and so on.

I think that some of the Ssraeshza fights are really quite interesting. For example, after you get past the traps on the way to the High Priest, you'll need to deal with 12 adds during the fight itself: some by mezzing them, some by banishing them, some by off-tanking them. That's a lot of work for different people to do. It requires good teamwork and communication. The High Priest himself is unslowable so you need good tanking and healing: and you have to sustain that over a period of time, you can do it right for several minutes and then screw up and lose.

(One time we had the High Priest down to 3% when one of the lesser priests repopped. We failed to pick up the add right away. We had the High Priest to 1% when the lesser priest began healing the High Priest: 20%, 40%. Lots of screaming about that: so three offtanks turned to attack the lesser priest, as we missed the repop of another lesser priest and a disciple or two -- soon it was all chaos, High Priest nearly to full health, adds slaughtering randomly and the High Priest summoning and snuffing casters with glee: sure victory was turned into a full raid wipe in perhaps 30 seconds. Wooo, were we mad! But it was a very interesting fight, indicating that even when you have a tough mob at 1%, you haven't won: it still takes teamwork, communication and coordination right to the very end.)

But as everyone has noted over and over again, the trend seems to be more and more to the one basic high-dps-low-hp model, which requires: solid tanks, quick heals, heavy dps to kill it fast.

Suppose many mobs were challenging -- maybe more than half, instead of just a few rare ones -- because they had effects: charm; a damage shield that the mob keeps renewing even though you keep dispelling it; knockback and stun; AoE snare with an agile mob that bounces around the room; silencing; random turning (I'm TIRED of you poking me in the ass, Mr. Rogue!) instead of pure mathematical aggro; mezzing; blindness; rampage that lands on a random number of melees in range instead of just one; whirl till you hurl; and so on.

What if higher level trash mobs might have any of these abilities? Then your group would have to recognize and deal with the situation right on the spot. What if higher level named mobs would generally have one or two of these characteristics, not necessarily always the same? Then your raid would have to recognize and deal with the situation, right on the spot.

In those kinds of situations, versatility would become immensely valuable. Versatility within a single character, and within the teamwork of a group or raid.

(I can hear the objections: it would take a lot of coding changes to implement characteristics like these. Ummm, yah. Creating an interesting game that evolves over time takes a lot of ongoing work.)

So:

This is going to assume that ... Further content is static, that is the encounter never changes.

Until we get some indication that further content will be creative rather than static, I think your assumption is a good one, Murtak. And your conclusion is then correct, that jack-of-all-trades (= versatility, being pretty good at several things) really isn't worth much compared to excellence in one of the three specializations that we need.
