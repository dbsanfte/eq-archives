Jaruden
The 2 most important things to consider if you're building a system for the future are 1) Memory, 2) Video Card. You can almost never have too much memory, and it keeps adding value past the point a processor would. Video cards are a bit limited since they change so quickly, within a year of buying the top end one, it'll be at half the price and a generation behind if not more. But try to get as good a quality as you can for the price, and definitely something that supports dx9 so it handles games later on.

Processor speeds are mostly irrelevant, I don't even think they sell things under 2 ghz anymore, and most anything over that is not getting much use for gaming. A 1.5 ghz machine with 1 gb of ram will out perform a 3 ghz machine with 256 mb of ram in most standard gaming . And cost a lot less.

Don't go with name-brand pre-built PC's.
1. You're going to get reamed on a deal that includes the monitor, keyboard, speakers, mouse and printer. Please don't do this.
2. They put substandard parts in these pc's.
3. That kind of pc has zero to little future upgradability.

Go Custom.
If you're not comfortable with building a PC, now is not the time to learn. There's too many incompatible parts out there, and unless you KNOW what you're doing, it's going to be a nightmare.

There's probably hundreds of people in your local area who build computers as a small side business. Check your local Greensheet/PennySaver (local advertising sheet) for businesses near you.

As a custom builder, I can guide you to things to look out for:
1. Get an up-front price on labor. I normally charge $400 for a very basic build, increasing with the complexity of the computer.
2. Get receipts for the parts. Get the original boxes for the parts.
3. Give the builder a budget. Have them submit to you what they can build for you within that budget. Make sure they include their labor and an operating system in that.
4. Expect to pay between $1500 and $2500 for a custom built pc. The higher the price, the more state-of-the-art and 'bells and whistles' you're going to get.

If you'd like to build yourself a nice pc, here's a recipe that was my latest build, if you use this recipe, you won't have incompatabilities:

Case: DreamerX Black (just get a nice case with at least a 350w powersupply)
MB: MSI Neo - FIS2R
CPU: Pentium 2.8 Gigahertz with 800 MHz Front Side Bus (FSB) (go with a 3.0 gig if you can afford it)
RAM: Corsair TwinX 512meg 800 FSB (Factory Match, do not accept if not in one single case).... remember, one stick per channel on the MB
HDD: Seagate 120gig 7200RPM Serial ATA
CD: DVD Player & a CDRW (don't go with the combo's, they get a lot of returns)
Video: Asylum GeForce4 FX5200 Ultra
A Drive: Generic
OS: WinXP

All told, it was under 2k with labor. Oh yea, and this pc freaking SCREAMED through the bazaar with full clip plane :D

Boy, I need to do something about this 866 Mhz P3 with 512 ram.

If I get a new motherboard, CPU and ram, will I have to reformat my second hard drive? I know I will need to format my C:\ drive, but will I need to do that with my F:\ drive?

You don't need to format anything. You'll have to reinstall your OS (Windows) because it'll freak out at a different motherboard, but it won't really give a damn what you do with the processor and RAM. Reason being, it has to run drivers for things like your USB ports on your motherboard, and when those change it gets confused like a little kid who just had their homework switched. It's expecting 4+4 but it's seeing 3+5, and it doesn't have the slightest idea what the hell to do. CPU and RAM, on the other hand, are pretty much handled by the system firmware (that first screen at bootup) and it should just automatically detect the new hardware and adjust itself.

So you'd have two options. Clean install, or reinstall of Windows. Basically, reinstalling is like "upgrading" even though it's to the same version, and it'll keep all your programs currently installed. People often take that route to fix some problems with Windows. Sometimes it works, sometimes it doesn't. I always just do a clean install since it saves a possible headache. Clean installing is pretty much making a second installation of Windows in a different directory and it'll boot from that stock version. All your old software will still be on your hard drive, but most programs require modifications of Windows' system and boot-up files. So you'd have to reinstall those. The ones that don't you can just make another shortcut to yourself rather than reinstalling, though reinstalling is probably a safer bet. If you're installing Windows 2000 or XP Pro, you can do something called dual booting if you clean install. This means at every bootup you'll be given the option to boot into the new installation of Windows, or you can boot into your old (and probably error-filled) version if you need to for some reason.

There's no reason your secondary drive would need anything, especially a reformat.

*sigh* No, it's not possible to keep the pressure even. Listen. One end is locked down first, leaving the heatsink somewhat levitated over the unlocked end. It doesn't matter what you attach the other end with, your thumb, a tool, tin cans and string, a monkey's ass, it doesn't matter... You're puttng pressure down on the unlocked end to lock it into place, and the heatsink is not immediately level. It IS putting more pressure on one side than the other. That's all there is to it. You may reduce the risk by using a tool rather than a monkey's ass, but the risk will always be there.
