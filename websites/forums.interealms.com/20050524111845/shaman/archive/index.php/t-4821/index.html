I think all shammies spend the first 19 or so levels of their life, maybe even up to 24, soloing. Before that, 1) it's hard to find groups that actually work better than just random attacking and running away, and 2) with your baby class spells, you really don't get the full impact of "the perfect group" yet. Meaning your cleric heals as well as you do, you hit almost as well as the warrior, and your buffing spells give a total of +4, and your slow is not even noticable.

I grouped off and on throughout most of my career, from 18 to 26, then from 34 to 44, and then off and on after that. I found that the point where I learned *the most* about playing my class well was 36-44, in Dreadlands. Yes, dreadlands, that horrible icky place. That's where I learned which buffs the tanks want (because you don't have HoS or FoS then, so you have to prioritize... I can do dex now, str later, or I'll go OOM), where your quickness/alcarity spell is, how to slow a mob once the main tank has aggro and not before (or yer supper), how to cannidance like mad cause you'll always be OOM due to too many needy tanks wanting too many extraneous buffs that really don't help much ("stamina buff now!").

In the Dreadlands, partly because people are short of temper and groups are so fluid, your tanks will be very specific about what they want and when they want it, so you can get a feel for what works and what doesn't, and when you know better than them, and vice versa. Also, I found that the 39 pet really doesn't cut it at that level, or the 34, but the 44 pet rocks, so at 44 I started soloing exclusively for awhile. I was also sick of bad groups in DL at that time :) I wanted some peace and quiet... and now, at 51, I still like my peace and quiet. But I do know how to handle a group. (Buff the main tank first, no matter what, ignore the guy who demands "zings" while your trying to slow a mob, buff when you can but don't go OOM, know when to throw a heal on and how much of a health bar your heals will cover...)

I know plenty of shamans who group from 44 on up, but I enjoy being able to go around and find my own place and just grind in peace. I also love to raid, (paradox there), so I get my group time in then... but I do burn out, so soloing allows me to refresh my temper and patience. :)

So if I were you, I'd solo up to 18, then group in paludal because that's the best xp anywhere, and groups are cheap and easy to 26, then solo to 36 and go to DL (yes, I got groups at 36, warriors *luv* shamans), group there until you are sick of the idiots and you've got a good feel for pull groups and maintaining buffs, then go solo again. Group when you want after that, but Shamans can solo all the way to 60 and sometimes, the peace and quiet is so wonderful. :)

No offense, but you sound like a person who is taking the shaman class seriously, doing research, knowing your spells and strategy, you already know the basics of raiding, you know the higher end of the game, and you aren't "muleing" the shaman by duo-boxing it and neglecting your shammy in favor of your monk/warrior/rogue (that makes me cry. Love your shammy. Don't bot it! Shamans rock!), I personally think you'll do fine.

Roxxanne
For exp grouping, I think the most fun and important "skill" for the shaman to develop is good situational judgment. You have such a variety of skills, there are too many choices and not enough ticks and mana to do it all (though after lvl 24 we have some of the best mana regen around, esp with your fungi).

The way I look at it, in a typical exp group you have to decide how to allocate your mana and attention on each pull among these separate roles:

1. Healing (direct heals, regens, bodyguard for robe casters)
2. Buffing (how thorough will you be giving out haste, stat buffs, resist buffs, utility spells)
3. Debuffing (SLOW, mal*** line, cripple line)
4. Spell damage (DOT's, nukes, JBB, epic)
5. Crowd control (root parking, off tank add, pet tank add)
6. Melee (bashing caster mobs, straight melee)
7. Self mana regen (cannibalizing, medding)

In a typical exp group, I usually do some of all of these in the course of an evening. The art of being a shaman is having good judgment about how to operate most efficiently in different situations, which depends on the other classes present and the mobs you are facing. A good shaman understands the spells and capabilities of the other classes well, and has a deep bag of tricks for applying our many skills to the maximum advantage of the group.

Enjoy!

Christabel
It isn't till level 44 or so that you have good enough channeling to reliably cast through meele. So a lot of tactics you'll use commonly after 50 (like root parking) are just not viable in the early levels. I would say that some important things to learn are:

1) How to prioitize your mana use so that you don't blow it all in one place and regret it sorely 1 minute later

2) How to prioritize buffs (in order of importance) for each class, and how to prioritize classes in order of importance. Ex... The druid crying for focus can just sit tight and wait/hope.

3) To be a quick thinker when the unexpected happens (especially in using your heals). If an enchanter starts getting torn to pieces then rare is the shaman who has the presence of mind to catch him before he dies. Become a shaman who doesn't let that crap happen.

4) How to stagger and time buffs in an exp group so that you know when it's time to refresh. Many shamans just want people to send them tells. And that works ok. But if you're a shaman who keeps the buffs fresh without being asked, tanks will remember your name (even though its not such a big thing).

5) How to stay cool and keep your head when people expect you to be everything at once. You're gonna go to raids where you just wanna pick up your monitor and throw it at the wall. But you still have a job to do. And you acomplish nothing by going berserk. So when people start getting rude about buffs, just know that it's the way things are always gonna happen.

6) How to control your pet in a group, and how to recognize situations where you shouldn't summon one at all.

and finally

7) Go through 55 or more levels without mentally locking yourself into an official "job description." As much as a lot of shamans despise playing healer or CC, you should always jump on those chances and be extra attentive. Excellence in going above and beyond is how a player makes a name for himself.
