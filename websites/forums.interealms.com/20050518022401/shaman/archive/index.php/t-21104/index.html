On the LR v ID argument, I think it fair to point out that the data he generated was an extreme case, where a druid was the melee. Coprolith
Hello Shamans

I'm the author of the parses linked in this thread and i would like to comment on the breakpoints in AC/agility.

It's been clear for a while now that the AC number listed in the UI is actually a composite number, the sum of an avoidance AC and a mitigation AC. So if there are breakpoints in mitigation and/or avoidance at all, you would expect to find them at rounded mitigation or avoidance AC numbers, not rounded values of the listed AC. For instance, if i have a listed AC of 1099 it could very well be made up say, 578 mitigation AC and 521 avoidance AC (just pulling some numbers out of my hat, you get the idea). If i were to bump up my AC 2 points with a AGI buff, my listed AC would be 1101, my mitigation AC would still be 578 and my avoidance AC would now be 523. So there's no reason to assume i would suddenly see a jump in avoidance just because i crossed the 1100 listed AC mark.
Secondly, considering that it took a parse of ~150k swings just to show with a reasonable statistical certainty that 100 AGI adds only 3%, you'd never notice such a bump during normal gameplay underneath the normal statistical variance. Same goes for mitigation; if the breakpoints exists, the actual jump is too small too notice.

Personally i don't think these breakpoints exist at all and that they are a myth brought into the world by the bad statistics that have always plagued defensive parses. You need 4 datapoints to see a breakpoints, each point having a uncertainty smaller then the actual jump. Considering that these bumps can't be more then 1 or 2 % at a time, we're talking samplesizes of 200000+.
But even if they do exist, they wouldnt occur at neat AC values, nor would they be noticable during normal gameplay, so for all practical purposes, AC can be considered a smooth function.

But parsing against a druid has absolutely zero consequences for the conclusions. Sure, the avoidance numbers themselves are much higher the you'd see during normal gameplay fighting blue con mobs, but the qualitative behaviour doesn't change.
The programmers at SOE did not write separate combat codes for every possible permutation of classes fighting each other. They've got one code which takes as input the defenders defensive skills, worn AC and agi. Then the attackers offensive skills are checked against that. The actual avoidance and mitigation numbers that result will vary with class, but how change depending on agility, AAs, etc, is always the same.
Everything else you see in my parses is in perfect qualitative agreement with existing parses made against NPCs, so why wouldnt the agility results be? The only real difference is my parses have better statistics then you could get parsing against NPCs, so it verifies all the hard work done before me.

Coprolith
Ah yes, i remember that post, great work from the wars. However from how i understand it, the theoretical model they use is based on the pure assumption that the modal damage of a mob is a step function. That's not an unreasonable assumption by itself, the programmers could be using lookup tables, where a damage distribution is selected based on the mobs ATK and the PCs AC. With lookup tables, you'd expect to see jumps in mitigation every time you cross over from one table entry to another. Unfortunately, parses don't seem to support this model since damage distributions appear to change smoothly with AC.

Mind you, that doesnt rule out the fact that the program indeed uses some sort of lookup table for mitigation and/or avoidance vs AC, its just that the lookup table must be so finely spaced that you can't notice the actual bumps.
