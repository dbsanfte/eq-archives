Well, I finally got to ask a few questions, second from the last. Was worried I wasn't going to get called...

Scott Hartsman, one of the developers answered the AC question, and touched on others. Baellish (I'm sure I didn't spell that right) was also there, and one other guy I can't remember the name of.

Regarding AC Softcap:
His answer was basically that AC is made up of four components internally (yes he said four). He said there is no hard number entered anywhere for a softcap. He said he could see where one or two of those components might not add much after a certain point, but it wasn't coded with a specific number. He also made the comment that various components might be more or less effective against different mobs with different attack.

He did specifically stress though that it did not work like Wis/Int where after a certain number you get reduced returns.

This jives with the theory that the softcap is entirely relative to the mob you are fighting. 1100 AC might give diminishing returns vs. Fungus Mushrooms, 1000 against Sebilis frogs, but you might still not have enough at 1400 when you get hit by Tormax.

Other questions:

Will Rangers/hybrids receive some attention during caster rebalancing?
Short answer: yes. Longer answer... he phrased it in such a way that I got the feeling hybrids were low down the priority list, and wouldn't get a ton of attention, but he did say yes they would be looked at. I specifically pointed out fizzle rates for level 60 hybrids, and they indicated they didn't know there was anything abnormal there, but they would keep it in mind.

High-end weapon rebalancing (i.e. the Rogue/Warrior upgrade where Rangers weren't ugpraded):
The short answer is: they didn't really know that some class might have been left out. He did specifically write this one down though (Freem peeked at his sheet afterwards), so they may have a look to see if we were left out accidentally.

Longer answer (this was touched upon for several questions): they have totally revamped how the developers interact with each other, and they are using a new method for communication and interaction for the Planes of Power expansion. They hope by this to avoid things like Epics with totally different utility or power for different classes, or other things that totally don't match up. He mentioned that this was something they were also doing for on-going development, and as such, he would pass this through the same system and see if somehow Rangers were neglected with the weapon buff. The interaction/communication thing was also brought up with regard to caster balance.

Archery auto-attack:
Same answer as Dallas. Yes they think it's a good idea. Yes they'd love to do it. But it falls lower on the priority list than lots of other stuff, so it's not going to happen right away. He said it's probably three weeks worth of work, so it's not totally trivial to implement, and as such, won't be done in the near future.

Hitting mobs in walls:
Same answer as Dallas. It's a core engine problem that can't easily be fixed, and won't be for some time.

That's all I can remember at the moment. An "official" transcript of all the questions will be posted on Caster's Realm.

I recently had a colleague that I respect put together two Java beans that are used for file transfers within this configuration management system that we work on. One bean is simply a wrapper around an XML document DOM tree to extract information about possible locations for this file, protocols allowed, and all sorts of other miscellaneous details. The second bean uses this data to actually perform the transfer. As it was originally written, it would have required me to write driver logic to connect the two beans. I asked why this was done this way and the response I got was a nice "Duh! That was dumb, wasn't it?"

The person in question wrote a defect report against their own code and then fixed it in the very next development cycle. Now the XML descriptor bean is contained inside the file transfer bean and all I do is construct the bean with a minimum set of initialization data (URL to get the file transfer document) and then tell the bean to transfer the files described in that document. The only thing I get is either an exception if something went wrong or execution continues with the next statement.

But that is an example of what programmers will do when facing short deadlines with seemingly difficult problems. It's very common in the industry and I am sure that every programmer here who has more than a few years of experience can think back to code they have written that, in hindsight, they realize should have been done differently.

Hitting mobs in walls:
Same answer as Dallas. It's a core engine problem that can't easily be fixed, and won't be for some time.

Really? I'll fix it right now for them.

1) There is already a subroutine that detects whether or not you can hit a mob partially in the wall with melee. We know this because we can still melee mobs that are partially in walls, yet if we have a mob targetted and it's sufficiently thru the wall that it's on the other side, we can't melee it.

2) We know EQ can calculate the range from you to the mob. It has to do this every time you fire an arrow anyway to make sure you are within bow range.

3) We know EQ can tell when you have a clean line of sight to a mob for firing arrows. i.e. "You can't hit them from here."

So each time you fire an arrow, if you have a clean line of sight to the mob (use the same check they use in #3), instead of your character being at current range from the mob, the function internally would project your character forward to within melee range (via #2) and see if a melee hit would have landed on the mob partially in the wall (use the same check they use in #1).

So If you could have hit the mob with melee if you were in melee range even if it was partially in the wall, you have a clear line of sight to the mob, AND you make a successful archery check, then the arrow hits the mob.

It's pretty easy to cast stones at programmer estimates, especially if you haven't done a lot of programming. Once you begin to work on systems bigger than your senior design project you begin to develop an appreciation for just how expensive some of the hidden complexities in any code system can be. The simplest things to the layman user can translate into unbelievable complexity for the developer. Been there done that.

Simple problems your simple solution fails to avoid:

Did you want the user to be able to actually DO anything else, like turn, move, type, recieve tells, open inventory, etc, during your while() loop?

What happens if the user clicks AutoArchery, but is already AutoAttacking?

What if Call_Archery_Attack() doesn't exist as a routine now, but is instead all wrapped up in Call_Attack(), perhaps with some hideously creaky reliance on global data which has been worked on and debugged over the past 5 years to the point that it is working now, but will require a lot of time and testing to replicate and/or rewrite entirely to support the new function?

What if someone opens the trade window?

What if he runs out of ammo?

What if he casts a spell?

etc etc. I could go on.

My point - don't be too quick to jeer at programmer estimates. Sometimes they actually do know what they are talking about, and the young, clueless hotshot with his first compiler really CAN'T do it faster, and the older but equally clueless management type really CAN'T make development easier with "synergy, training, and focused resources" or some other equally inane drivel.

I personally think the VI developers do an amazing job. I shudder to think what would happen to my own company if we had to push out a formally QA-ed, full tested version of a several million line software product every 2-3 weeks.
