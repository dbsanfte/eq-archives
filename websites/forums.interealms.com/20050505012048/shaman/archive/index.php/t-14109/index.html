CrustyOldShaman
Things have to change in EQ, I think we all accept that. However, I wonder if many of us are happy about the manner in which SOE go about implementing changes.

A recent example of this is Monk mitigation. SOE's method was to think of a number 'X', slap it on test for a short time, and then put it on the production servers. In a short time, the number of ex-monk fungis hanging up in the bazaar was remarkable. Eventually SOE decided that 'X' was too great a nerf, but by then it was rather late in the day.

And now we are seeing the same thing again. The exp. level nerf is being removed and, at the same time, 'X' exp will be shifted from PoP ZEMs onto group bonuses. 'X' is decided by a meeting, followed by placing on test.

I think its reasonably obvious that the size of 'X' is dictated by the need to spread the 61+ population out. Too great an 'X' value and many zones in PoP would be deserted and old-world zones packed out, too small a value and possibly PoP remains packed like a sardine can. I don't understand how SOE think they can get the correct value of 'X' from dabbling on the test server - the population there does not represent production servers.

I'd have thought the sensible method would have been:

1. Patch with the exp. level nerf removed. No value of 'X' at all. See how this affects zone populations.

2. Patch with maybe 20% of the supposed final value of 'X' and see how that impacts on zone populations.

3. Increment X until zone populations are balanced.

Do other people think this would be a more sensible method of making transitions in EQ rather than the 'big bang' approach?

Maybe some of the really game changing patches in the past would have been better off to start a bit slower or even in 2 chunks. But after all the year has only 365 days to spent and every 10 percent increase in patch downtime results in a estimated 30 percent increased outcry of the player base ;)

As another thought, who decides after let's say 40 percent of a change, that it's 'enough'? Given the fact, that you never will please all players with one change, you must draw a line somewhere and with your approach you would never find out, how the 60, 80 or 100 percent of a change would have worked out.
