Every iteration of the MMORPG genre new developers / teams get the chance to see what they did right vs. what they did wrong, what worked, what didn't work, and tune it before release.

They also get the chance to review what the competition is doing and how folks react to it as well...

In addition to that - every generation of games is able to utilize better technology, better development tools, and better scripting engines as the MMORPG genre itself grows and expands and the tools used to develop those virtual worlds grows with them.

The developers are more experienced building the virtual worlds each time. The marketing reps., producers, and designers are better informed as to what folks want, and the tools and system hardware are better able to actually BUILD those games - so is it any wonder each iteration things look better.

A quote from the vangaurd faq that really makes me miss the old days of dreading CRs.

"Vanguard: Saga Of Heroes will employ a variation on the traditional corpse retrieval death penalty – you die, you return to your bind point but leave a corpse behind and that corpse contains the items and coin you had in your possession. You will generally need to return to that corpse and loot it to regain the items and coin.

Here’s why: a CR (character corpse retrieval) with experience loss has two components: a static penalty based on your level or some such (the experience loss), and a dynamic penalty relative to where you died.

The trick with the static penalty is to balance it such that it stings but doesn't overly frustrate (easier said than done, yes). Death is part of the game -- it means the player is taking risks, entering new areas, exploring, etc. We don't want to over-penalize that and discourage exploration. But, then, if there is no penalty, it's not a risk. By the way, as you all know, there are a couple of approaches to this: conventional exp loss, exp debts, not allowing levels to be lost, etc. Regardless of the precise implementation, we think experience 'loss' is better than losing an item or your skills -- to us, that's too harsh.

The dynamic death penalty is something we feel is very important. If you die on a hill outside the city and someone else dies in the depths of a dungeon, your two CRs will vary significantly in both difficulty and time invested. And they should. That hill outside isn't supposed to be a risky area, and the rewards are set accordingly. The bottom of a dungeon, however, is a nasty, scary place with awesome treasure. In other words, the CR helps us control risk vs. reward. As a side benefit, it can also help community by creating interdependence as one often requires assistance to get their corpse back from those depths.

What we don't like is newbies losing their corpses. Fighting to get to your corpse is fine, but not knowing where it is at all is a problem. In this and in other areas, the CR style death penalty will vary a bit from the more traditional approach -- more on this later."

One of the biggest problems with early EQ was the penaltys for death were too severe, which forced people into "XP Grind" mode. The sense of adventure was out-weighed by the sense of "dying sucks".

That is the best observation of the early game dynamic I've ever seen.

I started playing in beta IV with one RL friend. Whenever we tried something new, we ended up dying in a very very bad way that ultimately ended up completely frustrating us. Dying and paying a penalty is fine - in fact, it's necessary. But if that penalty is too harsh, it can really suck the fun out of the game. In the original and Kunark areas, one or two deaths would destroy all your hard-earned exp for even a long game session. Death hurt that much. And in the original game, only clerics had rez, and only at level 49. When we started, there weren't any level 49s. There weren't any level 29s. If you die, starting running for your corpse, and hope you can get it back.

We started playing in Qeynos Hills. One day, he was grouped in Blackburrow and checked out that hollow tree. For those of you who don't know, that hollow tree will drop you all the way to the bottom of BB. A very bad place when you're level 6 with almost a full set of cloth armor. We literally had no chance of getting his corpse out - he lost it.

Mind you, I don't think it was an entirely bad thing. Death used to be a really traumatic experience. My heart would start pumping and I'd have to fight back my anxiety and try to focus when I got into a hairy situation. The notion of dying was very unpleasant, because it was a gigantic pain in the butt. My first character was a human monk. When I died, I got to run back - sowless - to my corpse - usually many zones away - while blind as a bat.

Now, dying is for the most part trivialized. Many classes can rez, many players 2-box one of those classes, many zones have graveyards - the overall penalty for death has been reduced so much it's hardly a traumatic event any more.

I understand that balancing the death penalty is very difficult. Make it too harsh, and people will get frustrated and angry and ultimately quit. Make it too lenient, and you remove an element of excitement and danger from the game. I hope Vanguard can get it right.

Now things seem more programmatic. Dissect how to kill the boss or get experience quickest, and avoid as much of the rest of the game as possible, really. Time it all down precisely and get the maximum return on investment. You could play it in a handful of zones with a sliderule and a calculator. The new players getting into the game seem to slip right into the grind mode/PL mode that is the current feel of EQ. Whether it's Vanguard, EQ2 or WoW, the #1 thing is whether or not the game is just flat out fun to play. Everything else is secondary to this and it's easy to lose sight of this salient fact. EQ for us is simply no longer fun- it just took us a lot longer to realize this than many others (yes, we're slow learners). Not one single friend I've made over the last 3 years still plays regularly, though not all of them have taken the final step and cancelled their accounts. Once you remove online friendship, EQ's flaws become more pronounced not surprisingly.

BlargX's comments above reminded me of something. In fact, last night while playing D2 with a friend he said out of the blue "Did we ever have this much fun playing EQ?", to which I replied "Yeah, but it was a long time ago.". My friend said that the last time he had that much fun was:
- before the monk nerf
- before PoP

That really got me thinking. Those two events occurred nearly simultaneously and wound up destroying our enjoyment of the game. We both played epic monks with no raid gear whatsoever, so we logged in to discover that we could no longer team up or solo and no one wanted us in groups. PoP mobs destroyed us in mere seconds and there were no more dungeons to explore. We couldn't even adventure with our other buddies in raid guilds anymore because they were flagged for tier 2+ and we couldn't even get a trials group with our now worthless monks.

To compensate, we both started two boxing and switched to 4 brand new chars to try and adapt to the game and progress. In hindsight this was a huge mistake as we never had as much fun in EQ since that time. We should have left EQ right then but we didn't (go go addiction).

The moral of the story is that all future gaming experiences for us at least will be judged solely on the fun and entertainment provided. I will be perusing the reviews and boards intently before wading into any new game until I know it's fun.
