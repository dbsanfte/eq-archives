Based on the comments of some folks here, I took a chance and picked up a copy of City of Heroes when it was released last week. Here are my initial impressions:

1. Graphically it is a beatiful game. The costumes, animations, particle effects and the world are very well done. Each ability has its own unique animation, and I'm guessing there are well over 100 different animations.

2. There is a huge potential for diversity in creating your character. I have seen a bewildering number of costumes and body forms, none of them remotely alike. The down side of this is once you make your costume your stuck with it for the life of your character.

3. The skill sets available make nearly every character unique in one aspect or another. I grouped with a person who had the same archtype as me (defender, more on this below), but had trained a different set of skills. We complemented each other nicely.

4. Game play so far has been engaging. The quest system at the low levels, at least, is well thought out and expands in flexibility as you level up. I have been very impressed with it so far.

5. Grouping is a whole new experience. My experince thus far is that the flexible and overlapping roles ensure that everyone makes significant contributions to groups.

6. Of interest to shamans is the defender archtype. There are several different roles that this archtype can take, including a cleric-like role, but I wanted something that loosely paralleled a shaman. I came up with a radiation skill set defender (both the primary and secondary skill sets), which allows me to solo nicely, but buff and debuff well in groups.

7. There are guilds (called Super Groups) and raid level encounters (called Task Force missions). We got a GM announcement that said the Task Force missions were bugged last night. So far I haven't been involved in either of these.

8. There is no elaborate player based economy. Besides skill points, rewards for victories are "influence points" that you can spend at NPC stores. You also rarely get two other types of items, inspirations, which are short duration buffs, and enhancements, that are used to increase the effectiveness of your super powers. These latter two can be traded among PC's. You use your influence points to buy inspirations and enhancements.

9. The game runs very well on my system (Athlon 1.4, ATI Raedon 9600, 1mb ram), and seems remarkably free from bugs. I haven't had too many problems with lag, and experienced only a couple of LD's. Easily as stable as EQ, and with less overall lag. I haven't tested CS yet, but I did send one petition that netted me two emails. I figured it out myself tho, so I never read the emails.

All in all, I really like the game. I'm not sure it will replace EQ at this point, but it has been a real breath of fresh air.

In this post I will try to go through some of the basics in CoH, I may miss some importent stuff but ill try to list the basicis.

First of all, heres abit of info on how the game works:
It's a twist game, you're constantly active unless you dont want to be(and it shows) or you simply dont have enough endurance, I dont even watch tv when im playing anymore :P

Classes

The 5 different classes is :
Defender
Blaster
Controller
Scrapper
Tanker

The 5 differend classes, each contain 4 to 6 differend primary power sets and each class has a secondary power set containing 4-6 secondary power sets. Each power set got around 9 differend powers.

Some of the powers may overlap each other but not so much that they all blend in together. For instance, defenders share 5 of their 6 primary power sets with controllers, controllers gain those 5 power sets as secondary power sets however. Which means their slightly weaker at utilizing those powers then defenders(healing from the empathy power set is around 25% weaker for controllers).

Defenders is basicly a support class, they got various debuffing/buffing powersets as primary, usualy 1-3 differend heal types thrown in there.
Their secondary power sets being DD's, debuffs and some CCs.

Blasters is what int casters is in EQ, they deal alot of damage, either in single target form or AEs, they have some abilities to CC but not as much as controllers. Primary powersets is such as assault rifel, Ice Blast, Fire Blast ect. Secondary can be Electrical mainpulation, gadgets ect.

Controllers is the enchanters of CoH, however, each powerset does it differently. Mind Controllers does it via mez and stuns, earth does it via AE roots and single target roots ect. Their secondary powers is usualy based on healing, buffs or debuffs.

Scrappers is the melee power house, they deal huge amounts of damage, but have to be in melee range. Their primary power sets is based on damage, such as Katana, Martial Arts, Claws ect. The secondary power set is defensive, regeneration, super reflexes ect.

Tankers, pretty self explainatory. Their primary powerset is defensive such as Ice Armor, Stone Armor ect. With secondary powersets being offensive, Super Strength, Energy Melee ect.

All in all, I havnt seen a single class that I would say is totaly over or under powered yet. Everyone got a place in the food chain, and everyone is more or less able to solo(some more then others due to their powersets being more geared towards it).


All classes can chose up to 3 differend power sets from the common power pools, these include but are not limited to teleportation, Super Speed, Leaping and Leadership.

Levels:

What levels gives you is, new skills, new enhancements, more damage/accuracy to your previous powers, more hp/endurance(mana).
You can only chose powers from your skill tree(archtype) and you can only put enhancements into powers you allready posses. Starting at level 6 you can chose a power from a common powerpool(Travel powers, minor non-combat healing, minor group buffs ect.).

If you're playing with a bunch of friends even if you get ahead of them theres still options to play together unlike in EQ.
You can /sidekick someone 3 levels or more below your own level, which means they fight almost as good as yourself(1 level below you for 3-10 lvls below yourself, 2 for 11-25, 3 for 26-40 levels below yourself). They wont have the diversity(they dont gain any powers atall from this feature) or the HP as a guy the same level as yourself however, which means they have to be extra carefully. It is extreamly easy to catch up with a buddie just playing along side each other, atleast in the begining.

Enhancements is how you boost and make your powers "unique". There's tons of different enhancements, first the 3 types is Training, Duel Orgin and single orgin, with the first being the weakest and the last being the strongest. You can place enhancements into your differend powers depending on the type of power. Take a damage power, you can place damage enhancement, range, re-use time decreasement, endurance reduction on. Some powers my have duration, knockback, stun component or some sort of root component in them and theres enhancements for that too. The maximum enhancements you can add to a single power is 6, each start out with a single enhancement slot to beging with.

Is it an item-centric game or how does character progression work?

The short answer is no. You don't have to find items, there is no currency, etc.

Character progession involves 3 things:

1. Acquistion of new powers from different power sets. You get two power sets when you create your character, and one power from each of these. On each even level (2, 4, 6, etc) you get to pick a new power. At first you're limited to the two power sets you chose upon character creation, later you have access to more.

2. Additions of new slots to powers you already have. Each super power has slots that you can use to enhance that ability. On odd levels you get two new slots that you can use to put on any existing power.

3. Enhancements. - These are items you obtain in battle, purchase from npcs using influence points (earned as a reward for defeating foes), or trade with other pc's. The go into the slots you have for each super power. You can modify the performance of your super power by changing enhancements.

I find it a very unique, intuitive way to use your powers.

This game is campy, and allows you to play around with your character. You can have a battle cry - F-10 sends your cry out into whatever channel you've selected as your main.

You can change your battle cry by selecting ID under the main menu. ID also has your profile - so you can change your story and background as you go.

It is fresh enough that it encourages those small acts of creativity that made EQ fun and entertaining way back when. While fighting "The Lost," a homeless gang, we competed for the honor of having the worst battle cry. "Take you tax cut and get a job!" "Tax Cuts Save Lives!"

Makes me think back to the fun EQ days - when I'd come out from behind my spell book to find myself face to face with the glowing eyes of a troll. "Pretty lady read book?" Ack!

Supergroups are fun to design -- Salvation's Army's leader is the Grand Pooh Bah, the captain is the Only Slightly Less Grand Pooh Bah. The members are the Bottom of the Pyramid Scheme.

It's got fun animated emotes - you can haul out a newspaper and start reading. You can roar, do jumping jacks, or lecture.

As you move from contact to contact, some random stories begin to emerge. Unfortunately, as each of the group works through their missions at the early levels, the stories are often repeated. But as you move up, the number of contacts you have increases. Presumably this will cut down on story repeats.

One very handy difference - when a zone gets too crowded, all future visitors to the zone are give the option of going to a copy of the zone. Kings Row, or King's Row 2. There is a lot of kill-stealing, in part because sometimes it's hard to tell that someone else is working on a group of mobs, in part because the game encourages you to attack random mobs as you wander along. But it's really not the issue that it was in EQ -- everyone just moves on, no one gets upset.

All in all, CoH has done a good job of giving me a break from EQ. It's fun to play for fun again, if that makes any sense.
