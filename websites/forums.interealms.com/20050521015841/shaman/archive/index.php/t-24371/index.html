Macnair
But they will give us solid footing for figuring out the class issues and helping you to understand our points.

Huh? No really huh? How can those documents help us (the playerbase) to understand anything, when they are NOT published?The most positive available understanding of this is as follows.

The current problem is that the designers do not know what the various classes are supposed to be and do. So when players ask questions about class design, class balance, class anything, designers can't help giving lame answers, because they do not know what the classes are supposed to be and do.

When they finalize class definitions, then the designers will know what the classes are supposed to be and do. Then they will be able to answer player questions intelligently. And so they will be able to help us understand their points. Because then they themselves will understand their own points, and might therefore be able to explain them in understandable ways.

I am not confident that this is the right way to read Brenlo's answer, but it's the most positive way of reading it that I can see. Alternatively, he may just have goofed and said something dumb when he was meaning to say something intelligent. Or he may have thought he was making a joke. He does that from time to time, and his jokes don't always work.

If indeed these definitions are intended to be in-house documents to help game designers answer player questions, the intention remains pretty silly, for a number of reasons.

(1) It builds in frustration for potential new players. "Hmm," I say, "I bought this game, I need to make a character, how bout a shaman? I wonder what's the official SOE definition of a shaman? I'd like to know that, so that I can see if that's the kind of character I want to play." Sony's answer: "Ha ha, it will be more fun if you make a shaman, level it up to 70, and figure out once you get there that this isn't what you wanted: you really should have rolled up a beastlord." But actually that's not creating more fun. That's just creating frustration.

(2) It does not stop the flow of information, only the flow of goodwill. The player base as a whole will deduce the overall content of these documents. It may happen rather quickly, like cracking the 1.5 epics; it may happen more slowly, like solving the 1.0 epics; but it will happen. And people will post their findings online, compare notes, and it will become a matter of public knowledge that the Sony definition of ClassX is blahblahblah. When we do, we'll be almost right: we'll be more cynical and biting about it than Sony is. Instead of having the information with a positive sense of goodwill regarding what ClassX is supposed to be, we'll have the information, but with a harsh sarcastic edge.

(3) The point of secrecy here appears to be plausible deniability. Plausible deniability doesn't work well in other places, and it doesn't work here either. If SOE publishes their class definitions, people will say, "Hmm according to your information here, ClassX is supposed to work like such-and-such, but it isn't happening that way, and here's why." Good Quality Assurance values that kind of feedback from interested customers; and customers value it when Quality Assurance responds well to their feedback. But it appears that SOE just considers that this kind of feedback might be embarrassing. So if they can keep us from knowing the definitions, we can't demonstrate when one of the definitions is not being implemented properly. That would leave them free to ignore or ninja patch or deny the validity of any criticisms, without ever having to admit there was a problem that they needed to fix.

Sony would be far better off having serious conversation about class issues with the player base, which by now includes a huge amount of wisdom and knowledge about game dynamics.

Alternatively, they would still do reasonably well offering their own clear information: this is the definition of ClassX that we are working with, this is the rationale for it, we may still tweak it some but that's what we have in mind. If you want these skills and abilities then this is the class for you; if this doesn't appeal to you, choose a different class.

But it appears they are choosing the least helpful strategy: keeping basic information about how to play the game out of the hands of the players. ("What class should I play" is about as basic as information in this game can be.) This strategy does not create a better game. Instead, it creates frustration instead of fun, sarcasm instead of goodwill, and deniability instead of customer appreciation.
