Sithrax
I played a lot of WoW in the closed and open betas and I found the game to be pretty fun. The world is very vibrant, and if you have played the other Warcraft games, they got the feel exactly right. In many respects, the PvE experience is a streamlined version of EQ. Nearly everything in the game is laid out in a logical manner. Quests are easy to find and the rewards are almost always worthwhile (and stated up front, too).

It's possible for any class to solo and get meaningful xp/loot, which is important to a lot of people. WoW is much less of a grind than EQ - the death penalty is pretty minimal and xp comes fast in any case. The combination of quest xp, plus the xp you get from killing mobs along the way means you are pretty much always progressing.

I just can't say enough to convey what a difference these changes make. In WoW, you ALWAYS have something to do and it really helps to reduce the feeling of "grind xp all day" that EQ tended to give you. This is especially true early on, when all the quests are fresh to you. Some of the quests are fantastic - the early Tauren and Undead quests really fit into their backstory wonderfully. The McClure vineyard quests in the human starting lands are also great, and revolve around reuninting two young lovers from feuding families (only including early quests as examples so folks can check them out without too much effort). There are also plenty of less interesting collection/slaughter quests, but even these are good for when you just want to log on for an hour and make some progress with your character.

There are a few things that worry me about WoW. The biggest problem in my mind is the lack of differentiation between PvE and PvP abilities. There have been a number of nerfs/changes made to balance classes in PvP which have been a major detriment to the PvE game. To me, it just seems crazy to try and balance the two using the same ruleset, but that's what Blizzard is trying to do. I don't begrudge the PvP folks their fun, but I don't enjoy PvP and the fact that it impacts my gameplay experience so much (with the various "balancing" efforts) makes me unhappy.

The emphasis on being able to solo is a blessing and curse. In EQ, you were forced to learn good grouping skills early. Zones like CT and Dalnir weren't forgiving of mistakes and tended to force you to learn basic things like aggro management, how not to ruin crowd control, etc. This is not the case in WoW. The abundance of soloable quests into the top levels of the game mean that player grouping skills are often in a sorry state. When you start doing high-level instances with pickup groups, this can lead to some really ugly situations. If you always group with friends, this isn't a problem - otherwise, you have to be careful. I do wish the game would create bigger incentives for grouping, and put them in early so that players get a chance to develop these skills from the beginning.

The high-end raids are still largely untested (most were not released to the beta testers). The jury is out on these events until enough people in the raiding guilds are level 60 and able to try these out. The raids that were tested publicly looked interesting, but not enough data was available for meaningful conclusions at this point. In two or three months, the situation should become clearer.

As with any new game, WoW is in its "honeymoon phase" when everything is new and shiny and bright and everyone loves it. This happens with every new MMOG. The longevity of the game, and its ability to retain the interest of high-level players, is still up in the air. We'll just have to wait and see how it turns out. For now, I'm having fun with it (still regularly playing EQ though), and that's all that matters to me.

That's an interesting question. It's true that WoW doesn't require you to group, but there are significant benefits to grouping once you get to a certain level.

Let's review where WoW items come from. First, you can loot them from random mobs. Second, you can buy them from vendors, who actually have fairly decent stuff for the level, which is somewhat affordable. Third, you can buy droppable items at the Auction Houses (automated Bazaars with some catches attached to them). Fourth, you can tradeskill yourself, which comes naturally as you level up, or buy tradeskill items from other players. Fifth, there are NO DROP items in instanced dungeons suitable for one group. Sixth, there are quest rewards. And, of course, there is raid content, but we haven't really seen it yet.

Of all the options available to you, quest rewards and the NO DROP gear in instanced zones is generally best, from what I have seen. And you usually need a group to finish Elite quests or go into instanced zones at the level when the rewards are still good. Similarly, if you try to do regular non-instanced dungeons, which tend to have named mobs and better loot, you will need to be grouped if you want to get the gear at the level when it will benefit you. Remember, almost all gear is level-limited, so wiping out a level 10 dungeon when you are level 20 won't help you much since by that time you will be able to get better items elsewhere.

So yes, grouping is optional, but it tends to get you better equipment for the level and you also get to see more of the game content. Soloing is still eminently viable and there are many quests with decent rewards for soloers, but groping is encouraged, just not mandatory.

Also, it's not quite true that the death penalty is inconsequential. Sure, if you die outdoors, you lose a few minutes of game time and a few silver pieces to repair the armor, no big deal. However, if you die in an instanced dungeon and you don't have a rezer on hand, the penalty is substantially higher.

Overall, it looks like WoW and EQ have similar risk-vs-reward structure, it's just that the WoW one is more flexible and differently tuned. So far, I am liking the WoW model better, but we will see.
