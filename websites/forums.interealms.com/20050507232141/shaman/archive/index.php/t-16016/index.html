Dandi
In more and more threads (not just on these boards) I'm seeing statements that seem to reflect the opinion that if you are not squeezing maximum exp out of your groups, putting on precisely optimum buffs, and getting exactly the right balance of classes in the group, that you're not playing "right". I've heard that: You're stupid if you group a chanter rather than buying KEI and grouping more DPS. You're lazy if you use group buffs instead of stronger individual buffs. You're completely out of your mind if you get AAs other than canni5 before getting 65.

Is it really not possible to play "right" unless every move you make is the statistically most effective one? Does doing some things differently in order to have more fun make you a Bad Shaman, unworthy of these boards? Isn't there room for personal preference anywhere? Grouping chanters so you can let them haste and tash and don't have to stop to go buy KEI? Using group haste so you can skip some hassle? Going for fun AAs just because they're...fun?

I'm not saying that the number crunchers aren't accurate. I'm not saying that it's wrong for someone to enjoy that approach. And I'm certainly not saying it isn't nice to have those facts available. But when did maximizing your numbers become the only socially acceptable style? Is it just because powergamers are the folks most likely to frequent message boards, so that's just the perspective I see most? Or has something in the culture of EQ changed? I'm not a powergamer but I am an old timer. And I'm getting tired of hearing that I'm a newb or not a good shaman because I have additional priorities beyond exp.

Can't we all just...get along? *snickers*

Thoughts?

The number crunching can probably be laid at the feet of PoP. Prior to PoP, gaining experience took a considerable amount of time. Gaining .xx dps per hour wasn't going to have a major effect at the end of the day. In PoP, that same extra dps might mean an extra aa or two per day.

To a lessor extent, the new generation of players isn't helping. In most cases, they're racing to catch up to their friends. After 50+ levels of optimized leveling (pal caverns, etc), they've probably gotten use to what it takes to get to the next level the quickest.

For us 'old timers' who remember spending a month in a hell level (a blue a day?), experience today, even outside the planes, is pretty damn good.

As you stated, it's not the number crunching that annoys me, it's the fact that if you don't follow the optimal route planned by the number crunchers, you're somehow unfit to play a shaman.

I was told the other day I was a pretty good shaman. Not because I used single buffs over group buffs (I don't), not because I time my buffs (I don't), not because I knew that SltW was .056 more effective then ToA (I didn't). It was because I rarely got agro from the tank after slowing and if I did, I stayed put until he had agro back. He couldn't care less what I buffed him with, he just didn't want to have to chase the mob around. Am I good shaman because of that, nah. It was, however, his perception of a good shaman.

I guess that's what it comes down, perception. The extra dps might be perceived as the only thing that matters to some, while having a good time might be my perception of what matters.

I think, in part, that's what Dandi is trying to get across. Whatever perception you may have of our class, please remember that it's only your perception/opinion. We're all different. Prejudging someone because they don't see/do/feel the same way you do, is a dangerous path to go down.
