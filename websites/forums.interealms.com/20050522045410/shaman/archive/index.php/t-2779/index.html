Now to the original topic...

Most guilds I've seen have developed their own system. You'll need to sit down and decide how you want to do it. Afterlife's DKP system is a very good one to look at for ideas, but its pretty intensive.

First you'll want to set a range of what loot can be worth, standard is pretty much 0 to 20 points. 20 points being the top of the line end game type item, 0 being stuff that has very little to no use to anyone on the raid (stuff that drops off mobs that is so crappy that even most twinks wouldn't use it type stuff). There are some things that will also be considered into the point value: How hard is the item to obtain, how much demand is there for it, is it class specific, is it tradeable, how fast can you get more, etc.

Then you'll need to start setting point values that members will receive for killing certain mobs. The point value will mainly be determined by the point value of the mob's loots and the amount of people it takes to kill the mob. Time it takes to kill a mob, time it takes to clear to a mob, amount of people needed to kill, etc all take part in determining the point value of the mob. But the goal is to have the value of points awarded to the raid as a whole to be very close to the value of the loot dropped.

You will also need to have a system keep track and display current points after each raid. You'll probably need 4-5 people that are going to track all raids and loot drops so that at least one person is online to keep track of the info. Those 4-5 will also need to be constantly keeping up with new loot to get point values assigned before the items start being obtained, also they'll need to keep track of any upgrades/nerfs to items and do possible rerating of point values, including retro point changes to players that got the items already. You'll also need a way to handle trade ins for items that are no longer used by the player and are droppable and the player gives that item to someone else in the guild.

When you first change over you'll also need to have a period of time where points are given out for participation but loot is not deducted during that time. Give out the loot the current way you are doing it but don't charge the players points. Do this for a 60-90 day period. What this does is gives you a good way to start awarding on points based on participation for the last few months. You can't start up with everyone at the same points from scratch because then you are putting everyone at equal footing, the hard core players on every day and the ones on once a month, you need some time to build up a good amount of points based on participation. This time period really depends on how often you raid, but you'll probably want to get your most active every day type player to around 60-80 points before starting, that will give you enough time to get a good spread on points. The very active will be near 80, the active will be in the 50s, the less active in the 20s. Now everyone has a good judge of how often each plays and loot can start being docked from the points value. Once that active player gets a couple of items he'll be lower than the active player and the active player will get items etc.

Now you need a way to determine who gets the item, some go just straight highest points win, others like Afterlife use a teir based system, others let players bid points for the items with highest bidder winning. Our guild does straight highest points wins, you put in more time with less reward so its yours.

You'll also want to set up guidelines on when players can't be in some rolls. Mains should always get gear over alts regardless of points. Some items like FT items, Haste items, etc should go to the right classes, but in any successful guild this shouldn't be an issue.

Best thing to do is have an officer set up a system, then let officers discuss it and make changes till it looks good. Then let the guild see it and discuss it, once you get to a point where it looks good, go with it. During this time is when you start tracking raid info. Just record who is at every raid, what loot drops, what is killed, etc. You can fill in the points later once you figure out what they are worth, don't bother for now, just keep a list of that info. Using the new channels is a great way to keep track of who was there, just have everyone on the raid join the channel and /list the channel to make a list of everyone there.

Each guild is different, so each system tends to be different to fit the needs of that guild.

Michael J. Kaspar
We use a point system similar to what Wendolyn talked about in the thread mentioned above.

We provide more ways to earn points, but lately loot has exceeded points coming into the guild, so this somewhat prevents hoarding. We award points like this:

1pt per level 50-55
2pt per level 56-59
3pt at level 60

If you join the guild at level 60, you start at 17 pts. Points are also awarded for AA levels.

You earn 1pt per raid, and 1pt per objective mob killed. Some of our raids are not pointed, but that is announced ahead of time, and its usually because you don't need a full guild force to kill said mob.

Every item in the game is pointed 1, 5, 10, 15, or 20. If there is a question on point value of an item, it is adjusted based on member input on the raid. If an item is droppable, it is generally 5 pts higher than a comparable no drop item. Large disagreements in value (very rare) are handled by officers.

Our system has been tweaked by circumstances, and we do have an "officer override rule" to prevent a non-raider from looting over a raider if we need to implement it, but that is rarely the case. Furthermore, everytime the system has been challenged, there hasn't been a suitable replacement for it. It does have flaws, but they are small. The system prevents arguments, avoids hassles, and initially keeps recent recruits below veterans.

The system was one of the main reasons I joined the guild, and I think it has withstood the test of time. It's significantly more fair than ANY random type system out there.

My Guild uses a point system and we are only 40-52 in level. The Merit system only works in a "vacuum" and does not work in real life. Unless your a really tight guild where everyone has been around each other playing the game for the last year and everyone has always had a "help your guildmate out" attitude your guild will not survive long.

Our system has 2 cycles the first one being the build and equip the membership cycle and the second being the twink and bask in your uberness cycle. Both use the standard attend a raid earn X points. We also mix in some NBG on low end items and alternate ways to earn points.

The first cycle some items have class restrictions placed on them by the leadership so that a CoF does not go to a cleric to twink his lvl 20 warrior but goes to a Melee present at the raid and who has the points to bid. Then a random is called for those who want to "bid". The winner gets the item and the points are deducted. Simple and has a bit of chance. We also have a hand down system for that person to give the guild his RBG since he has a nice new CoF and get some points back. For those members who have a life but still want to be part of the guild and have their chance at loot we offer alternate attendance points for gathering quest items, recruiting, and etc.

The Second cycle is for after the guild has a good base of members and or equipped fairly well. This cycle we will introduce closed bidding where if an item drops and someone at the raid wants (some items may still have class restrictions) then they can send a /tell to the loot officer and the highest bid wins. This type of bidding will be used at raid/mobs that we have done 100's times over and all possible good drops have already gone through the first cycle and the right classes/members have that item.

It really depends upon your guild.

the Point System:

The point system, as has been stated several times, is best for a raiding guild. It emphasizes going to guild events. The more you do, the more points, the more loot, the better all around. The rich don't get richer, since if they spend less time while others spend more, they'll have less points and soon the other will bypass them. If they spend equal time, they'll equal out. And someone that's new to a guild can typically upgrade a lot of gear very quickly simply because nobody that's been in longer wants it. However, it isn't casual friendly and there's no easy way to make it casual friendly. You might have a person that does a lot for your guild but doesn't play during your guild's primary raid hours. There's also the commitment it takes to keep track of point values and points earned. It's also not the most invite friendly and loot must be allowed to rot to protect the integrety of the system.

A great thing for it is that people can complain about the system, but rarely about the people. A nice perk for the officers to make up for the increased workload.

the Officer/Loot Committee:

Officer decision has it's good and bad points. The biggest bad point is mistakes will be made. Officers are human and fallible. If your officers are greedy SoBs, you're hosed and odds are, eventually your guild will collapse because of it. It also has it's good points. It's a bit more friendly to casuals or off hour players and invites. It's also less labor intensive, as long as your guild isn't full of loot whores who whine about every call. Basically, responses to an announced loot call should consist of one "thanks" and many "grats". In the long run, with a good officer crew, it'll balance out.

As your guild grows though, especially if you don't get a matching increase in number of drops, people will complain more and more.

/Random:

Random, well, in the long run, it's sorta fair, in theory. But in the short run, it's really, really annoying to see the super rare item you've drooled over for a long time go to a person that's done squat for the guild. Not to mention some people are lucky and some aren't.

I see it as a progression guilds go for. With a small guild, you might go with random or officer. But as you increase in size, you typically shift away from random to just officer. Later still, when your size and levels allow for more constant raiding, you'll go to DKP just because the officers are sick and tired of your whining. (I'm an officer, if you can't tell. *grin* )

My own guild is getting closer to the time when we'll switch to DKP. Not quite there yet, I think. Need to step up another tier or two, but soon as more and more of our members hit 60+ and want to raid more, it'll become more desired.

The following is my old guilds loot system it is a DKP and addresses the posted question at hand.

1) Points counting

First slain of an uber mob : 8
Attempt on an uber mob that we haven't killed before : 4
Slain of an uber mob : 4
Common raid (includes armor farming) : 2
Showing up for more than half of raid period : 100% points
Showing up for less than half of raid period : 50% points
Late night raids : 50% points

It is basicly 2 4 8 , actually it is based on 1 2 4 system, but I doubled it . The reason why I doubled it is to avoid odd numbers like 0.5. To be fair, people aren't able to get full points as conditions listed above.

2) Loots trading

People who has the highest total points gets the loot.
People who sends tell for the loot loses 2 point.
Points cost for a loot = ( number of people who send tell ) X 2 + ( number of raid people ) / 5

For example, I got 6 tells for a plate BP in a 23 people raid (tov farming), so the cost will be 6 X 2 + 4 = 16 . The people who has the highest points ( 68 ) gets the BP and then loses 16 points, so he has 52 points left.

By this way, we avoid the trouble of making a database of 500 items and avoid the trouble of tuning the cost of each item. And it still reflects the value of an item.

Late night raids were only counted as half value so people that played 24/7 couldnt amass as many added points as someone that worked and needed to log to sleep.
This was a medium sized guild about 40ish members just with a large pacific rim contingent.And they effectively punished themselves with the late night penalty.

Use the example of the plate BP above it cost the winner 16 points.now the same size raid force goes in to TOV again 4 months later and a plate BP drops only one person sends a tell and the BP ends up costing them 6 DKPs instead.

No system will satisfy all members, all of the time. Under DKP systems, casual players will sometimes complain, not seeing the fairness inherent in the principle that says, "if you do more, you get more." Under officer decided loot systems, both non-officers and officers alike succumb to various misapprehensions, bias, selfishness, and favoritism at times. Unjustifiable calls are inevitable, just given the human factor -- and this can and does create turmoil within a guild's ranks.

In my opinion, though, a hybrid system is best -- one that tracks participation as well as loot, but allows the guild officers to use this information to make informed decisions when awarding drops. This can be as detailed as the guild officers want it to be, with the general principle being the more information conveyed, the better.

Strictly speaking on a DKP vs. Officer's Decision system, I think either can work fairly well. Guilds that have used both types of systems have survived and even flourished since the early days of EQ. It just depends a lot on the guild composition and temperament. In any case, I think a /random system is absolutely the worst system, as exceptional merit can effectively be nullified by bad luck.

Points cost for a loot = ( number of people who send tell ) X 2 + ( number of raid people ) / 5

For example, I got 6 tells for a plate BP in a 23 people raid (tov farming), so the cost will be 6 X 2 + 4 = 16 . The people who has the highest points ( 68 ) gets the BP and then loses 16 points, so he has 52 points left.


The 4 points come from the 23people / 5 = 4 (rounded down I assume)

Arorot
Hehe its not that deep on a reasoning level its just that most guilds go by a FOH DKP system which is really complex and takes constant retuning of item pt values.
Can you imagine what kinda headache every expansion must be for whoever maintains the point values of items? (hears POP knocking on the door)
Or even when a guild moves to the next level of encounters say from kunark dragons to velious uber mobs youll either need to retune the value of a kunark class BP or up the price on anything in velious.

To answer the question about backing out , no thats never occured.But then if you REALLY want the item at hand the point cost shouldnt deter you.Remember the person with the most points wins the item anyways.In addition it allowed foir the person needing the upgrade more to attain it.A warrior with a thurg plate BP would be less likely to send a tell (and lose points)for a Kael one if five other warriors were still in barbed ringmail or whatever.

Also it helped keep one person from dominating the top of the pile.But we had two people hit negative numbers.like neg 3 or neg 1 DKPs after a raid.Once someone was in negative of course they would only get anything by default (which still cost minimal points of number of raiders divided by 5)
You dont really know the cost of the item til about a week later.
We were updating fairly regularly.Just a simple spread sheet on our web page at a glance would tell you who all had how many points.GL or raid leader just kept track of who got what at how many points.
Everyone kept a fairly close eye on their points as well so if say a vindi BP dropped and i had 80 pts and another shaman on the raid only had 50 they wouldnt bother to send a tell.

Just for point of reference most in the guild were seasoned raiders,id guesstimate 80% or better were already decked out in TOV gear with some luclin pieces thrown in here and there.
But to me the beauty of the system is it should reflect a fair value on any item dropped no matter the raid level of the guild.
