"ghetto-XXX" is probably politically incorrect

Determination of "PCness" all depends on careful analysis of the denotation of the word, the connotation of the word, and whether the combination of that denotation+connotation would be deemed offensive to some subgroup of the population.

Realize many English words have denotations, which are the precise meaning of the word as used by the speaker; and a connotation, which is the associative implication of that word (often an emotional associative implication). It's the combination of denotative+connotative meaning in English which gives our language such rich poetry, songs and literature.

The terms "ghetto-mez" and "ghetto-gear" do have connotations and denotations which several prior posters are glossing over. A modern denotation of the word 'ghetto" refers to a lower-class inner-city region/culture, and its specific connotation in this slang usage is closest to: "inferior". Political correctness concerns centers around the combination of denotation+connotation of the word ghetto in this usage; namely does the association of "ghetto" with "inferior" imply that the people living in inner-city area are themselves inferior?

IMO, the use of "ghetto-XXX" as an noun may be borderline offensive to some people who live in the inner city. Since anything deemed "offensive" to any subgroup is "politically incorrect", this phrase is not-PC.

Don't shoot me as a PC-cop. I am just explaining the analysis process to determine PCness.

Etymology of the word ghetto comes from 17th century Italy where parts of the population (Jews) were forced by political regulation to live in certain (less desirable, generally) parts of the city. The ghettos in Italy were abolished in the late 19th century but the practice continued in parts of Eastern Europe until the end of World War II.
The term was coopted in America to signify the various neighborhoods that certain economic castes were forced (or able, depending on which verbage you like better) to live.

The comparison between poor, dilapidated, overcrowded conditions and that which is lower quality, out of context, or substandard is a perfectly reasonable and colorful hyperbole. I don't know much about the term after it was coopted in America, but my best guess would be that the phrase was adopted by the citizens of the ghetto itself and not by its neighbors looking for a negative descriptor.
