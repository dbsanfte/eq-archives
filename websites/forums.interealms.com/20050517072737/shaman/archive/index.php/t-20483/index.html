Gardeltron
We who play EQ sometimes don't see the big picture. EQ is a money making enterprise owned by a large corporation. EQ exists because it makes a large profit for this corporation. And true to almost any corporate ethos, the more money EQ makes the better it is.

So how do you increase your profit margin with a service product like EQ? Not only do you charge a monthly fee, but you regularly introduce new content to keep customers from getting bored with the product. Each time EQ releases a new expansion, they see an additional influx of cash that supplements the already sizeable income they recieve from monthly fees. In fact, expansions must be so profitable that corporate managers in charge of EQ probably pressure their employees to turn them out as fast as possible.

Such pressure means that developers and designers must focus on producing new content as fast as possible. Furthermore, that new content must appeal to a wide variety of customers, from the ultra-elite end game users to the casual gamer. How do you keep up with such pressure to create? You try to build content that incorporates established norms of design that don't require complex adjustments to existing content. You assume, for example, that class balance is fine as it is. Then you add content that increases the demands on the abilties of those classes, while at the same time giving the classes slight adjustments to existing tools to deal with those demands. This means you save time because you don't need to redesign class rolls with each expansion.

It appears, however, that this formula does not work for shamans, because the established design norm for shamans was to make them comparatively weak in all of their abilities while making them powerful at debuffing mobs. Unfortunately, the utitlity of debuffing does not scale linearly with increasing mob power. Rather, since debuffing takes a finite amount of time to apply, the value of debuffing experiences a problem of diminishing returns with increasing mob power. At some point, mobs are so powerful that debuffing becomes inconsequential to the outcome of each encounter. When the power of debuffing is thus mitigated, the shaman class is left without any meaningful contribution to encounters.

As much as the people of this community appreciate the patchwork that SoE applied to our healing, the fact remains that the shaman class has been designed out of the game in GoD. There appears to be a lot of interesting and challenging content in the expansion, but in their rush to produce it, SoE lost sight of the role of the shaman class. The old formula doesn't apply to the new content.

Now that GoD is "old content", developers are already busy at work with yet another expansion, with its promise of additional cash flow to sweeten the bottom line. Few resources can be spared to fix what was broken in a game that has become so complex that predicting the outcome of new design innovations on game balance is becoming increasingly difficult.

Gnofr
I am under no illusion that player enjoyment of a "game" has much to do with EQ development priorities. Profit is a rather pervasive driving force. That doesn't make it simple. Even with the prevalent focus on short term return on investment in our modern business environment, there are those looking to the future. It's hard to carve a piece out for yourself if you can't see past this quarter.

Maybe SOE's priorities for EQ are solely driven by short term business models. Certainly they're more aware of all aspects of the competitive arena of the gaming market than any of us outside looking in could possibly be. Yet seeing Sony's products over the years and considering the wealth of research implied thereby makes me wonder about what EQ's highest and best uses might be.

If I were looking at how to establish a secure and dominant market share across a broad spectrum of technology burgeoning into cyber space and had EQ within my clutches, I wouldn't squander it solely to short term profits. I would value it far higher as a research vehicle with a myriad of possible simultaneous uses.

How does an established consumer base react when we do XYZ? Where XYZ is anything programmers can implement within the game environment, from simple nerfs and single-click upgrade purchases to the introduction of instanced zones to the replacement of the entire graphics engine to name but a smidgen. That's without even considering the research potential of the mixed virtual and real economy and its relevance to yet unestablished virtual property law. Then there are all the possibilities for psychological study and experimentation with avatars, groups, guilds, etc. with an eye toward market development and social engineering.

As an aside outside such a game as EQ, how long did it take for competing companies to wise up to the war of words on the Web and their place in molding and maintaining a market? Doesn't anyone fondly recall cross corporate cultural complaints concerning competing content? It used to be so easy to recognize that sort of troll. Ah, progress... Or is that propaganda? I'm amazed to think how many people take whatever they're fed at face value. It's as though we're desperate for simplicity and despite the obvious overwhelming complexity of life, stubbornly stick to stupidly simple solutions.

So it's amusing when people wonder why folks take stuff in EQ so seriously since it's only a game. To most of us who play and probably to many of the developers, it's "only" a game. Sony didn't grow into the multi-national corporate giant it is today by being that short-sighted. I wonder what EQ really is to them?
