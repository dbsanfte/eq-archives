howler
Buying/Selling:

Simply put, pain in the arse. The lack of relevant sort features on the search menu from brokers, or the menu you can place in your house, is not just annoying, but it's the greatest limiting factor of the auction system. When players can sort items based on what class they are, based on the stats on the item, etc, then buying and selling will be revolutionized.

Items:

Items need a more developed description. Most of the time, you can loot something you can't use yet, but you don't know if you could use it in the future because the descriptions are vague at best. What would also be useful would be some type of tutorial/quest to make sure each person knew exactly what types of armor/weapons he/she could use. If items are based on a skill level, that's fine. Say heavy plate is 100 skill and light plate is 50. Show the skill levels, but also show that the heavy plate can only be used by guardians, templars, etc while the light plate can only be used by the broader classes of fighters.

Labeling certain items with their effects would help tremendously for the economy too. Store bought food increases hp/power regen by a certain amount. Player made food, especially really good food, increases that regen even more, but because nobody knows for how much, it hurts a provisioner's sales. We can see how much armor or power or health is on an item, why can't we see such things for food or spells?

Skills:

Which leads into skills. I don't want to complain that there are too many skills, but when I hit that skill button, good god! I scored in the top 1% on the SAT's, and I haven't heard of half the words on that list. Though details are wonderful, simplifying some areas of the game would go a long way towards dissipating confusion.

Maps:

Not available in some zones, which is fine and understandable. But the maps for the zones we do have are not customizable. Being able to mark off certain parts of a zone with camp names, or the names of certain mob spawns, etc is incredibly useful but lacking at the moment.

Spells:

I absolutely love the idea of a tiered system of spells. The difference between the apprentice I and adept I or adept III can mean the difference between doubling or tripling your healing/damage output, the amount of hp buffed, or the degree to which a mob is weakened. If you are more worried about updating your armor during the first 20 levels of this game, you're really focusing on the wrong thing. Getting adept I or III spells should be your number one focus, especially if you're a mage or priest.

Tradeskilling:

Though I haven't had much experience with this aspect of the game. I have crafted most of my app III spells. The silver I saved was worth the time I spent learning how to craft and the pain of harvesting all the necessary components. I must recommend to you all that you max out your harvesting abilities on the Isle. Being able to harvest uncommon or rare components, even if you're not a tradeskiller, will do a lot to pad your wallet. Once the market improves with an enhanced auction system and more buyers/sellers who know what they're doing, these uncommon/rare components are the keys to the really nice tradeskilled items.

Grouping:

Lacking. Because people regenerate health/power so quickly after a battle is finished, the need to group with other people and the need to know precisely what you're doing - what your role is - seems lacking. At least, this is my experience before level 20. My small group of friends doesn't fit into this category, but it seems like the vast majority of people I meet fit within this category of noobies. I don't blame the people so much as I do the game design. In EQ, you were forced to learn the basics early on, else you were in for a long downtime. Though nobody likes downtime, downtime is the reason why people become less noobish.


These are just my initial impressions. Haven't gotten too far into the game, but I think it's solid. Most of these things are easy things that can be fixed, which looks promising. If SOE was willing to copy a few things from WoW like the mail system, and when they work out some of the basic things, this game could be rocking in the next few months.

Before the subclass is chosen, many skills do not have a cap and others may not even apply to one character while they will to another. For example, at level 11 a character wishing to be a templar will eventually be able to wear heavy armor, whereas another wishing to be a shaman will only be able to wear medium armor. I like the idea of not knowing exactly what my character will be capable of right off the bat.

Second, the information you would like available in game is actually widely available outside of the game via message boards. Also, you can speak with other players higher level to obtain information regarding the best armor choices.

Third, store bought food and player made food have the exact same hp and power regen. If you don’t think so, then purchase some coffee and compare it to your summoned water – they are the exact same power regen. The difference is the duration per unit of food/water.
