The thing that really sets good IT personnel apart is something that isn't taught in classes: personality.

The typical arrogant ass who works in IT and knows "everything" isn't going to be on the fast track for the best jobs. Work on your customer service skills, and you'll go far. As an IT professional, the people in the business are your customers, and if you treat them like dirt, they will disregard your advice, and you won't be the first one people think of for promotions, raises and prestigious projects.

Also work on public speaking. The computer professional who can get up on front of a room full of executives and explain an IT project with enough pretty pictures that they got it is truly rare.

I'm pretty similar. I found I didn't like programming much at all, and I'm much more into the technical side of things.

The most valuable (and the best paid) IT workers tend to be the network guys. If you can, learn routing protocols. You can practice on a cheap linux system using something like gateD or zebra. Understanding how large private networks interact with the internet over multiple net connections is important. Few know how to do it well.

Learn to be a DBA . You have an upcoming class on Oracle/SQL? If taught well, that will be very, very valuable. If not, you can still build on what you learn there.

Learn Linux. At minimum, learn how to configure it, learn how to install and configure modules, and learn the anatomy of the file system. If possible, investigate DHCP, Web, DNS, FTP, and routing protocols.

Hardware knowledge will only take you so far -- anyone can build a computer, and many people know how to troubleshoot them as well. You can certainly make a living doing hardware troubleshooting and/or helpdesk style jobs, but I wouldnt recommend it.

Large networks are so much more versatile and fun. You can get a job where every day is different, because you never know what a large and diverse network will throw at you.

The bigger the company, the more specialization you can get away with. Speaking from experience, you can be the sole IT guy for a 20-person internet biz, and you have to know it all -- desktop troubleshooting, people skills for help desk support, network skills, server and database administration, the works. In a large company with a 20+ person IT department, you can focus on one thing -- IE, you can be a DBA, or a hardware technician, or a network guy.

Here's what I'd recommend:
Network design/topology knowledge is key.
Learn cisco switches and routers
Learn the Microsoft stuff since Sales/Accounting/whatnot will always be using MS so most every company has some MS network setup.
F5 and Alteon knowledge can be useful.
DNS is important so understand bind 8 and bind 9 in addition to whatever the heck Microsoft uses.
Network protocols are good stuff. Understand at least the basics of all common tcp and udp protocols.
SSL, Learn all you can about this because more and more traffic is ssl-ified every day.
Databases, take a basic database class. If you like it, continue learning more. If you don't, then just learn the basics of supporting Oracle and SQL server environments.
Learn Solaris 2.6 and 2.8. Most shops use at least some Sun stuff.
Learn a flavor of BSD or linux. If you know any one of these then the rest are pretty easy to extrapolate. BSDs and linux use many of the same tools which are frequently somewhat different from what you'd see on a proprietary unix.
Learn basic shell scripting, bourne shell at a minimum, if you plan on doing anything with unix.

Disclaimer: I'm not an MIS person. I'm a programmer/architect who designs and implements systems and infrastructure. Hence I know what a sysadmin, network engineer, or MIS needs to know since they are my customers.

Looks like great advice so far. I've been in IT since 1990 after a six year tour in the Air Force.

Just to add to what Coryn said in regards to the specialization...in my experience, smaller companies give you an opportunity to be involved in more aspects of IT.

A large company might have a help desk, a desktop department, a systems department, a networking department and a telcom department. Working in the desktop section, you may never get to see how the servers are built or how the network is routed. This of course depends on the company.

A smaller company might have 3 people that do all the above. You get exposed to more. You get to see if you like the networking side and working with routers and switches etc. or if you really prefer to work as a systems engineer and keep the servers running. After some time you can then start to concentrate on the area that interests you most.

As Nekoken & Aizle said I think there are some skills that will help no matter what area you end up in...

TCP/IP | DNS | DHCP | Basic routing & subnetting
Basic troubleshooting tools- ping, tracert, etc.
MS office
Linux
Sun
Organizational skills
Project management

And Myrea is absolutely right, personality; and how you deal with people can take you a long way.

I love working in IT and can't imagine doing anything different.
