This is kinda like asking somebody what kind of car is best to get. Somebody can give you a lot of reasons why to get a certain kind, but when it comes down to it, its a matter of personal preference. I personally have never liked Fender Strats, but that doesn't mean they're bad guitars.

A couple of things though:

1) Try not to get something with single-coil pick-ups. It probably won't sound as full.
2) I personally like a Floyd Rose bridge as it gives more versatility than a standard bridge (ie whammy bar). They're a pain in the butt to tune up when you put new strings on, but they work well after that.
3) Pick a guitar with a body style that is easy to play while sitting down. I bought a V guitar (not a flying V, there's a difference) back in the day and loved it a lot. Problem was I had to stand up to play it or put the inside of the V on my thigh which was kinda akward.
4) Look into your favorite guitarists choices. See if you can find interviews with them. Common questions are questions on equipment and why they chose what they did. If you're going for a certain sound, this will give you a lead on attaining it.

That said, my personal choice, with $250 would be to get a low end Gibson or an Ibanez. If the whole $250 is going to the guitar (I'm assuming you already have an amp) you can get a fairly sweet Ibanez. Gibsons run you a bit more, but they're tried, true, and wonderful.

As a few examples, I'll list a Brands and a couple guitarists that play them. These are mostly older artists since I dont' feel newer artists show sufficient mastery of their instrument to manufacture any kind of personal sound or style. Just my opinion though.

Fender
Eric Clapton, Jimi Hendrix

Gibson/Les Paul
Jimmy Page (Led Zeppelin), Slash (Guns N Roses)

Ibanez
Joe Satriani, Steve Vai, John Petrucci (Dream Theater)

Also, don't forget to check the used market. $250 could you get you a smokin deal on a great used guitar. Just get a 2nd opinion when you look at it.

Good luck on your decision

Well, take this as a bit of a contrarian opinion.

I've been a strat guy for about a decade now. Recently my tele has been getting more play time, but I still own two strats. It depends upon what you like and what music you like to play. I'm not much for metal, so I really can't stand Floyd Roses, and I'm not fond of humbuckers. Most of my guitars have single coil pickups, and I like the definition you get.

However, none of that really matters right at this minute. The important thing is getting you a decently made guitar that plays well, stays in tune and (don't underestimate the importance) that you like the look of. You want a quality instrument that will stick with you for a while, but it's got to be something that makes you want to pick it up and play.

If I were putting together a $250 starter kit for a beginner, I believe I would spend as much of that money as possible on a guitar, and find a little used Peavey solid state amp in a pawn shop for $50 or so. The Peaveys won't convince anyone that you're playing through a Marshall stack, but they're dependable, pretty bulletproof, commonly available, and cheap.

For a guitar, the best ones I've seen lately for cheap have been Yamaha Pacificas. Solid wood bodies (important), decent necks and fretwork.

Yamaha Pacifica 012 (http://www.yamaha.com/cgi-win/webcgi.exe/DsplyModel/?gEGU00004PAC012)

I'd also look hard at Fender's Squire line, they've gotten quite a bit better in recent years.

Squier Standard Fat Strat (http://www.squierguitars.com/gear/gear.php?partno=0321700)

If you have a friend that plays, bring that friend with to the stores. Have them check out whatever you're looking at to make sure it plays well.

For Oatasgai, I have a couple suggestions. Not knowing much about the situation, I'll just give you my standard "keep it in tune" advice. For guitars with trems, it's especially important, but I do it to all my guitars.

Lubricate all points of the guitar that will contact the string other than the tuners. At Radio Shack, you can buy some liquid Teflon in a syringe-type tube - that's GREAT stuff for this. Put a drop in each slot of the nut, and a drop on each saddle at the contact point. This will really help keep the strings from binding. If you can't find this stuff, use a little graphite (pencil lead) or even a small amount of Vaseline. Just something to keep it slippery.

No insult intended, just trying to be helpful...

The noise that your friend is referring to is 60-cycle hum, I'm guessing. Single-coil pickups will be affected by 60-cycle hum to some extent. It's bad under fluorescent lights or especially by neon beer signs. This hum is why "hum-bucker" pickups were invented. A humbucker is two single coil pickups wound opposite with opposite magnet polarities. This causes the 60-cycle interference to be cancelled.

Do you need them? I dunno. Personally, I don't care for humbuckers. There are folks that love them and sound great with them. Some Fenders come with humbuckers, but Fender is known for its single-coil pickups. Probably the most important thing to think about is what style of music you want to play. Single coil pickups are great for blues, country, rockabilly, some classic rock and possibly jazz. Humbuckers are really good at classic rock, metal, modern rock, and jazz.

If your tastes lie towards modern rock, you're probably best off gettting a guitar with humbuckers. If you're more into blues (Stevie Ray Vaughan) or country, look towards a single coil guitar.

As far as the Ibanez you mention, I have not played any of them. It's likely a korean made model. Nothing wrong with Korean guitars, I own one. However, the progression is normally seen as American>Mexican (or Japanese) > Korean > Indonesian (or Chinese). I'm sure the Ibanez would be playable, if it floats your boat.

Just one more point on the new vs. used - your first guitar is likely not going to be your last. If you really get into it, you'll probably find yourself moving into another instrument in a year or two. A friend once told me "buy used and play for free". What that means is you're likely not to lose much money on a used guitar, as long as you don't beat the snot out of it (or cover it with stickers, or drill huge holes in it). With a new guitar, you probably won't ever get back what you paid for it.

I'm probably making this FAR more complicated than I should. Guitar building has advanced enough in recent years that it's hard to find an unplayable dog out there. Just about anything you choose with a brand name is going to be at least playable.

I'm sure you'll do fine and really enjoy playing.
