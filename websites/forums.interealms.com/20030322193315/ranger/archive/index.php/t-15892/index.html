Damage in Everquest

In EQ, Damage relies on a two component combat formula. The exact details of these is not hugely important so I will outline the important factors as they effect your EQ experience.

The two components are the chance of a mob To Hit (commonly referred to as "Avoidance") and the damage the mob does when it hits (commonly referred to as Mitigation).

Mob chance To Hit

We are now in the position where hundreds of analysed logs exist which examine various aspects of the game. But one over-riding conclusion can be made from any examination of these. No matter your Agility or AC or anything else, the same Class and Level character will be hit the *exact* same percentage of the time by the same mob.

This is important. Because it basically makes discussion about AC very clear. Avoidance CANNOT be changed by a player (other than specific AA abilites mentioned below) as it is a fixed value based on the class you are playing, your level and the mob you're fight (its ATK/Level, whichever, or both, is irrelevant).

One of the other conclusions you can make, by examining all the evidence, is that Monks have the best chance of a mob missing them. Block will avoid a hit exactly twice as much as Parry and the innate To Hit percentage before Block/Parry/Dodge/Riposte is lower anyway. This confirms what we will all have seen - in an XP group, high AC Monks destroy Warriors in terms of tanking abilities.

Mob Damage

The most interesting thing to come out of the various analyses which have been done is that mobs can only hit for 20 discrete values with their regular swings (we can ignore Bash/Kick, they are the same but with different values attached). Every single mob in the game will only ever hit you for one of 20 individual values.

Those analysing this have also seen that the 20 values are a linear progression and can be expressed as a value multiplied by an integer between 1 and 20. This value is described as DAMAGE INTERVAL - or DI.

The actual hit the mob connects for will be a formula which includes the above portion plus a DAMAGE BONUS (DB). We can express *all* possible mob hits, therefore, with the following equation :-

Damage = DB + (DI * X) where X is an integer between 1 and 20.

All mobs in the game have a specific DB and DI. This varies wildly, and provides some differentiation.

There is a wild variation in this. And as I'll go on to discuss, the variation can make a big difference in the way the mob can be fought.

Now, unlike To Hit, players *can* effect this. Why? Because the integer used in the formula (X) is determined by your Armour Class. In reality, it is only effected by *part* of your Armour Class - but we can ignore it in the interests of clarity. It *also* means that because there are only 20 possible values, once most of the values for this Integer (based on your AC) are grouped at the low or high end, raising or lowering your AC will stop having a noticeable effect *for that mob*.

Other mobs will still show a difference based on your AC value but for a single mob, there is a Collar and Cap on the amount of reduction to the damage taken that your AC will offer. This is what leads to the ultra-common mis-conception that AC is "capped".

Conclusions so far

1. You cannot effect your Avoidance. The mob will always hit you the same as anyone else of your Class and Level
2. The mob can only hit for 20 values
3. AC will reduce the damage taken when the mob hits you
4. Below a certain level of AC, the damage taken will stop rising
5. Above a certain level of AC, the damage taken will stop falling

AA, Defensive and Stonestance

Now, this is fairly straightforward. But today we have some additional skillsets which effect the way this basic formula operates and udnerstanding how this works will help with the way mobs are dealt with

Combat Agility - this will effect the mobs To Hit. There isn't much more to say about this other than that it is the only player choice that can be made which will change the Avoidance between two players of the same class and level.

Combat Stability - this will effect the Integer. It appears to work in the way AC does - it changes the distribution curve for the Integer. This means that Stability will not change the maximum hit a mob can land. It also means that for very high level "uber" mobs which are hitting "over the top" and your AC is not high enough to start mitigating, that Combat Stability is unlikely to be useful. In other words, Combat Stability can make a good AC a little better or make a nearly adequate AC into a good one. But it will *not* make a poor AC useful and it will not lower the damage taken when your AC is already excellent for the mob you are fighting.

Defensive - Now, this is important. Defensive basically Divides the Integer by 2. This means that for most mobs - where the DI is about a tenth of the DB, the damage taken will be reduced by roughly one third. It also means that for mobs with a low DB and high DI, Defensive can make a much larger difference. *But* it also means that mobs with a high DB and low DI, the effect of Defensive can be very small.

Stonestance - this is almonst identical to Defensive, except it divides the DI by 10 instead of 2 and only lasts for 10 seconds. What it means is that for mobs with a low DB and high DI, Monks can take virtually no damage while Stonestance is up.

From agility below 75, to wisdom/intelligence point/mana ratio at various softcaps (200 wis/int, 255 wis/int etc), you WILL find diminishing returns in just about every stat in Everquest. Even some stats like +mana and damage below a certain level have or at least HAD at one point, hardcaps. Both exist, both are well documented. This is to make certain that certain classes are going to be better at things than others, that no caster will ever tank like a warrior, that no uberclass will emerge.
