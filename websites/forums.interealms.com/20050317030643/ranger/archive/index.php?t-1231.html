Salesmen, especially in the used automobile industry, are notorious for working several deals on the same piece of merchandise expecting most of them to fall through. When they have a sweet car, they will play one buyer against another trying to jack the price up. Occasionally they make up other buyers to see how bad you want the car.

Good for walking away from the lot and letting them know that they have forever lost one potential customer. Consider writing the owner and documenting what happened on the lot. Name names, tell the exact truth and talk about how it felt to have the scumbag lying jerk call you a liar to try and save himself.

Business is business, but a major part of any business that wants to stick around is keeping to your word. I remember one time walking away from a car deal because I caught the salesman lying to me, went back to get my own car, which was going to be a trade-in, and found another salesman trying to sell it to someone else on the lot.

As I was berating the idiot, the people who were looking at it left the lot so they lost two sales and got bad word of mouth that day.

Personally, I follow the credo of never trusting any salesperson who is selling something used. Here's my basic rules:
1)If you aren't a mechanic or knowledgeable about automobiles always get a neutral third party to access the vehicle before even thinking about purchasing it. If you don't know someone who can help you out with this frequently you can take it to a service station and have it looked over (sometimes free, sometimes for like $50.)
2)Always get it in writing. If it isn't in writing weaseling may occur. Verbal deals for automobiles are notoriously sketchy at car dealerships.

I've never actually bought a vehicle from a dealership because I don't trust them enough. Too many times I've watched car dealers try to take advantage of friends of mine and their lack of mechanical knowledge. I find my cars via the local newspaper or Auto Trader. Sure it may take a month to find the right deal but you usually get a much better idea of the history of the car since you are actually talking to the owner rather than a greasy salesman.

I was 20, having just walked out of college and gotten a killer job, and goin to every single ford dealer in San Diego County looking for the one specific truck I wanted. (A 1999+ ranger 4x4 with a manual transmission). NO dealers had it. They said that trucks fitting that description were incredibly popular and sold instantly.

Finally, I find a dealership with one in stock. As I get there, someone is closing the deal on it. A nice salesman offers to try and BEAT the other party in filing closing papers, but I decline and leave the lot discouraged.

The next morning I get a call from the salesman saying the sale had fallen through because the buyer was buying it for his son, who didn't have a driver's license yet.

I drive down there again. I take the truck for a test drive. It was PERFECT. Had a whole 14000 miles on it. Had every single feature a ranger can have, minus running boards and the automatic transmission, neither of which I wanted. I said I wanted it, and we sat down to work out a loan.

I had no pay stub yet. I had no proof I was employed. My sole resource was being able to put down a $5000 deposit. It took them several phone calls to several banks, but they managed to find me a car loan. It was at a pretty high interest rate, 12%, and over six years, but it worked -- I used that loan to establish AAA credit and refinanced after 6 months to a much lower rate.

Just writing that not all used car salesmen are evil, although my story is a bit scary, that a 19 year old with $5000 to his name and no proof of employment can get a $22000 car loan. Still, a damned good experience for a first-time car buyer. I was pretty lucky.
