Cane...my good man...let me offer some advice:

I, in the not too distant past, started a hard core strength and cardio regime. You must limit yourself when you start. You have a lot of energy right now, and you will feel the "want" to push yourself. Do not.

I suggest not even measuring how far you run. Distance is not a good communicator of how well you are doing when it comes to a cardio program. What you should be doing is timing your runs. Start with 10 minutes with a 2 minute cool down. That is the way to go.

The end result, after about a month to a month in a half would be you, running for about half and hour with a 2 minute cool down. On average, emphasis on average, the body does not start to "burn fat" during cardio excercise until about 20 minutes into the workout. After the 20 minute "barrier", the body is burning around 95% fat for energy. That is why 30 minute jogs are the pwn!!! :)

Good luck, and if you need any ideas on other things to do, call me or pm me...I knows a few things :)

Cane, I have to agree with Kluden here, you should measure your time not your distance, after all some days you are going to be moving slower than others, but your body is still benefiting from the exercise.

Also, I cannot stress enough the importance of stretching! In addition to my daily workout routine, I stretch 3-4 times a day. In the morning, before my workout, after my workout, and before I go to bed. The stretching before working out will keep you from accidentally pulling muscles, you don't want to get shin splints from running. Stretching after your workout, and in the morning, and evenings, will help you stay limbered up and keep your muscles from getting as sore. (There are other advantages to this too, such as you learn that the more limber you become the more you can twist and contort into all kinds of positions during your sexual encounters, lol) Anyways, moving on, I am sorry, but that saying no pain, no gain is for the birds. Yes you should have a little muscle tension, minor aches, after all you are working muscles you haven't in awhile, but if you push yourself to the point that you are "hurting" then you are doing more damage than good.

You need to also keep in mind that your body burns the most fat when you are between 70-80% of your target heart rate. Don't push yourself over that 80% we don't want you to have a heart attack. A good link with a target heart rate calculator to find an estimate of your target heart rate is:
http://stevenscreek.com/goodies/hr.shtml Check your heart rate about every 10mins during your workout. If it is to high, back off some, if it is to low, push harder.
