Quoting:
"The authority of Congress to enact a law such as DOMA, however, is specifically and clearly granted in the Constitution: 'And the Congress may by general laws prescribe the manner in which such acts, records, and proceedings shall be proved, and the effect thereof.' One may disagree with Congress's decision, but there should be no question of their authority in these matters."

And this is where the rubber meets the road, insofar as our current disagreement is concerned. The U.S. Constitution was intended to be a limiting document. The history of the creation of the Constitution is replete with instances of the following litany: "If a power is not specifically granted to the government, then it is specifically prohibited." For the purposes of explication, I direct you to the infamous "general Welfare" clause in Article I, Section 8 of the U.S. Constitution:

Section. 8. The Congress shall have Power To lay and collect Taxes, Duties, Imposts and Excises, to pay the Debts and provide for the common Defence and general Welfare of the United States; but all Duties, Imposts and Excises shall be uniform throughout the United States;
As you are well aware, the "general Welfare" clause, like the "commerce clause," has been used to justify a variety of unConstitutional schemes. As the argument goes, "the Constitution gives Congress the power to provide for the general Welfare, and thus Congress is authorized to amass funds for <insert favorite pork-barrel scheme here>."

However, immediately following that clause is a list of specific, individual powers granted to Congress (e.g. Establishing Post Offices and post Roads). And in this list we note something curious: There is an enumerated list of specific powers related to the Defense of the United States (e.g. Provide and maintain a Navy). The curious part stems from the fact that in the initial clause of Section 8, Congress was granted power to "provide for the common Defence."

Thus, there are two ways in which we can view the "common Defence" portion of Section 8:

  1. The Framers granted Congress the power to provide for the "common Defence" of the U.S., and then turned around and, in a wholly redundant gesture, granted them the additional powers to provide a Navy, raise an Army, declare war, etc.
  2. The Framers granted Congress the power to provide for the "common Defence" of the U.S., and then went a bit further and specifically enumerated what providing for the "common Defence" entailed.
All the evidence points to #2 above. By specifically listing "provide and maintain a Navy," "declare war," and so forth, the Framers were simply saying "these enumerated items are what we mean when we say that Congress has the power to 'provide for the common Defence.'"

Which brings us back to the "general Welfare" clause. A similar argument holds for the "general Welfare" clause as holds for the "common Defence" clause: Congress was not granted broad powers to provide for the "general Welfare" of the U.S.. Instead, the Framers specifically enumerated in the subsequent clauses of Section 8 what they meant by "general Welfare" in the first clause.

Which brings us back to Article IV, Section 1:

Section. 1. Full Faith and Credit shall be given in each State to the public Acts, Records, and judicial Proceedings of every other State. And the Congress may by general Laws prescribe the Manner in which such Acts, Records and Proceedings shall be proved, and the Effect thereof.
An examination of the second clause of Section 1 reveals that Congress has the Constitutional authority to do two things: (1) prescribe how a citizen may prove to Vermont that he was married in Montana (for example); and (2) what the effect of such proof will be. However, nowhere in the second clause is Congress granted the power to nullify the first clause.

Doubtless this will raise the question of "but it says they can prescribe the 'Effect thereof.' What does that mean if it doesn't mean they can choose 'no effect whatsoever?'" Again, recall the litany "any power not specifically granted is specifically prohibited." To say that the "Effect thereof" portion allows Congress to choose "no effect whatsoever" is to say that the "general Welfare" clause gives Congress broad powers to promote the "general Welfare," whatever that may mean at the time.

The history and proceedings of the creation of the U.S. Constitution clearly implies that, had the Framers wanted to grant Congress the authority to completely nullify the first clause of Article IV, Section 1, they would have specifically granted that authority somewhere in the Constitution. The fact that they did not do so indicates that, from a Constitutional standpoint, Congress doesn't have that authority.
