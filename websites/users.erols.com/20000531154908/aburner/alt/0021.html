I think his point was that whether you believe in God or not isn't something that happens at a philosophical level. You just do or don't, and he doesn't. It isn't a matter of objecting philosophically.

I have a friend who's the least defensive atheist I ever met. He'll cheerfully admit that the arguments both for and against God's existence are inconclusive. (In most cases, he feels both sets of arguments are nonsense, and I agree with him.) He just, quite simply, doesn't get it where a supreme being is concerned. He compared it to trying to describe color to someone who was colorblind, or light to a blind man. (I am not giving his name because some of his fellow atheists would tear him a new orifice for saying things like this.) ;>

Me -- I'm a believer and always have been, despite having been raised by two agnostics. It took quite a few years to figure out that what I'd always felt and sensed had a name -- God -- but it's always been there. And trying to convince me otherwise would work about as well as trying to convince me there is no such thing as green, or as the sun.

That doesn't prove anything, of course. It's always possible that I'm delusional, for example. (Or that we're all delusional, or....) But, in my case, believing in God is the simplest and cleanest explanation of what I perceive and live with.
