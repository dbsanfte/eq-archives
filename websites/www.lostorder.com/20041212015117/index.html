The Elder Scrolls IV: Oblivion Interview

By: Freejack @ 15:27

I found out a former co-worker of mine is going to be a producer (as well as several artists I used to work with) on this game, which got me looking into what it's all about. I enjoyed many parts of Morrowind, though not all of it. I'm real glad to see that Bethesda is really going to a lot of effort to make Oblivion something truly special. GameBanshee interviews the Gavin Carter, the game's producer. Here's a clip from the interview:

GB: What notable changes, additions, or enhancements do you have planned for character creation and advancement?

Gavin: Character creation in most RPGs is an abstract process. You stare at menus, pick some options, roll some dice, and a character is spat out at your feet. The entire process relates very little to the experience of playing the game. You're largely taking guesses at what skills or stats might be fun or useful to you before you play. In Morrowind, we tried to move away from that abstraction by having character generation be an actual section of the game you experience.

With Oblivion, we're taking that concept even further. Our character generation sequence takes place as a flight from the emperor's assassins, and as you play through the opening, you get to experience different aspects of the game before you commit to a character type. Naturally, all the old options for creating your own custom class will be making a return as well, all wrapped up into this fluid experience.

Read the full interview here.

First Impression of Vampire tM: Bloodlines

By: Ganelon @ 11:48

I wasn't going to get this game until I read a review of it on Cinescape.com which described it as more roleplay and less FPS. The review then described it as a roleplay game of the caliber of KOTOR, and this peaked my interest. So, I abtained and installed it last night.

On the first bootup, I got this refresh error on a black screen, which I was able to resolve following some instuctions on the games support page. It's a bad way to start, but I got thru it relatively quickly.

You start the game by creating your character. You first pick the sex and then the Vampire clan, and this determines your appearance in the game. There are no further customizations your toon's appearance, which is a minus IMO.

The Clan doesn't just pick your appearace, though. It actually defines your attribute maximums and the ability/Dicipline path your character will take.

Once the clan is chosen, you then distribute your points on your character sheet, which holds the starting values for your attributes, skills and feats. As you gain experience, you enhance your character and become a more powerful creature.

Then the story begins.

You are a newly made vampire, whose 'Sire' created you without the permission of the city's Prince. Thus, your Sire is executed by that Prince, who mercifally decides to spare your life and provide instructions on the rules of the Kindred.

Rule #1: Maintain the Masquerade. Keep the existance of Vampires a secret from humanity. If you show off in front of humans, the Vamp Hunters will start showing up... and if you go too far, other Vampires will destroy you. Game over.

Rule #2: Maintain your Humanity. You must keep the demon beast in check. Wanton acts of violence feed the beast. Over-feeding, which is draining the prey dry, is definately a no-no that will let the beast take over. If the beast gains control, you are destroyed.. for a beast cannot follow rule #1. Game over.

After being let go buy Prince Lacroix, you are given quests to prove yourself worthy of his mercy. Along the way, you will get other quests that may or may not follow the main storyline. The progress in the game is non-linier. You can basically run around and do whatever quests you want, in whatever order you wish to do them. The quests reveal alot of the Kindred culture, as well as the White Wolf World of Darkness setting.

One note: this is not a game you want to play around the kids. Profanity, partial nudity and dark themes are part of the experience. It is a game for adults.

Interaction with NPCs is much like in KOTOR. You have canned replies with various tones and attitudes that will cause the NPCs to react appropriately. The vampire NPCs are rich in character and are all very unique. The human NPCs are not so unique and are mostly food opportunities, except for the quest givers and storyline characters.

In the early game, there is not much combat. It is mostly exploration and storyline progression. This surprised me, as I expected the game to be like Deus Ex, where it is mostly a FPS with a storyline presented in cutscenes. Bloodlines is more like KOTOR in that you accomplish quest goals that may not be combat related. Some of the higher quests are done very well, with many miniquests that lead you to the main goals.

One of the early quests you are given is to clean out the ghost that haunts an old abandoned hotel. This is a detailed creepfest of a quest, with bags of spooky atmosphere and supernatural phenomona to keeps you looking over your shoulder... cuz you'd swear you just saw something moving over there. It's one of the best made game experiences I've played in years.

Technically, this game is a stunner. It uses the Halflife 2 engine to superb effect. All the bells and whistles. The sound is also top notch. The choice of music is appropriate and will round out the immersive experience.

To sum it all up, I recommend this game for those itching for a single player roleplaying game. If you are looking for a FPS, I'd go elsewhere.

Atriarch Developer Interview

By: Freejack @ 11:16

FileFront interviews Atriarch's lead designer, Serafina Pechan asking questions about World Fusion (the name of their company), how they have so far survived the six years of development, potential pitfalls, and:

Q: What would you say Atriarch successfully does better than other MMORPGs?

A: Two things... uh... make that three

* Player Construction
* Player Influence
* Epic Alien Culture and Atmosphere

1. Player Construction... We have a unique player-building system that is only found in Atriarch! Players are given building blocks and can piece their own style of building together... not just a pretty structure, but a functional building! For example, if 100 different players build a power plant to supply energy, then there will be 100 different types of power plants, not 100 cookie cutter buildings. The make-up of each power plant can be different as long as the structural and functional requirements are met. My guess is that other games haven't done it yet, because they haven't figured out how to have so much variety without killing bandwidth... hehe... we have ;-)

2. Player Influence... What a player does in the world matters! We believe that when players have the opportunity to make a difference beyond their own individual player-character, then that is when we truly move from being a "game" to being a "world"! When the world responds to your character's actions in the world, that's cool. Knowing your character can make a lasting influence to the culture, story and politics, no matter how small or large, that's compelling! I want to be in a world like that, so I'm making it!

3. Epic Alien Culture and Atmosphere... This ain't no human game! There are a lot of really fun games out there. Yet, few present new storylines and cultural genres. We all grew up on science fiction and fantasy stories that were bizarre and different and vivid and complete. We want to recreate the sense epicness. We want to transport people someplace new and stimulating and weird and believable! November 17, 2004

California Guitar Trio pays a visit to LOA Member

By: Brimstone @ 10:20

As reported by Fryn, here.

As most of you know, my husband and former LOA member Andy has brain cancer and has been blind and bedridden for almost a year now, having lost almost every ability he used to have- to run, to teach, to play in his bands... even saying yes and no can be difficult now.

One of Andy's favorite bands is the California Guitar Trio, a band formed out of Robert Fripp's League of Crafty Guitarists. Excellent, excellent musicians Andy got me hooked on before we were married. Well, I keep up with their tour schedule and releases through their website, and a few months ago saw that they were playing in Lexington and then Birmingham with a day in between. I thought maybe, just maybe, one of them could come visit Andy and play a bit if they happened through Louisville or better yet got a gig here.

So I e-mailed them and asked if they would consider it. They wrote back right away to say that they would be glad to play for Andy, and they did- all of them. The whole band came and played a private concert for my husband today. Andy was as thrilled as I have seen him in months. He was smiling, nearly crying - he can do so very little any more, but thanks to these guys, today was different. If Andy can't go to the concert, well by God this time the concert came to Andy. These guys were all so gracious, so humble, so unassuming, I could hardly believe it.

I just got back from the wonderful concert they played to a full house. They dedicated their last song to "their friend Andy, who wasn't able to be here tonight..." I'd held it together for (nearly) the whole show, until then.

There are so many things that make me think the human race is going straight down the toilet- people getting impatient over parking places; cussing out store clerks for missing buttons off their Tommy jackets - stuff that makes you want to just be a hermit. But then there are these guys, who without any convincing, question, or promise of reward, said and honestly WERE glad to come and play, to bring joy to a few people at a time. For us- Andy, me, our son, and for Andy's parents, this is a story that will last our lifetimes. I just had to share.

If anyone wants to know any more about this band, e-mail me and/or visit their site at www.cgtrio.com - I owe them every plug I can give them, for their talent and for their compassion.

Peace,
Jenni aka Fryn

We recently received a response from CGT:

11/17/04 - Thank you for your kind email messages. We had no idea the impact of our visit to Andy's home. When I saw Andy smile while we were playing our guitars at his bedside, I knew we were doing a good thing.

Very best wishes from the California Guitar Trio to Andy, Jenni, their families, friends and all their friends at lostorder.com - Paul Richards
