Pirates of the Burning Sea Interview

By: Freejack @ 15:05

HomeLan interviews Flying Lab's Russell Williams about the studio's pirate-themed MMOG. Here's a bit of a clip:

HomeLAN - Have you expanded on the game's amount of professions, classes and skills since your initial game announcement?

Russell Williams - Our approach from the beginning has been on professions and skills, not classes and levels. A profession is a job with benefits and responsibilities, not a skill package, and that conception has remained intact. You can be a merchant, a pirate, a naval officer, a privateer, or a free trader, and all of those are jobs you can get, keep, lose, quit, rejoin, or change over the life of your character; doing so has no effect on your character's skills or abilities. They aren't tied to skills at all, except insofar as some professions give you special opportunities to learn relevant skills.

Our skill system has expanded into a two-tiered approach. The first tier is basic skills that relate to being the captain of a ship. These are always-on modifications to your ship's stats, to which you and your officers can contribute in varying amounts. The second tier is active-use techniques, which are more advanced and will be introduced as a free content upgrade after release. You'll need to master the basic skills before tackling the techniques.

D&D Online Q&A

By: Freejack @ 23:51

So Computer and Video Games has an interview with Turbine's lead designer, Ken Troop. I've made no secrets that I have my concerns with Turbine's products, but maybe there is yet hope...

What valuable lessons have you learned from developing and launching Asheron's Call's one and two, and how will/do these influence your work on Dungeons & Dragons Online?

Troop: Too many to list, but I'll pick a couple of the big ones. One lesson that has shaped our dev philosophy extensively is that we need to provide fun that isn't strictly tied to achievement.

Achievement is fun on its own. But if the means of achievement isn't fun, you've only made half a game. A lot of massively multiplayer games have fallen into that trap.

With D&D Online we're trying to make a game that will be fun to play, even if at the end of the night you didn't score a huge pile of loot or a new level. Just playing needs to be worth it.

The second has more to do with our process of making games. We really pushed ourselves to get a playable version of the game much earlier than we have done in the past, and to iterate on it.

The result is that we've been able to see and assess the changes we make, rather than debating about abstractions on sheets of paper. We've got a long way to go, but the game today is already feeling like a game, and we think it's a fun one.
