Author Topic:   Lost little songbird, far from home
Vaelyna
 posted 03-30-2001 10:34 AM               
Nothing much had ever really been expected of her. She was loved by her father and knew she had half-siblings out there somewhere, but other than that she had no family. Her father worked, day after day, coming home smelling like soot and the downtown alleys. She could remember the shivering cold gripping her tiny toes as she curled up in the little room they called their house, making its way up her legs and crisscrossing over her torso, to make her tremble and cough all night, even as she slept.

She didn't remember her father very well, but did remember anything he brought home was for her. A slightly charred blanket full of holes, but delightfully soft against her skin; a little dolly, missing a leg and half her hair, but with real glass eyes; a feast of warm bread, buttered and sweet, with the sharp tang of garlic; all this she could see in her mind's eye, but of her father's face not even a glimmer of memory remained.

His voice, however, remained the clearest memory of that time. A soft voice, with a gentle lilt. He sang to her sometimes, when he thought she was asleep. He rarely spoke, but every time he did, she loved to listen. He didn't call her by name-indeed, she hadn't had a name-though if he were to address her, he would use the words, "My little songbird."

It wasn't very long after her sixth birthday her entire world would change.

She remembered her father started coming home with a cough different from her own; it was worse, somehow. His voice was rough, as though stiff bristles had been swiped through his mouth. Most of all, she remembered a smell unfamiliar to her then, but familiar now; brandy. Her father changed, too. He became sullen, no longer singing to her or bringing her home a little treat. Because of this, she learned to be quiet and hide else her father's internal darkness would be aimed toward her. She never did really speak, unless he asked her a question, and even then her replies were monosyllables.

The night her father didn't return home first brought a shameful surge of relief and a tinge of puzzled worry. As the night slowly progressed and a silver sphere hung in the sky with all its gently luminous glory, relief faded into very real worry. Her father was never late. Where was he?

She paced the room-the tiny attic of a little tavern, ramshackle and old, so old the ground creaked with every step. The window was so grimy, accumulating the mess of dirt over the years, that she couldn't see through it anymore.

Where was her father? Why was he late?

When footsteps sounded on the stairs beyond her home's door, she ran toward it with a glad cry, opening the thick slab of wood and barreling down the stairs with a breathless "Papa!" tumbling from her lips.

Only it wasn't her father. A small, skinny man-the innkeeper, she remembered-took her by the ear and looked her over. She winced, and he laughed. "Ye'll fetch a good price, princess, ye've the looks of ye mam. Yer papa ain't here no more, see, and ye me prop'tee now. I've no need for a lil kid like ye, so's 'm gonna sell ye, see?" When she didn't answer, he pulled her by the hair.
"Let's get ye cleaned, I need th'best price outta ye, brat. Damn mutes," he added under his breath.
