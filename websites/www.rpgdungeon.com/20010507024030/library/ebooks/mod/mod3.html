The Mark of Destiny

Chapter 3



Quint waited behind the tent, crouched in the shadows, grateful for having worn all black that fateful day the Lyceum had been attacked. By straining her ears, she could barely make out the words being said inside, but try she did.

"What say the gods, Eldson?" she heard a voice command. She was positive the red haired man had said the words -- his temperament certainly matched his hair if that were the case.

"They're unpleased, my prince," replied another voice. "But they are understanding. They do not blame you. They blame that Master of Arms for not keeping his students in line. You had no way of knowing she would not be there, and they will forgive you this once. But if you fail another time, I don't think they will be as forgiving."

Quint's eyes had grown wide at the mention of the Master of Arms. Surely they aren't talking about Master Lyon!

"That was always a failing of Lyon's," the first voice said. "He catered too much to his students' will and desire. Instead of requiring discipline, he required nothing." There was a pause. "But that fire I've always admired about him is still there. It still burns as bright as the first day I meet him."

Quint stumbled, and hung onto one of the tent lines to right herself. "Oh gods," she said out loud, terrified at the thought of Lyon being in league with this . . . murderer. It was only then that she realized that inside the tent it had grown quiet. She stood still, seeing if anyone had heard her, when she heard a footstep behind her. Pivoting, she found herself face-to-face with the red haired man.

For a moment, they merged into a powerful figure and both knew that together, nothing could overcome them. The humans, the planet, the universe which surrounded them meant nothing compared to their existence. They grew high enough to reach the clouds . . . and then the feeling burst into a million pieces of scattered light.

"Milord Rowan, are you all right?" A balding man with brown eyes was looking fearfully at the red haired man.

Both Quint and Rowan shook themselves free from the other, claiming their individual thoughts and souls. Quint gathered her wits about her quicker than Rowan, and without really thinking, ran away from the scene. From behind her, she heard Rowan yell for his men to get her.

She wound her way through the trees, making sure not to make too much noise, or at least not enough that those chasing her could hear over the noise they were making. Suddenly Lyon was in front of her, the moonlight glancing off his pale skin, making him appear like a demented angel. Somehow she managed to stop before crashing into him.

"Master Lyon," she exclaimed breathlessly, momentarily forgetting that he was her enemy. "We thought you were dead!"

"Anything you've heard has been greatly exaggerated," he bowed grandly. "But you know, that was exactly what Brian said what I first saw him."

Quint turned pale. "You've seen Brian? If you've hurt him . . . "
Lyon laughed. "I don't believe you're in the position to be giving ultimatums."

She turned to run away, but Rowan and his men suddenly filled in the open spaces. Rowan approached her cautiously, as if afraid of their earlier feelings.

"So you're the one the gods fear." His voice was neutral.

Their green eyes met. "Why?" she softly questioned.

The prince tore his gaze away. "It's up to them as to whether or not they tell you." He took her arm. "Why don't you come with me and see what they say?"

Angrily, she jerked her arm out of his grasp. "Why don't they come here?"

Immediately, Lyon was there, wrenching her arm behind her back, and then forcing it up. She gasped in pain, suddenly sure it would break if he moved it another inch.

"You're not in any position to be ordering people, or gods, around," he hissed in her ear. He pushed her forward, still tightly holding onto her arm. "Follow the prince."

Swiftly they made their way back to Rowan's tent, where the balding man awaited their arrival. The moment she walked through the door, blazing red eyes turned upon her, studying her every reaction.

"Quint." The voice was unnerving and sent a shudder up her spine. "Do you truly not know the power you possess?"

"What should we do with her, my gods? Should we kill her?" Lyon asked the gods who inhibited Eldson's body.

"No," a faint grin touched the corners of Eldson's mouth. "Wait until we get to Pathageron. The man who calls himself king will kill her, or we will kill him." And then the red eyes flared up, burning its way into her soul. Gratefully she fell into unconsciousness.

 

Brian awoke with a jolt, suddenly back in his own body. His arms had cramped up sometime during the night and he unconsciously rubbed them, trying to get the blood flowing once more. It only took a slight while before full realization hit him -- he was free. Curiosity stole over him, and he looked at the rope which had held him. Squinting to get a better look, he noticed that where it had been severed, it looked as if an animal had chewed on it. He wondered what could have possessed an animal to chew . . .

| Quint! | a voice not quite his own roared in his head, but he was thinking of other things and so he did not even notice that the voice sounded different.

Quint! his thought echoed and he dropped the rope he had been holding. Quickly he made his way to the horse Lyon had left behind and mounted. Then he stopped. Where am I going? He knew where he wanted to ultimately end up at from his dream, but he had no idea as to how to get to that point. He screamed in frustration and listened to its echo, a slight grin on his face.

"That sounded nice," he said to a nearby oak tree. He paused, shaking his head. "Talking to trees, oh gods, I'm losing it. Though whether or not I ever had it is a good question." Brian paused again. "Now I'm talking to myself. I'd better get moving before I start holding a political discussion with a bird." Nudging the horse, he started heading north and abruptly stopped. "But where am I going?"

| Wander. | The suggestion sounded as good as anything else he had thought of. At that moment the horse decided it wanted to go off toward the south and Brian made no move to stop her.

Or him, it, whatever this thing is. If she sees some good grass somewhere else, I'm not one to deny her dinner. But how am I going to find my way to Rowan's camp? He feel into a depression, his thoughts dark and heavy.

The horse whickered, pulling him out of the endless circle of guilt he had pulled himself into, and he looked around.

How did I get here? Was his lone thought. The horse had led him straight to the edge of a camp which he instantly recognized. His eyes skirted across the camp, searching for Quint. Oh, like they're just going to leave her out in plain sight? Get with it, stupid bard trainee. He found Rowan's tent and with no better plan, decided to make his way there, sure that Quint would be with the prince.

The next time the moon was hidden behind clouds, a shadowy figure snuck across the dark camp. So far, so good, Brian thought when a hand suddenly grabbed his collar, jerking his feet out from under him.

"Who are ya?" The man's breath reeked of garlic. "What do ya want here?"

"I'm a messenger," Brian quickly made up. "I have a message for the prince."

"Oh, a messenger," the man sneered. "I'm sure." He hauled the trainee to his feet. "But just in case, let's take you to visit his highness. You can give him your message personally." Briskly the soldier led him to Rowan's tent.
A soldier who stood, guarding the entrance to Rowan's tent, raised an eyebrow as they approached.

"Says he's a messenger," the man who held Brian growled. "Watch 'im while I go speak to his highness."

The other soldier nodded, then lifted the flaps so that he could get through. He stood in silence until Rowan recognized his presence.

"What?" the prince looked angry at the interruption.

"Sire, there's a messenger outside who says he has a message for you."

"A messenger? I'm not expecting any messages from anyone." Rowan and Lyon exchanged glances, both curious. "Send him in."

"'mediately, sire." The soldier stepped outside, and then right back in. "He's gone, and the one who was guarding him is dead."

Rowan rose to his feet, his features clouded with rage. "Find him! Have this camp searched. Look in every corner and box. No one is to rest until he's found!"

"Wait," Lyon's voice was calm. "There's no need for that, my prince. I know where he's gone."

 

Always wipe your dagger, Brian recalled Lyon's words as he wiped his dagger on the dead man's tunic. A bloody dagger rusts faster than a clean one. Besides, it just looks bad. He sighed slightly. No one will be able to say that Lyon was a bad teacher, just a traitor. As if being a good teacher could cover his crimes. Shaking his head clear of memories, he stood up, and sprinted a distance away. There was no immediate alarm call so Brian figured that nobody had seen him do the deed. After waiting a few more moments, he started walking toward the middle of the camp, hoping to learn the area and possibly where Quint was being held. Maybe someone will mistake me for a soldier and tell me about the girl who was brought in earlier. Or maybe they'll put me on dish duty.

"So, do you always wander around, looking like a dead fish?" a huge man suddenly appeared by Brian's side. "You must be new here. I'm Harley."

"Kevan," Brian quickly thought up, reluctant to give up his real name. "I just got here today. Would you mind showing me around?"

Harley flashed him a big grin, showing a mouthful of mostly white teeth. "It'll be a little interesting to give a tour in the dark, but we'll stumble around. Follow me." He motioned Brian over to a tent. "This is where a lot of the practice weapons are kept."

Obediently Brian followed the man around the camp, learning its basic layout. After a half position or so, they returned to the middle of the camp where they had started.

"Do you have any other questions?" Harley wandered.

"Well," Brian made himself blush. "I heard about this girl . . . "

"There are no girls here, only men." Harley replied, eyeing the trainee. "Unless your tastes run in that direction."

"Oh, no," Brian corrected. "I heard that earlier today the prince captured a girl. A girl that the gods want him to kill."

"Oh, that girl," Harley replied. "I've heard about that too, though I'm not sure if I believe it or not. In truth, I don't really pay much attention to the gods, hoping that if I leave them alone, that they'll leave me alone. But, the prince does claim he talks to the gods -- perhaps through that priestly looking fellow, Eldson. So there might be some claim to the story and we do seem to have the most uncanny of luck at times . . . " He feel silent, contemplating this thoughts.

"Do you think I could see her?" Brian broke in.

"Why?" Harley looked at the trainee, suddenly suspicious of his intentions.

"Well, you see I made this bet with this guy, William, right? And we bet ten gold pieces on her looks. I say the reason that the gods want her killed is because she's so ugly that nobody can stand to look at her for any length of time. William says that the gods want her killed because she's so beautiful that the want to make her a goddess -- and everyone knows you have to die before becoming immortal." Brian caught his breath. "If you come along with me, you can be the judge."

Harley was laughing. "All right, and then after this, we can talk about other women whom nobody wants to kill." The trainee followed the big man to a tent off to the right of the camp. "In here," the man said, waving him in.

Brian stepped inside, pausing at the entrance to let his eyes adjust.

"Brian," Lyon's voice rang out next to him. "I was getting worried that you weren't going to show up. How nice of you to finally decide to join us."
