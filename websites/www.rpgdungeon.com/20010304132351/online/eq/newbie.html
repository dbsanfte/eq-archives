EverQuest
Newbie Guide



Ima Newbie

So you've read all the reviews, listened to your friends brag about it, upgraded your computer, and finally saved your $50 plus some extra for the monthly fees and bought the EverQuest game. Now what?? Well, that is what I'm about to tell you…

Let me first start by saying this is not going to tell you how to set up speech macros, hot buttons, or even how to make your character move. That is what the Everquest tutorial program, players guide, and handbooks are for and I suggest you make use of them before attempting to play the game. Instead, I wish to give the inexperienced player some tips to help you progress through the lower levels of the game a little easier than many of us others did.

Character Creation

Let's talk about your character selection first since you have to do that before you can play the game. What to choose??? What to choose??? So many races and classes and heck, you even have to decide on a male or female character. Lets start with your sex. NO, I don't mean when you had it last, I mean do you want to be male or female? It does not appear to affect game stats or play whether you are male or female so this should be the easiest choice you have to make at this point. Now that you've picked a gender, the fun begins.

I cannot emphasize enough that you need to choose your character wisely for the style of game play that you are looking for. The wrong selection now means hours of wasted game play if you decide later that you picked poorly. Unfortunately, most bad choices will not show up until much later in the game as your character gains the ability to adventure outside the newbie areas.

After choosing a sex, you're next choice should be if you want to play a character of an evil or good race. My recommendation is to choose a good race for now. You can always start your evil character later when you know much more about game play. The reason I say this is because evil characters have a difficult time traveling outside the city zones that you start in. There are far and away more good races than bad and believe me NPC's will attack you on sight. You have little or no chance of surviving these attacks until you obtain much higher levels (as if the higher level monsters weren't bad enough). So for now lets rule out being a dark elf, troll, or ogre. Although, I do encourage you to play one later on to enhance the RPG experience.

Now we have ruled out 3 races for your starting character. Which of the others will you choose? Well let's make that decision after you decide what class you want to be. While you will see a lot of class variations, there are really only 3 classes: magic user, multi-class, and fighter. Oh wait, I don't see the multi-class, fighter, or magic user listed on the chart. Well they are there just under different names and let's discuss them now.

Magic Users

Classes that I consider magic users are ones that can only wear cloth as armor and are very limited in weapon choice (usually a staff or dagger). They do have weapon and defensive skills, but they fail by comparison even to the multi-classed character, let alone a pure bread warrior. Instead, magic users rely on powerful offensive and defensive spells to win battles for them.

They are normally the most intelligent of their races and that often means the weakest as well. Hit points are very low for magic users as well as the damage they can do with their weapon, which usually means sure death for the magic user who doesn't have enough mana for the job at hand. Intelligence and wisdom are key factors in determining you mana count so be sure to put plenty of extra bonus points in there at the time of creation.

Magic users are the most difficult class to advance in, but in the end they are probably the most powerful. These classes are also the least expensive. The cloth armor that a magic user wears is very inexpensive compared to the other armors available and while some spells at upper levels can cost nearly 30 platinum, (plat) a steel plate breast piece can run 600 plat or more. Some examples of these classes are the magician, wizard, enchanter, and necromancer.

Multi-class

What I consider to be a multi-classed character is any class that uses both magic and warrior skills. Many of them can wear any armor they choose up to iron, and, in the case of the cleric, they can even wear the prized steel plate. The multi-classed characters also have the ability to use most any weapon they choose, the loser in that category being the clerics and shamans who can only use blunt weapons. They also get to cast spells, which is why I call them multi-classed.

The spells of the multi-classed character are not often as damage-oriented as the magic users'. Of the good damage-oriented spells available, the multi classed character will find himself struggling to have enough mana to use them more than a couple of times before relying on their warrior skills to finish the job. Instead, multi-classed spells prove to be much more beneficial in nature. Their spells range from healing, to hit point and armor class buffs, and even to spells for speedier movement. These classes progress a little easier than a magic user due to the higher hit points, better armor, and weapons.

Multi-class characters have the honor of being among the most expensive classes. If buying spells wasn't enough, try putting armor and weapons on your character as well and you'll find yourself being very stingy with the coppers.

Multi-classed characters are well rounded and are always in demand for group hunts due to their healing spells. Those same healing spells help them advance levels faster. While the warrior and magic user are sitting around waiting to heal from the previous fight, the multi-classed character has healed himself and is off to the races again recovering mana while battling it out with the sword or mace. A few examples of multi-classed characters would be the cleric, shaman, ranger, druid, paladin, and others.

Fighters

There only seems to be two fighter classes in EverQuest at this time: the warrior and the monk. (I personally would like to see some more) The warrior and the monk have a good number of hit points compared to the other classes and don't use magic of any sort. Just to give a idea of the hit point advantages: the warrior has about double the hit points of a pure magic user and on average about 10 hit points per level more than a multi-classed character. Well what does that mean? What is the big deal about 10 hit points? In Everquest you can obtain a maximum level of 50. Well you do the math. That's around 500 more hit points for the warrior in the end and lets not even discuss how many more hit points than a magic user he will have.

The nickname for the warrior is "tank" and there is no wonder why. With all their hit points, armor, and lack of intelligence, the name just seems to fit the bill. You'll normally find the warrior on the front line in any battle and is counted on by the other classes to keep monsters off them as they work their magic.

The warrior advances relatively easily due to the large number of hit points, choice of weapon and armor, and the extra damage skills they learn early on. The warrior gets the added skills of kicking, bashing, dodging, dual wield, and parrying just to name a few (some of the multi-classed characters get some of these as well, but never all of them and only at a much higher level than when the warrior learns them). These not only help dish out extra damage but help the warrior avoid it as well.

The warrior class is expensive to arm and equip but at least they don't have to buy spells too. The monk class is probably the least expensive as they don't rely on weapons and armor as much as the warrior does because the monk's body is his weapon and a good one at that. They are to me the most fun to watch in battle at higher levels because they have some of the most fascinating and deadly moves in the game.

A Final Word on a Few Tables

Let's face it - starting a new character in EverQuest can be a tough experience, especially if you don't know a lot about the game. The creation screen alone can be overwhelming if you don't know much about the options available, and many players go through several starting characters before finally finding the right one for their style. Fear not - for this week's eXcursion, I've put together several charts that should provide some guidance for starting out in EQ. I've played the game with every race, and I can assure you these tips come from a wealth of experience.

Since it can be difficult to decide where to begin your game, I've thrown together a chart that tells which cities are good for each race to begin questing in. Below that is a chart listing the names of the three best continent starting points in the game. Finally, I have a chart that provides some of the best class/race combinations with the pros and cons of each to help you pick a character that suits your playing style the best.

Seal Thy Fate

Well, now that you know a little about the different classes it is time to choose one. You want my recommendation? Well, I'm sorry, but I just can't give you one. I have personally played a shaman, warrior, enchanter, and wizard and each have advantages and disadvantages as mentioned above. They have all been very fun to play and perhaps if I had limited myself to only one, that one would be of much higher level. But, I enjoy the change of pace from time to time and I encourage you to try other race/class combinations as well. However, I encourage you to get the feel for the game first before starting a second character.

After choosing a class you will see the available races light up to the left. I recommend choosing the race whose stats best suit the class you have chosen. For example magic users seem best suited to be high elves, gnomes, or the like because of the higher intelligence factors of these races. A barbarian warrior is tough to stop because of the extra strength of that race. Dwarves also make excellent warriors as well as my personal favorite to be a cleric. Humans seem to be the medium that all other races are judged by. What I mean is that a human seems to be average in every way.

So what race are you going to chose? If it comes down to a tough decision, remember that some races have infravision and others do not. All things being equal, I'll take the one with infravision every time. And, after you play the game and see just how dark that screen can get at night or in a hallway, you will too.

Now it is time to spend your bonus points. After choosing a race and class, the game highlights the most important stats for you. For example, if you chose to be a barbarian warrior you would find the strength and stamina stats highlighted in green. Everquest has done this for a very good reason and you would be foolish to ignore it. Spend those bonus points in the areas highlighted as they are crucial to your class. How you spend them, though, is up to you. You can ignore the primary stats altogether, you can dump them all in just one area of your primary needs, or you can spread them around a little. It's really a tough decision to make.

If you are a warrior you can dump most in stamina for better hit points or into strength for higher damage hits and the ability to carry more. Or, you could balance the two out. Again, it is a decision you are going to have to make and I really can't say which is the best route to take except in the case of magic users. I seriously suggest that you put every point you have into mana generating stats like intelligence and wisdom. That is your key to survival in this game.

    TIP: be sure to spend all your bonus points. The game doesn't care if you spend all or none of them and will create your character regardless of how many you use


The next step is to choose a name. You may already have one in mind and if you do, great! Enter your name now and see if the game will accept it. Remember that Everquest is an RPG and common names are not normally allowed through the filter. Also, if the name you have chosen is already taken it will reject yours as well. If you have a problem thinking of a name then use the random name generator that Everquest has graciously provided for some of us less imaginative people.

    TIP: do not use vulgar or otherwise untactful names. Even if you can get it through the filter somehow, the GM's will change it as soon as it is noticed (which won't take long). The name they give you will not be your choice nor will you be able to change it later. It is also likely to be an undesirable name as punishment for your indiscretion.


The starting city for each race is different. Some races even have more than one starting location. Basically, it doesn't matter where you start, as the programmers have done a very good job of creating nice newbie zones at every city. You will not likely want to adventure outside of these zones till you are level 5 either. So make your starting city selection now to finish your character creation.

A Hero's Babysteps

Congratulations, you have just made your first Everquest character and, having finished the tutorial program and read the players guide and handbook, you are ready to start your adventure! So go ahead and select the character you just made and enter the game.

When you first appear in the game you will notice in your inventory a parchment, a book, a weapon of some sort, food, water, and two spells (if your character has the ability to cast spells).

Let me first explain about your food supplies, as the guidebooks don't seem to answer all of the questions that arise. You eat and drink automatically. When your character gets hungry or thirsty the game starts searching your inventory for drinks and food. It will take from the first food and drink group it comes to. Then the game finishes searching your inventory and if you are low on food and drink the game then, in a most annoying way, advises you over and over till you get more. If you run out of food or drink you will not be able to heal naturally in the game anymore. Also, your stamina starts to fall after a length of time. This could prove deadly if you are caught on the road somewhere, so always carry a good supply of food and drink with you.

There are a number of forms that food and drink can come in so don't make the mistake that I did and go searching the vendors for the same type you started with. Any type will do and there doesn't seem to be any real difference in how long they last. So if you buy food and drink, get the cheapest you can find.

There are some classes that can summon their own food and drink. While this saves money, you will have to do it each time you log on and as you progress in levels you will find yourself just buying it instead to save game time. Some classes can also forage for food. This also proves useful, but you might starve to death before you are successful at lower levels. Every class can also fish for their food. But, you will find yourself losing money till you gain a few levels as fishing rods do break pretty easily in this game. Even in the upper levels fishing is a tedious, time consuming act, and I think most folks would rather buy their food and continue adventuring rather than wasting valuable game time trying to catch your next meal.

 

    TIP: as a newbie you will notice that every time you die you get a new set of food and drink. This will happen till you are level 4. So if you get low on food before you reach level 4 just let yourself get killed or attack a guard and they will gladly do it for you in a hurry and you will have a fresh set of food and drink. Also you may want to do this just before you make level 4 so that you have a small supply to last you as long as possible before you have to start shelling out those coppers for them.


Player vs. Player

The next thing I want to discuss is the book you will find in your inventory. This book allows a player to become a PK (player killer) and participate in PvP (player verses player) activities in the game by turning on your PK switch. Essentially that means your name will become red, while all non-PvP players have blue names.

Please note that the system that Everquest has in place now does not allow a player that is PvP to attack a player that is not. If you want huge PvP excitement then you should check out the PvP server that Everquest has created for players like yourself. If, however, you want to just add a little excitement from time to time, then stay on a regular server and play as a PK.

My recommendation, however, is to place that book in the bank for now. You can give it to the Priest of Discord anytime you desire. Why do it now when you are going to lose to anyone you face, anyway? Also, I have noticed some of the cowardly, low life, childish PK's hanging out in the newbie zones killing the just created characters. What pleasure that could bring them I have no idea. Beware of this and wait till you advance some to participate in PvP.

The First Quest

Now lets equip your character, scribe your spells into your spell book, and memorize them (all of this is covered in the tutorial program). After that is done you need to find the guild house for the class you have chosen and give the letter on the parchment to the appropriate person. (The name will appear on the parchment…to read it just right click on the parchment and it will open a screen with the instructions on who to give it to) When you have given your guild master the parchment he will reward you with a small amount of experience and some type of armor. This armor is always some form of cloth like a tattered shirt or a robe if you chose a magic users class. Go ahead and equip that now as well.

    TIP: you will notice while you are at your guild master that you already have 5 practice points you can spend. Some people spend practice points as soon as they get them and it is fine if you do the same. My personal philosophy has been to save those practice points for skill activation at latter levels and also to use them to gain good understanding of trade skills. It seems a waste to use them on things you will gain quickly from fighting such as defense, offense, or a weapon skill.

Having equipped your character with their meager belongings I think now would be a great time to look around the city you are in and learn who sells what and where everything is. You may have found all this out already as some guilds are tough to find. Notice all the items that are for sale and make mental or written notes as to where these items are so you can find them again quickly later on. Be sure to find your bank as you will visit it often. While you are there, you should go ahead and deposit your PvP book. This way you will have that slot available for more treasures.

Also, you will want to talk to the NPC's of the city and see if any of them have quests for you including your guild master. Some quests are quite easy to complete, even for the beginner, and can have big benefits for you. All completed quests yield experience points and most of the time an item of some sort. You may find this item very useful, and, if not, you can always sell it and put the money gained towards something else. You will also notice your faction standing increase as you complete quests. This will help you later in the game because, as your faction with the people of the city you are in increases, the prices the merchants charge for goods decrease and the merchants also pay more for goods you sell them. A higher faction rating may also make some quests available to you that were not previously.

Adventuring

Now that you have your character equipped, learned of a few quests, and explored your home city it is time to start your adventuring. Having looked around the city you already know where the gates (or in the case of Wood Elves, lifts) are. As you exit the city you will always find guards posted there. Those guards serve several purposes. They are there to protect the city from would be invaders and to give its citizens protection from the dangers of the outside world. Remember where they are and take notes of any others you might see while adventuring because they will save your life time and time again. For the first level or two I don't recommend leaving the site of the guards so that if you get into trouble you can run to them and they will eliminate the foe you were unable to handle.


The basic strategy for the first couple of levels is simple. Kill. Rest. Kill. While that strategy never changes much, it does take on new meaning in later levels, but we are not here to discuss that. We want to help you become able to adventure outside your starting area by gaining levels and learning how to fight. Here's how:



The Warrior

The warrior will have the easiest time fighting as all he has to do is select the target and attack it while occasionally pressing the kick button (you should have hot buttons set for your melee attack and kick attack). With the warrior's extra hit points he will have no problem taking on the creatures found in the starting zones and can, in some cases, even take on two at once. However, trying for three is probably asking for trouble at this point.

So what is my big tip for the warrior class? Well I don't have a big one. Only that you should make full use of your ability to kick an opponent and use it every time it is available to you. Also, avoid entering fights unless you are at full or almost full health. You can rest by the guards between fights to avoid sudden unwanted attacks. The reason for this is that many of the creatures wandering around in this game don't mind attacking in pairs or even trios. If a creature comes along while you are in a fight it will often join in. And, unless you started at full health, you may not be able to finish off your first foe, thus losing out on valuable experience points that you could have gained.

9The Multi-class

The multi-classed character will find things a little harder than the warrior as your hit points will be fewer. A good strategy I have found for the multi-classed player is to use any buffing spell you have on yourself before entering the war zone and always start off a fight with your offensive spell. This will chisel away a few hit points from your opponent and make the fight a little more even.

During a fight as a multi-classed character I rarely try to get off any spells. I let my sword or club do the talking for me. Often as a fight begins to wear down and you look victorious your opponent will attempt to run away. This is an advantage for spell casters in that the warrior will have to chase them down but the spell caster can just lay the offensive spells to them and may never have to leave the spot from which they were fighting except to collect the loot. If your mana looks low, however, you may wish to chase rather than spending what you have remaining.

Most of the multi-classed characters have some form of a healing spell or another. So once the battle is over you can use your remaining mana to heal and normally fight another opponent right away. You will eventually wear down in both health and mana and have to take a break like the warrior and magic user do.

Now, many of you probably want to know if you can heal yourself during battle and the answer is yes but it is difficult. First of all don't make the mistake of healing the creature you are fighting as it will be your current target. You must press the F1 key first to select yourself as the current target then cast your spell. If you get hit while casting your spell it will be interrupted and you will have taken a hit without trying to deliver one. If this happens too many times or the creature is hitting you for more damage than your spell will heal, then it is time to find a guard to help with your problem.

The Magic User

The poor magic user will find the going the roughest as they are by far the hardest to advance. Their frail bodies have few hit points and the damage they can deliver from a weapon is minimal to say the least. So how does the magic use make the next level? Simple, if you follow my advice.

First of all, never engage in a fight with multiple opponents (unless you out level them by a good bit). The chance of one of these opponents hitting you and interrupting your spell is too great. So, if you accidentally engage more than one just run to a guard and try again. There is no reason to lose your health and have to wait for it to regenerate because you tried a fight that you might only win one out of five times. My next suggestion is to start the fight as far away from your opponent as possible. By doing this you may be able to get off two spells before your opponent can reach you. This will make your fight much easier.

Also, I know that I have harped about how a magic user has little offensive and defensive capabilities, but I want you to use your dagger or staff and take a lick or two. Don't do this at the expense of losing the fight, however. This will help raise your fighting skills some and make you better at getting off spells without being interrupted. After you advance a few levels and get new spells you will find yourself grouping a lot. This is a good thing to do, but the magic users' role often finds them standing in the background casting while a tank is blocking for him. This will only have a negative effect for the magic user as he will continue to gain levels without learning any fighting skills and this will lead to the magic user being totally dependant on groups to advance as they cannot stand up to an opponent solo.

My advice then is to spend some time alone working on those fighting skills even if it is with a lesser creature. And, if you get a safe opportunity to join in the battle while grouped, by all means do so…but never at the expense of someone's life. The others in your group count on you to do the most damage. As a magic user advances, his spells can do more damage faster than the rest of the group combined. So bear this valuable information in mind as you progress.

Victory

After a victory will notice the corpse of your foe sprawled out on the ground. You will want to collect loot from the corpse (if any) as soon as possible for two reasons. First of all, corpses of low level creatures don't last long and if you dally around it may disappear. Also, you may also end up with another fight on your hands, whether you are ready or not, if you stay in the hostile area too long.

    TIP: as much as any of us hate for it to happen, you will die sooner or later in this game. Most of the time you will know exactly where you were. But, there will be those times when you got turned around while out looking at the sites or you just fled the wrong way in a panic. What I try to always do if I know death is inevitable is to use the command "/loc" before I die. That way I will know the exact coordinates of my corpse and will not have to rely on someone else to stumble across it for me or just happen to find it out of dumb luck. I'm not sure if I like this command being in the game as it takes something away from the gaming experience and there have been several debates about it. But, as long as it is available to me I am going to use it. It is your choice if you wish to do the same.


As you kill a few things, your inventory may begin to fill up with the loot you have collected from the corpses (or so you hope). I recommend that you always go sell before you get to full or you run the risk of having to leave something on the ground for someone else to collect. The best time to do this is after a difficult fight because your health will be low and you can get your selling done while you recover. After you sell the loot, stop by the bank and deposit those coins you just made.

After a few trips you will probably have enough to start buying things. What you choose to buy should be dependent on your class. A magic user would not want to buy a sword for example, and a warrior surely can't use a spell. Purchase things that will help your class the most. Pay attention to the message area as well. People often sell their used items (which in the game is just as good as a new one) or items they have found or made for prices far lower than you can purchase them for from a vendor.

As your character gets equipped better and you gain a level or two you will notice how easy the creatures are that gave you fits before and eventually you will not even gain experience points from them. That is a good warning sign that you have out stayed your welcome in the newbie area under the protection of guards and that it is time to move on. This is the time when grouping becomes a real game factor and the fun really begins. I'll discuss these things in a future paper sometime, but for now I must go.

I wish you all excellent gaming adventures and good health. God bless.
