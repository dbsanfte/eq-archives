Sunflower on a Garbage Dump



      In these woods, beside a footpath that's been widened and rutted by trail bikes and trucks, there's a garbage dump that's thirty years old and still growing.  A mound of smashed glass, car parts, plastic bottles, beer cans, newspapers and washing machines, this is a typical garbage dump with one exception; on top of it is a sunflower.
     Its leaves area bit wilted and its head droops in the rubble and mold.  It will never model for Organic Gardening and Planters Peanuts won't bag its seed for their low-fat snack line. But here it is.  A sunflower.  A real live sunflower-- growing out of the garbage in these woods!
     Imagine landing on this scrap heap and putting down roots around nuts and bolts, plastic and styrofoam, soggy phone books, monogrammed doormats, and used condoms that saved so many from entering this world of planned obsolescence.  Imagine forging through decades of mildewed mattresses, bedsprings, battered mufflers and fenders, musty sofas, rugs, tires: a huge tossed salad of junk with motor-oil dressing.  See yourself pushing down to this sunless soil composted with the remains of our convenience.  And here, in the slime and the ooze; here, with your amazing alchemy, you transform the mummies of human desire into your golden bloom. 
Sunflower, how do you grow in these woods?  Who planted you here?  A carrier pigeon?  Luther Burbank?  the Jolly Green Giant?  How many days, weeks, months, years did it take your transient seed to root?  when did you strike paydirt and scream, 'Hallelujah!  Glory be!  Amen!?'
     Although this variety is dwarfed due to circumstances beyond it's control, I see no signs of canker, smut, mosaid mottle, nematodes, rhizome rot or strangle weed -- and it's face is dimpled with enough seed for a squadron of grackles.  Yes, here it blooms, soft, gemlike, a holy citadel whose root thread far beneath the festering cesspool to  drink from the waters of Lourdes.  Waving in the foul vapors, crowning the Land of the Great Consumer, transcending the slough of massmarketed dreams, this sunflower is a miracle. A miracle holier than a levitating army of New Agers with bellybutton crystls.  A miracle surpassing Christ's water-walking, dead-raising stunts.  A miracle all the more miraculous since the star of the show isn't man or his divine son, but just a brainless bright sunflower on a garbage dump.
     Pissing in the breeze, upwind from the dump, I remember those commonplace miracles Walt Whitman extolled.  The play of shine and shade on the trees as the supple boughs wag.  A leaf of grass that's the journey-work of the starts.  The narrowest hinge of your hand putting to scorn all machinery.  Today, whose eyes are moteless enough to see them?
     A titmouse plucks a seed from the flower and flies away.  This sunflower, after its tremendous struggle against odds, is still lavish with seed.  Commonplace miracles aren't enough, never mind what the poets say.  We need more miracles like this sunflower, this outcast bloom that upstages its suburban cousins.  We need an abundance of miracles all over the world, an international insurrection of flowers, jungles, and rain forests.  We need a baboon to do a fund-raiser for its own kind, juggling twelve human skulls at one, on the Oprah Winfrey show.  We need floacks of loose-boweled geese to strafe Manhattan, yodelling in 112 choruses that it's fit to be leveled and turned into a cattail marsh.  We need all the eagles who distrust the God we trust to fly from the back of dollar bills, leaving gaping holes in our legal tender.  We need Molotov-hurling mandrills to wage guerilla war on the Pentagon.  We need narwhales, marlin and sawfish to bore holes in submarines and destroyers.  We need whales to read Moby Dick and then direct and star in their own movie, casting Japanese pirate whalers as Ahab and crew.  We need bolshevik bisons, bulletproof pandas, and gorillas who can bend zoo bars in their bare hands.  We need new floods and new Noahs who mate with all species on the Ark II, crossbreeding the oneupmanship out of man.  We need billy goats to eat all the toxic waste in the world

and shit a Fort Knox of gold bricks that are invested in mutual security funds yielding a high interest rate in survival.  We need chimpanzee infiltrators who leap up on the conference room table, unzip their Brooks Brothers slacks,and piss on Exxon's Board of Directors.  We need pistol-whipping daisies, orangutan evangelists, and manatees trained in the martial arts.  We need armies of cattle who picket the slaughtehouse, bellowing, 'Hell no, we won't go!'  We need their votes in Congress and the Senate; we need them to speak up and talk articulate Americanese, and write declarations of equailty that confrom to Strunk & White's Elements of Style.  We need to extend the usage of the word 'we' to include first-, second-, and third-person singular and plural,with or without the person.

     Standing here, by this flower-crowned garbage dump, I believe that only those of us willing to live the life which they envision, who pass from dream to reality with eyes wide open, will provide the force needed to transform this graveyard we call civilization-- immediately.  I believe that as soon as we invade the nearest planet, we will find there a bigger, smellier garbage dump, crowned with a taller, leafier, more golden sunflower than the one in these woods.  I believe, as long as this wounded earth orbits the sun, that this flower will grow, until its most genetically determined seed bears a bloom tenacious, it bursts our frozen hearts with but one microscopic spore of windblown pollen.  Not for an instant do I believe in the clockwork of reform and its paranoid logic desired by those who copulate with abstractions to rid themselves of the ghost of time.
     Now I fully expect this vagabond flower to start speaking any minute and let fly a mindblowing haiku, magic incantation, canticle to the sun, or a declaration of independence from man.  I want to hear a barbaric yap flung over these woods, a rhapsody to the birds, a belt of blues endearing all endangered species, a nomad song of the Niger, Andean flute solo, Jamaican Reggae--anything but our national anthem, or Frank Sinatra singing 'My Way.'
Yes, I'm all ears.  Eyes and fingertips too.  My toes are twinkling, my face aglow.  It's like being in the company of the gods!  The ones who flew down to fornicate and whoop it up with earthkind.
      Why hold back in a world of precarious existence?  That's what this sunflower is singing this dogday afternoon at the beginning and the end of time.  Why stop at mere survival when the sky's the limit?  Steal the thunder from the gods!  Turn sandwiches and soups into Rabelaisian feasts, walks down the street into odysseys rivaling Homer, and the touch of your lover into a Song of Songs that would give Solomon a hard-on in the grave.  Let your senses take root in your heart and be your own blooming miracle.  And if, by doing so, you grow more food for the soul and produce less garbage of desire, then three cheers for your fertility!
     If you need time to consider these options, I recommend an  immediate vacation.  Then come to these woods where weekend pioneers push the last frontier.  See them stake their claim with trail bikes, chain saws, and beer cans.  Watch erosion and mud slides become commonplace.  Come to Jamaica.  Come to Walden, Aspen, Mozambique, Jerusalem, Rome, Baltimore.  Come to L.A. and see the sun fight the smog and lose.  Come to God's sprawling acre where everglades and icebergs glisten in the acid rain, and apples hang ripe in the global-warm day.
You ask for a miracle in this mutant age?  Then join me here, where birds hold their noses when sampling the finest hoers d-oeuvres, where old plastic never dies and never fades away, and where I or you, pocketless of a dime, may purchase the pick of the earth . . .
     But please don't pick the flowers.

by Bob Davis
