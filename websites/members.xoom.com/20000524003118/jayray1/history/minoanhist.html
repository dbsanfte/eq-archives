The Minoans

1600 BC --- 1200 BC

CRETE: EUROPE'S FIRST CIVILIZATION

After 50 years of Greek rule the Minoan people revolted.  The rebels invaded the courtyard of the palace of Knossos.  Fire began to eat in to the wooden columns which supported the upper chambers.  Salvation could only come from the intervention of the gods.   Wearing the ceremonial robes of the predisposed Minoan monarchs, the foreign king prepared for the sacred ceremony.  By the fountain in the throne room the holy water was made ready, to wash away their sins and cause divine favor to fall once again upon the king.  The king never reached the water of expiation.  The beams supporting the ceiling columns crumbled.  With a roar, the ceiling and the upper floors fell in, burying everything in layers of rubble.  It was the late spring of a year around 1200 BC.

Minoan culture was located on the island of Crete and spread out to nearby islands.  It was really the fortunate geographical position of Crete, at the center of the trade routes between three different continents, 

between 2500 and 2000 BC, metal fever exploded.  Gold, silver, and above all tin and copper necessary to the making of bronze began to be the most prized merchandise available, from Egypt to Greece and from Italy to Mesopotamia.  Due to Crete's numerous ships sailing throughout the Mediterranean packed with oil, grain, wine, and other Cretan goods which were then traded for loads of tin and copper the Minoan's found themselves making huge profits. 

Within a few decades the Minoans had created a vast maritime empire for themselves.  Naturally enough, the wealth this created had to be protected by a force at sea.  All the ships, in fact, were well armed and 'merchant-seamen' were able to transform themselves instantly into warriors.

For centuries no nation risked contesting maritime supremacy with the Minoan fleet.  The dominion over the waves was so complete that the simple presence of Minoan ships was enough to assure complete protection for the wealthy cities of the island.

A DIFFERENT RELIGION

The Minoans did not build grand temples and altars to glorify and worship their gods.  The Minoan religion was very simple, the people worshipped the natural phenomena that they saw all around them and which manifested themselves in a wide variety of ways.  For example the bull, an emblem of Minoan civilization, was venerated as a symbol of strength and fertility.  Whenever there was a religious holiday, the Minoans would flock to the courtyard in front of the palace of Knossos where a sacrifice to the gods would be made.   Once this offering had been made and the sacrificial rites were ended the games would begin.  Such games spread to Greece and later became the model for the Olympics.  The Minoans loved races, boxing, wrestling and unarmed combat, but there was one particular sport that they loved above all: bull jumping.  Young men would mount the backs of angry bulls and attempt to do acrobatic feats on the bull.  When the acrobat had finished his display, the bull would put his head down and charge.   That is when young men and women of the crowd would attempt to jump over the bull's back  as it charged.  They would then attempt to pull the bull to the ground where it was killed.

GRANDEUR AND DECLINE

Crete was at the height of its power around 2000 BC.  During the four centuries before that Crete had consolidated marine supremacy and was trading all around the Mediterranean Sea.   However, the island of Crete was beset by a number of catastrophes.  Around 1700 BC an earthquake seems to have destroyed the royal palaces (although it may have been an invasion from the Achaeans from the Greek mainland that was responsible).  Around 1600 BC another catastrophe or series of catastrophes occurred.  Some of the damage may have been done by the volcanic eruptions on the island of Thera which virtually exploded and may have caused a tidal wave of such dimensions that many palaces were destroyed and never rebuilt.  Also around this time the Minoans may not have been able to withstand the marauding Greeks.  Another catastrophe ended the Greek presence, but the distinctive Minoan culture would never recover.  The future lay with the Greeks on the mainland.
