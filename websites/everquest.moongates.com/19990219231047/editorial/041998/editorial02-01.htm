So what's the most important aspect of an online game? - editorial by Abzzstain
Page 1/2
There are lots of possibilities, of course, like gameplay, accessibility, lag, etc. But after spending thousands of dollars and several years in online role-playing games, I think I can safely say that the most important feature of an online game is its communication system. By that I mean the ease of use and readibility of the chat text. Amazingly though, it often seems to be tacked on as an afterthought, or in the case of Ultima Online, apparently given little thought at all. (Or maybe too much thought.)

You'd think it would be a no-brainer. After all, the only real difference between an online game and an offline game is the ability to interact with other people. So it just makes sense to give extraordinary attention to the means of providing that interaction. Think about it. In the end, it doesn't matter how wonderful a game you have. Eventually, people are going to have done everything there is to do, conquered everything there is to conquer, and seen all there is to see. But what will keep them coming back (and paying) is the community that forms within the game. After the initial rush of excitement over seeing a new game, the players start to form relationships with their fellow players. They make friends, make enemies, form guilds, even fall in love. And that is what will keep them coming back, long after the glitz of the game has ceased to impress them.

For example, take the old RPG Neverwinter Nights, which ran on AOL for something like six years. Even when it was released back in the early '90s, it was old technology. It was buggy, plagued by hackers, was just 16 colors, and for all but the last six months of its life, it was $2.95 an hour or more. If you'd been there more than a month or two, there was probably very little you hadn't seen and done. Yet it was always full of paying customers, right up until the day AOL pulled the plug. Most of them had been around the same little game for years.

Why was a horribly outdated game so incredibly popular? It's very simple. The community of players there was as close as any real-life community. There was always someone to talk to ... always something going on. Many people who met there ended up getting married to each other (myself included). Probably more by luck than design, Neverwinter had an awesome chat system that I still haven't seen rivaled. It was easy to use and easy to read. Nice and simple.

On the other hand, take a look at UO. Go ahead and take a long look ... you have time with all that lag. Now here is a chat system that just begs to be dragged out behind the woodshed and killed with an axe. Gawd, what a mess. You couldn't follow a conversation in Ultima if you were the only one talking. Don't even think about trying to talk with a group of, say ... two or three people. I mean, really ... your text overlaps one guy's, then someone else talks, and her text overlaps everyone else's, but you haven't finished reading the first guy's text yet so you grab the damn mouse and try to highlight his text, only just as you do it disappears from the screen entirely, so you curse and drive the mouse back to the second person's text, and now hers disappears, so you click on the damned journal, and by the time the lag lets you see what you missed, you've missed more comments .... arrrgggghhh!!!! And that's just with two or three people ... try and talk to more people than that, and your monitor starts shaking violently. (Well, mine does anyway ... or maybe it's just me.) What an incredible waste of bandwidth. I'm sure it looked like a great idea in a room somewhere in Texas, but apparently little thought was given to how it would go over with people who had to actually use it. Someone should have told these guys that you shouldn't have to reach for a mouse just to talk to someone. Think about it. Your hands could be busy typing, or something.

So don't even think about having a nice, quiet, intimate conversation with someone in Ultima. (Remember, online games ARE about relationships. You may not agree with me now, but at some point in the future you will.) I've said for a long time that the success or failure of an online game depends upon how well you can have a "quiet" conversation with someone. Neverwinter was great in that regard. You could yack all day with someone standing next to you, or across the realm. Or several someones for that matter. Simplicity at it's best. Ultima, on the other hand, makes it virtually impossible to talk with anyone who can't see you, and damn near impossible for anyone who can. And of course, nothing you say is private. You have to use a separate chat program like ICQ just to find out where your friends are.
