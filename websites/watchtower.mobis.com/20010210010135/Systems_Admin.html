One of the more obvious differences between corporate information systems and those of small businesses is the presence of personnel trained and experienced in keeping the systems running at peak performance. These Systems Administrators not only specify, install and properly configure the systems, they provide the daily maintenance and watch guard needed to insure that the systems are available when needed; every business day.

While it is true that many people are capable of installing basic operating systems and possibly even setting up a network of computers, the comparison between that situation and a proper corporate systems group would be akin to the comparison of a weekend mechanic and the pit crew for a high performance racing team. The weekend mechanic may well be a highly intelligent individual who excels at his chosen profession during the week and is capable of keeping his car running. However, the racing team performs the tasks needed for normal operations on a daily basis and has become extremely skilled at those operations. They have also developed methods, skills, tools and procedures to take the vehicle well beyond the capabilities of a normal car. Members of the team have specialized in certain functional areas further than would be practical for one individual and they have learned to rely on each other to provide solutions that go beyond the obvious and mundane. The team's entire reason for existence is the advancement of the common goal and, therefore, the work of the members reflects that same position.

This comparison may seem a bit melodramatic, but its basic tenants are truthful. Many small businesses are currently using either self-managed systems or "by the hour" consulting services.

Due to changes in the Information Industry, both of these solutions have become more expensive in both direct and opportunity costs than is practical. In the case of the self-managed systems, unexpected down time and lack of advanced services build costs that many businesses do not consider; much less track. If a business uses a standard computer or network consulting service, it is likely that the person performing the service is not certified or highly experienced. Regardless, it is still the case that they are only called in when a failure occurs and this continues the problem of unscheduled down time. Additionally, the business by which the consultant is employed is most likely involved in the retail sale of equipment and software. This leads to a probable conflict of interest of which the small business owner is, most likely, unaware.
