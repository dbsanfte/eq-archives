Extra BG - Sacrates
Mark Brockington had some words to say on the Developers corner about BG and the games linearity. From what I personally saw this game was as non linear as you could get. Basically the ultimate contrast to your typical Anime console style RPG. Check it.

It's about as non-linear as you can get and still have a cohesive story. The chapters are not physical constraints that you travel back and forth between. They are specific triggers that advance the main plot, occurring when a condition is fulfilled. When "X" destination is reached or "Y" object obtained, etc. The sub-plots are independent of the chapter events, and can be done at any time. Basically, if you wanted, you could probably visit 80% of the game's locations before you even triggered the end of chapter one. That doesn't mean you can "jump" to chapter five or six, but you CAN visit a lot of the areas in which they would take place (and do sub-plots there). The events that would trigger the actual chapters would not be available because you haven't set up the circumstances that would cause them to happen. Villain "Y" wouldn't be at "X" location yet, because you have not exposed "W" or found "S." If you have to talk to Bob about Mary, you can't do it until you've heard about Mary. The main plot has a beginning and an end and certain things that must happen in-between(chapter cues) for you to get to that end. The game-world itself is largely wide open, and the emphasis you put on actually progressing the story is entirely up to you.

Planescape Info - Bowmeister
The ever vigilante guys at VN noticed this one. Greg Peterson of Interplay posted a very informative bit on The Backroom message board - lots of juicy info on Planescape:Torment, the newest rpg to use Bioware's Infinity engine.

There were a couple of questions about Planescape on the other board, so I am posting the answers here (since they do not directly affect Baldur's Gate)

Planescape: Torment is being developed in-house at Black Isle Studios. Guido Henkel, best known for creating the Realms of Arkania series, is the senior producer. Chris Avellone is the lead designer. Also wokring directly or indirectly on the project are both Zeb Cook and Colin McComb ((the original co-creators of Planescape for TSR). The rest of the team has equally impressive credentials. Our first web site will be up in about a month. The game will take place in the AD&D campaign setting of Planescape and will be significantly less 'traditional' than Baldur's Gate. It will be a much more mature, edgy, setting. The characters and monsters will be VERY high level, and there will be significant political forces and intrigue involved. It will be single-player only, because the game will be focused much more against internal character development (i.e. your actions shape the game) than external development (killing monsters and getting loot).

You will play the role of an immortal character, who has lived for thousands of years, and experienced hundreds of 'lives'. The catch here is that every time he takes enough damage that would normally kill a mortal, he falls into a temporary coma (while he recorporates) and all his memories are wiped clean. When he awakes, he has no idea where he is, why he 'died', who killed him, etc. Over time, he has created hundreds of friends, multitudes of enemies, has become an arch-mage, super-hero, master-thief, etc. Unfortunately, none of this helps if he can't remember any of it...

The main game story is shaping up to feel more like Fallout2 in length versus Baldur's Gate - there will not be the same huge numbers of sub-quests, but there will be a greater degree of replayability because you can play the game so differently depending upon the personality you chose to embody (for example, if you want to be a bad ass, everyone will fear & hate you -- if you want to be friendly, everyone will try to influence or control you) Planescape is NOT a setting for the wimpst. The game engine will be a modified version of the Infinity engine developed by our friends at Bioware. The first on-line previews are up in most of the online game zines - we are also featured in both Nov PC Gamer and CGW (and probably other magazines that I haven't received yet). Our advertising starts in December magazines.

The game is due out Summer '99.

System Shock pieces - Sacrates
Ken Levine was over on the boards slappin the info here and there. These guys aren't be shy with the System Shock love?

on the game editor and multi-player: ShockEd, the editor we use to create the game is about the most flexible piece of software in the universe. Terrain is made in the editor, object shapes are built in 3d max, and mesh models (npcs generally) are built in max, textured and animated in alias. so there's no reason why someone with the editor, those pieces of software (anybody got a spare sgi lying around?), a computer and lots and lots and lots of spare time couldn't make their own shock 2 levels.

i don't know if lg plans to release the editor, as they own most of it (we just own the pieces we created for shock 2, which are huge, but the underlying editor code is theirs). I will ask them. However, I can tell you now that deathmatch is not a planned feature. This doesn't mean that this can't change, but we have other plans for multiplayer (insert evil laugh here...)

on design philosophy around multi-player: I think the Corp is a good idea, though my philosophy (for what it's worth) is to focus on what you do well. I think Unreal and Quake do deathmatch real, real well. I don't think they do content heavy, immersive, sci-fi horror RPG with incredible object depth and complexity well. The guns in Shock 2 are great, but they're not the focus of the game. Our multiplayer focuses on the real Shock 2 experience. If were were to spend a lot of time building deathmatch levels, that's that much less time the team can spend building single player and our brand of multiplayer. I think it's the right decision, but I'd be curious what people have to say. The only thing I beg them to remember is that there's only X amount of stuff in a game (though with Shock, X is a pretty big number). How do you want to divide that X up?

on the rotating stars in System Shock: That code (taken from the ORIGINAL dark engine (though I think Jamez wrote it) was in all the prototypes, but now we have a new, super duper 16 bit color sky/stars rendering technology.

Anyway, Rob Fermier was over on the RPG Usenet newsgroups and smacked this up.

on when work began: We first went to Looking Glass with the general idea of a System Shock 2 sequel (though we didn't have the license at that time) about a year ago, in the fall of '97. We started really ramping up the team heavily in the beginning of '98.

Amen to that, brother! EA seems pretty psyched about the game and it looks like they are really going to be getting behind it, marketing wise. They certainly have a lot of muscle to throw around. Back in the days of the original System Shock we were just one of a fairly small handful of out-of-house EA products, but these days out-of-house games are a lot more common in the EA lineup.

on the interface: We are constantly refining the interface, sending it through playtest cycles and generally ripping it apart and putting it back together again better than before. However, we have settled on something that we like a lot, which includes both elements of the original and some of the more modern enhancements of 3D games, like mouselook. Configurable controls are part of the Dark Engine, so rest assured that they will be in System Shock 2.

Posting more BG goodness - Sacrates
Our favorite Snufflufugus over at BG Chronicles just found some updating going on from Scott Greig  and Greg Peterson on Baldur's Gate. (What else?) You BG fans out there will want to snuggle up to this biscuit.

You might experience some CD-ROM spinup delays on the minimun install, but the recommended install (approx. 500 meg) will definitely not have any of these. On the recommended install, the only time the CD is accessed is on area loading. There will definitly not be any HD spinup problems, because we are constantly accessing data off of the HD (streaming music, sounds, animations etc.).

There are visual feedbacks for all events in the game. There is a variety of options for the amount of messages that are displayed, and every audio cue also has a visual reminder too. For example, when a character reaches enough experience to advance to the next level, a chime is played. We also display a golden '+' sign over the upper right corner of the character portrait.

Alignment plays an interesting role in the game. BE careful which NPCs you allow in your party and how you treat them. I have experienced a number of instances where NPCs get in arguments with each other and start attacking one another, or wander off, or refuse to obey your commands (especially if you are asking them to do something inconsistent with their alignment).

If you initiate the game, you have total control over any cheating... 1) You can allow players into the game, but not allow them to bring their characters (i.e. they have to create one from scratch) 2) You can allow players into the game with their characters, but with 0 experience points and no inventory. 3) You can allow players into the game with experienced characters, but no inventory. 4) Or you can allow them in with experienced characters and all of their inventory. You also have control over the control you allow other characters to have - for example, you might have the only ability to pause the game. If one of your characters starts to screw around, you pause the game and boot him. An interesting side note - you can boot the player, BUT keep the character. So say someone has been playing for a while and then gets out of hand. You can boot the player, but allow someone else to come in and role-play the character (so you don't have to start the game over, or create a new character). The morale: as the game leader you will have as much power as you want to manage things. As a player, be careful which games you join...

However with that said, Rex Dickson of Cinematix just spewed some goodness VN's way with these few fine words on a new revamped Revenant page. Check it:

on why Revenant is different: Revenant is not a traditional RPG in the style of the Ultima series or Might and Magic. Revenant is not a point and click action fest with no story. Rather, Revenant is more a blend of several genres all fused together into one. This is what sets us apart. We have dialog and plot like an adventure game. We have realtime combat that plays like a 3-D fighting game. We have tile based isometric artwork that seems pulled from the pages of a children's fairy tale book. Mix these elements together and you can begin to see the type of game Revenant is.

on combat: Combat includes 4 basic modes. We have weapon based combat, hand to hand combat, bow combat, and stealth mode. Weapon mode features three basic attacks: a thrust, a swing, and a chop. As you gain experience you can learn variations off of these base attacks. These variations will eventually lead to chain attacks that link moves together in a multiple hit attack combination. In certain situations, an interactive move can be performed. Interactive moves include fatalities, throws, and power moves. These are not reserved for the player alone however. Expect monsters to pick you up by the head and throw you, punch you in the face and send you flying head over heels; even pick you up and powerbomb you. Did we just say pro-wrestling moves in an RPG? You're damn right we did. Hand to hand mode works much like weapon mode with the 3 basic attacks being punch, thrust kick, and jumping sidekick. Variations can be learned like the uppercuts and spin kicks. Interactives and combinations are also included for some serious martial arts combat.

For those players who prefer bow combat, we have included a multitude of bows and crossbows to pick off enemies from afar. Finally there is stealth mode which enables you to sneak in and out of the shadows to sneak up on enemies and surprise them with assassination type moves. Don't expect to get away with it easily though, our monsters have some of the most wicked AI yet seen in an RPG. With hearing, sight, and path finding, monsters will often chase you through an entire level if you attempt to run away. To get a first hand look at Revenant's combat system, check out the demo movie we have posted up.

System Shock newsbit - Sacrates
Ken Levine was over on Tetracorp's unofficial SS Board and had a few good words to say about System Shock. Check it out:

on design philosophy around injuries affecting gameplay: Some very good points here, got me thinking (bad news for everyone...) All right, ready for some design philosophy spew?

1) Moderation is key. But also, I think it's important to give the player choices rather than handicaps. For instance...does he want to wear the armor that slows him down but protects him more or the armor that is thinner but keeps him spry. This way, if the player is slow, he's not just anxious to get to the point where he's not slow. He's made a choice and gaining a benefit from it. Good game design (i think) is generally about choosing between two things that both have good and bad.

2)re: moderation: somebody said he once heard that a great game aboard a space ship would have one bunk for every supposed crew member. The first thing I thought was: well, that's a lot of work. And a lof of repetition. And then I thought, there will be a few hard core types out there who will go out and count the beds and then be thrilled that the work was done to properly simulate this aspect of ship life. But I think most people will have an impression of "hey, another crew quarters...yippee..."

Now this isn't to say that you shouldn't strive for the FEEL of this. Shock 2 has restrooms, plenty of crew quarters, security stations, mess halls, the whole nine yards. But our focus is gameplay. The space ship will feel lived in, that is job one from an environmental standpoint. But will it have one bed for every crew member? Well, you're just gonna have to count them for yourself (grin)...
