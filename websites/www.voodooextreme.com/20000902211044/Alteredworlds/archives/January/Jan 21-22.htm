U:A and the party - Sacrates
Lady Moi was on the boards and had some light to shed on the ongoing situation with parties. Been a while since we got some goods on U:A and just gets me that much more toasty for it.

It's important to understand that very rarely does any 1 thing cause a feature to be dropped from a game. If pathfinding had been the *only* issue with the party, I'm told it wouldn't have stood in the way. As Trellek states, and Capn'Bill confirms, it really wasn't an issue in and of itself. But, as a part of the larger picture, the party was technically difficult to add for many reasons.

I was just in the office with Audball, Jackl, Capn Bill and Trellek asking about just what happened. Apparently there were issues with how you interact with the party members, how to make the party members feel really alive and interactive, how to make the party members pathfind intelligently, how to draw all the polygons on the screen and keep the game moving quickly enough, how to make the party members engage in combat intelligently, etc... then, there were all the other issues the team was dealing with regarding the game *in general*. In the face of all the obstacles at that time, this feature seemed just too daunting.

The design team was worried about how 'alive' the party would seem. The solutions to the technical problems lead to this arrangement where you wouldn't see the members (they'd follow behind), you wouldn't interact with them, and they'd automatically attack. Suddenly, this feature looked lifeless. What good was it doing anyone?

Okay, now add to this the situation with the changing personnel on the team, the dramatic changes in the design staff in particular, as well as changes to the programming staff.

When you roll all this up together, you can begin to see why some decisions are made.

Also, understand when you ask someone a question, you are getting their subjective memory about the course of events. This decision to drop the party was made about a year ago (says Capn Bill, I think he was speaking generally, so don't get married to that number. ;) The team makes decisions and moves on with the design and programming of the game. The whys get left behind and people just remember this little tidbit or that little tidbit. So please, allow for a little flux between answers that different people give. We all have a slightly different experience... and we also care about slightly different things, so when time comes to remember it, we think about it a little differently.

Now, in terms of the companions... we're still playing with how they are going to work exactly so there isn't an answer to your question yet. :)

Middle Earth spewage - Sacrates
Here is a game that is getting more controversy than who shot J.R.(Not of the Tolkien kind) As always we have spotted more Developer board goodness and are dishing the sauce your way. Can't  wait to see the first actual screenshots of this booger. Here's Steve talking about PvP and death.

1) When a character is "knocked-out" do they die if unaided for a long time or do the eventually wake up over time?

It is possible to bleed to death if you are unaided for a fairly long period of time. You will wake up, however, and be able to search for help before death ensues.

2) Will a character die instantly if he/she fell from a great hight? or just "knocked-out" no matter how far you fell?

You will not be permitted to fall from great heights.

3) I guess this is a stupid question, but a character could drown right? (meaning permanet death under water)

If you stay underwater too long, your character will be forced to the surface. There is no way to force your character to stay under water until dead.

4) Could a character die from posioning? I guess it would take strong posion, and left un-treated over time to cause death.

Yes, poison will be quite deadly. You will have time to search for the antidote of any particularly deadly brews before death ensues.

5) Is there any other ways for a character to die? (ie. exhaustion, exposure, dehydration.etc)

Incessant lack of food and water, prolonged severe element exposure, untreated wounds, being eaten by a spider and having your throat cut are some of the many possible champions of permanent death.

Character death is a preventable occurance. Be prepared and your won't die.

Tank Characters in the game.

You will not gain money in Middle-earth through quests alone, but through trading with others and participating in the economy in any way that suits you. Middle-earth will definately be quest based however. While you can increase your skills through practice, you will not gain any new abilities based on those skills without finishing quests. If you want to learn how to make a new piece of armor, you will need the appropriate skill level AND be taught how to craft the item from a NPC. We want to provide both a free form environment for the hard-core players AND a more structured goal oriented environment for the casual player.

It will also not be possible to be 100 times more powerful than a newbie. We are being very careful in considering all gameplay elements to keep the standard super-high-level-killing-machine syndrome out of Middle-earth.

A bit on Classes.

It would nice to be able to play more classes. We have some pending plans, but I can not share the details at the moment...

More on Death and mistakes.

While it may be heartbreaking that a character may die if left unattended for five minutes, it's just how it is. The AI will be smart enough to avoid obvious danger... this means that the people who log out during combat will be at a disadvantage. Of course, if you are a Pker and are trying to avoid an angry mob, you may have problems. ;)

A whole slew of goodies.

1) It is not possible to die of old age.

2) In the event of your permanent death, your family will get all of your posessions. If you have no family, the items are liquidated.

3) Dying of disease is a distinct possibility.

4) Dying of cold is absolutely possible.

5) Sleeping will not be required.

6) Taxes? While I personally hate taxes, it will be commonplace for many settlements to garner income from its inhabitants.

7) Any guld dues will be handled manually in most cases.
Oh, and thanks for some NEW questions. ;)

NOX - Sacrates
Not that I know what it means, but it sounds er....different. Westwood just announced their newest title in their growing line of gameworlds. This time it's an action/RPG Title by the name of NOX. Check it.

Westwood Studios Announces Nox

Action-RPG to feature devious combat, detailed world

LAS VEGAS, January 22, 1999 -- This fall, Westwood Studios proudly invites gamers around the world to experience Nox(TM), a game of savage combat and sneaky tactics set in a mystical land filled with discovery and deceit.

An action/role-playing game, Nox is the first of a new franchise for Westwood Studios, known for the 10-million selling Command & Conquer(R) series, the Lands of Lore(R) series, and the critically acclaimed adaptation of Blade Runner(R).

Nox's lightning-fast action is fueled by a combat system that's easy to use yet gives players tremendous gameplay depth and infinite possibilities. With more than 100 unique spells and weapons, players must use their wits to create devious combinations and tricky tactics.

"In Nox, you'll have to be fast, smart and it'll help to be devious," says John Hight, executive producer of Nox. "It's not just a matter of pointing a weapon or casting a spell. It's a matter of thinking ahead, designing combat combinations, setting traps, playing dirty tricks and staying one step ahead of an opponent that's doing the same."

The battle happens on the world of Nox, which game designers have filled with intricate detail. The top down viewpoint gives players an expansive view of the world, but there's a twist. True line of sight means never knowing what's around the next corner, creating an intense level of suspense.

The dynamic world of Nox is further enhanced with the addition of an advanced physics system that delivers realistic interaction between players and the environment. Roll boulders to block doors. Move candelabras to light a darkened room. Break through walls. Use water to extinguish flames.

The single player game will take you through the three lands of Nox, pitting you against vicious monsters and powerful wizards. Driven by an epic story, you'll progress up eight skill levels and learn three schools of combat: Red, which teaches the many deadly uses of fire, Green, which uses the power of nature, and Blue, the discipline of illusion and trickery.

Nox promises an intense multiplayer experience with five multiplayer options: deathmatches (teams and free-for all melees), capture the flag, hot potato, king of the hill and scavenger hunt. Multiplay will be supported through Westwood Online, an online gaming service with more than a million members.

Guild Commands:
/guildinvite - Invite a person to join the guild, if this person is to be an officer, include the argument (as in oscar) for the rank. Usable by Guild Leader and Officers, but only the Guild Leader can make officers. You would also use this function to promote a member to an officer.
/guildremove - Remove a member from the guild. Only the Guild Leader may remove Officers.
/guilddelete - Disband your entire guild. Only the Guild Leader may use this command.
/guildstatus - Will return information on whether the named person is a member, an Officer, or the Guild Leader.
/guildleader - Transfer guild leadership to.
/guildsay - Sends a message to all guild members.
/guildwar - Initiates a guild war against the guild of Guild Leader which appears in the field. sets the duration in real days of the guild war. This defaults to one real day if left blank. The opposing Guild Leader must send the same command with the of the other Guild Leader to fully initiate the conflict.
/guildpeace - Ends a guild war before the duration has expired. is the name of the opposing Guild Leader. Both sides must send this command to end the war prematurely."

Septerra Core Preview - Sacrates
Here's something we'll start seeing more and more of as it gets closer to release. Some console style RPG anime lovin for your eyes only in the form of an Interview done up by the guys over at the Firing Squad.

To understand the story behind Septerra Core, you must first get acquainted with the geography of the planet. The planet has seven continents that lie in concentric layers over top of one another, like the layers of an onion. The richest people, known as the Chosen, live on the top layer and consume massive quantities of resources and energy and dump refuse on to the lower continents. You play the role of Maya, a junk scavenger who lives on the layer immediately below the outer continent and you make a living by gathering the most useful refuse to sell. The Chosen have recently discovered artifacts that lead them to believe the answer to their past lies somewhere deep in the core of the planet, so they mobilize toward the center and down through the layers and countries that lie in their path. While the Chosen move down into the lower levels, the nations of the under continents grow restless in fear, and war seems imminent. As Maya, it is your destiny to make your own quest to the core of the planet amid all the chaos and discover the secrets that lie within. You'll find that the layers of the planet are actually a big puzzle and it must be solved by moving the continents around into place. If this is not done, the world is doomed, but if the continents move to solve the puzzle and unlock the secret, then great destruction will ensue anyway.

Desslock on BG - Sacrates
Desslock finally slapped up his thoughts on Baldur's Gate. Makes for good reading. The results are obvious gang but Desslock always has a skewed take that I like. He's just a skewy kind of guy. Here's the perfunctory blurb.

[On whether the game is a good adaptation of AD&D]:

The real-time adaptation of AD&D's combat, which is really the core of the game, works exceptionally well, modeling dozens of weapons and the 100+ included spells in the game in a manner that provides visceral, immediate feedback, is visually appealing and eminently controllable, since you can pause the action at any time to issue new orders to your characters. Almost all of the monsters, spells and magic items, and even some of the locations and characters in the game are taken straight out the core AD&D source books and related lore. Even though BioWare took some liberties with the AD&D rules in the interest of game balance, as the number of spells available to certain Mage specialty classes was increased, for example, and the abilities of Paladins and Rangers were tweaked, only an ideologue would deny that there's never been a more faithful adaptation of AD&D.

Muzyka did caution that the new areas are designed for higher-level characters, though, so don't expect to romp through them with your first-levelcleric.
