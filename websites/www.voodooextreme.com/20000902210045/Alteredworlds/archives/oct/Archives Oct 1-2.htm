Another week (well maybe a little more than that), and we've gotten a whole lot of stuff done. We are down to 10 Quests that need to be fixed for everything to be working. This may sound like we haven't gotten tons done, but some of those will be coming off in the build tomorrow, and we added a few things that really needed to take as high priority as a Quest.

We had a big meeting with QA on Monday and went over a bunch of the problems that they were having with the game. There seemed to be two main problems: Random Encounters were too tough, and a group of Golden Geckos could take out a 40th level character. There were about 40 other things that we talked about as well. Some of the things we have already dealt with, others we should figure out by next Monday, and others are a part of the ongoing balance testing that we are doing.

One of the big problems was that partymember chem use can be set to "Any Drug, Any time" and the partymembers are really taking this seriously. Once you go into combat, your partymember that is set to this will use all their AP's every round to take any chem they have in their inventory. So one tester had Sulik (a tribal guy) boost himself with three shots of Buffout and one shot of Jet in the first round of combat. And Sulik was just getting started - by the third round of combat he had take every chem in his inventory. Needless to say this is getting changed.

We still have the random encounter bug hanging around that when Bounty Hunters get killed they turn into Shelving Units or Fruit Tables.

We are also working on toning down some of the language in the game. I'm not talking about four letter words, but rather exchanges between NPC's that make a group of 4 or 5 25 year old guys from California wince. We are all pretty jaded and if it gets a reaction like that out of us, then we know it is too much. But don't worry we are still using that Mature rating as much as we can handle it.

Other stuff on the game is going well. The artists are working on "fluff" art that is being put in just to make the areas look even better, and they are working over certain pieces of art that look good, but could look even better.

We are getting pretty close, but we are going to work on the game until we feel it is balanced well and that it lives up to the first one.

Writer/Researcher Nancy Grimsley on elves in the Fourth Age: I've noticed earlier posts about elves and have wanted to add some comments about elves in the Fourth Age of Middle-earth, so I'll tie on to your post. JRRT tells us that Celeborn went to dwell in Rivendell with other High Elves after Galadriel departed with Frodo and the others. Also, he indicates that the Eldar or High Elves are the only elves able to choose to depart Middle-earth. The Lesser Elves do not have that choice.

Being a writer and researcher for YE's Middle-earth (and a Tolkien devotee of many, many years) I'm a great one for quotes. So here are some references.

Regarding Celeborn after Third Age, from Prologue of LOTR: "It is probable that Meriadoc obtained assistance and information from Rivendell, which he visited more than once. There, though Elrond had departed his sons long remained, together with some of the High-elven folk. It is said that Celeborn went to dwell there after the departure of Galadriel; but there is no record of the day when at last he sought the Grey Havens, and with him went the last living memory of the Elder Days in Middle-earth."

Regarding elves departing Middle-earth, from JRRT's Letters: "But the promise made to the Eldar (the High Elves - not to other varieties, they had long before made their irrevocable choice, preferring Middle-earth to Paradise) for their sufferings in the struggle with the prime Dark Lord had still to be fulfilled; that they should always be able to leave Middle-earth, if they wished, and pass over Sea to the True West, by the Straight Road, and so come to Eressëa - but so pass out of time and history, never to return. The Half-elven, such as Elrond and Arwen, can choose to which kind and fate they shall belong; choose once and for all." Letters, p. 198.

"If you or I or any of the mortal men (or hobbits) of Frodo's day had set out over sea, west, we should, as now, eventually have come back (as now) to our starting point. Gone was the 'mythological' time when Valinor . . . existed physically in the Uttermost West. . . After the downfall of Númenor, and its destruction, all this was removed from the 'physical' world, and not reachable by material means. Only the Eldar (or High-Elves) could still sail thither, forsaking time and mortality, but never returning." Letters, p. 186

The Lesser Elves (as opposed to the High Elves) are the people of Northern Mirkwood and of Lórien. Letters, p.176

on elves again: Since we are very concerned to preserve the integrity of Tolkien's Middle-earth, I've been doing very careful reading. So if you'll humor me, I'll just pop in another couple of quotes.

When Gandalf spoke to Aragorn after the defeat of Sauron, he said: "For the time comes of the Dominion of Men, and the Elder Kindred shall fade or depart." I take this use of the term "Elder" to refer to the High Elves.

And in Appendix A, Tolkien writes: "At the end of the First Age the Valar gave to the Half-elven an irrevocable choice to which kindred they would belong. Elrond chose to be of Elven-kind, and became a master of wisdom. To him therefore was granted the same grace as to those of the High Elves that still lingered in Middle-earth: that when weary at last of the mortal lands they could take ship from the Grey Havens and pass into the Uttermost West; and this grace continued after the change of the world."

At minimum, though, there is evidence that elves continue to dwell in Middle-earth, which is the important point.

Fallout 2 newsbits
This first post is from Chris Taylor who was on Usenet recently and had this to say:

They have the same SPECIAL stats you do. They have the same derived stats you do. They have the same skills you do.

Their armor is part of their prototype, but if it is a human character it is based on the type of armor they are wearing. We had to let the armor come from their prototype instead of the item since:

a) we wanted to have some characters that were wearing armor but that you could not loot the armor off of their body.
b) Monsters, Monsters, Monsters.
c) To give Dogmeat cool stats that were above and beyond the average mutated dog.

While the player has Perks, the critters don't.

The critters do have some stats that control their behavior that the Player doesn't have (or need -- since it's the player). The critters also have some special combat stat modifiers that act as a replacement for Perks. (Like the ability to give Dogmeat a higher Critical Chance percentage than normal.)

Their damage comes from their weapon. They may have a Melee damage modifier (to make some monsters more powerful than others.)

The next one is from Feargus Urquhart, Enjoy.(Classic porno name) - Sacrates

Critters (what we call every NPC person or monster in the game) use the same exact character sheet as the player does. The one thing we can't add to them is PERKS or TRAITS. And we can't increase their level, so all critters in the game are level one - in essence. However, we do have control over their armor values, hit points, melee damage, critical chance, Action Points, and sequence. Basically a critter has two pages for a character sheet, one just like the character, and another where we can modify those kinds of things. For example, the stats will determine how many action points the critter has, but on the second sheet we can add or subtract points from that value.

As to the Power Armor - I am pretty sure the Strength the player gets from Power Armor is done in the game engine through and Invisible Perk - so critters wouldn't get that benefit.

You want even more BG lovin? Well yer gonna get it because Luke Kristjanson posted on the board again with this bit of BG happiness. Seriously, one more BG post and I am gonna pop like a balloon. Good Christ on a cracker! - Sacrates

on the experience cap and reasons for raising it from 40,000 to 59,000: That's because this monster of a world we've created is bigger than we thought. For the record, no one has hit the cap yet... we just theorized that they might before the end of the game.

As for the question previously asked...experience will always be what is capped. It's just simply one of the trade-offs for multi-classed characters that they progress slower and may not get as high a single classed characters.

on the bare minimum system requirements: One of our test boxes is a P100, so potentially you could run the game... though the battles may look like those old Bugs Bunny Flip-it books. I would suggest trying to run the NID... If you can run the NID you have a chance, though the main game will run slower (more on screen etc). "Barely playable" was the comment from one of our programmers. Emphasis on the "barely." A 1 meg video card or less than 16meg of ram and you can forget it totally.

on dual-classing: When you dual-class, you cannot use the abilities of the previous class until you are one level higher in the new one. This includes proficiencies and weapon abilities.

The traditional P&P penalty is no experience for the encounter in which the previous class' ability was used and half experience for the adventure. This is not enforceable in BG, so the use of any of the previous abilities is simply prohibited.

Hollywood sound effects
Black Isle Studios just tossed this Press Release our way discussing a Hollywood sound studio working on all of their sound effects etc. Check it out! - Sacrates

Irvine, California, October 1, 1998 - Black Isle Studios(tm), the role-playing game division of Interplay Entertainment Corp. (NASDAQ:IPLY) today announced that award-winning sound designers will lend their names to three Black Isle titles currently in development.

The distinguished group of Hollywood sound design professionals were brought in to further complement the existing in-house sound team.

Black Isle's Fallout 2(tm), set for release this fall, will have David Farmer of EFX Systems and Paul Menicini ("Babylon 5," MORTAL KOMBAT, ISLAND OF DR. MORREAU) on board as sound designers. Farmer's previous credits include sound design on ARMAGEDDON, CONAIR, MORTAL KOMBAT and MORTAL KOMBAT 2. In addition to Fallout 2, Farmer is also lending his talent to Black Isle Studios' most recent endeavor, Planescape: Torment(tm), set for release in 1999.

When Farmer first started working on Interplay's titles, he realized how different sound for games is from sound for motion pictures. "I was really surprised at how complicated the whole process was - it was almost like starting over," Farmer said. "Our biggest challenge was to make large sounds for smaller speakers. We're used to using huge theater-quality speakers, and we had to find a way to use different frequencies to make sounds to imply size without just turning up the volume."

EFX Systems, who employs both Farmer and Harry Cohen (STARSHIP TROOPERS, BLADE) along with DSP will do sound for the soon-to-be-released Baldur's Gate(tm), which features over 1200 sound effects. DSP is currently handling sound design for the "Xena: Warrior Princess" and "Hercules" television shows.

Interplay is no stranger to top-notch sound design talent. They have worked with several award-winning sound design teams in the past. The Academy Award(r)-nominated design team at Weddington Productions(tm) worked on the original Fallout(tm) game as well as STAR TREK(tm): Starfleet Academy(tm). Weddington received Oscar(r) nominations in sound design for THE FIFTH ELEMENT, ALADDIN, and STAR TREK(tm) IV. Burbank-based West Productions(tm), who also lent sound design to Fallout and STAR TREK(tm): Starfleet Academy, were honored recently with an Emmy(r) for their work on an episode "The X-Files."

Here is another Battle tip. Ambushes are pretty dangerous in BG, you are walking down a hallway and before you know it, you are surrounded and under attack. It can work both ways though. In dungeons there are ample opportunities for this one. There are lots of narrow hallways and corners to hide in. Take one, unencumbered character (thief is preferable) to act as "bait". Send him/her up the hallway to investigate. Hide in shadows (if possible) and get in amongst a group of baddies. Surprise the heck out of them (become visible etc.) and make a mad dash back to where your party is waiting at the other end of the corridor (make sure you are being followed). Have a good area effect spell ready to go, lightning bolt really shines through here and cast it down the hallway. It bounces (if aimed just right) back and forth down the hall , cooking everything in it's path (so make sure everyone is clear). Bam, a group of bad guys dead and only one person was ever at risk. Get the fighters to mop up afterwards.
