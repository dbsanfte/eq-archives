Big ass RTK info - Sacrates
Looks like Sinjin (Cool name points) got a little feisty (No pun intended) with his fingers and spit out one mother of a post. Pretty much he covers all the bases here so go ahead, read on.

1. Yes, there is all kind of unique and cool inventory items / weapons / potions in the game. There are over 400 inventory items alone. Some magic, some not, but all pretty cool.

2. There are two lock picking modes, one is just a dice rool for those who don't like (or don't have the dexterity) manually picking them. There are 27 different combinations of trapped locks, some poison, some bladed, etc.

3. The wilderness areas are unique so there are plains areas, mountains, canyons, woods, etc.

4. Gold coins that get changed automatically into gems in certain increments to lighten your load as you accumulate wealth.

5. Mainly personal inventory. There are one or two other ways, but I'm not going to tell. We'll see who figues it out. Maybe I'll tell down the road a bit.

6. Important conversations are logged in a journal and can be played at any time for any given chapter.

7. Tehre is some interaction with the 2D portions and the 3D objects sometimes you can pick them out and sometimes not. Our goal was to integrate the look as much as possible, this is where the real time lighting really came into play.

8. The combats are varied in size and formation. We tried to present the player with really different scenarios. Yes, there are some dragons... they do have to fit on the screen, right?

9. The game will succeed or fail on merit, not on hype. We have tried to make the game engaging and challenging. If we have done that sales will take care of themselves. All that being said, PC Gamer had almost 20 pages of coverage in the Oct. issue. The marketing guys put in prequels written by Ray in several magazines, huge maps/posters in CGW and PCGamer. We've done quite a lot actually. The other thing you have to remember is that Sierra Studios just released Caesar III and is releasing Half-Life, Mask, and RtK all for Thanksgiving so there is an incredible number of AAA offerings by Sierra Studios this holiday season. Some entire companies don't have this lineup, we're just one of the Sierra brand divisions!

10. We have tried to make the first chapter accessible and fun! Let us know how we did.

Now, I must rest my fingers.

Even more action happening over on the BG Boards for you guys to see. Greg was there as usual, this time talking about spells.

You can cancel a spell at any time before 'casting' by R-clicking the mouse button.

For example, you decide to launch a fireball.

You click on the 'cast spell' icon, click on the 'Fireball' spell icon and up comes a targeting cursor. You notice that your thief is too close, and would get fried in the explosion. You R-click to cancel the spell, and choose 'Magic Missile' instead. You do NOT lose the Fireball spell.

More ME love biscuits - Sacrates
Thanks to ARGNet for these little news sausages. Couple of interesting things  posted by Janus Anderson. Good lord we still have a long wait for this game though! Although it will be interesting watching this game evolve. The first one is from Janus talking about Player run Housing etc.

One of our goals is to implement a guild system that will support special abilities that the members pay upkeep for. To qualify, however, you will have to use the name 'House' instead of 'Guild'. We believe that the word 'House' is more appropriate than 'Guild', since our system is broken up into smaller 'Families'. Although anyone can have a minor house (with all the support that you would expect for a guild), there will be a fixed number of 'Major Houses'. Major houses will be unique in the fact that, for a substantial gold upkeep each month, members will be awarded unique and special abilities (such as a swordsmanship bonus for the fighters' guild etc.)and a direct link from our main website. You may want to convince the guilds joining your association to change their name in advance if they wish to be considered!

When we get around to accepting applications for 'major houses', we will not make any requirements for the naming of such as long as it is in the RP spirit. We will not, of course, accept names such as 'the PK Masters' for major houses, although people can name minor houses anything they wish. We would, however, appreciate it if people would use the term 'house' in some large scale guild titles, since we are trying to set up an association between small groups (families, orders, or cults)uniting for a broader cause, rather than focusing on individual associations as much. To clarify a bit, a 'family' has a similar structure to a 'house' except it is designed on a much smaller scale and oriented to people who wish to RP the social aspects of getting married, adopting children and such. I would love to elaborate on the system but I'm still under orders to withold some of the juicy-bits for press releases.

For consideration as a major house, we will require an appropriate title, creed, and history as well as a set of laws and purposes that the house members strictly adhere to. Since we will award special abilities, specially dyed guild tabards, and web links to those limited few who are selected, and we will require the house members to adhere to the laws and purposes designated. Failure to comply could ultimately result in the loss of major house status, since the basic purpose of the major house structure is to further role-playing in Middle-earth.

Interestingly enough, the ability to construct and play your own midi files(even in synch with other players) was written in the design documents at the beginning of this project. I'll probably even add some code that will detune and/or change durations of notes for those players who are lacking skill =)

System Shock goodness - Sacrates
Noticed over at VN that Rob Fermier made quite a few comments on System Shock on the Boards and in Usenet. For the complete package run your ass over to VN and read up on it, otherwise here's the first half of his Usenet speech/comments etc.

Warning: pointy-heading game philosophy & semantics spew ahead. You've been warned. :-) Apologies in advance if I sound overly argumentative, that's just the old high school debate reflexes kicking in -- I definitely appreciate that you (and probably many lurkers as well) are concerned about us screwing up a great game, and we take those concerns very seriously. That said, on to why I think RPG elements do not portend the trouble you are expecting...

In every game your avatar in the game world has certain limitations and abilities that are imposed by the simulation. The player never controls 100% of the activities. In Shock, you decide to run down the corridor, but the simulation imposes constraints on how fast you run and how fast you turn. You may decide to shoot, but the game imposes limitations on rate of fire, kickback, inaccuracy, and so on. This is certainly true of the original System Shock.

The biggest difference between Shock and Shock 2 is that those capabilities, rather than being fixed, are also part of the larger simulation. So in the original Shock, you're be able to run at one speed, period. In Shock 2, your running speed is determined by (among other things) your agility stat. Is the act of running any more or less simulation based or out of the player's control in those cases? I don't think so. In fact, in the "RPG" case, you have MORE control because you as the player have made the decision of whether to get that point of agility or improve your hacking skill, and so forth. This same logic applies to the player's maximum health, falling distance, radiation resistance, weapons ability, and the like.

Is it "unrealistic" that your physical and mental characteristics improve considerably over the course of the game, which is a relatively short time frame by real world standards? Of course. Is it any more of a realism problem than the ability to carry piles of weapons and equipment, take near-lethal damage and recover just by using chemicals, and alter reality by save/load? I don't think so. I definitely think it's worth it for the interesting gameplay decisions that come out of shaping your character's development and really bringing a lot of these factors into the simulation and having them be dynamic over the course of the game. We could come up with some thin fictional explanation for why you change so rapidly, but would there be any point?

The purpose of this game system, like any other, is that it adds value to the game, not that it is an integral part of a realistic simulation. To be ludicrous, we could simulate your need to sleep or go to the bathroom -- that would be realistic, but not fun. So, we don't do it. Having the player's characteristics alter the simulation is the reverse; it's not that realistic, but it is fun. So, we put it in.

A more dramatic impact of RPG-esque elements onto System Shock 2 is the addition of "mini-simulations" that aren't a part of the main 3D world. When you go to repair a weapon, there is a fairly abstract separate simulation of repairing it, taking into account your skills, equipment, resources, and so forth. There is no separate mini-game for the player (as opposed to the character) to excel at and be a better repair-er. That's just not what the game is about -- it's about terrifying atmosphere, rich story and setting, exploration, and combat. By keeping the simulation of repairing, hacking, weapons modification, and so on fairly abstract, it lets us have more of those systems, and also lets us have those systems unfold more carefully over the course of the game.

BG blurbs - Sacrates
I missed this choice piece from Jim Bishop yesterday on the boards discussing the accuracy of the adaptation between BG and AD&D. Other than that, not much else floating around. We'll toss the bone your way as soon as we get some though.

Glad you asked. There are really two parts to the question: is it faithful to the AD&D rules, and is it faithful to the Forgotten Realms setting.

BioWare's Infinity Engine is the closest, most faithful transliteration of the AD&D rules that is possible with current technology. That sounds like a hedge, but I mean it as very high praise. A handful of things had to be changed, but in those few cases the spirit and balance of the game has been preserved.

But it is, after all, the Forgotten Realms setting that's going to sell this game. And the setting _is_ as good as it looks on the screenshots and previews. The level of detail is just amazing, from the lush game screens to spell effects. There's also a very nice touch with the equipment screens, where a right click brings up a hand-drawn illo of the weapon/gem/tool along with a handy description. It's as though every time you've said "If I made a D&D computer game, I'd include X" they wrote it down, and incorporated it.

So yes, it's true to the AD&D game, and yes it's true to the Realms.

ME bit - Sacrates
It's been a while, but Stephen Nichols was back on the all new Middle Earth Dev Board and had a few sparkly things to say on permanent death in ME.

Permanent death relies on a few design ideas that still need to be realized:

1) The player power curve needs to be realistic. We can't have single players that can take out 10 opponents without a scratch.

2) There needs to be an enforced waiting period before you gain the ability to permanently kill another player. We're currently thinking about 1 month's worth of play time for the character before enabling the murdering option.

3) To kill a player, you must have disabled ALL opponents. After ALL opponents are disabled (or have fled), you can take out the unconscious folks.

4) The civilized game world must react negatively
when you murder someone. Shopkeepers should call guards, sherrifs should want your head. Of course, if you leave the area in which you have murdered, you'll be out of harms way. Guards should be believeable... not teleporting instant killers.

5) Pulling the plug during a battle should have NO effect. The AI system will take over such characters and allow them to continue fighting. Of course, if you log back in while the battle is in progress, you should be able to resume. This should allow your buddies to guard your AI controlled body in the event of a network failure.

All of these options should help to make permanent death feasible.
