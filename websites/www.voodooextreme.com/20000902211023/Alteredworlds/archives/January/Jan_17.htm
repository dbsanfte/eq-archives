One big ass ME post - Sacrates
Stephen Nichols had a little time in between working on Middle Earth and visiting RealHampster.com. He has a bit to say on all subjects in the game. No, not his personal favorite Hampster model either. Not that he owns one of course.

I'm happy to respond to the issues that you have raised:

1) Unrealistic Permanent Death: While you are absolutely correct that players will adapt to just about any game system, there is a breaking point for most people. To model combat to the same level as the reality of Middle-earth would make the game very difficult -- more difficult than any other computer game around. Of course, playing styles will adapt to fit the rules so long as the game is enjoyable. There is a reason why most people in Middle-earth did not adventure... it's both because they have real lives to worry about and they are afraid of the possibility of death. The role of a computer game is to entertain, not hamper the fun of players with ultra-realistic rules. If permanent death is too easy to fall prey to, it will ruin the game for the majority of players. Certainly, I see the merits of such a system and find it to be very attractive to the hard-core roleplayers, but to be successful we must set our sights on more than the hard-core players. The purpose of our permanent death system is to provide both an avenue of player-driven justice and to give risk to any unknown situation, not to encourage players to stay at home. My goal is to make sure that you can protect yourself from permanent death if your spend enough time to prepare. To implement a more susceptible form of death would translate into severe frustration for the average player. The risk of our permanent death system still exists, not to the extreme degree of reality, but it still exists. If you venture into monster-infested areas unprepared, you do risk permanent death. What does it matter if the game must expend your health points to knock you unconscious first? Does that really eliminate the risk? No. It makes permanent death a manageable risk. Certainly, between players, permanent death must be a premeditated act that is purposefully carried out. Is that such a bad thing? Some would argue that it skews the feeling of the permanent death system, making it a tool of the powerful. Is that any different from a more realistic system? I would argue that a more lenient system of permanent death would encourage more abuse.

2) Character Persistence: Simply starting with a fixed number of characters per server is not a valid solution to the problem. Say we sell 50,000 copies in the first six months of the game? A fixed number of NPCs may work in that scenario... until we sold another 100,000 copies. Also, having PCs replace NPCs limits the possibility of us creating persistent NPCs that grow with the game. What do we do, remove the local shopkeeper and put a player in his place? What if that player want's nothing to do with a shop and wants to adventure? This is where this idea breaks down. Not only that, but we can not provide an AI system sophisticated enough to accomodate every players play style. I guarantee that most of the players in the game will be nomadic and will travel all over the map in search of activities. If that is the case, what would a persistent character system do when you logged out in the depths of Moria? Should it walk home to handle your daily work? I think not. Such a system would remove the control of your character from you. You really would have no idea what your character had done since you logged off last. Not to mention that with such a system, permanent death would be even more rampant. These persistent characters would become a favorite target of player-killers simply beacuse the AI will not be as good as the player (making them an easy target). Ultimately it comes down to the issue of scalability. We can not afford to put up 20 servers just because each server is bogged down processing the AI for players that are not playing. That is not a financially viable option. We calculate our server expense by estimating how many players we will have at peak times. Of course, under this system, the peak times would not exist. Every minute of every day would be a peak time... and the peak would keep on growing with the number of copies sold. Now, here's where persistence is allowed. If your character is subdued and bound by either a monster or player, your character continues to be active after you log out. This lets the giant spiders bind you in threads and hang you for later consumption. If you log out, your character does not. Your character will stay logged in so long as you are captive... once freed and moved to safety, the standard 5 minute waiting period takes precedence before you finally disconnect. As for there being less need for NPCs as the PC population grows, all I can say is that you are very optomistic. Most players will not want to do the "boring" jobs that the NPCs do (especially if their job is chosen for them due to NPC replacement). Being forced to do menial work is not fun, and a game should be fun. Also, while computer technology is constantly evolving, we have to settle on a server platform early on in the development cycle. We simply can't develop features that may be supportable some time in the future, that's not a good way to do business.

3) Reputation: Our reputation system is based on location. If you murder a shopkeeper in the Shire, you are a murderer. Kill an Orc, and you may get a medal! NPCs most certainly will defend themselves properly, dishing out permanent death to those who are deserving.

4) Global Chat: I'm really not interested in what might have happened in the books if a concept of global chat existed. That has very little impact on the playability of the game. Having a simple method of communication is necessary in this kind of game. This is true simply because of the nomadic nature of the player. Players will not sit at home all day tending their shop, they will travel the map searching for adventure. This constant travel syndrome makes it impossible to stay in touch with your friends in-game without some form of direct communication. What we have here is an approximation of the Tolkien universe where people from all over the world play at varying times of the day. This is undeniable. For players to keep in contact with each other through their varying play times, a form of direct communication is necessary. Certainly, if players were in the game 24/7, global chat would be less important because people's behavior would be more predictable. Again, if ultimately an issue of ease of use, not role-playing.

5) Multiple Characters: While you may not be able to play with family members at the same time, that does not mean that they can not help one another in the game. It is a simple idea to grasp that family members may share goods to further their in-game standings. But that is not even the most important reason. If we only provide one character slot per server, it would be most difficult for players to experience different facets of gameplay without erasing other players on their account. We will not be shipping with 10 servers in place, more probably we will ship with just two at first. This means that you can only play have two different characters at any one time. Part of the fun of this kind of game is the ability to play it from different points of view. It would be impossible for you to create a new character on a server where you are already well known and work that character up in a familiar setting (unless you erase your famous character which has lived for a long time). Without the ability to create multiple characters, you will find people growing tired of the game in time. Many players do not want to erase their favorite character so that they can make a new one, and to force them to do so would be quite illogical in my opinion. Again, this comes down to replayability and flexibility. Letting people sample the game in all of its forms is not a bad thing... it can only be a good thing. The exploitation can be combatted with other approaches that do not remove the flexibility of the game.

6) Why call it a roleplaying game? Because it is. Nothing stops you from playing a role in this game. Nothing at all. We are giving each of you the flexibility to tailor the game to your particular tastes. Some will role-play (hopefully many), and some will not. That's out of my control... I can not force people to role-play, but I can encourage it. To enforce role-playing is too limiting in my opinion and would turn away the casual player which is so vital to our financial success.

7) Why are you putting the name "Middle-earth" on the label? Because the game is based on the Middle-earth universe. Certainly, it is not a simulation of the world, but an interpretation to a medium that it was not meant for at conception. That's not to say that the translation will lessen the work, but certain concessions for the gameplay must be made for it to be a viable product. That is my ultimate goal, to make a viable product that offsets the millions of dollars put into it. To be viable, we must make the game playable by the average person, not just the hard-core roleplayer. Unless, of course, we had enough hard-core roleplayers willing to pay $100 a month for the privelege to play. But for some reason, I feel that resource would be in short supply.

Drakan Interview - Sacrates
Head on over to the Flamelands to get your taste of this "hot" game. This could be one of those gems that comes along and really surprises a lot of people. I sure hope my boy Stu and the gang can pull it off.

What makes the enemies in Drakan (aside from the fact that they weigh many tons and breath fire) different than the enemies in any other game?

Shaun Leach -- For the most part, we have tried very hard to limit the AI's access to internal information that is not available to the player. This means that the AI actually has to use sight, sound and touch to move around and interact in their enviroment. So unlike some games where the moment the player presses the fire button the AI reacts, our AI doesn't know anything is going on until they actually see that something very bad is coming at them. The other benefit is that the AI actually has to see or hear the player before they'll do anything. This is unlike most games where the player crossing some invisible marker causes them to start doing something. I think that this alone gives our AI a more "natural" feel instead of a bot feel and gives us a lot more game play possibilities.

EQ Security forms - Sacrates
Are you one of the few chimps who feel as if they are bending over and letting 989 get medevil on their Credit Cards while signing up for Beta 4? Abashi was on the boards to grease you up with a bit of love oil on this very subject.

First, it is secure. The page itself is unsecure, but the frame with the credit card and other personal information is secure.

Second, your credit card number is hundreds of times more likely to be stolen by a gas station clerk than over the internet, even if it was unsecure.

:1) There is no notice from my browser (I tried 3 browsers to be sure) that I am entering a secure site

Configure your browser to tell you that you are entering a secure site. My guess is one time this happened and you clicked the "dont' tell me this again" checkbox causing notification to be disabled.

: 2) There is no "secure icon" in my browser window that >ALL< all other sites give.

This is because the site is an unsecure page with a secure frame within the page. You can verify this by right-clicking in the frame with the credit card info and then clicking on "properties". This will show you that it is indeed secure.

: 3) You are asking for my mother's maiden name, something that only people that need to verify details of a credit card account ask for. WHY do you need that info? No other place I have ever had to do business with a credit card asks for that.

This is incase you forget your Station password so that your identity can be verified with a high degree of confidence over the phone. Mothers maiden name is a standard way of performing this. If you don't like this, just make something up. However, keep in mind, if you forget what you put in this field, it will be impossible to verify who you are should you forget your station password.

: 4) Please give me a phone number I can call to give the credit card info out over, or else provide me with some kind of assurance that the www site is secure.

You should now be convinced, and I shudder to think how anyone could think giving thier number over the phone is somehow more secure than giving it out over the internet. Even if the page was unsecure, which it is not, it would be more secure than giving it over the phone. However, if you must have a phone number, try 1-888-STATION.

Elysium Interview - Sacrates
Just noticed over at VN this little Interview with John Cutter that's up over at the Elysium Livewire. Stop by and read up on it. This game looks tasty.

There is "magic" in Elysium, but the natives don't call it that. We've come up with a really innovative magic system for the game, but I can't go into details about it. This system will allow us to have a very large number of effects that are easily accessible to the player. There are no scrolls, books, or spells in Elysium, the magic is more natural. We've tried to simulate "wishcraft", so players will be able to think, "Gee, I wish I could " and there will actually be a way for them to do that.

We wanted to keep the excitement of real time combats, but the player does have the option to slow down or stop the action while he or she is using the game interface. I don't like games that force you to fight the interface instead of the creature(s) on the screen. If I'm being attacked while I'm messing around in my inventory looking for a healing potion, I get really angry and frustrated. We're going out of our way to avoid these and other frustrations in Elysium.
