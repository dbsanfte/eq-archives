Phase 4 Pwipe:

There will be a player wipe prior to the beginning of Phase Four. This pwipe will occur at midnight P.S.T. on the 7th of February. All players may register one character for reimbursement. Use of this code word will allow players who desire to change their account name to do so. Please do not enter any other information into this /feedback, all your character information will be recorded automatically. When the time comes for you to be reimbursed, the GM will ask you for the account under which you registered and the code word you used. This character will be reimbursed once on ONE SERVER of your choosing. The maximum number of levels that will be reimbursed is 35. Characters of a level above 35 will be reimbursed at level 35. Monetary reimbursements will be given in amounts appropriate to your reimbursed level. It is recommended that you register your character on or before 11 P.M. P.S.T. on February 7th.

Server Down Time:

In preparation for Phase Four the EverQuest servers will be down from midnight of February 7th until Phase Four begins on February 10th.

Virtual Life: Baldur's Gate

By RIC LEYVA Associated Press Writer

NEW YORK (AP) - The Dungeon Master is out of a job.

The computer has taken over and the legions of Dungeons and Dragons diehards who made that cerebral table game a cult classic are ecstatic, their excitement due to "Baldur's Gate," an ambitious undertaking that delivers on nearly every front.

It was a hit before it ever got to store shelves, selling fast in pre-orders over the Internet as word of mouth spread. Even those who've never played Dungeons and Dragons are getting hooked. It's a slam dunk for anybody with a taste for the kind of fantasy, myth and legend popularized in "The Hobbit" and "Lord of the Rings" trilogy.

"Baldur's Gate" (Interplay, five CD-ROMs for Windows, $49.95) faithfully follows the complex rules set down a decade ago in the second edition of Advanced Dungeons and Dragons, a hugely popular role-playing game that uses special dice, along with pen-and-paper, to compute conflict outcomes and keep individual statistics.

Taking the game successfully into cyberspace - something tried before with mediocre results - required a monumental two-year effort to build its vast, detailed graphic landscapes, extensive variety of inhabitants, animals and monsters, as well as the astounding artificial intelligence system that governs play.

There's no human Dungeon Master anymore to make often arbitrary judgment calls, which is a good thing, since you can't get on the bad side of the impartial artificial intelligence in "Baldur's Gate."

The end result is a lush world of ancient good and evil, where hand-to-hand combat and spell-casting are as common as hunger, thirst and tired feet, where friend and foe can be hard to tell apart.

Players find themselves sucked into a complex story of intrigue seemingly destined to bring all-out war in the land known as the Sword Coast, where the largest city is Baldur's Gate, named for the great explorer Baldurian, whose disappearance is one of many mysteries ripe for investigation.

Choosing to take the form of one of the several races living together in the mythic realm, players can be human, elf, dwarf, gnome or a mixed breed half-elf or halfling. Callings include warrior, priest, sneak thief and sorcerer.

Traveling between towns is complicated by an array of mythical monsters including ogres, tree sprites and nymphs, giant spiders, ghouls, gibberlings, hobgoblins, skeleton swordsmen and the pesky Xvart (evil creatures the size of a Smurf), along with more recognizable wild critters like wolves, bears and ferocious dogs of war.

Safety is far from assured in a town because there are assassins, thieves and spies roaming among relatively benign citizens who may have valuable information to share or needed commodities for sale. Players must gain knowledge, learn magic, build strong reputations and recruit allies to form groups of up to six members, which increase the odds for survival in the fiercely hostile environment.

Weapons come from a medieval arsenal, ranging from spiked clubs, swords, daggers and battle axes to slings, bows and war hammers. There are also magic rings and amulets, along with potions and enchanted weapons that deal out greatly magnified damage.

The graphics, viewed from an overhead perspective, are stunning, especially on high-end computers with topflight video cards. Characters are sometimes a bit small to be fully appreciated, but the bird's-eye-view, which allows players to see a wide area, makes that minor fault a necessary evil.

The game's artificial intelligence is a wonder, building player profiles based on gained experience as the quest proceeds, among many other things. So many variables enter the equations that even the brainiest players will be forever challenged.

The multiplayer option in "Baldur's Gate" will be disappointing to some because the group leader remains the only player who can make many decisions, with other five players taking the parts of group members controlled by the computer in single-player mode. The supporting players can do certain things on their own in battle, but most other moves require approval from the group leader.

A true online version, with dozens of players involved all having individual autonomy and full decision powers, isn't available yet, but is something developers say they are looking into. It would be tough to come up with, considering the server logistics of handling dozens of players at once and the immense number of calculations and tabulations that would require, but it's a nice dream.

"Baldur's Gate" is a rarity in computer games, a harmonious blend of strategy and adventure with plenty of hack-and-slash, enough variety to please almost any taste.

WoT Weekly - Sacrates
Glen got around to doing his weekly update on the progress of Wheel of Time. Here's the bit to get you pumped up.

Mark just returned from his epic trip (insert groan here) to Raleigh.  According to his report, it was time very well spent.  He went undercover with Tim, Steve, Cliff, Myscha, et al. to covertly merge all of our recent changes into the new engine build and do a few other super-secret things (including see Prince of Egypt with Mark R. and wife).   Just for those interested, he also got a chance to take a close view at the current state of Unreal Tournament and, naturally, it rocks.  The merges went very smoothly, and we're very anxious to move on up to 221, but likely nothing that risky will happen for alpha, and that's what we're all focused on right now.

Speaking of which, the entire team is working hard to make alpha happen.  We just played a huge multi-player arena match tonight to gauge performance and, while we had to share the game with a bunch of bugs, it was a lot of fun.  It definitely demonstrated that we've got our work cut out for us, but we also enjoyed it immensely.  One step closer.  Now, we have to look at all of the logs and figure out what happened and why.

The level designers continue to polish and add content to the existing maps.   We're very close to the point where you can walk from the beginning of the game to the end; there are lots of red marks on my white board today (indicating completed tasks for each of the levels), but there are still a few more to go.  The lds are really producing some wonderful maps which will only get better with each iteration.  Some maps that I anticipated would be relatively small (the Ways, Saving the White Tower) turned out to be monstrous--in a good way.  There's a lot more content there than I had been planning on, and it's wonderful.

Robert is here this week again to work on decorations and artifact effects, but he didn't show up alone.  Robert is traditionally an off-site artist who works remotely from our home office in Boston. (you have to have been following some of the bizarre news reports about the acquisition to get that joke).  He's made the trek here with his wife and newborn son to check out the area for suitable living quarters.  If he finds some, there's a good chance that Robert (and the Robertettes) will join us permanently in the immediate area, which would be great.  I'd been hoping for something like this for some time.  His interaction with the rest of the team, especially as it concerns the special effects, is invaluable.

While Paul, Julie, and Marc continue to produce the wonderful Aes Sedai textures that have made them famous, Joel has been focusing on new skins--including a fairly disturbing projectile for Legion.   Have you ever seen a soul vomited up?  I'd be surprised.   Well, Joel has--and now he's shown the rest of us.  Take my word for it: it's not pretty.

Ryan, Mike, Jess, and Aaron continue to focus on their areas of responsibility and we're seeing both leaps and bounds in AI performance and artifact effects, as well as huge strides towards network replication stability.   Again, there's lots of work to do, but now we're seeing some real progress based on all of the architecture laid down a while ago.  It's been Ryan's dream to design an elegant and flexible AI system that Legend would build on from product to product.   It's becoming clear that this approach was correct; the initial design is now really paying off.  Each day, we see that much more behavior than before.  Soon, we'll have to tone our creatures down so they don't kill us too easily.

It's a very stressful time right now.  We're watching the game come together before our eyes.  While there's a ton of stuff there, putting the machine together is also revealing how many holes there are, or which pieces aren't working correctly.   At this stage, there's almost as much ahead making it all work as there was creating it all in the first place.  But this team is focused and excited.  As always, I'm very proud to be working with all of them, from the people who started on day one with the prototype shots to the people who only showed up at the party recently.   Everyone who works here is very talented and dedicated, and the game shows it.

Ana-Interview - Sacrates
The Lead Artist on Anachronox (Lee Perry) has been interviewed over at Ion Storm Daily Informant. Here's a bite to get you going.

Seriously graphically innovative games like Xenogears, the FF series, Metal Gear Solid, Dungeon Keeper, etc. I hope a good deal of the game comes off with a console RPG environment feel, and not just trying to make things realistic as possible. My theory has always been that realism is not the best goal to aim for yet, with every item that looks spot-on realistic, something else has to me made that much more cooler to not look out of place. I don't think technology is quite there yet to make things look like life, I'd much rather take a cool style and run with it in another direction. Games like Grim Fandango are a testament to how great things can look when you hit the ground running away from reality. The Q2 engine is surprisingly flexible for this kind of environmental stuff when you start getting creative with it and breaking more than a few traditions.
