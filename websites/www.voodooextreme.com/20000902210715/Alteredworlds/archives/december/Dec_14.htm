Weekly WoT - Sacrates
As always we get some more juicy goodness on WoT's development from my sweaty Gorilla friend Glen. This weeks sounds even juicier with a little news on getting close to an Alpha.

This last milestone was a big one. We're getting dangerously close to alpha, so by necessity, lots of elements of the game are falling into place. Let me discuss what we've delivered.

The White Tower primitive set is beautiful. My hat is off to the level designers and the texture artists for creating some of their best work yet. The architecture is conservative, yet powerful. The textures are rich and compelling--definitely the most colorful art to date. Every scene is a masterpiece. And the maps that the level designers pulled together for this delivery are wonderful as well. There's a ton of stuff here. Tim's distinct Shadar Logoth maps really capture the essence of a ruined city. Warren's multi-player and single player maps based on the Portal Stone world simply rock. James' Whitecloak single-player stuff is outstanding--I can't wait to see the AI up and running inside of it. And both Scott's rendition of the Ways and his multi-player map are great; just ask him about the magic bread. Warren was right (in his .plan); when we finally looked at everything we were delivering, everyone was amazed.

We delivered two characters: the player character and Mashadar. Mashadar is rather unconventional, but all the better for it. Against all odds, Steve B. managed to get both the player character's skirt and her cloak animated for the milestone; he suspected that he'd only be able to do one or the other. She looks great, and Mike Fox has been on an integration kick, so it shouldn't be long before we see her in the game. In fact, she might already be there. Speaking of mesh and animation integration, many of our characters are now running around in the game. Ryan's AI is growing by leaps and bounds. It's wonderful to see these guys actually smartening up, doing appropriate stuff in appropriate environments. I guess that comes with actually putting the game together, but I still find it very cool.

In between bouts of minor civil disobedience (light globes anyone? how about flaming skulls?), Aaron's pumped out some terrific stuff. Our spell effects are shaping up, Mashadar can be placed from the citadel editor now, and lots of unexpected cool features are appearing--such as last week's rain and snow. Robert has been cranking out the art assets for these effects, but was just recently distracted by his new baby boy! Congratulations go out to our resident effects artist! Although he's taking a few days to teach his new son how to play catch, he'll be back shortly. How's that for dedication? I hope you all appreciate it by buying three copies of WoT each!

Sam has his hands full with sound effects, and I'm still in voice-recording and UI land. Sometime soon, we should actually start seeing some of these cutscenes. The storyboards have a lot of promise. Cross your fingers.

In addition to the milestone, we've had a few other developments lately. Specifically, Legend has welcomed a few new faces. First, let's talk level design. We've been lucky enough to snag some (more) of the best people in the business.

Matthias Worch (a.k.a Langsuyar), accomplished level designer, is joining the Legend team. I've known Matthias from way back; based on his work with Beyond Belief, he was one of the first lds chosen for the WoT team. He was an off-site free-lance designer, though, and quickly got snapped up by Ritual who brought him to the States. I was overjoyed when I discovered that I was going to get another chance to work with him. Also, David Kelvin (a.k.a Kevlar) has jumped onto the Legend bandwagon. I'm not as familiar with his work, but he already has a bunch of experience behind him and everyone has nothing but great things to say about his maps. Finally, Mick Beard (a.k.a.Vermoord) has joined the crew as a contractor. All of these guys are clearly very talented, and in addition to our already stellar group, we're ready for just about anything. Note that they probably won't be working on WoT initially. Legend's got lots of other brands in the fire. We're just getting warmed up!

On top of all of this, we've also hired one Joel Walden, an experienced texture artist. James' recommendation didn't hurt, but Joel's talent got him the job. I'm really looking forward to getting Joel into our new art "studio" and seeing what he can do. He's a great guy, and I expect great things from him. It's good that we'll be moving to our new offices soon. I think we've pretty much outgrown this place.

Lastly, I wanted to mention the new three page spread on WoT in the latest Next Gen which should be out on shelves as you read this (although I'll be danged if I can find a copy!). There's lots of new screenshots and some new info, so make sure and hunt yerself down a copy!

Thanks for coming, but I've gotta run and write more scripts for tomorrow (I think I'll have the Trolloc sing, "Ring around the Rosie").

Kings Quest Review - Sacrates
The boys at Games First have really hit the Crack Pipe and tossed up a Review of Kings Quest MOE giving it 4 stars. Head on over and check it out.

The King's Quest games have been the most successful adventure series ever to hit the PC with over 7 million copies sold. Roberta Williams pioneered the graphical adventure movement back in 1984 when King's Quest I: Quest for the Crown was introduced. It was the first game to fully support the newly introduced EGA graphics card. Mask of Eternity is the eighth installment in this classic collection. Long gone are the days of 16 or 256 color sprites and text parsing commands such as "Open door". Sierra has put the three plus years since King’s Quest VII to good use by implementing a 3D environment and support for 3D acceleration. Many "up and coming" adventure game developers cut their teeth on the King's Quest series, including designers who eventually went on to make such classic series as Space Quest, Leisure Suit Larry, and Gabriel Knight.

5 Star Thief - Sacrates
Looks like the GI Ho's over at Games First have decided they were rubbed the right way with Thief and gave it a solid 5 of 5 stars review. Head on over and give it a read.

First of all, Thief--unlike most of the 3D shooters it might initially be mistaken for--is not a shoot-‘em-up extravaganza featuring some anatomically ridiculous hero who defies physics, the law of averages and karmic cycles while obliterating mutants, techno-monstrosities and civilizations in his wake. In fact, Thief holds little truck with games characterized by lots of noise and action, which isn’t to say that it lacks these qualities. This game offers plenty of action, most of it of the sweaty-palmed, hair-raising variety. Yet Thief demands that players exercise stealth, silence and economy of action in order to proceed through the game. Players stand a better chance of sticking around if they choose the surfaces upon which they walk, proceed slowly, and stick to the shadows. In addition, Thief is one of the only action titles I can think of which rewards players for patience and thoroughness and encourages players to cover their tracks. So when players sneak up behind surly manor guards or fanatical zealots and blackjack them, for instance, it is crucial to dump the body--in a dark corner, behind a column, in the sewer--lest a passerby discover the crime and start screaming, literally, bloody murder.

Revenant News - Sacrates
It's about time we got an update from these guys. And boy what an update it is! Nothing like information on a Revenant Editor! This news gets me warm in the gorilla like nothing else. Check this update to their news section.

The Revenant Editor

Recently it has become common for games to ship with their editors and tools intact. Coupled with the rise of the Internet, this practice has led to online map and modification communities based around games like Quake, Doom, Starcraft, Unreal, and many others. As we looked around the industry we noticed that the RPG genre was severely lacking in this area. Shortly thereafter we made the decision to ship Revenant with the editor intact.

What this means for gamers is the ability to build, write, and script their very own RPG's. Perhaps the best aspect of the Revenant editor is it's completely tile based. You can simply scroll through the available tiles and drag and drop to build. Since they are aligned to a grid the tiles snap together like puzzle pieces. Most people should be able to build and light their first rooms in less than 15 minutes. The thousands of tiles available are divided into categories such as Forests, Caves, Labyrinth, Dungeons, Town, Castle, and many more. Many specialized tiles are available as well, such as exits, items, containers, and a plethora of animated 3D effects. Once you build your areas you can begin to light them. Revenant's lighting system is beautiful to behold, supporting thousands of different colors and intensities.

For those gamers who wish to get even deeper into the Revenant editor, our scripting system will be included in the game as well. With the help of our scripting documentation, aspiring designers will be available to script in-game cut scenes, special events, traps, exits, enemy artificial intelligence, and conversations. More advanced users can use 3D Studio Max or Softimage to create their own tiles and characters. Cinematix has also committed itself to supporting the Revenant online community with new tilesets, characters, and monsters if the demand is high enough. What we have mentioned in this article only scratches the surface of what the editor is capable of. As with most editors, the Revenant editor will be technically unsupported but we will be including documentation which can be used as reference material.

The Revenant development staff has put a massive amount of time and energy into creating a world born of our imaginations. It is our sincere wish that game players everywhere will become immersed in the world we have created. However, nothing would be more satisfying than to see what all of the gamers out there can come up with the Revenant editor in their hands. We hope you look forward to playing in our worlds as much as we look forward to playing in yours.

EAX and Direct X - Sacrates
AGN3D got word from someone at Aureal that EAX is not part of Direct X. I thought this way fairly interesting and thought i'd pass it your way.

Now that MX300 reviews are emerging, I have noticed that some refer to EAX as a feature of DirectSound3D, or that Microsoft has included EAX in DirectSound3D in some way. I'd like to set the record straight on this. From talking to Brian Schmidt, the Product Manager for DirectSound at Microsoft: Microsoft has not included, and currently has no plans to include, EAX in DirectSound. There is no special deal with Creative whatsoever. Creative is simply using the standard DirectSound property set interface (like many other companies) to enable EAX. Microsoft has not done anything specific for EAX, much less endorsed it in any way. The perception that there is some kind of deal between the two seems to exclusively stem from some cleverly worded Creative press releases. If Microsoft does include reverb functionality in DirectX, it might be their own, or Creative's, or someone else's. Since reverb is not on the list of DX7 improvements, my guess is that this won't be a topic until DX8, probably due in 2000. BTW, the IASIG also has not adopted EAX as their industry-open reverb API. Instead they are pushing ahead with the I3D2 reverb API (an API that Creative at first supported, but now seems to be backing away from, instead pushing EAX 2.0).

U:A goods - Sacrates
As always, Sith has been spanking the U:A monkey and gathered up some goodies for you chimps to munch on. First one is from Audball on EAX versus A3D in the sound department.

At this stage in development, it would NOT be easy to add full WaveTracing support to *this* game. (Consider editor changes, in-game changes, database changes, potential modeller plugins/exporters to facilitate surface tagging, etc.). Simply passing a set of geometry to the Vortex2 processor is probably indeed simple, but that alone does not give you full immersion. Geometry itself isn't enough; surface properties must also be taken into account, and I see that as the bigger hurdle. (There are obviously other issues, such as how our world geometry is divided / organized / loaded, and how that affects downloading to the audio processor, which affects framerate, etc., etc.)

The decision to implement EAX was made before the A3D 2.0 SDK was out of beta, and our production schedule reflects this. EAX may not provide as mathematically "correct" a representation as WaveTracing, but it can be tailored to give good effects (we'll have to wait and see how EAX 2.0 does things). If we have time to revisit the issue before we ship, who knows, maybe we'll put support in.

More Thief - Sacrates
I think you guys realize that we won't post every review we see on the Net of these games. At this point we've posted enough Thief Reviews to make damn sure you understand this game kicks everyone's Papa Smurf. Here is an interesting debate between two guys on Thief over at Planet Review. Stop by and check it out.

Here is where Thief shines and surpasses any game currently available. Thief requires that the gamer pay very close attention to the game's sounds and surroundings. Where do I start? Thought that footsteps were important in games such as Half-Life or Quake 2? Well, Thief takes this to an entirely different level. The footsteps are dependent on both floor surface and movement speed and Looking Glass executes this perfectly. Carpet, you know that squishy soft stuff, is your best friend in Thief while marble and ceramic floors just cause you headaches. Also, running in Thief makes a very distinct and different noise than walking on the same surface, like in real life. Now, putting all of this together, Thief takes advantage of 3D Audio to the fullest. Being able to interpret where footsteps and sounds are coming from heightens the Thief playing experience. It supports both A3D and EAX and man it sounds awesome on a Live! or MX300. A great feature implemented by Looking Glass is the soldier conversations. While sneaking around on various boards, Garrett will overhear conversations between two guards and just singing by lonely guards. All these conversations are unique and usually add a dose of humor to the rather intense gaming atmosphere.

Tomorrowland - Sacrates
3DGN just kicked up a little piece on upcoming Sci Fi RPG's. Only three this time but they should be doozies by some up and coming companies. Shadows of Reality looks spiffy as all hell too. Stop by and scope it.

Our final contestant this week is Shadows of Reality (formerly BlackOps), a massive undertaking from the relatively small and unknown Nevolution Studios. SoR is a Herculean endeavor, a planned cohesion of the online gaming community and the first person shooter in an RPG experience. Using Monolith's LithTech engine and drawing influences from the world of Cyberpunk fiction, I personally believe that this game, if ever completed, could completely revolutionize the gaming community and create an entire new genre. Picture a game like Ultima Online or Meridian 59, but with the gorgeously rendered first person interface of the Lithtech engine. Not a run and gun deathmatch game, Shadows of Reality presents players with the unique role-playing experience of mercenaries and hackers, all struggling to survive in a harsh and deadly future. If Nevolution can complete their ambitious title, they may find an elusive market in a cross section of 3D gamers and RPG fans, all eager to get in on the action.
