Ana-Preview - Sacrates
We haven't exactly seen a ton of Anachronox goodness coming our way lately. Although Gamers Digest have poked up a bit on this gem that should get of a few of you Ion junkies stoked.

The plot of Anachronox is pretty amazing, and vivid if you want to slap words on it. The game is basically centered around the city of Anachronox, located within something called a "Sender" in space. What's a sender, you ask? Well, it's exactly what it sounds like. It's a big object in space, where if you get close to it, it will rocket you across the cosmos, faster than light. It "sends" you across galaxies. Obviously, something such as this would have became sought after by many different races because of its importance to space flight. Many wars erupted over control of the Senders. Now, in the present of Anachronox's universe, the many Senders are lucrative trade centers, and the hubs of galactic intermingling and culture.

Wizardry 8 goods - Sacrates
Games Domain have gone and kicked up the biscuits on the upcoming Wizardry 8. It's about time we got a little lovin on this beasty. Check this.

From early descriptions and screenshots, Wizardry 8 seems to closely resemble Might and Magic 6: The Mandate of Heaven in interface and graphic perspective. Like in MM6, you control a party from a first-person perspective (not exactly intuitive, but functional) and guide them around realistic terrain. Of course, given that W8 will likely be released close to two years after MM6, there are notable graphical improvements. First and foremost is 3D hardware support, with support for Voodoo, OpenGL, Direct3D -- all of the good stuff that gamers have come to expect over the last year or so. Sir-tech confidently proclaims that W8 will be "the most visually stunning RPG ever", a bold proclamation to say the least.

Middle Earth mini Interview - Sacrates
Was just over on the boards and noticed Stephen Nichols dished out a good bit of ME goods that you chimps will want to munch up. It should be interesting to watch as this game evolves in light of the upcoming Asheron's Call and Everquest releases. Things are good for Massive Multiplayer games!

Maybe they haven't updated the FAQ because they're not finished coming up with ideas that most people will hate and then modifying them so that everyone else will hate them too.

Actually, we have not updated the FAQ because we're too busy MAKING the game to update the FAQ every day.

: This is a recurring theme for the dev team. Let's take some examples:

: Permanent death. A lot of people were strongly against this for some stupid reason. So the dev team decided that they would have permanent death, but that it would be unrealistic permanent death, so that the few people who thought permanent death was a good idea would have some reason not to like their implementation of it.

Yes, our system of permanent death is not created in the image of the real world. To do so would make permanent death far too easy to fall prey to without serious planning before every adventure. Now, while that may work for a perfect player like you, it most certainly would not work for the majority of players. If we truly copied the real world, you would die from the first arrow that sticks you in the head. While realistic, that would be entirely unfun for 90% of the players (myself included).

: Character persistence. There's been a lot of support for this idea since it was introduced on this board. Of course, the dev team has hinted that they're looking at something kind of like this, except that it won't be true persistence. It will be persistence for just a few minutes, so that if you're being chased you can get caught before you disappear. This eliminates most of the advantages of persistence, but keeps the problem with being attacked and killed when you're offline.

You really seem to be quite clueless if you think that we're going to have every character that has ever logged into the game just milling about under AI control. Imagine, if you would, hundreds of thousands of players that really serve no purpose to others in the game. All they would do is eat CPU time and bandwidth. That kind of character persistence is just not happening. We'll just have to do with the temporary forced AI control.

: Reputation. It's obvious that some kind of reputation system is needed to govern NPC behavior. So the dev team, instead of implementing a true system of character reputation indices, came up with this 'criminal flagging' concept under which reputation ranges from 'very, very bad' to 'neutral'.

You're not entirely correct here. I don't remember ever outlining our reputation system in detail on this board. Certainly, part of our implementation includes the idea of being a criminal or murderer. That's not the only impact, however. There is indeed quite a complex system that tracks your standing with different social groups and modifies NPC behavior appropriately. You are not just 'very very bad' or 'neutral'. I will not detail the system further at this time.

: Global chat. Global chat sucks. I know that, you know that, the dev team knows that. There are a few people who insist that it's not that bad. So the dev team decided to go with not-quite-global chat, which is just as unrealistic but not as useful.

Global chat does not suck. It will not ruin the game for you either. It is an easy way to contact friends and plan in-game activities without large amounts of travel. It's not realistic, to be sure, but it IS a feature that the masses will enjoy. Be sure to turn your Global Chat off if you don't like it.

: Multiple characters. This turned into a classic Band-Aid Solution scenario. The idea of multiple characters was pretty well discredited when someone adequately explained the idea of "one character per server". As with global chat, some people thought multiple characters would be useful if they weren't abused. As a concession to these people, the dev team came up with the truly brilliant idea of promising to allow multiple characters, on the same account, on the same server, and then trying to find all the ways that multiple characters could be abused and patch them with special-case rules.

We are planning very few "special-case rules" to handle multiple characters. While some of you are sticklers about this subject, I have to ask you to again consider the mass market. It is my experience that multiple people in a family play this kind of game. Not only that, but they like to be able to play in the same world to help one another to further their goals. We can't just say play on another server or buy another account if you want to play with your brother. That's just bogus. There will be people who use their multiple characters as slaves to one another. Most players will not make the time investment to maintin the abilities of multiple characters, so the need for other players in the game is not diminished.

He also did a bit of bitch slappin to a particular poster there who had one too many hampsters lodged up his ass. I edited a bit of this for obvious reasons so you guys get the meat of the interesting game related stuff.

We are not trying creating a Middle-earth simulation, but a fun game for the masses. I want to foster roleplaying, not enforce it. The average man on the street is not a roleplayer. That's a sad truth. The game should adapt to each user to ensure that each and every user has a good experience. Each of our decisions are based on what will make the game fun for most players. Our permanent death system allows players to adventure regularly and , if properly prepared, avoid permanent character death; it also allows the player the EXCITEMENT of the possibility of permanent death if they are not quite prepared enough. This will foster group play in a major way. Our global chat, in-game email and bulletin board systems give the average player easy methods of staying in contact with friends. This is vital for a vibrant community to exist in a world where people are going to be so very nomadic in nature. Our reputation, karma and stolen property systems hold players responsible for their actions, providing both excitement for the criminal who is avoiding justice and giving a rock solid foundataion for security of other players. While you espouse that the world can govern itself, I say to you that it can not. This is a GAME, not a life for most players. I can't think of too many people who want to investigate murders and track criminals. Surely there will be some, but not enough to balance the number of violent players there will be. Most players will want to create a character and have adventures. So that is the way we are skewing our system. We have taken many steps that our competitors shy away from that will foster roleplay like no other game in this genre. However, we will not be focusing on the realistic nature of the gamepley. Our focus is to make a fun, fair game that is playable by the average person. To focus too much on realistic implementations would exclude the majority of casual players.

And a bit more on character customization.

You don't understand what we have to do to properly incorporate a new race. Unlike Everquest, which you did not mention ;), our characters are fully customizable. This means that just about every little detail is changeable. Everquest allows you to choose between less than ten faces per race and sex. Not only that, if you get a green shirt in Everquest and put it on, your character does not look any different. Your character's appearance only changes when to pur major items of clothing on. Therein lies the difference. We must create enough clothing options for each race and sex combination (8 althogether). This is quite time consuming and including all the monsters, it's quite a job for our animators to handle.

Our artwork provides us enough options so that we can indeed have Eldar elves looking very different from wood elves. Unique appearances will not be a problem.

More BG love - Sacrates
PC Blitz has also gone and dished up their own take on this beast. Personally I love the feel of the game. It just feels like your playing the old school RPG's with that D&D atmosphere. Well, except for when it does that wacky hitching when you scroll! UGH! We need that 3D patch soon Bioware!

The control in Baldur’s Gate is less about actually controlling your party of characters than it is more about the game interface. The interface in Baldur’s Gate is seamless, you have full control over everything your party members do, their marching formation, their use and organization of inventory items, and even customizable scripted artificial intelligence for your fellow party members. The player is also given full control over whether the game is real time or turn based, this is mainly just for combat. Fighting by yourself against a couple of skeletons in real time might be easy, but later in the game coordinating a few archers, mages and fighters against a pack of varied monsters is a different story, and the ability to pause the game and plan out your attack character by character is definitely useful. One of this reviewers' main bones of contention with this game is the AI scripts for your party members. More often than not if you let your party members abide by their scripts in battle they usually do what you DON’T want them to do, or even just sit there and do nothing. The ability to make your own scripts is a very useful feature, but I feel that the majority of people aren’t going to want to take the time to do this.

BALDUR'S GATE HITS #1
SETS INTERPLAY SALES RECORDS
(Over 175,000 Units Sold Through In First Two Weeks)

IRVINE, California, January 14, 1999 - Black Isle Studios(tm), the role-playing game division of Interplay Entertainment Corp. (NASDAQ: IPLY), announced today that Baldur's Gate(tm) has become the #1 selling title in early reports from many U.S. and Canadian retailers, #1 in the United Kingdom according to ChartTrack, #1 in the official GfK chart in France and is leading the Media Control chart in Germany. Retailers throughout the rest of Europe, Australia, Hong Kong and Singapore also announced incredible consumer anticipation in their markets, with record levels of pre-sale activity.

To date, sell-through on Baldur's Gate is estimated at over 175,000 worldwide in the first two weeks that it has been available at retail. Developed by the Canada-based software company, BioWare Corp.(tm), Baldur's Gate began shipping to retailers on Wednesday, December 23, 1998, and has set new Interplay records previously held by Fallout 2(tm) and Descent(tm).

"It is unusual to have a game work on a worldwide basis like this and it certainly is great news to have success with a product on such a scale," said Interplay Chairman & CEO, Brian Fargo. "We had to make a critical decision to spend the extra time to get the game right and delay the release, so it's especially satisfying to see it sell through to this level. The success of Baldur's Gate just adds to the positive momentum that we have enjoyed since the release of Fallout 2 in October."

Baldur's Gate is an epic role-playing game set in the Forgotten Realms(r), TSR(r)'s most popular Advanced Dungeons & Dragons(r) campaign setting, on five CDs featuring an enormous game world filled with nearly 10,000 unique scrolling game screens, heralding over 100 hours of gameplay. The gripping non-linear adventure spans seven chapters, with dozens of subplots that branch in and out of the main narrative, and the seamless adaptation of the AD&D rule set allows the game to be played by anyone.

While Fallout set the stage for those players, it is Baldur's Gate that finally delivers what those disenchanted folks had been longing for. However, as we've said, it doesn't stop there. Q: I live out of the country, can I have it delivered to a friend in the US and have him FedEx it to me?
