Soulbringer Preview - Sacrates
Sharky Extreme just posted a nice Preview of Soulbringer for your eyes only. (Great Bond flick BTW) Head on over to Sharky's and read the rest, here is a biscuit to tide you over until then.

As the work-in-progress title suggested (RPG), Soul Bringer sets out its stall as a classical role-playing game set in a fantasy realm. The game will commence in mid-winter (so be sure to wear your mittens) upon the player's arrival in a sleepy village in search of your uncle Andrus. Throw any thoughts of familial endearment aside. Andrus' task is to relay his knowledge of sorcery and magic in exchange for gold. Yes, Andrus is truly a bastard of an uncle. Who needs an uncle like that when you've got a pawnbroker? Nevertheless, you will need to learn how to master and control magic by searching for and attaining the necessary riches for his tuition fees. The village and its folk will provide you with your best source of riches. Ultimately you will be able to barter for said riches by undergoing certain quests for the village people (no not the band that sang 'Y.M.C.A' and 'In the navy'). From there on it's entirely up to you where you go and what you do. It's as open ended as that. The future will be 'entirely in your hands' as they say.

BG Crack - Sacrates
Luke was over on the Developers Board again posting some of his thoughts on weapons. This is fat so I thought I would post the whole shebang since it's fairly dead for news today.

Since you've collected them so nicely, I thought I'd address these concerns for the good of all, instead of bouncing around the board :)

1) No S/M vs. L damage. This is a big issues that's been beaten to death, however it took two rounds longer 'cause my longsword was only doing 1-8 damage.

Two rounds longer than what? The same battle replayed...where? BG has been balanced with our weapons as they are. It does work.

2) Can't divide magic missle attacks between opponents. I think this was decided simply to make battling opposing mages more difficult.

No, this was done because combat is a real-time adaptation. Directing the spell against more than one target would require an additional targeting step. Nothing to do with making opponents more difficult.

3. Crippled Classes. Again, beaten to death,

Sure, dual and multi-classes have better abilities and stats than the combo-characters such as the ranger, bard, etc. What is new about this? The single-class goes up in levels much faster, and have a couple special abilities the combo does not. There's your trade-off.
As for broadsword being the best weapon in the game? Um, perhaps this designation is best left until it is played? We don't have a broadsword, by the way. Short, long, bastard, two-handed. Each have their advantages and disadvantages, and it will be apparent.

4. No scimitars. (although this is much less an issue now that all weapons are reasonably generic).

I Agree that these should have been in. They aren't, but will be in the future. We picked the basic spread of weapons (as we did with character classes. The optional Druid class came after cleric, hence the unintentional loss). Yes the most popular character in the Realms uses them, but the ability to re-create Drizzt was never a thought in the forefront. Same deal with two-handed fighting. It will be dealt with in the future.

5. Class options: Ranger/Cleric but no Ranger/Druid, Cleric/Mage but not Druid/Mage, no Fighter/Mage/Druid. I still can't understand why these options weren't implemented.

This is an issue in the way that character information is stored. Allowable combinations are common to both dual and multi. This will be considered again in the sequel when we have time to restructure the basic characters, now that the process is better understood, though some classes will still not be available do to the rule inherent in AD&D. For example, by the way, Ranger/Druid is not possible due to alignment conflicts (ranger must have good, druid must be true neutral). They exist in FR, sure, but are a special sect of the followers of one particular goddess. Rare as hens teeth, and will remain so.
Druid + mage does not make sense, considering they cannot use written magical items. Since the cleric and cleric sub-classes overrule combinations in regards to weapons allowed, the same considerations would make for one odd mage :P

6. Mage spell acquisition. AFAIK mages must find spells and don't automatically receive additional spells of their choice for their spellbook when advancing in level. (this may have changed, I hope so but I'm not sure) I can see it now, a rogue opens a chest and the mage is in the background rubbing his hands together mumbling "come on, papa needs a new scroll of fireball"!

Not sure which way you are falling on this one. Aside from their starting choices, Mages in BG must buy or find their spells, regardless of level. They do not simply "know" them on level-up like clerics.

Just an additional note to no one in particular; the general feeling I get is that we have left weapon balancing until now. This is not the case. Weapons have been in lock-down for some months now. Balancing currently involves the toughness of encounters and such, within the context of the game as it is, meaning "balanced for the weapons and spells and whatnot that the game WILL use." The game will not be harder because we do not have L damage, nor would it be easier if we did have it. It would be (and is) balanced accordingly.

We have stayed as true to the AD&D rules and the FEEL of the system as we could, and will continue to do so. Some changes will be made in the future, but I stand by the work we have done. To start slagging the game because of the few (!) discrepancies at this point is getting close to tunnel vision. Take a step back and keep the big picture in mind. It's a great game, you know, and it FEELS like AD&D.

And he caps it off with this bit O goodness.

Why use a two-hander instead of a longsword?

Well, it's AD&D, so they both strike the same number of times in a round. The Longsword hits once for moderate damage, the two-hander hits once for more damage. The longsword does a little less damage and hits sooner in the round(better speed factor), the two-hander does more and hits a little later. Maybe you want that extra bit of damage for a hobgoblin so you use the two-hander. Conversly, maybe you want to disrupt a mage quickly, so you use the longsword (or shortsword, or dagger).

Additionally, if you have a bow equipped in a "quick-weapon" slot you cannot have a shield equipped. Might as well pack that two-hander, unless of course you still want to disrupt that mage...decisions decisions.

As for Role-play reasons...you try and tell that Conan-sized 18/96 fighter that he should use a longsword.

EQ Beta info - Sacrates
EQVault found that the official EQ beta page has been updated with some Q and A's. You'll defenitely want to scoop this out.

Has the EverQuest beta already started?

Yes, the closed beta has already begun. A small testing phase (called Phase .5) began right after E3, Phase One began July 15, 1998, and Phase Two began on September 15, 1998. Phase Three will begin in early November.

How many people will be involved with Phase Three, and does it require an NDA?

Phase Three will require an NDA, and will involve approximately 5,000 testers.

When will Phase Four start, how many people will it involve, and will it require an NDA?

Phase Four will begin in early December and will be open to those who applied for a small fee. An NDA will not be required for Phase Four.

Where do I send my money and how much will it cost?

Everyone who correctly filled out the beta application form (and as long as the information is still valid) will receive an email within the next several days detailing the Phase Four beta test. All the information you'll need to get started in Phase Four will be available in the email.

Why is the beta application no longer available?

We have received all the beta applications we need, hence the reason for removing the form.

Lord British speaks... - Bowmeister
With yet more RPG goodness, Desslock has put up the first part of a MASSIVE interview with Richard Garriot aka Lord British of Ultima fame. It looks like if you have any questions that remain unanswered, this interview will cover them :). Check out the first part of the interview here, and here's a snippet to whet yer appetite.

Desslock: You've indicated that this will be the last Ultima to feature the Avatar as the player character, and that as the ninth core game in the series, Ultima: Ascension will mark the end of the "trilogy of trilogies" with a truly epic story-line. Are you satisfied that the plot of Ultima: Ascension has shaped up in a way that meets those ambitious goals?

Richard Garriott: Let me answer an even longer question than the one you've asked. Whenever I talk about Ultima: Ascension to people who are relatively familiar with Ultima series, I like to introduce the story by first talking about the events in the series that led up to Ultima IX [Ultima: Ascension], and what I see as the most important elements of previous Ultima stories.

Like you said, I see this game as the end of the "trilogy of trilogies", which is really the way I feel about the game. If you look at the first three Ultimas, they really only related to each other by name. They were set in different worlds, had radically different design approaches, and they didn't have epic plots.

They were largely just "Dungeons & Dragons" style, medieval adventure games. I only really tied the stories together after the fact, whenever I started the next game in the series.

Middle Earth info - Sacrates
Stephen Nichols was over on the ME Boards slappin around some good mofo for us ME fans. These guys have got a lot riding on their shoulders with this game. If there ever was a name brand that is revered it's Tolkien's Middle Earth stuff. I would say some of these guys can get very "anal" if you start fiddling too much with Tolkien's work. Anyway read what Stephen had to say on some subjects of interest.

On System Requirements: We can't be any more specific about system requirements at this time. Our estimates (and running prototypes) show that a P300 would be a fine minimum. We may make even less detailed polygon models for the lower spectrum of machines. That decision will wait until we are nearing completion.

On Fog of War: Lets pretend that every adventurer is really careful and constantly surveys their surroundings. That explains why people can be seen coming from behind. ;) On the other hand, we could (and probably will in some areas) make seeing other players a function of line of sight. Not really a fog of war, but something similar.

Zones etc: You're right, there will be a pause between room changes (under a second in most cases). Would you prefer the alternative provided by UO (i.e. Walk, pause, Walk, pause...)? The room-based approach simplifies a great many things and allows us to control the saturation of players in any particular area. No matter how fast our servers are, no modem will be able to handle the information needed to update more than 30 people at one time (and that's optimistic). What do you do if you walk into a crowded area with 200 people? Certainly, there are solutions to this, but they are all pretty ugly to implement and provide peculiar behavior to the player. Let's take UO for example. Have you ever been walking about in UO and suddenly see a castle appear right next to you? Or perhaps you may see a naked person appear for a split second before his clothes. Those are all side effects that arise from trying to provide the world information to each player when things come within a certain distance. I'll choose the room-based approach (with whopping large rooms) over the walk, lag and see bizarre artifacts approach any day.

On Katmai and 3DNow: Yes, Middle-earth will be optimized for both 3DNow and Katmai technology.

And here is some juicy biscuits on Weapons.

I'm going to throw this out for folks to gnaw on, because it is an obvious question no one has asked.

What about missile weapons?

If because of the way BG is set up, you can pretty much fire at anything within your range of sight, what is the difference between a short bow, long bow and composite bow? In AD&D they all do the same damage (with "to hit" adjustments) but given that almost all missile combat in BG is at short range, why would you prefer to own a long bow over a short bow?

I am throwing this out to illustrate something... which is that while we will be as close as possible to AD&D rules, there will be areas that we will have to stray from somewhat. The missile weapon issue we have already addressed (or we have a plan to address) but I'd like to hear people's opinions on this...

So, since this is a development board, let's discuss two areas...

1) How should weapon damage work if we cannot offer separate damage for S/M versus L monsters? (People shouldn't get too nervous about this... 95% or more of the monsters in the game are S/M)

2) How should we resolve the missile weapon thing? (For bows and crossbows)

This is asking for feedback... the game is in final balancing right now and a tweak of a +1 here or there to adjust weapons to make the system work better is totally appropriate.

Different damage versus Large creatures is out...

I just found this out today - but it is because I am finalizing the manual - there are some references to Large damage here and there, but no mention in the damage tables...

Large damage went away because of the following (from a programming standpoint):

1) There are NOT very many Large monsters in the game.

2) It would require adding another item information 'slot' for weapon damage against Large creatures.

3) It would require adding another monster information 'slot' for monster size.

Though this doesn't sound like much, it adds significantly to the complexity of the game... it adds an extra processing step when you attack in order to ascertain the monster size, it adds another step to ascertain the proper damage table to be used. It may not sound like much, but when 95% of the time you are battling kobolds, wizards, bandits, hobgoblins, etc, etc it takes its toll...

The balancing issue comes down to final tweaks - not total code rewrites. A perfect example of this is we are going over EVERY character class in the game from a quality control standpoint to make sure we understand where we are relative to AD&D rules, and to make sure that the manual and the game play are totally consistent. This is not a very easy task especially because of the complexity of some of the classes, and the fact that we have to be 100% fixed in our application of rules, when AD&D allows flexibility on the part of the DM.

For example, the Paladin...
All of the core abilities were in, but we only have room for so many special abilities. We felt that Protection from Evil (as an innate ability) was more important than Cure Disease (once per week), so Protection from Evil went in as a special ability... however, Holy Swords? none in BG (though we may have them in the expansion) War Horse at 4th level? none in BG (but damn, would we love to do horses soon) Now to balance some of this out, we allow Paladins to specialize in weapons, which is NOT allowed in AD&D 2nd edition rules... so you can see how there is a little 'balancing' here and there that is needed to make the game 'work' in a computer setting.

The weird thing about the Large damage issue... I have been playing the game for a month now, and if I weren't helping with the manual I would have never noticed the absence... I manage to kill all my ogre mages, it just requires a slightly different application of tactics... fighters are NOT weak characters in the game... throw in weapon specialization and you will be slicing things up without too many worries... ;)

The next logical question is how this differs from Spiritual Hammer. Three types of quick slots: quick weapon, quick spell and quick item. Quick weapon and quick spell are determined by your class. For example, a fighter will get 4 quick weapons, but no quick spells (obviously). A wizard will get 1 quick weapon, and 3 quick spells.
