The Longest Journey Interview
By the Sultan of Smack: Sacrates
With: Ragnar Tornquist (Producer)

There are games that come along from time to time that really capture the imagination and not let it go. A game that immerses you in a world that seems so real you can't tell the difference from where you sit and what you see before you on the screen. The Longest Journey being made by Funcom is one of these games. Enjoy the screenshots we have dished out for you and sit back, relax and let Sacrates and Ragnar caress your brains with some goodness that is called "The Longest Journey".

Sacrates: Let's get the typical stuff out of the way first and tell the Altered readers who you are, what you do and what the meaning of life is.

Ragnar: My name is Ragnar Tornquist, and I'm the producer, writer and lead designer of The Longest Journey (TLJ), which is being developed by Funcom here in Oslo, Norway. My "oeuvre" so far includes Casper for the Sega Saturn and Playstation, published by Interplay, and I've dipped my toe into a couple of other Funcom projects during my four years with the company, but TLJ has basically occupied my life from dawn to dusk the past two-and-a-half years. As for the meaning of life? Doing what you love and getting paid for doing it. Which I'm lucky enough to have learned first-hand.

Sacrates: Well, this game is a visual feast for the eyes. Let's hit some engine related questions. Could you give an idea what type of system we'll be needing and what will run at good frame rates in high res.

Ragnar: TLJ runs perfectly on my P2-400! The game is a visual feast, true, but it's not a hog; it actually scales well to most hardware, though the minimum spec will probably be a P-166 with some kind of hardware acceleration. In software-only mode (Direct3D), you'll probably need a P200-MMX to get an acceptable framerate, but it depends on what you're able to live with in terms of performance.

Sacrates: What kind of V2 features will you be using in the game? What kinds of bells and whistles can we expect. What types of 3D effects will we be seeing?

Ragnar: As far as V2-specific features, we're not doing anything too flashy; mostly, the V2 will provide smoother framerates, better lighting, prettier textures and a general feeling of bliss! There aren't any lensflares or gigantic explosions -- it just ain't that kind of game. But the V2 will enable you to play using the high-polygon version of April, the lead character, and I'd say that's reward enough. As for 3D effects, we're using dynamic, coloured lighting, and real-time shadows (which are just as detailed as the characters themselves), and we might throw in a couple of surprises for the game's release. You'll just have to wait and see.

Sacrates: About how much of the game is 2D and what is 3D? Will 3D be used to improve frame rates and such similar to what Diablo 2 is doing?

Ragnar: It's about 50/50 in terms of 2D and 3D (think Resident Evil 2, Grim Fandango). All the backgrounds, environmental animations and objects are in 2D, while the characters and vehicles are in 3D. However, the entire game is, in a way, 3D, since all the scenes have been created in 3D-Studio MAX 2.0 and exported using our proprietary tools into the game-engine. This allows the real-time characters to be lit perfectly in relation to the backgrounds, and to be scaled and Z-buffered (i.e. move in front of and behind objects depending on where they are on the floor) while still preserving the complexity of the original, pre-rendered scenes. Considering the complexity of the backgrounds, it'll be a few years before real-time
technology is up to the task of doing it all in real-time; we wanted a very
detailed, very textured, very REAL world in which to place our characters, and this technology allowed us to do that very well.

Sacrates: How detailed will the NPC AI be? Will they have lives of their own or will they be at preset locations in the game?

Ragnar: Since TLJ is an adventure-title with a heavily scripted plot, the characters will appear at preset locations. However, some characters -- e.g. pedestrians, waitresses in taverns -- are controlled by semi-random
parameters, making them look a little less scripted than what we're used to seeing in adventure-games. But mainly it's a very controlled world, although that doesn't mean it's predictable. Unless you play it twice, that is.

Sacrates: What type of NPC interaction will there be? Will it be pre written scripted stuff or will there be branching dialogue?

Ragnar: Most dialogues having a branching structure, and unless you play the game 3-4 times you'll never hear all the dialogue. What you say, and how you treat, certain characters, will have an impact on the gameplay. Being rude to someone in Chapter One may cause you problems later on, and while you can never "get stuck", you might have to find alternative solutions to problems. Most characters also have an extensive database of information on other characters, locations and inventory-items, and you'll be able to ask them about all these things, provided you know what to ask about. Not everything is relevant to solving the puzzles; a lot of it is there just to provide a detailed setting, to establish relationships and conflicts, and to allow the player the ability to explore and experience beyond the boundaries of the plot. I'd say in terms of character interaction TLJ is more comparable to a game like the first Gabriel Knight than a lot of more recent adventures --
the dialogues are very, very important.

Sacrates: How will the story be told? Will there be scripted sequences using the actual game engine (ie. Half Life) or will it be pre-rendered cinematic sequences.

Ragnar: A good mix of both, really. I'm a big fan of the game-engine type of story sequences, as they tend to be less intrusive on the flow of the game, and there will be a lot of those. However, to create really atmospheric, emotional moments, and to surprise the player, we're also including over 20 minutes of pre-rendered FMV. Mostly, though, the player will need to "discover" the story on her own, through dialogues, books, general observation, and even through dreams.

Sacrates: One of my personal favorite questions now is sound support. What type of sound will you be using in the game? Will you have SB Live support at all? Will you be taking advantage of 3D sound?

Ragnar: Sound is one of the areas where we've been investing a lot of time and effort. We've had an in-house musician working full-time on the music for over 2 years, creating a very atmospheric, very cinematic score, and we also have one man dedicated, full-time, to doing the sound-effects. As for 3D-sound, we looked into doing it at the very beginning, but since our camera is fixed it won't really be very effective except for spot-effects, so we've limited ourselves to panning and fading. We support DirectSound, but no specific hardware. The quality in our audio is in the actual music and sound, and not in the real-time processing of it.

Sacrates: What type of RPG Elements do you think will be in the game? Will there be typical stuff like Inventory etc?

Ragnar: Inventory, branching dialogues, books to read, potions to mix, items to
manipulate, monsters to beat, ancient mysteries to uncover...we've got it
all. There is no actual combat, however -- April, our heroine, prefers
brains to brawns -- and there are no stats to improve. TLJ is, for the
better part of the game, a pure adventure.

Sacrates: What kind of Audience are you looking to attract with The Longest Journey? RPGers, Adventure gamers etc?

Ragnar: I'd love to say "everybody", but unfortunately, that'll never happen. You can't please all the people all the time. First and foremost, we're making a game that adventure-players (like me, and most of the TLJ team) will love -- this is a true point-and-click adventure with all the latest bells and whistles -- and hopefully hardcore RPG-players will be intrigued by the immense detail in the two worlds (one of magic, one of science), and the number of characters you meet and interact with. Additionally, I think this is a game that's more accessible to the casual player than most PC-games, as it's pretty forgiving (you can't die), requires no hand-eye co-ordination to speak of, looks beautiful, and has a very complex, very involving story. If you're only into first-person shooters, or fighting-games, or RTS-games, this may not be your cup of tea. But, then again, why not try something new?

Sacrates: Out of all these great features we see with your game how do you think the game sticks out and possibly breaks news ground?

Ragnar: Technologically, we're doing a lot of interesting stuff, especially for an adventure-game (historically the least tech- and feature-driven genre) -- like high-polygon characters, dynamic, coloured lighting, detailed shadows, a skeletal animation-system with real-time interpolation, lip-synching -- but where we break new ground, so to speak, is in the sheer size and complexity of our worlds, the quality of the graphics, and the complexity of the story. We really have built two (plus one -- you'll just have to wait and see) worlds from the ground up, and we've spent every waking moment these past two-and-a-half years doing it. I think people will be really intrigued by the setting, and, after that, they'll be engulfed in the storyline, which is a lot more adult and "serious" than what most adventure-gamers are used to.

Sacrates: Is this entirely a puzzle based game? Or will there be a lot of interaction with NPC's etc?

Ragnar: We have a large variety of puzzles, ranging from simple (and not so simple) inventory-based puzzles, to logical puzzles, to conversation-based puzzles, and even some action-based puzzles where quick thinking is key -- although, as I've said earlier, you can't die. Like I also stated earlier, character interaction plays a big part in TLJ, as does exploration, as does a general sense of curiosity. The puzzles are all integrated -- I hope -- well into the story, so the player should never feel like she's just "unlocking doors" to get to the next location. It's a puzzle-based game, sure, but not like Myst was a puzzle game. TLJ is first and foremost an adventure.

Sacrates: What is your opinion of the 3D market at the moment? What do you expect to see or be doing in the next few years in terms of 3D technology?

Ragnar: The next generation of adventure-games -- two to three years from now -- will, graphically, be doing what TLJ is doing now, but in real-time. In other words, realistic, detailed environments where the freedom to explore is almost boundless. The challenge lies in presenting the world to the player in an interesting way, eschewing the first-person perspective for something more cinematic, with dynamic cameras, staged scenes, reaction-shots, engine-driven story-sequences; a living, breathing environment where the player still has the ability to move at will at all times, and to react to everything that's going on while it's going on. 3D will also allow genres to merge better; the pure-bred point-and-click adventure-game is on the way out, making way for games that mix and match genres to provide a much more interesting and well-rounded gaming experience. 3D will allow us to do this, and that's a good thing. Not because the adventure-genre is stale and old-hat; quite the opposite, actually. The building-stones of a good adventure -- exploration, conversation, manipulation of the environment, and puzzles -- are found in most modern games, from RPGs to action-titles. 3D allows us to take down the walls between genres, and to work towards that perfect gaming experience.

As you can see, I have been thinking about this for a while ;)

Sacrates: Also in terms of 3D, what do you think of the new 3D sound cards like SB Live etc and do you expect that to be a big part of the experience?


Ragnar: Definitely, yeah. The next generation of PC games -- and even some recent and upcoming releases, like Half-Life -- will pay more attention to sound than ever before, and it's about time. Sound is one of the areas developers have been ignoring, which is a shame, because, done right, sound can add so much to the experience. Expect to see 3D sound play a big part in games in the future, as well as sound modeling -- modifying sounds in real-time based on environmental factors such as space, texture, humidity, etc.

Sacrates: About what percent of your game is complete now? When can we expect to see this game in stores?

Ragnar: We're about 75% complete at the moment, and we're looking at a release somewhere in the first quarter of next year. That said, we're taking our time to do this right -- we're not going to rush it out the door. So no definite release-date as of this moment.

Sacrates: About how many hours do expect the average gamer to put behind The Longest Journey? You would think with a name like that quite a few?

Ragnar: Hopefully -- though it is difficult to tell at this stage -- the average gamer will spend about 30-40 hours with "The Longest Journey". There's certainly enough to look at, listen to, read and experience to keep someone busy for at least that long. An experienced player who's simply intent on getting through as fast as possible (with a finger on the escape-key) might do it in 10-15 hours. But, again, it's difficult to tell. At any rate, it's certainly not a small game, with over 120 locations and 50 speaking characters to interact with.

Sacrates: The game looks very cinematic looking at many of the current screenshots. Can we expect to find changing music in relation to what is happeing in the game or will it be preset music to the current level or area we're in.


Ragnar: To some degree, yeah, the music will reflect your current situation, but since the game is heavily scripted, the music is not really "interactive". That said, we don't have background music playing all the time (something that can soon get quite irritating); music is there to provide a "punch" in specific locations, specific situations, and so it doesn't really have to be very interactive as long as it appears on cue. Which it does. When you least expect it.

Sacrates: So with all that said, how do you feel The Longest Journey will stick out in todays market? What do you expect to really blow us away with?


Ragnar: I think I've probably said more than enough already, so I'll be short and to the point; incredible graphics, cool technology, and a story that's not just a rehash of your basic recover-the-ancient-artefact-and-destroy-the-evil-wizard storyline. And fun puzzles. And a heroine with attitude. And...well, you'll have to     wait and see.
