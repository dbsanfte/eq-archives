Deliverance from the dark - Sacrates
Mike Duffy, the man with the plan from Studio Blue broke down and ripped out a big ass devlopment update. For the whole thing just go over to VN, they were willing to sacrifice load speed to accomodate that beast. Not me, i'm just gonna give you little lovin.

Over-anticipation - When exactly does one begin to show off stuff about a game in development? With some companies it's when they first come up with a concept. I think the Dragonriders of Pern game falls in this category, as do many others. The problem here is that the audience gets really excited about a game, only to get really annoyed when it is not released even years after the announcement. Also the initial excitement generated by the game is lost if it takes too long for the game to come out. For example, when the first screenshots of Septerra Core appeared in Computer Gaming World, I was extremely excited about the game as were others. Then came months of nothing. No new info, no demo, and most of all no game. That was over a year ago. Still no game. So now when Septerra Core does come close to being released, they have to build up excitement all over again. All the people who remember the original announcement of the game are all wondering, "So where the hell have you guys been all this time?" Our game, Deliverance From The Dark, is over a year away from release at best. If things get delayed, it could slip to a spring 2000 release. I don't want to get people too excited about the game only to have their excitement turned sour by a long wait and accusations of vaporware.

Infinite Worlds bits - Sacrates
Sean L Palmer was on the boards discussing a bit on the name change and graphics in Infinite Worlds.

on the graphics: Infinite Worlds is not a Quake-killer or an Unreal-killer by any means. It's not a high-fps game, and the graphics, as you stated, are dated. However they are, how should I say this, "Good enough", "Competitive". And they will be getting better.

If we were to make a game with Unreal quality graphics we'd have to scrap the entire game so far and make a new one. We did these graphics so that the original game could be kept intact while making it more visually pleasing. Anything more is gonna have to wait til after this game's released.

on the current level of light: We'll definitely be doing something about the dungeon brightness for the next release. We did a fairly last-minute change to fix a lighting issue and also to deal with some races having the dungeon way too bright and it seems to have broken things. Bear with us.

I think instead of upping the ambient light for races that can see better, we'll be increasing the amount of light that all the lights in the game put out, depending on race. So in pitch darkness it won't be bright as day like before with the Saris, but the Saris will definitely have things brighter than Humans will. Especially where there is already light in the room from a torch or something.

Also.. be aware that there IS a spell which will make things brighter... but I'm not sure which class can cast it, or at what level you learn it. IMO it ought to be a newbie spell, and you should be able to buy torches in the store if you're not a mage, but that's not my department..

on the meaning of the name change to Infinite Worlds: Interplay liked the name. They picked it from a huge list of other names we came up with. It's mainly referring I think to the fact that the release version will allow people to make custom levels and let other people play those new levels on their custom servers..

on how much variety there will be with weapons, armor, and attire to establish appearance for characters: Wellll not quite.

More BG bug info - Sacrates
BG Chronicles poked a bit more BG updates up. This time Scott Greig was over on the Devlopers Corner yacking at the mouth again. There should be a limit as to what these guys should be saying. I mean, I can virtually write a FAQ right now off the top of my head without using notes from all this info. Oh well, to each his own.

On whether characters can choose different targets for their multiple attacks in one round: No, sorry. This is kind of moot anyway. It is rare that you have more than one target in weapon range anyway. The exception is of ranged weapons. We have discovered that ranged weapons become too powerful if you can target multiple targets in the same round. You have to aim after all ;).

As per D&D rules, all attack rounds are the same length. If both persons have the same number of attacks / round, the person with the faster weapon goes eariler in the round. This is real important when attacking mages or priests. You will want to use a quick weapon to disrupt those pesky mages. If the weapons have different ranges, each persons's attack round starts when they get within weapon range of their target.

You will only get infravision if only one character is selected and that character [someone with infravision] is selected. It used to be if any of the selected characters had infravision but it [was] on too much and had stopped being an advantage to those who had the ability.

If anyone finds a trap or secret door then everyone becomes aware of it. There is no logical reason for this, it just works better in multiplayer, although a few people did try to sucker their friends into setting off the trap for them. With friends like these...

UO Lawsuit update - Bowmeister
I noticed this little tid bit over on Gamespot. I haven't been paying a lot of attention to this as it's basically a joke, but it's still nice to check in once in a blue moon and see who's winning.

A San Diego judge today rejected oral arguments of attorneys representing Electronic Arts and Origin Systems, Inc. The EA lawyers wanted to have the lawsuit brought by certain Ultima Online gamers moved from superior court to small claims court. Instead, Judge Judith McConnell set a trial date in superior court of February 11. At that time, opening arguments will take place in Case No. 718593: Ken Hinther, et al., Plaintiffs v. Electronic Arts, Inc.; et al., Defendants.

"This is big news for us," George Schultz, the attorney representing the six gamers said today after attending the hearing.

The case will now be heard in superior court which requires that money damages sought by plaintiff's be at least US$25,000. Small claims court on the other hand sets a limit of $5,000 for damages.

EA's attorneys can still file a written motion to have the trial moved to small claims court - and they likely will. Calls to an Electronic Arts spokesperson were not returned at press time.

Today's decision has no bearing on the earlier rejection by Judge McConnell of Schultz' request to have his case heard as a class action lawsuit (which potentially would open the case up to thousands of unhappy gamers). Although Schultz said today he would likely bring a new motion for class certification and will also amend his original complaint. These moves by Schultz will address the recent release of the Ultima Online add-on, The Second Age, which Origin is charging North American gamers for $6.99. Schultz says UO add-ons had been promised to gamers free of any charge.

LMK update - Sacrates
Those wacky biscuits over at Larien Studios have kicked up a fat mother of an update. Lots of good stuff that only your mother would make you eat. We're talking grade A spinach LMK update news. Here's a small bite for the complete meal head on over to the LMK site.

AI wise the main focus in the last weeks has been speed. I've stuffed the npc's with so much stuff to do that I started getting some performance problems. This has a lot to do with the fact that the entire world is running in real time. Even if there is no player on a particular spot, the npcs are stilling acting realistically so that when you get to that spot, the state of the world looks like it's been lived in. Which brings me to an interesting observation made by Bert the other day. André, an artist at Attic, had made this rendering of an axe laying on a chopped piece of wood. When we received the rendering, our immediate reaction was, this is cool but he rendered both objects together, which given the goal that every object which looks like it can be used or moved can actually be used or moved, wasn't ok. So we were going to send him an email asking him to render them apart, when Bert said something so trivial and simple, that I nearly choked. He said: "Hey, this is cool. It really makes the world look alive". Since making the world live is my departement I started wondering why he would say such a thing. After all, I have all these AI tricks built in which make the characters appear like they are doing something sensible in the world, and then a stupid axe (sorry André ) lying on a chopped piece of wood made him think the world looked alive. Why was that I wondered? It took me some time to realize a fundamental thing I had been overlooking. If you browse through books about artificial life you'll find frequent occurences of the phrase "emergent behaviour". The meaning of this is that life seems to be not a property of some local or atomic elements, but rather of the whole of these elements from which life "emerges". I'm very well aware of it, and the goal of having all these AI tricks in LMK has always been to come up with a combination which was going to suggest "emergent" behaviour, but somehow, so engrossed with the specifics of doing specific actions, I managed to forget about it. I had like this list of things my characters had to do, they have to eat, they have to talk to each other and so forth, but the basic reason why I was doing this had completely slipped my mind: I wanted to suggest life. And what better way to suggest life than having a woodcutter who forgot his axe while he ran off to his wife to eat? So I'm busy now with something that might more than all my tricks suggest life, but as I'm not sure it'll make it to the game, I'll just shut up about it for a bit.

Runesword news - Sacrates
Over at VN I noticed that Adam West had some good things to say over on the Usenet about Runesword. Read on oh chimply reader.

I haven't posted in some time, so I thought I'd drop a note and encourage you to stop by our web page and check us out. Our Beta is nearing.

RuneSword is a construction set *and* a game. It's about playing *and* creating.

RuneSword is an editor for the "Dungeon Master" in all of us. It is all Windows; there's no manual coding in a text editor. We have property sheets for every object in the game and basically, you click and go. The intent is to make it easy to get in, do your thing, and get back out.

Some of RuneSword's features are unique (at least, we think they are so far). We're happy about Themes, the "grist for the mill" of the Random Dungeon generator. A Theme is a collection of elements --- rooms, creatures, items and what have you. The idea here is you can build part of a module and have the editor fill in the rest using one or more Themes. So you have a Dark Temple and you want to populate it. You make a few rooms by hand. Drag in an Evil Temple Theme and click. The computer fills out the rest. This can be used to assist you in completing a module or add randomness while you're playing so even you the creator will be surprised.

Another feature is that it's completely component based. We're going to have Monster Manuals and Treasure Troves that you click and get what you want. And you can build any object from scratch or use an existing and tweak it. Want a Dragon? Click. Done. How about a wand? Click. Done. Add a spell to that Mage? Click. Done. And I like that Trap. Click. Done.

All of these things, Themes, Monsters, NPC's, Characters, Items, etc. are objects, and can be made and stored up in libraries like other components and traded around the web. Anyone can add to these libraries. Maybe there was an excellent artifact or demonic vampire you've used in a campaign before--you could just contribute those to the library, and maybe you'll find them pop up in someone else's tome.

And that's just the beginning.

Check us out. Let us know your thoughts. Because you make the game. We're just writing it.

RTK goodies - Sacrates
So here I am listening to Manson's newest CD on this new Sound Card and loving every second of it. But you know the best part? You got it, I can listen to this great music and read some great RPG news at the same time. I can also chew gum and walk at the same time also. Man of many talents eh? Anyway, Sinjin Bain was on the RTK boards of love and had this goodness to spout.

on the scope of the game: The major areas of the world you will visit; the city of Krondor (including the sewers), the wilderness travelling, Haldon Head, the Black Pearl Temple are as detailed as we could make them within the context of a shippable game. As it stands now, the game already requires 2 CD’s.

The action takes place in a small geographic region along the coast of the bitter sea, the map in PC Gamer is of all of Midkemia. We though people would like that more as an insert. The smaller geographic region is explored in great detail... so, again, it's a matter of scale, not of size. The game is huge.

on the story and quests: RtK has a much richer and more defined story [than Betreyal at Krondor] that required some tradeoffs in the openness of the world and number of side quests, this is something that has been reported in many previews so far. Fortunately, it seems as if the quality and depth of the story is so good that this is almost always viewed as a positive. The side quests are deeper and hopefully contribute to the fullness of the overall story. It is a three tiered story structure; first, the overall game-long story... second, individual chapter specific stories and quests... and third inter-chapter related events and story elements. We are really trying to do something new here… and that is present a level of story that has previously not been previously accomplished. This is an area we really wanted to excel in for obvious reasons (Raymond E. Feist.)

You really don't "go to some guy" to get quests, it's a bit more subtle than that. Curing the overall quest, for instance, in one chapter you have to solve a murder. No one overtly tells you this is the quest, you discover the quest yourself and then solve it. In addition to this quest within the chapter there are other smaller subquests, and then even under that layer, again, you are free to poke around the city to explore/fight/find treasure, etc.

The game is "progressive" in nature in that you move through the environment and story, it is not circular where everything looks the same.

on difficulty settings: We have hardness levels that will affect how the game needs to be played. I don’t want to get into all the details of this but in general, for instance, you would expect that the harder the setting of the game the more dangerous combat is and the more creative solutions to combat and spellcasting / creation the player must come up with. In addition you would expect that inventories of vanquished opponents will differ between hardness settings (you probably won’t find great potions just lying around in the hard setting, you’ll have to find / buy the recipes and ingredients and brew ‘em up… sorry.
