Westwood Studios Unveils Swords & Sorcery

Epic Role Playing Game from RPG Legend D.W. Bradley Due in 1999

LAS VEGAS, NV, Dec. 22, 1998 -- Westwood Studios proudly announces Swords & Sorcery: Come Devils, Come Darkness(tm), an epic new Role Playing Game from legendary RPG designer D. W. Bradley. Sword & Sorcery is the first game from Bradley's new development company, Heuristic Park, and the first to place his distinctive visions in a 3D world. Gamers will be able to explore the worlds of Sword and Sorcery when the CD-ROM game ships in the summer of 1999.

Swords and Sorcery is a classic RPG complete with statistics, spell casting and character classes. Players will be able to build parties of up to six characters, choosing from 10 races - from humans and fantasy staples such as Elves and Dwarves to a feline race and an elephantine race. The game's 15 character classes range from Warrior to Monk. The player assigns skills and attributes from the numerous characteristics available to them.

"This is the game I've always wanted to make," said Bradley, the creative force behind Sir Tech's Wizardry V, VI and VII. "With Swords & Sorcery, we're giving players an amazing amount of freedom with character development, control, viewpoint and combat. The result, I think, will be as pure an RPG experience as you can have without a bag full of 12-sided dice."

Swords and Sorcery can be played from both third and first person perspectives, and from the point of view of any member of the player's party. This gives the player more choices whether in combat or solving puzzles. Characters can also be grouped together to multi-task on separate objectives simultaneously, in separate locations. Combat takes place in phased turns.

For the first time in an RPG, characters have full continuity between single and multiplayer games, allowing them to be transferred between the two play modes with their statistics, abilities and inventories intact. Multiplay will be over Westwood Online, an online gaming service with more than a million members.

The game's grand quest spans three distinctive environments, each consisting of four regions, which make up the world of Gael-Serran. This land is under threat from the evil High Priest Cet Ude D'ua Khan, who is planning to rise up and conquer all, following his awakening from an entombed sleep. Only the Staff of Death, which wields the power of the Black Fire, can defeat the dreaded Et. First, however, players must recover the legendary blessed and cursed Mavin Sword. Through such quests, Sword and Sorcery's sub-plots and adventures unfold into an epic RPG experience.

EQ tidbit - Sacrates
Finally the EQ team has wisened up and have begun to get more info out to the general public on how things are going etc. Obviously Brad (Spawn of Satan) wasn't getting the job done with his every other week posts. Here's an additional post from Gunther as an appendage (Not that kind of appendage damnit) to Theseus'  post on Hackers etc. Check it.

On Hackers etc: The Development team has coders working closely with some very good outside 'hackers', in order to ensure that the game is as hack-proof as possible when it goes final.

On High level characters: There is a periodic need for a group of high-level characters to test a specific game function, or dungeon. Testers are chosen and are given 'buffed' characters to use for these tests. They are not allowed to use these characters in normal play, and if caught doing so will be banned. These tests are always done in the company of GM's or Guides.

In final, there will be no 'buffing' allowed, and 989 is developing very strict and no-nonsense guidelines for the GM staff that will prevent abuse of powers (who wants to lose thier job and paycheck over buffing a buddy in a game?).

Rest assured, that the other games of this genre have been observed, and the lessons learned by those games are being taken into account by 989 in the development of customer service policies and guidelines for Everquest.

QFG 5 Review - Sacrates
Talking about a game that has been getting some bad reception. This game is getting eaten up like Chocolate hidden on a Fat Farm. (Small aside: Ever wonder how a hit man would take out Richard Simmons? Just spray a nice chocalate mist on him and watch as his own fans begin to eat him alive.) Anyway, IGN.PC does the bitch slappin here...so head on over and read up on it.

Quest for Glory V's storyline is much as you'd expect it. The king of Silmaria has been assassinated, and the kingdom is in trouble. After poking around the main city a bit (and running into several friends from your past adventures) you'll discover that the new king is being selected by a process known as the Rite of Rulership. Five entries are given five great quests to complete and the overall winner is proclaimed king. Obviously you join in and the adventure begins. The quests are extremely dangerous and to make matters worse, that assassin is still out there trying to pick off candidates. In order to survive, you've got to be quick, or smart, or strong. Although the tale starts off a little hokey, if you're like me, it won't take but about an hour before you're sucked into the story and continue play simply to see what happens next.

RTK Review - Sacrates
Since I haven't posted many of these chipmunks I thought I would get around to tossing one or two your way. Like I said before, i'm not a big fan of tossing you every single review of a game that all have basically the same take on it as the last one. Here's a bit from Gamespot's.

While Betrayal at Krondor was primarily a first-person-perspective game, Return to Krondor opts for a stylish, but occasionally disorienting, variety of third-person perspectives. Unlike the suddenly abundant third-person-perspective games using a "trailing camera," in Return to Krondor the camera angles are stationary in each scene, although you can choose from a variety of camera views in most key encounter areas in the game. As you move your party through the gaming world, using either directional keys or by simply mouse-clicking on a targeted location, you prompt automatic shifts in the game's camera perspective. The frequently shifting camera perspectives is often disorienting, since clicking on a location at the top of your screen regularly results in your characters actually moving towards the bottom of the screen after an involuntary camera switch. In the vast majority of outdoor locations, you can't readily switch back to a previous perspective, making it easy to initially lose orientation as you suddenly view the gaming world from a different angle. Veterans of the Alone in the Dark series or similar games are likely to adapt quicker to Krondor's new environment, but most gamers will require a lengthier-than-usual period of time to adapt to the game's controls.
