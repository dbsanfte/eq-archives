Big Red Rock Entertainment to license Monolith’s LithTech 3D Engine

Engine Being Used to Develop an Intense New Role-Playing Game - Mystic Isle: The Darkening

Nashua, NH (February 18, 1999) - Big Red Rock Entertainment, Inc., a New Hampshire based interactive entertainment developer, announced today the licensing of the LithTech 3D Engine, from Monolith Productions, for the production of their first title, a 3D Role Playing game entitled Mystic Isle: The Darkening. Mystic Isle has been in development for some time but is being rebuilt from the ground up, using Monolith's LithTech. "We are pleased to be able to add another talented developer to the growing group of LithTech licensees," comments Monolith CEO Jason Hall. "LithTech’s cutting-edge technology will allow the team to push the envelope and create a complete new role-playing experience for players." Mystic Isle: The Darkening is a role- playing game where the player takes on the persona of a young man or woman and his/her friends in a world where peace has reigned for almost 6000 years, but is about to come to an end. The player will guide them through a series of adventures using wit, strength and strange, mystical powers. With a phenomenal storyline involving multiple branching plots as well as a myriad of subplots and quests to allow the characters to grow, Mystic Isle: The Darkening will surprise the player again and again. Set on the enchanting world of N’Evaria, Mystic Isle: The Darkening will bring an entire world to life for the player to experience.

As Christopher "Kattahn" White, President of Big Red Rock Entertainment and project lead on Mystic Isle: The Darkening describes, "Mystic Isle: The Darkening will have gameplay similar to Fallout, Fallout 2 and Baldur’s Gate and will have a graphical presentation reminiscent of Final Fantasy VII and Dark Stone."

Some of the numerous features this game will sport include real-time rendered environments using LithTech Technology, on- screen 3D characters that interact with the environment and non-player characters (NPC's) in the game. NPC's will have goals and will work to achieve them whether they are interacting with the players or not. Certain events will be scripted but many creatures will simply act according to their nature and what happens will happen. Mystic Isle: The Darkening will adhere toa non-linear gameplay with 'plot entry points' that allow the character to bypass the main plot if they want. The AI designed into Mystic Isle: The Darkening will even allow the game to finish itself!

The EverQuest Team is pleased to announce a major enhancement to Trade Skills!

We've just updated our Test Server with over 1,000 new items, the vast majority of which are created exclusively using the various trade skills available to players in the game. The rest of the Phase 4 servers will be updated with this new data shortly.

The following is a new section of the EverQuest Beta Manual that will be updated soon explaining the various Trade Skills and how they work:

TRADE SKILLS

Trade skills in EverQuest allow you to construct useful items from component pieces. This will allow you to make materials which will help your character in their profession, as well as providing a secondary source of income. To get started with a trade skill visit a merchant of the skill you wish to learn. They will have the books and kits which you will need to begin your career.

Once you have carefully read a book appropriate to your Trade Skill and acquired the component pieces necessary to build the item, you may attempt to construct it. Start by right clicking on the container used for the trade skill. This will open a window to which you drag the component parts from your inventory to the available slots in the container. Double check that all of the correct parts have been placed in the container, then click on the COMBINE button. If your character skillfully worked their trade skill you will get a new item(s). If your character blundered, you will usually lose all of the component pieces placed in to the container.

Levels in EQ - Sacrates
Here is Brad talking a bit on Levels and twinking in the game. Personally I think they have found a pretty good balance so far. We'll see soon enough though. But I agree there is no reason at all a Level 1 through 10 character should be strutting around in full Plate unless of course he is an esteemed Webmaster of course. (Hint, hint)

There are 'soft' level limits in the game. What I mean by this is that if a level 1 guy gets plate mail, he can wear it, but it doesn't benefit him nearly as much as a level 30 player. This, combined with the fact that we will adjust the game's economy to slow deflation (which is inevitible), will combat the negative effects you are worried about.

We are against hard level limits (meaning a level 1 player simply cannot wear plate) for a couple of reasons -- the largest being it causes a stagnate economy, where trickle-down doesn't work. And also because it stifles replayability.

I've played MUDs where there were no level limits on items (not even the soft ones we have in EQ), and there were not hoards of newbies running around with dragonscale armor and flaming swords. You can avoid it my making sure you have a dynamic, player-driven economy.

I forgot to mention in the previous post the fact that we are also striving to balance items and skills, which will also minimize the 'twink-factor'. While items are indeed important in terms of your armor class, damage dealt, etc., you also have skills which cannot be twinked, which matter a great deal during combat. It's a tricky balance, because you still want items to have value and noticible effect when you wear them (or don't wear them), but it's doable.

Weekly WoT - Sacrates
Glen just keeps the lovin coming in like a Prostitute without teeth. Sounds like they are going into crunch time over there on WoT. Looks like another Milestone coming their way.

One great thing about crunch time is watching it all come together...

For months--even years--we've been creating assets, programming behaviors, and designing gameplay--sometimes in disparate corners of the world.  Putting it together is a painstaking process, as it reveals gaps, bugs, and inconsistencies.  But more often, the results take your breath away and allow you to understand the scope of what we're accomplishing.  Ahem.   I'll stop spewing rhetoric now and get on to some examples.

After too long of a time working with placeholder interface art, we're starting to see the trickle of real assets.   With some preliminary work from Marc, Paul has produced the basis for the standard and minimized interface.  In combination with Jim's implementation, the game is getting prettier and much easier to play.  I'm hoping to get all of the new assets in the game tomorrow, but the comps already look great.

Turning down a Mardi Gras party invite over the weekend (don't worry; I found out later that the average age of the participants was about 65), I settled down to do some single-player content design for a few of the maps targeted for our second revision of alpha.  I'm pleased with a lot of what came out; now we've just got to figure out how to pull it off.  Speaking of maps, now that we've established the baseline of the game's content, the polishing effort is going along wonderfully.  James showed me sections of the Ways map which look 100% better (and they were beautiful before).   Scott has been working on a Shayol Ghul arena map so that we can use the environment for the cutscenes.  Warren has his work cut out for him implementing all of the water-based puzzles I came up with for the Bowels of Shadar Logoth map on his plate.  And Tim's been working hard on three of his maps; I should get to see his latest stuff shortly.

Mark and Jim continue to implement UI functionality.  Ryan's been focused on optimizing the AI code, and has already seen immense success in that arena.  Jess almost has the interface ready so that the lds can start really tweaking monster behavior.   And Mike is working steadily on the base NPC monster behavior.  It's cool to start playing the game and see the monsters suddenly start behaving as designed.    The AI team deserves a lot of credit.  They've got a huge job bringing these creatures to life--naturally, AI can suck up as much time as you give it--but this team is really dedicated.  Kudos especially go out to Ryan who barely remembers what the light of day looks like.  He's doing an admirable job forging and guiding the AI effort.

Aaron continues to improve multi-player performance, and tweaks artifact effects in his off-time.  Today, I saw an enhanced Fireball, Balefire, and Earth Tremor--and they looked great before.   Every time I see an incremental improvement to these effects, it's hard to remember that the public will be seeing the end result for the first time.

There are two notable additions to our extended team.  To ensure that the artifact sound effects kick as much ass as they have to, I've brought on an entire new studio to dedicate to the effort, one known well throughout the industry for the high quality of their work.  And, not to be outshone, I've contracted with an additional high-profile animation house to bring the introduction to life--again, this studio is known far and wide for their killer material, feature film as well as game-related animation.  Both of these groups are top notch, and I'm extremely happy to have them add their talents to this project. 

Note that none of this should be interpreted as anything negative about our current team.  Andy has been working like crazy, delivering some wicked monster sounds and about to dive into cut scene effects and music--and Eric has been pumping out wonderful environmental effects.   Also, our other animation studio is doing a great job.   The reason I brought on these additional resources is to supplement their efforts; we need to create a lot of great material, and soon. 

Speaking of cut scenes, the big news is that our first group has successfully imported all of the required environments from the game into the scripted animations.   Although the scenes I've seen so far are great, they've consisted only of character animation.  Thursday, I should be receiving a new shipment containing a bunch more, with the characters firmly ensconced in their environments.  This will be very cool.

That's about as much time as I have.  As I wrote yesterday, I won't be updating next Monday as I'll be in Italy taking care of my fiancée during her surgery.  Many thanks for all of the letters I received concerning Sabrina's health.  She'll be absolutely fine, but we both really appreciate the sentiment.  It's heartwarming to know that some people out there actually care.  Again, thank you very much.

Until I return!

GM help in EQ - Sacrates
Has your Barbarian's butt hair accidentally got caught in a doorway that won't reopen and your sword just isn't sharp enough to cut through those thick bastards? Well call a GM. Here's the scoop.

Dear Phase IV Beta Testers,

Here are a few guidelines for getting assistance in the game via the "/petition" system.

1. How to /petition properly. When you need assistance from a GM or Guide in the game, use the /petition command. This command is used exactly like /tell - Type in /petition (message). Please include as descriptive a message as possible about what you need to enable us to answer you faster without a prolonged question/answer session.

Example 1: "/petition I am stuck in the geography in Blackburrow at location X,Y,Z, please get me out." Example 2: "/petition I have a question about skill x. When I hit the button for skill x, it does action y, is that correct? I thought it should do action z"

In both these cases we can help promptly and accurately without having to request more detailed information from the person needing help, thus speeding up his answer, and helping us get to the next person more promptly.

2. Please do NOT /tell to GMs/Guides except in response to a direct message or instruction from that GM/Guide. We are working off of a Queuing system, and since the people in the Queue have asked for assistance first, they have first priority. Also, /tells are sometimes lost when zoning, and your local neighborhood Guide or GM is zoning.. a LOT :) Once a GM/Guide responds to your petition, the rest of the communication is done in /tell. Thanks for your cooperation on this.

3. When the server goes down (not just the zone, but the worldserver) all existing /petitions are lost. If you were waiting for assistance and you experience a server failure, when you get back into the game, you can re /petition. If it was just a zone crash, you will get a message indicating that you already have a /petition in the Queue, and we will be with you as soon as possible.

4. Please be patient :) The last several days have been stressful on all of us, as the stability and crashing bug were causing an inordinate number of petitions, many Guides, and even GM's were (like some of you) unable to get into the game, and we were not able to help everyone in a timely manner. We apologize for this, and are working to increase our Guide staffing as quickly as possible, without losing the quality of personel.

Thanks for your time and effort in helping to make Everquest the best game ever.

Odium at NGO - Sacrates
Here's a game that's got a lot of chimps up in excitement. Next Gen has kicked up a Special on this game that you'll want to see.

Is the technology used for the construction of the title your own or was it licensed? You must have come up against problems with the construction of the code. What were they and how did you overcome them?

"The technology is completely ours. Not that we have anything against making a game using someone else's code. I believe it's quite funny to see every game developer writing exactly the same, let's say, sound code for any new game. But simply, there's no previously constructed engine we could use to make Odium. As for the problems with programming your own code, well, we don't have too many. Four programmers are writing the code and it shows; they are geniuses. I was the programmer myself a couple of years ago, but right now the level of programming is raised so high I don't understand what those guys are talking about anymore - too abstract (not to mention all that 3D stuff!).

"Let me give you an example of the problems we have. As you probably know, we mix 2D rendered isometric backgrounds with real-time 3D characters. So each frame we have to send the 640x480 background, then cover it with characters that have in total, let's say, 5000 polygons (of course it's not exactly how it works, but it gives you an idea). Funny thing is, pushing this 640x480 into the video memory is so slow (because of the PC's architecture), that if we got rid of that, we could push another 5000 polygons without affecting the framerate..."
