Deus Ex: The Interview
by the king of sting: Sacrates
with: Chris Norden

Sacrates gives it to you turtle style  in this new Interview filled to the brim with  exciting 3D RPG goodness. Him and Chris Norden get down and dirty (Not that way damnit) on all things Deus EX. So bend over, grab a frosty brew and take it like the turtles do.

Sacrates: Let's get this out of the way first, Introductions. Self explanatory here, please feel free to let the Altered readers out there know who you are and what the hell you’re doing.

Chris: OK…My name is Chris Norden and I’m the Lead Programmer and Assistant Director on Deus Ex. I do lots of programming and pulling all the different parts of the game together (art, sound, levels, etc.) Before ION, I worked at Origin (Jane’s Combat Simulations AH-64D Longbow), and Looking Glass Austin (a bit of work on Thief: The Dark Project and lots of work on an unnamed project (R.I.P.) using the Thief technology)

Sacrates: So here we are talking about Deus Ex, (Or going to talk about it) but let's skip a few beats and jump to the end first to get everyone’s interest. What is Deus Ex going to offer us that other games in the genre can't?

Chris: Deus Ex is going to be an extremely immersive role-playing experience. There’s going to be lots of character interaction and development, problem solving, exploration, discovery, and of course combat. We’re striving to make Deus Ex a lot more interactive than most RPGs out there in that you’ll be able to solve problems like you would in the real world, not just like the designer had in his mind. Solutions to problems will fall out of what you carry with you, who you talk to, and your immediate (and not-so-immediate) surroundings. How you interact with (and react to) your environment can be very different than how your friend would act in the same situation.

Sacrates: Ok so now that you've explained why Deus Ex is gonna make a few gamers happy, let's see what's under the hood that makes it tick. The Unreal Engine. Ooooh Aaaah. (Single player response) Projectile vomiting (Multiplayer response) What is this engine doing for your lives to make it less complicated? (And possibly more complicated?)

Chris: The Unreal engine has made our lives much easier than I ever would have imagined. Tim Sweeney and the Digital Extremes guys have put together an amazingly flexible engine that can easily be adapted even to complex games like Deus Ex. We’ve had to add lots of different "RPG" things (like a conversation system and editor, a highly realistic combat system, a windowed user interface, etc.), but the fact that the original engine is so flexible has made our jobs much easier. The level editor (UnrealEd) is also the most complete level editor that I have ever used (and I’ve used quite a few). The real power of the editor is not just in editing raw geometry, but how all of the game data is so cleanly tied into the overall level. I’d hate to think where we’d be if we didn’t have the Unreal engine as a starting point.

Also, you’d better suck down that projectile vomit that you were just spewing…Tim and his crew have totally rewritten the networking systems for Unreal, and the performance is excellent. That’s another one of the great things about working with the Unreal engine…when the public or the licensees have a problem or a suggestion, Tim listens and does his best to make improvements to the engine.

Now I know this sounds like a lot of pro-Unreal sucking up, but I can’t even calculate how much time we saved in the prototyping stage. We’ve had almost all of the major game systems up and running in a playable form for quite some time now…it’s done wonders for our designer-programmer feedback loop. Even now, as the systems are being implemented in their "final" form, we’re progressing very quickly for a game of this magnitude. Designers have a lot of control over their levels without having to interact with programmers or artists every few minutes for new data.

Sacrates: With those earlier questions said, are you adding enhancements to the Engine itself? Similar to what Half Life has done with the Quake engine. How much code are you going to retain and how much will be original?

Chris: Well, we’re adding quite a number of enhancements to the basic Unreal engine: better player and NPC damage models, more detailed and complex animation, more realistic physics, better NPC AI, much more realistic combat (using real-world and near-future weapons), player skills and augmentations, a very cinematic conversation system, a really slick user interface…the list goes on and on. Our other programmers Albert Yarusso (Looking Glass - British Open Championship Golf) and Scott Martin (Sonalysts - 688(I) Hunter/Killer, Fleet Command) are doing wonders to get all these systems working flawlessly with the engine. When we’re done with the game, you won’t be able to tell that it’s based on the Unreal engine. Deus Ex is most definitely NOT just a big TC (total conversion.)

Sacrates: How is gameplay handled within the Engines framework? Is it going to be level based or will it be chapter based etc.

Chris: It’s hard to say…it won’t be like anything you’ve ever played. Technically, it will be level based, but you won’t be able to tell when one level ends and where the next one begins. Everything is tied together using local and world maps, and the story pushes you along smoothly. There won’t be a "level complete" screen with kills, secrets, and time. Instead, somebody (or something) may give you more clues that will lead you to a different geographic area with different goals and different people to talk to. You’ll also be able to travel freely between certain locations even if it’s not necessary to do so. Exploration is not discouraged and is often rewarded…the cautious and perceptive player will have a very different experience than the trigger happy player. Deus Ex will be a very story-intensive game…to get the most enjoyment out of it you’ll really need to think instead of just blasting everything in sight. Of course, you could blast everything, but you will have a much more difficult time finishing the game when everybody around you is dead.

Sacrates: What kind of Interaction can we expect with NPCs in this game? Will it be large dialogue trees that we respond to or something akin to Diablo with prewritten responses. How deep can we expect NPC interaction to be?

Chris: Almost all of the conversations will be short and to the point. We feel that large dialogue trees do nothing but confuse the average player, and we would rather tell a story than have a bullet point on the box which says "over one million lines of dialogue to click on." Quality over quantity. Having said that, I think that our NPC interaction will be some of the most involving and interesting you have ever seen in a game. Part of playing an RPG is making choices…some easy and some tough. The choices in Deus Ex will actually mean something. If a character asks you "How do you like my store?" and you respond by saying "It sucks, old man…and so do you!", he’s not going to help you later on in the game. On the other hand, if you’re polite to him, he might just give you a critical piece of information that would otherwise be a real pain in the ass to extract from some computer system somewhere else deep in the game (for example.)

Sacrates: How many NPC's can we expect within the game and how good is your AI system? (Extremely popular question at the moment) Will the NPC's have a life of their own or will they be at set points throughout the game guiding the players?

Chris: Lots, great, and yes.

Currently, there are over one hundred unique NPCs in the game, and everybody will have something important to say to you…not just "Nice weather we’re having. Yup."

Licensees are not allowed to use all of the Unreal AI...most of it is Unreal-specific. So, Scott Martin has been working on our own AI system using the basic functionality in the engine as a starting point. You’ll see some pretty complex NPC behavior like hiding, ducking, jumping, using objects, and opening doors just to name a few. All of the NPCs will react to the environment as well as your actions and the actions of other NPCs. They will be allowed to have a life of their own, but we still need to be sure that they are at the right place at the right time to keep the player on track.

Sacrates: How is the story handled throughout the game? Obviously Unreal did a great job with very small cinematic touches such as the Skarj meeting etc. Will you guys be telling Deus Ex's story through similar fashions of Cut scenes or in game sequences or scripted scenes?

Chris: Story will be conveyed through NPC conversations and through in-engine scripted cut-scenes. You’ll also get story information through various computer terminals, books, email, memos, etc. found in various places throughout the game. You will also learn from your environment and by investigating your surroundings. Why does this normal-looking office have a high-security lock on that door? What are those strange-looking tanks deep under the water? Why is this chick hiring people to kill me? And so on.

Sacrates: What can we expect in terms of character development? How deep can we go in that department?

Chris: Expect lots of it. Your character will learn new skills (I won’t tell you how many just yet) and acquire new items, weapons, and augmentations. What are augmentations you ask? I won’t tell you that, either. There won’t be traditional role-playing stats (like strength, intelligence, wisdom, etc.), but rather a more tangible distinction between the capabilities of your character and someone else’s. I could play through the game developing my weapons skills, while you might play through the game developing your high-tech skills. Where I would use force to blast my way through difficult situations, you would tinker with devices and hack security systems. Or, someone could develop lots of different sets of skills to become a generalist, but he won’t be really good at any one thing…he’ll just be able to do lots of things at a slightly higher level than a beginner would. It’s totally up to the player as to how they progress through the game and overcome different obstacles.

Sacrates: How much of the game is action based and how much interaction? What can we expect in overall game structure? A lot of fighting to gain levels and experience so I can buy things or more adventure such as talking to people and completing quests etc?

Chris: Well, it really depends on your playing style. We’ve tried to balance the amount of pure action with the RPG elements, but the key here is that people with different playing styles will still enjoy the game in different ways. We have designed the game with an eye more towards conversation, world interaction, and exploration than just pure action, but the player will be able to choose how to play the game…we certainly don’t force you down the "righteous path." Every obstacle has multiple solutions within the game…here’s a quick example:

Player A will say "Well, knocking didn’t work, so I just blew the door off its hinges to get in, but then the alarm went off and the guards totally kicked my ass! Duuuuuude!"

Player B will say "I used my lockpicks and my stealth skills to pick the lock and sneak in undetected."

Player C will say "It was easy! All I did was disable the security camera and rewire the keypad to trick the door into thinking I had entered the correct code."

Player D will say "I just put on an engineer’s labcoat and used the security code that I hacked out of the mainframe. Piece of cake!"

Get the idea?

Sacrates: So here we have little Johnny A with his tendency to enjoy well thought out adventure/RPG games and his brother, Little Johnny B who likes to kill things (On the computer of course) and see bloody massacres on the screen. How are you going to get both of these guys to enjoy Deus Ex? Or will it simply be aimed at a particular group of players like (Possibly) Johnny A?

Chris: See previous 2 questions.

Sacrates: Speaking in terms of 3D technology and not Genre. What do you expect will be your most groundbreaking feature? What will separate this from a quickly oversaturating market and possibly take these styles of games to the next level? (ie. Trespasser with it's Physics, Half Life with it's story)

Chris: Hmmm…in my opinion, 3D technology has advanced to the point where everybody has pretty much the same feature set. Yes, we have "portal technology" and mirrors. Yes, we have procedural textures. Yes, we have colored dynamic lighting. Whoopdeedoo. However, all the technology in the world doesn’t make a game fun to play. Deus Ex will have great technology AND be fun to play…we use all of the technology available to us to enhance the overall experience. What’s our most groundbreaking feature? I would have to say "gameplay."

Sacrates: Obviously we have heard the controversy surrounding the new additions to your team from Origin. (Bob White etc) How are these guys working out so far?

Chris: They’re working out great. Bob White, Dan Rubenfield, and Marshall Andrews all have extensive RPG backgrounds from Origin and from their childhoods. They are all totally dedicated to Deus Ex, and have brought some great new ideas and skills to our team.

Sacrates: How deep are you into development right now? What percentage is Deus Ex right now in it's Development stage?

Chris: We’re cranking right along. We’ve got several maps done and our art department (with Jay Lee as the art lead) keeps amazing us with the awesome characters, objects, and textures. Programming is coming right along, also…all of the major game systems are almost functionally complete, and we’re working on interface and tweaking. Game scripting has been moving along as well, but we’ll continue to work on scripting and tweaking until the day we ship. I can’t really give a percentage complete…it really is just an arbitrary number that doesn’t have any real meaning. We will ship the game when it’s fun…I know everybody is sick of hearing that catch phrase, but it’s really true.

Sacrates: Sound....an obviously overlooked part of gaming. Are you going to have 3D sound support like Unreal etc? Will you have dynamically changing music etc like Unreal and many of the others?

Chris: I think sound has finally become a very important part of games these days. Looking Glass’s Thief: The Dark Project is an excellent example of the use of sound to enhance gameplay. Sound will play a very important role in Deus Ex as well. Expect a rich and varied soundscape…you’ll definitely want to play our game with the volume cranked. There will probably be some music, but it will be more like ambient sound and location-specific music rather than a continuous techno-pop soundtrack played throughout the entire game. If you walk into a dance club, you’ll hear dance music and loud crowd noise. If you walk into a bathroom, you’ll hear water dripping and hard echoes. If you’re outside at night, you’ll hear wind blowing, crickets chirping, and the grass crunching beneath your feet. We’re taking full advantage of everything that Unreal supports, including the latest EAX support from Creative Labs, so make sure you have a kick-ass sound system before Deus Ex is released.

Sacrates: A late comer question here regarding gameplay. How will combat be handled?

Chris: Combat will be in real-time and very realistic. We’ve got lots of real-world weapons (some taken right from the pages of Jane’s) and some really cool high-tech weapons as well. There will be realistic hit locations and damage effects for both the player and NPCs. Don’t expect to be climbing ladders if both of your arms are blown off. You’ll be pulling yourself along the ground with your arms if your legs get blown off. A critical shot to the head will most likely kill you instantly, and a million shots to an NPC’s hand will only piss him off, make him drop his weapon, and leave a trail of blood as he runs away in fear and awe of your immense power.

Sacrates: And last but not least a philisophical question. What's your take on the 3D industry right now? Do you feel it's becoming oversaturated or still in it's infancy? We see cutting edge titles like Trespasser and Half Life but between the lines we have a lot of titles that scream I am 3D..play me or die.

Chris: Argh…this industry can be really frustrating at times. There are way too many "me too" titles out there that show no innovation in terms of gameplay. It’s way too easy to license an engine and change some graphics and sounds and ship it...this scares me. All too often, all it takes is a clever ad and lots of marketing money, and your average uninformed consumer will buy it. Slap a famous name on it or hype yourself into the ground, and you instantly become visible to everybody. I won’t mention any names, but you know what titles I’m talking about. Let’s face it…the average consumer outnumbers the hard-core gamer by a huge number. However, there are still tons of great games out there…I wish I had more time so that I could play all of the games on my list. It’s games like Half-Life and Baldur’s Gate that distract all of us from making our game.

I don’t care if you have the coolest graphic technology in the world, if there’s no design and no gameplay, it will become little more than a cool tech demo. Don’t get me wrong…having good technology is essential to an immersive experience…a solid engine can make a good game look great. However, a good engine will do absolutely nothing for a bad game other than take pretty screenshots for magazines. How many times have we seen renders or mocked-up screenshots that show nothing about the gameplay itself? How many times do we have to sit through a five minute long rendered intro cinematic only to be left saying "Great, but what does this have to do with the gameplay?"

I’m sure you’ve noticed my continued use of the word gameplay throughout this interview. If you didn’t notice, I’m a big believer in substance over excessive flash. You still have to have a certain amount of flashiness to attract people, but in the end, it’s the gameplay that keeps them coming back time after time. Gameplay is what Deus Ex is all about.
