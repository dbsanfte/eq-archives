System Smack - Sacrates
Ken Levine was on the Unofficial System Shock Boards spreading a little lovin our way. Sounds like this gem is coming along nicely. There's a whole lot of SS goodness here gang so sit back, relax, grab a frosty cold one and enjoy. Here's the first one on story elements etc.

Thanks for this great thread. We've put A LOT of thought into the story. For better or worse, I spent my youth (well, it was only a few years ago) as a screenwriter in LA, and I am generally disappointed by most computer game stories because they are so on the nose and derivative. On the other hand, I think it's perhaps impossible to judge whether a story will be good or not based on the time frame it is set in. Given the sentence, "It all started 40 years after the mess on Citadel station" you, I or anyone on this board can come up with around a thousand different storylines, some of them good, some of them bad, some of them horrible, some of them great.

The tenets we've followed in developing the storyline are to keep the vibe consistent with the first game, to make the game creepy, to "progress" the settings of Shock 1 and expand the world to make it feel more real, and to utilize our numerous storytelling tools (cutscenes, email, logs, etc.) to feedback that storyline in a way that only the System Shock game design allows us.

I think I can say that many elements of the story will suprise you. But we are not out to re-invent the wheel. Shock 2 will feel like Shock 1, but with much more advanced technology, gameplay and storytelling techniques.

The next one is on what is missing from the System Shock game engine.

First, let me see if I can cleverly turn this question around. I might ask a similar question."What is missing from Quake 3?"

1) RPG: Character generation, development, skills and stats that actually affect the gameplay, experience points, traits, etc.
2)Gameplay: Rich storyline, logs, story relevant cutscenes (?), hacking, configuring, modifying, repairing, varied quests, and other elements too numerous to mention (think Shock 1 plus plus plus plus)
3) Engine: I'll use your notes:

Next one is on rotating platforms and moving objects being a trigger or transparent.

Yes to all the above. A moving object can be a trigger, transparent, translucent, have various levels of translucency. I think the object system in this engine is, without a doubt, materially the most powerful object system in any first person game. The amount of control is absolutely incredible, and completely from a designer side. Each object can have one or more of 500 plus paremeters (I've never counted them all, I think there might be more) set on them from whether it is a "trap" to its physics shape, to its sensitivity to arbitrary stimuli (hot, cold, noise, whatever you see fit). It really is (no hyperbole here) a marvel of modern game software engineering.

Finally in recap to this thread.

We're here to help. I just want to put my two cents here that while Shock 2's engine does stand up well to shooters, we really don't view them as our competition. It's sort of like comparing Quake 3 to Might and Magic 6 or Fallout (which is a great game in its own right. We're a rich RPG that happens to be blessed with a really powerful engine with an object system that supports really untapped types of gameplay.

It's nice to have your cake and eat it, too.

Next he talks about the Paperdoll.

The paperdoll really comes from earlier games like Underworld 1 and many others. Even XCOM and Jagged Alliance used these elements. The look of Syndicate is similar, but, hey, the look of Shock 1 and Syndicate is similar. They're both inspired by the same kind of fiction (cyberpunk, dark future stuff)

Talking about lack of new design.

If you mean "graphical design", there are two things to think about. 1) None of these screenshots are final, shipping images. 2) We have a debt to stay faithful to Shock 1. As a sequel, you are bound to give people a similar vibe to the first experience.

If you mean game design, I think one really needs to sit down with the game before they can accurately assess that. You are only seeing a tiny corner of the interface elemetns. There are literally dozens of multifunction displays that appear in these interface areas, with a corresponding wide range of functionality. I think you'll be pleasantly surprised.

Anyway go read this little piece if you thought Warren Spector's was any good yesterday, this one is great. Here's a sneak peak.

The plot lines in most RPG’s are basically the same old drivel, over and over again. I call it the standard RPG plot. I think you will find it familiar: You start the game as the great hero. (You know this because you are told so in the instructions.) You job is to kill the big evil guy. (Also, from the instructions.) You pillage and plunder everyone and everything to become strong for your final battle. (Of course the supposed bad guy is doing no evil right now, but you sure are.) When ready at last, you kill the “bad guy” who has been sitting there peacefully waiting.

Well, after 3 games of my own that retold this story, combined with all that introspection, I was ready for something more. I felt that role-playing would be even stronger in a more realistic setting. I felt that people are basically good, not merely for fear of some divine being, but because it was logical. Thus was born the modern Ultima design. I was really worried before we released Ultima IV: Quest of the Avatar, that people might think I’d gone off the philosophical deep end, but instead, it was my first best selling game.

Ultimas since then have had a deep sense of ethics, which I carefully separate from morals. By my definition, Ethics are logically based, while Morals are religion based. Thus, if you object to pre-marital sex because it is against God’s laws, that’s Morality; if you oppose it because of the risk of unwanted pregnancies, that’s an Ethic. I do not subscribe to any Morals, yet believe I am a very Ethical person.

DarkViper: Will there be other Multiplayer Modes?

Stephen Patt.: There will be two different types of Drakan Multi-player in the box: Deathmatch and something tentatively called "Queen of the Dragon". Deathmatch play actually has two variations: Ground combat only (i.e. no dragon) with sword-to-sword combat only, and aerial combat which includes ground combat, but truthfully we don't expect anyone to hop off their dragon for sword-to-sword play when there are other dragons flapping around! The second mode, "Queen of the Dragon" will have up to 8 people on the ground and only one dragon.  Everyone will be fighting sword-to-sword, while trying to find the Dragon's soul crystal.  Whoever finds the soul crystal can then approach the dragon without getting instantly toasted, mount the dragon, and rain fire down on all of the other players, raking up the kills!
