BG Bites - Sacrates
Hehehe...thought we became an Everquest News site there didn't ya? We're just getting a lot of flowage now that many Press pages like myself and others are finally in it and are allowed to talk without breaking the NDA. Anyway, Here's a few Bites from BG found on the Boards. First is on teh Experience Cap.

To understand the experience cap, you have to understand the design concept behind Baldur's Gate.

When designing Baldur's Gate, we wanted to address what we thought was the biggest flaw in many of today's RPG's. In order to deliver great plot-line, they had all become too linear. Many RPG's began to feel (in my own opinion) a lot like adventure games.

Baldur's Gate we wanted to be as non-linear as possible, while still allowing you to follow a major plot to get you (eventually) through the game. Want to crash through the main quest in 50 hours? Some people did. Want to savor the gaming experience and spend 200 hours cleaning out every nook and cranny? Other people find that more enjoyable...

In order to fulfill this goal, we had to design the ENTIRE game so that at any point you could wander into a new area and find it entertaining and challenging. The only way to do that was to populate the entire world for a narrow experience range of characters - say from levels 3-6th. The main quest line is designed to gradually increase in difficulty until the final battle is pretty challenging for characters at.... 89,000 experience points.

Many of you have found that starting out, if you wander away from the main quest line this game (in the outdoor areas) can be very hard. Likewise, many have found that if you develop your characters in the main plot and THEN hit the outdoor areas, the game is (relatively) easy (we still threw in a number of challenges throughout the game). The design of this game was an art as much as a science in balancing MANY factors to 'maximize' gaming fun for players of levels UP TO 89,000 experience points. Let's face it...kobolds just aren't that intimidating to a 10th level Fighter.

So the game is designed to be played and enjoyed MOST within the experience point ranges we have set up. I am flattered when I hear of people wanting to remove the experience point cap - simply because it means that they like the game so much they don't want it to end - they want to continue to adventure and continue to advance. We will have the expansion available soon - and perhaps additional add-on products after that. Nothing would make us happier than to get to a day where you COULD eventually start in one area and one game, and work your way through several games/chapters while continuing to adventure in the same fantasy world and with continuity of plot (i.e. same NPC's carried over, etc).

We're gamers too, remember...

Here's a bit on fallen Paladins.

A fallen paladin has strayed from the true path of his deity and from the path of goodness.

Though he may maintain Lawful Good aligment because it was a momentary deviation of behavior, he has forever fallen from grace.

He will ALWAYS remain a fallen paladin and will have lost all of his special powers for all time... for all intents and purposes he is a fighter, but WITHOUT a fighter's ability for weapon mastery and dual classing...

on the amount of additional character levels planned for the expansion: By levels you mean character levels, right?

We are still working on it. Remember that from this point out you pretty much double experience points to make each level. So to go from 89,000 and 7th level as a cleric, you'd need 450,000 to hit 10th level... so even if we said 2-3 more levels we'd probably be talking 3-4x the experience amount of monsters / quests than were in the original game...

Abashi's Great and wonderous Lag Faq - Sacrates
I could have gone and posted this with the rest of the goods below but I thought it was so long and luxurious that i'd post it here. Read on oh curious one. May Bhudda urinate in your footsteps.

Ok, what people commonly refer to as LAG has 1 of 4 sources:

1. Internet Latency - Distinguished by high ping times and/or packetloss
2. Connection Flooding - Data transfer rates at top of modem specification.
3. Client Slowdown - Distinguished by a drop in framerate, frame droping when making fast turns, mouse skipping.
4. Server Slowdown - Distinguished by a number of issues, more below.


1. Internet latency can cause things like doors not opening immediatly when clicked upon, trades not occuring as soon as you drop the item on the recipient, NPC's not reacting quickly when you try to talk to them, /tell's and /says taking time to reach the recipient. It is always accompanied by the green LAG-O-METER showing an indication of internet latency (high ping/packet loss). You may also see (when you or they are experiencing internet latency) a closely followed character disappear. This is caused when you are following closely and they make a fast turn or stop, thus causing you to run right by before your client is updated. The speed at which you run across the terrain is NOT affected by this issue.

2. Connection flooding occurs when the amount of data being exchanged by the client and the server exceeds modem spec. This can occur when there is a LOT of something going on in the immediate area. It has symptoms equal to #1, with the exception that instead of showing high ping and packet loss, it shows a high transfer rate. To give you an idea, the average transfer rate when idle with noone around is roughly 250Bps (that's bytes). When running through your average open area zone with average occurances happening on all sides, this goes up to about 500Bps. In the average dungeon, with your typical close quarter chaos, this goes up to around 1100Bps. In an all out free-for-all with a high number of people in an enclosed area, this approaches 2500Bps. The 28.8K modem is capable of transfering roughly 3500Bps, so thier is quite a bit of room to grow. This does not affect the speed in which you run across the terrain.

3. Client slowdown. This is caused by one of a number of things, among them, your video card, processor, RAM, and free harddrive space. It's precise syptom is a decrease in rendering performance. This currently occurs with a few spells and will be fixed by final. Low grade client slowdown may occur in resource intensive areas (high polygon count, large number of textures) with the minimal system config after final. This can also occur if a LARGE number of people get in a very small area thus increasing the poly or texture count. This can affect the speed in which you run across the terrain.

4. Server side slowdown hasn't been an issue since two weeks into phase 3 when the servers were being highly loaded. It was characterized by all the issues in #1, but without the LAG-O-METER indicating a problem. It is very easy to spot which is why it was fixed very quickly. Also, it was normal when this was occuring for the data transfer rate to jump between max and min every other second or so. Again, this isn't an issue any longer. This, if it does occur will not affect the speed in which you run across the terrain.

How does this all differ from UO? Simple. UO's infantile network code required server interaction for every step, every action you made. The client did not update the server with the current character position, the server updated the client. This is where you get the "Step..lag...lag...lag...Step, Step, step, Step...bounce back to where you were 3 steps ago" manifestation of lag. UO wrapped all the lag one could experience into one manifestion of lag.

Now I know some people are going to jump in and say that this makes the code in EQ hackable. Someone is going to say "I'll just write a client that sends phony update information to the server and I'll be able to go much faster!". This will not happen in EQ. The server will validate the update code received by the client to see if it is acceptable given the various effects on the character and take appropriate action.

So let's not call it lag, UO had lag. EQ is not suseptable to the Lag that was experienced in UO. People are playing this game now with constant pings of 1000 and 50% packetloss and will tell you there is "little if any lag". Think about that for a moment.

-Abashi, Everquest Guide

A Bit O' EQ goodness - Sacrates
Abashi was doing the over-time on the boards lately. This first one comes from someone who has no social life and thinks Sony is against all Solo players by forcing them to team up with others. Hell, I don't even think Abashi should have answered him in the first place. The kid needs to learn that this is what these types of games are about. If he wants Solo play tell him to go out and but Zelda on his Nintendo! Sorry had to get that off my incredibly masculine chest.

...is that EverQuest is a multiplayer game.

But to go just a little bit deeper, if EverQuest were balanced so that solo play was easy, then people who chose to group would get bored because it is too easy. By balancing Everquest so Group play is manageable and fun, then it makes things difficult for the solo players.

Solo play is certainly supported for people in EQ, you just have a limited selection on what you can choose to play, where you can go (and live), what you can earn, and how fast you can get there.

Everquest is balanced for groups, that (thankfully) will not change.

Next one is on Class balance etc. Abashi hits the nail on the head with this one as classes are extremely well balanced at the moment.

I want to stress that all classes will be balanced, no classes will "suck". Rogues are better melee fighters than any hybrid, and are only second to the warrior and monk. They have skills that are needed, and yes they can steal a bundle. The backstab is fierce, and thier best form of attack, of course it has nothing to do with "3x damage". Thier high agility, dexterity, and the fact they use fast weapons means they will get the max potential out of thier weapons probably more than any other class.

The rogue is best suited for group play where thier backstabs will have the most effect.

This next one is for you freaks who are fans of using foul names in your MP sessions.

Currently, we are far less concerned with literary names than with inappropriate and offensive names. Of course, the name policy is still being developed, not certain what well happen in final (beyond axing inappropriate and offensive names)

This next one is a mini review. Abashi is in Bold.

Must a mob actually die for experience to be doled out?

Yes.

: Shouldn't I get experience for chucking a rock or some other distant area effect at a hill giant, successfully doing some damage and then running like mad?

No, too abusable.

:Is experience divied up according to the amount of damage done?

No. Think "electoral college". He who does the most damage gets 100% of the experience.

:Is the amount of experience available from a mob predetermined, or is it variable?

Variable. Base value +/- level differential bonus/penalty.

:If the combat lasts a really long time and the mob has the opportunity to heal sufficiently, shouldn't that be worth more experience than a quick kill?

No, too abusable.

: Can I drag a combat out for hours by doing some minor damage, and then casting a calm spell on the mob, would this tactic rack up experience points?

No.

And an extra morsel on combat and XP.

Overall, it works like this:

1. The person who did the most damage gets the experience.
2. IF THAT PERSON is in a group, the experience is then split between the members of the group.

Next one is on colors used in combat etc.

When people are speaking of white, blue, yellow, green or black they are referring to the color of the message returned by /con.

Red: Indicates the creature is far above your level
Yellow: Indicates the creature is above your level
White/black: Indicates that the creature is your level.
blue: Indicates the creature is below your level
green: Indicates that the creature is far below your level.

People fighting solo usually have to go for things in the low blues (just a bit tougher than green, learned by experience). Good well rounded groups go after high-blues, white/blacks, yellows, the the occational low red (again, learned by experience).

On combat with Wizards etc, etc.

Combat skills are reletivly easy except for magi who have trouble with it when they stop using thier staff.

Magic skills are easy for Magi in one or two diciplines of magic. For instance, a wizard will have no trouble keeping up evocation, but will have a heck of a time keeping up Alteration.

A Necromancer will have no trouble with alteration or conjuration, but will have trouble with Evocation.

This is just due to the limited number of spells from that dicipline that they have.

As for non-combat skills, they will increase as you use them, of course, to do that, you must take time away from combat.
