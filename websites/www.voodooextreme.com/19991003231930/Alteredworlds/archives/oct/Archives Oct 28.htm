Extra BG Gravy
It's always good to see Greg Peterson slappin around info on the Baldur's Gate Devlopers Board. It would be even better if we saw one word in particular..."Gold". Which of course is what we haven't seen yet. Oh well, this sounds almost as good.

We are in the final push to get the game done.

One of the reasons why Bioware and Chris have not been posting very much lately is because they are putting in 16+ hour days to get the game done. We are close... but no cigar (not yet).

The final days are the hardest. A bug here, a bug there, can FUBAR the whole process. Add in multi-player and 100+ hours of gameplay and you have a finalization nightmare. We have our noses to the grindstone however.

We have not encountered anything that would make us feel the need to change our Fall '98 timing. If we DO change the timing, you will hear about it here FIRST (versus at some retailer) and it will only be for the best reasons which will be described IN DETAIL (and I guarantee you will understand the reasoning).

But let's keep our collective fingers crossed (I am planning to send some out for Christmas gifts to my dad and sisters - bought at the store even)(And don't worry - no way they hang out on these bulletin boards)

I just got a new revision today... so I'll be running a new party starting tonight and testing all the bugs I found last time around. (P.S. I played for four weeks last time and got to mid-chapter 3)

And here is a very meaty and enlightening post on how close BG is to AD&D.

It is hard to throw a number out, but something like 90-95% true to AD&D feels right to me...

AD&D is the greatest RPG ruleset/game of all time. IMHO no computer game will ever totally replicate the experience of an awesome campaign being run by an excellent DM. However, different doesn't always mean worse.

I can honestly say (after playing the game for a month) that Baldur's Gate is far and away the best computer RPG I have ever played. You would expect me to say that as a marketing guy and as an employee of the company, but I am sharing my personal opinion here with you all. It is so close to AD&D that it is bringing back memories of playing the game with my friends back in high school, and that is just with single-player...

There is no way that a computer game will ever be able to manage ALL of the rules within AD&D. The pen and paper rules were not designed with computer games in mind. They always allowed a great deal of flexibility on the part of the DM. So some rules don't make sense within the boundaries of Baldur's Gate, some rules were considered to be so obscure as not worth implementing, and some turned out to generate programming/system challenges beyond the value to the game.

We have been working very closely with Wizards of the Coast and TSR to ensure that we are being true to the spirit of AD&D. They are playtesting the game, are reviewing the manual, etc, to help us make this game everything it can be. Bioware has done a tremendous job taking a set of game rules that exceeds 700 pages (DM Guide, Players Handbook and Monstrous Manual alone) and distilling it to the most important 95% that should be in the game.

Ultimately you all will be the final judge. The game will sell well or poorly based on your opinions.

We are using this board to share with you any major (in our opinions) areas where we make a break with the rules. We ask for opinions because quite frankly some of the best ideas come from you. In most cases, AD&D rules will be followed to the letter (either visibly, or invisibly behind the scenes). On some occasion they will not. But the point is, we want the only surprise when you open the box to be how awesome this game is.

So if you make 9th level as a fighter (ain't gonna happen in BG, but I'm trying to make a point here) don't expect 75 armed followers to suddenly spawn at your side :)

Here's another update on BG's Development status.

Things are moving along on schedule, with everyone killing themselves to finish up. If I were to sum up to the two biggest challenges to this game getting done they would be:

1) The size of the game itself makes bug hunting and game balancing even more of a challenge.

2) Multi-player code. A challenge in the best of times - more of an art than a science.

In case people were worried about the recent announcement from Interplay and any impact it might have on staffing at Black Isle, you shouldn't be. Staffing at Black Isle actually increased slightly.

Trespasser Review - Sacrates
Ah....another tasty Trespasser Review comes our way. This time it comes from the Muffin boys over at AGN 3D. They gave it some pretty good ratings which is a good thing since my copy should be in the mail today or so. Also it has been spotted in stores now so you can expect to see many more reviews as they come in.

Trespasser can be an amazingly entertaining game. Figuring out the many puzzles is really satisfying. Usually the puzzles involve using the physics engine to your advantage like when you flip a trailer over and use it to gain access to the adjacent buildings' roof. The neat thing about it is that the trailer falls differently every time. Sometimes it rolls completely wrong and it's useless in helping you gain access to the roof. Other games have sequences similar to this but the difference is that they are scripted. When you script something there is no chance of a random outcome. In Trespasser, whatever happens, happens. If it screws up your chances to finish a level, then you have to reload a savegame and try again.

Baldur's Gate info - Sacrates
Luke was hittin the boards yesterday like a bottle of Jack Daniels. He has some good BG blurbs that you'll want to read up on like always. The first one is on Character class combos etc.

But it won't change anything for the main release, and I still don't think it will change a lot for the future. EG, it mentions Ranger/cleric (druid), which aside from these rules appears to be a special class of one specific goddess and intentionally rare(Meilikki). The multi clearly breaches the alignments of both individual classes (some form of good for rangers, and true neutral for druids). Such special-case classes may be inappropriate for the context of BG(along with some exotic races), even though they appear in the PHB or other sources(though I did miss that. Ah well. You do what you can :)

Anyway, we will likely not have every class and combination available, though we will continue to try to cover as wide a field as we can within the context of the game we are trying to make.

His next bit is on Spellbooks and Bard's with Armor.

1, spellbooks in BG are not physical entities, they are part of your character. So, yes your bard has a "spellbook," but he cannot place spells within it until he is able to use them. Does he start with spells? No, he must find/buy them and transcribe them in once he reaches second level (his first spellcasting level).

2, Nope, bards cannot currently cast while wearing armor. We had considered this at one point, but deemed it unnecessary. In BG, no class will be able to cast mage spells in any type of armor.

Continuing the Bard discussion:

The PHB specifically states that the bard must obey the restrictions on wearing armor and casting spells. We did consider allowing this at one point (I think it's an optional rule, but I'm not sure of the source), but we found that it just wasn't necessary. The Bard is quite useful as is, and switching armor in BG is relatively easy regardless(though admittedly dangerous in combat).

So the class is not "Trashed," we are just not using the optional rules your DM opted for.

Dues Ex love - Sacrates
Game Worlds Network has just smacked up a nice Dues Ex Interview with Bob White and Harvey Smith. Don't worry, my interview is with neither of them, it's with someone much higher on the Totem Pole of Love. Here's an excerpt from Witchboy:

We will have the player returning to maps numerous times. Each time, something interesting will happen. The maps are dynamic. In Paris, you can return to the Metro Station Block several times. Each time, things are slightly changed--characters move around, leave, architecture has changed, stores open and close, etc. In fact, the world will be set up to show the changes of the larger plot as you progress. In many cases, this involves creating multiple versions of a single map. Which is pretty easy for us, since once you create the bulk of the map, the minor variations are quicker to put into place.

In Deus Ex, the world is extremely interactive. You can pick up a chair and throw it across the room, push it out of the way, carry it with you, destroy it, etc. Objects can be picked up and added to inventory, etc. If you shoot out glass in a map, then return a week later, someone will have replaced the glass. In other cases, we will save the state of the world.

The game also allows for branching and multiple forms of NPC reaction. (If you shoot Nicolette's boyfriend and she hears about it, she will react very differently to you than if you choose to be more noble.)
