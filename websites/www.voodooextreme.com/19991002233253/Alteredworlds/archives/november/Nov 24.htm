BG Bits - Bowmeister
A big wet one to the guys at Baldur's Gate Chronicles for putting all the incomprehensible little posts together into one big incomprehensible post. Having said that, here's the latest off the Developer's Corner and the Backroom message boards...

The main protagonist is the person who is outlined in the Character Arbitration screen. So, if you are hosting a session, you can give someone else the main character/protagonist by changing their name to the selected slot. All the hosted server does is host the game. He/she does not have be the protagonist.

Incidently, the person who is at the top right of the character portraits in the game screen is the character who leads the party. That is all. He/she can be killed, but unless he/she is the main protagonist, the game continues.

When you export your character, you can give him/her a different name.

Just to clarify. In BG, we are allowing paladins and rangers 'weapon specialization' - the ability to allocate two proficiency points to a weapon category and become a 'Specialist'. Only fighters can receive ADVANCED weapon specialization - which allows three, four or five proficiency points to be allocated to a weapon category so that the fighter can become a weapons 'Master, High Master, or Grand Master'. Note that because fighters in BG will not be able to achieve 9th level, the highest specialization you will be able to achieve in the initial game will be High Master - and that is only if you spend all of your additional proficiency points in one weapons category.

Multiclass fighters can get specialization but not advanced specialization. Dual-class fighters are unaffected - if you dual class from a fighter your specializations are frozen at the point you dual class. If you dual class to a fighter you can get full advanced weapon specialization

If we could have, we would have released the game about a week ago to get maximum holiday impact (note timing on Half-Life, for example). However, the game is simply not at the quality level that we are satisfied with yet. It is extremely difficult to sit in on numerous meetings around here with everyone pulling out their hair asking "when, when, when!?" and not being able to tell them the exact date. We are doing the best we can and at least right now - the game is cooperating.

The Stealth skill (a combination of Hide in Shadows and Move Silently) must be selected in order to use. Attempting other actions (besides moving) while it is engaged negates the effect. The thief's first attack while hidden causes the effect to end immediately after and (if the weapon is suitable) is a backstab.

A thief's starting abilities vary according to race and dexterity, but a human (no racial bonuses) with a dexterity of 13 (no dexterity bonuses) begins with 10% Stealth, 5% Find Traps, 15% Pick Pockets, and 10% Open Locks. With racial modifications and the maximum dexterity a halfling could have (19), a beginning halfling thief would have 40% in Stealth, 20% in Find Traps, 35% in Pick Pockets, and 35% in Open Locks. With a dexterity of 13 (no bonuses), they begin with 25%,10%,20%, and 15%, respectively. Needless to say, they make very good thieves.

U:A Preview - Bowmeister
The guys over at Gamespot have slapped up a new preview of Ultima:Ascension. It's a good look at the current status of the game, along with some spiffy new screenshots (the gargoyles look cool :). Check the full preview out here.

Something that was very important to Mendelsohn and Garriot was that this game not only give closure to long-time Ultima fans, but also open up a new world to Ultima newcomers. You won't have to know the history of Ultima to play this game (a beautiful tapestry of history will fill in the details), but you will be rewarded for past experiences. Many cities from past Ultimas are revisited, and many old friends and foes make their reappearances. The Compatriots are back (Iolo, Shamino, and others) and so is Blackthorne. There are over 200 nonplayer characters (NPCs) in the game so far, and that number could grow to 250 before the game is finished, which should be spring of next year. Of those, some 50 will be characters you might have seen in your past adventures as the Avatar.

EQ Interview - Sacrates
Ah, a nice morning filled with Everquest goodness! Although still no word for Brad, he is evil incarnate. Anyway, the Interview is with Rosie Cosgrove and has some slick screenshots to boot. Head   on over and check it. Here's a snip.

There's a deluge of sources from medieval woodcuts and folklore through to Tolkein's bestiaries, Dungeons & Dragons and finally to contemporary games such as Diablo. What influenced your creations, and how did you decide on the expected forms (such as Trolls and Ogres) of the monsters (and characters) the game has a plentiful supply of?
When I was made lead I had a general idea of the style and appearance of the player characters, mostly based on how I would want to look walking around in a fantasy game. I wanted them to have richness, depth, and clarity, and the sexiness of super heroes. At first I thought they should fall into categories of strong and sexy, funny, or scary. As its turned out I don't think they fit into categories at all, maybe because they seem more like creatures than caricatures. I think a turning point in the characters was when I referred to the first one, a Dark Elf female as "her" instead of "it". For me it seemed like at that moment she became real and in a bizarre way I started kind of caring about her. Then again maybe I was just starting to crack from lack of sleep. For the monsters, designer Bill Trost, comp artist Rick Schmitz, and I let our imaginations go crazy in brainstorming sessions where we wrote long lists of cool monsters. Rick sketched up samples and we picked them according to what was best for game play and what was coolest.

EQ Preview - Sacrates
Those Console lovin, PC playin wannabes over at Next Gen (Hey we're their affiliate so we love em anyway) just slapped up another Preview to Everquest. Here's a blurb for you.

While people reading this may deny it (or wish they could with some degree of credibility), many gamers come from a tradition of pen and paper RPGs. For such gamers, the promise of a D&D game without having to go through the hassles involved (getting everybody in one place, finding a good GM, etc…) has always been the holy grail of computer RPGs. The main problem faced over the years, of course, is that the computer game worlds in which people played lacked the spontaneity and interactivity that could be offered by a pen and paper game. This is where the Massively Multiplayer RPG comes in. A relatively new genre for the masses, the concept of an online D&D game isn't really that new. Many of the most hard-core have been playing free online MUDs, or some of the pay-to-play services like Gemstone, for years. Meridian 59 and Ultima Online brought graphics to the fray and introduced the more casual user to the fun (and trials) of gaming with hundreds of people in a virtual world. Everquest is one of the first of the second generation of these RPGs and it aims to take the world one step farther than UO: it will be fully 3D.
