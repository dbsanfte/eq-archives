Asheron's Call biscuits - Sacrates
Here's a few juicy morsels dug up on Asheron's Call boards. Also is this Chat log from the other night which I missed. First up is Jason Booth explaining why there won't be any NPC training.

Actualy, everything is player run. We don't have NPC guides that train you in skills, or that your required to visit in order to advance. That would be very against the way AC has been designed - with freedom of play shaping your character, not pre-defined templates..

And here's a bit of mini Interview with Jason who pulls this guys pants down around his ankles and lets him have it.

Too make it more real? Oh please, if you believe that you were born yesterday.

They are getting lazy, plain and simple.

First, let me apologize if I come of a bit harsh, but I get very tired of seeing this type of BS around here.

As for being lazy, which you implied in your last post; listen bud, we've been working on this game for FIVE years, count them, FIVE. We started this company in a basement, worked our way through the living room and into our first office without getting paid. After almost three years of working on the game living off whatever we could scrape together, we finialy got a publisher who could back the product right. We haven't seen a cent from this game yet, and I doubt it will even recoup what we've put into it. It's a labor of love, and if you think we're being lazy then I think you need to go program one of these yourself and see how much work it actualy is. Pull out a calculator and figure out what it costs to employ 30-40 people for five years, even at minimal wage.

Actualy it is VERY important in RPGs for me. It help sets the mood. And since it is based around adventuring and killing it should have music to stir the blood.

Music only sets the mood if it is in response to what is happening at that moment on the screen, and it's not that same track you've heard over, and over, and over, and over.. Besides, the sounds of the forest better convey the feeling of being IN a forest.

And a game doesn't need to be delayed for music, since a whole other party would have to make it, it is just up to the designers to add some simple coding to add it.

If your going to add something to a game, don't do it because it makes a good bullet point on a checklist. Music is one of these bullet points. Sure, it would be great to have a score that recomposes itself on the fly to suit the mood of the scene, and remains interesting for the thousands (yes, thousands) of hours of play some people will put into a game like this. But not only is that incredibly hard to compose and design, but requires a really amazing sound system and quite a bit of system resources as well. Most games just stream several tracks off the CD - but how dynamic is that? How soon will you get sick of that music? The average game doesn't have the lifespan of an online RPG, and I'm always sick of the music before the game.

Ok, who out there would like us to delay the game for another year to design and impliment an interactive music system, which you'll want to turn off after a few days, raise your hand. Ok, who wants it this year?

Who would rather have things like house ownership, allegience wars, pets and riding animals instead?

I think your out voted..

And since they are playing with MS money, GO FOR IT!

No - we're playing with our money. Go read a book on publisher relations in any industry, music, games, whatever. You'll quickly learn how ignorant that statement is. We don't just wire money out of Bill Gates personal bank account every time we need it - get real.

Bottom line, if you want music, there are a lot of musicians out there who would love to have you buy thier CDs. If you want custom music, fire up a midi or MP3 player in the background and watch it drain your system resources slowing your frame rate.

Weekly WoT - Sacrates
This weeks WoT development update has been kicked up by our boy Glen. Finally he has some interesting words to say talking a bit about Star Wars. He also got around and posted a pic of the Development Team. (Luckily not while playing naked Twister)

Every week that goes by is one week closer to either E3 or Star Wars; take your pick.   Although you don't see people camping outside E3 a month in advance for tickets, you also won't see WoT at the theatre!  So while you're at E3, I'll be nice enough to let you play a WoT Arena match if you give me your Star Wars tickets, guaranteed!

Speaking of which, we just played such a match tonight, and while the sound code decided to take a time-out, the game itself was more fun than ever.  Chain Lightning is a lot more cool now.  All you want to do is charge up, run into the middle of a bunch of pansies, and watch the sparks fly!   It's very cool and much more balanced.   We're also definitely getting better artifact loading than before, but there's still room for improvement.

The level designers have been getting ready for the review tomorrow (although they still have a fun night ahead of them).  From what I've seen, they'll be wonderful.   James' map is almost finished and the interactivity and architecture is spot on.   Scott lost a bit of time to a cut scene environment, but his main map is still looking great.  Warren is still dealing with his own convoluted Canadian tax issues--which is why I didn't get a chance to see his map today, so I'm crossing my fingers and hoping for the best.   Tomorrow should be interesting.

A lot of focus has been placed on AI lately to ensure not only effective enemies, but fun ones.  I've been designing some different standard behavior patterns and Ryan has been coding some movement pattern tools which allow all of us to mess with how our characters walk/run/attack.  Mike F. implemented a new sound slot system into which Sam is sticking our effects, while at the same time he tweaks all of the creature animation.

Cut scene finals are trickling in, which is making Jim very happy.  He finally has assets to play with, testing and checking our compression tools to ensure quality, speed, and size are at the levels we need.  The cut scenes themselves are pretty cool too.   We'll have a bunch completely finished shortly (visuals, sound, and music all done) to show off at E3.

Paul and Julie are almost finished with the immediate UI tasks, and the final assets look great.  Marc continues to support the level designers in their specific texture needs.  Robert is still working through the decorations and effects.  And Joel continues to pump out his excellent skins, which already have made a world of difference.   As I've said numerous times before, we have some of the top artists in the business.  I expect nothing but the best from each and every one of them, and they never fail me.

Ward Six Interview - Sacrates
Jason McIntosh of Ward Six was interviewed by The Perfect one's Online Magazine (Who thinks these names up?) recently about their upcoming Q2 conversion and RPG Engine they are creating called "Renaissance". Here's a biscuit to bite on.

Renaissance is developing very quickly. We've been really quiet about it because we don't want to hype it yet since it's not fully functional. But we do have an excessively powerful interaction engine featuring an incredible scripting engine that will allow us to do literally anything we can imagine in terms of interaction.

The graphics engine is also shaping up, and already features volumetric lighting, seamless indoor/outdoor scenes, etc. We're working on a GUI and audio support now, as well as filling out the other critical features such as physics and collisions.

By May, we'll have a technology demo to show at E3. We're setting up meetings with publishers already, and things look pretty positive.

Vampire Bites - Sacrates
Here's a couple of Scooby snacks Rob Huebner left for all you Vampire fans out there. First one is on whether or not you can "feed" on innocents. Mmmm mmm good.

Well, without giving TOO much away, the answer is "yes" to pretty much all the topics you asked about. Our lead animator recently completed the first set of animations for feeding, and they look incredible.

If we a chance to release an AVI, it would seem a logical choice.

And a bit on Java.

Well, Java is very much like C++ or even C, so really if you know anything about programming an object-oriented language, you won't have a problem with it. 90% of what you read about in a Java book isn't necessary to program the script, its just the underlying syntax that uses Java.

If you go to "java.sun.com" and read the on-line docs to the point where you know how to write "hello world" and maybe an if/then/else statement or two, you're ready to go.

And here's Sam Brown on Editing.

There haven't been any final decisions made that I'm aware of, but this is a much more complicated situation than it seems. Because The Wheel of Time is not owned by us (we had to pay to use the license) it's a distinct possibility that no one else will be able to utilize those items specific to WoT. I'm not saying this is how it is, I'm just letting you know that this is a very sticky situation and I hope everyone understands that this really isn't open to negotiation...it's all part of the unfortunate business end of making a game based on a popular license.
