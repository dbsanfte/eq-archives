on random encounters: Let me see if I can do this again, without confusing others or myself...

The higher your outdoorsman the better chance you have of avoiding a random encounter. If you make your outdoorsman "roll", you will be prompted with "Do you wish to encounter (insert name here)." Followed by Yes or No. This way you have the choice or encounter it or not.

If you fail your outdoorsman roll, you will be forced into the encounter (like normal.)

Like Fallout 1, your Perception will determine how close you appear to your "guests." The higher your perception, the further away you will appear from them. The lower your perception, the closer you appear... to the point of being surrounded.

Some areas of the world map will give your outdoorsman skill a negative modifier, making it harder to avoid random encounters.

Hope that helps some.

on party member skills: Some party members have Key Non-combat skills that will aide the player.

If the NPC, who's key skill is outdoorsman, has a higher outdoorsman than you, his/her skill will be what is checked when doing your outdoorsman roll.

Dual class revised FAQ

On why your archer's won't shoot other party members: Maybe so, but in P&P and in RL you are dealing with a MUCH larger "playing field." You have a couple-hundred yards to fire your arrows before your frontline is in ANY danger of being hit. In BG, your view is smaller and your fighters are always RIGHT THERE. You cannot arc a shot over their heads either; they go right through. Yeah, firing into a melee should be dangerous, but in BG you would see your archer's usefulness drop through the floor. Gameplay issue. Too much reality in an unreal setting = bad.

On how the guards just "appear": Talk about a coding nightmare. Having the guards walk from location X, crossing areas and entering houses etc. would be a lot of work for something you would rarely see. Man-hours on that are not really worth the effect you would get. As for your thief, the guards do not IMMEDIATELY appear. There is a delay (albeit a very short one) wherein you "hear the guards being summoned" in which you can attempt to use your handy stealth/hide in shadows. You succeed, the guards appear but they cannot see you. You get out before they find you and you suffer no reputation hit. Scot free baby! You could also duck upstairs or out the door, but you had best be quick about it, and if you go upstairs they will be downstairs where you were for some time (in case someone returns to the scene of the crime, as it were). Hide and sneak back down. Thieves can get away rather easy if they are skilled, so the corresponding penalties are unfortunately quite severe. Maybe you can bribe the guards, thought most you can't. Maybe you can backstab them, though the reputation of a guard-killer drops like a rock.

Only gnomes can be multi-class specialists (in fact, it is their only option as a mage multi-class). Other races are restricted to the basic mage in a multi-class. Humans can dual-class a spacialist though.

When you change to a new class, your old abilities become "dormant". You then progress just as a normal single class version of whatever you have changed to, gaining proficiencies and abilities like normal. EG: if you were a fighter with 3 proficiency slots in large swords and dual-classed to a thief, your fighter abilities would shutdown and you would choose new proficiencies as a thief (one slot in large sword only) with new first-level proficiencies. Once you were one level higher in your new class than your old, your fighter class reactivates and you then use whichever proficiencies were better. They overlap, and you would use the 3 instead of the one. They don't add together. You can never improve on your old fighter abilites, but you don't lose them (well, you do for a while).

Mages cannot use shields.

Clerics and Druids AND their combinations with other classes are forever restricted by the ethos they must follow. There are huge danger signs on this class on the dual-class screen too, because if you start as a fighter and dual TO a druid, you LOSE any and ALL proficiencies in weapons restricted from the druid. You have mastery in large sword and dual to a druid? Say goodbye. Even when your fighter class reactivates you won't be able to use them. They are gone for good, and the slots are lost. No chance to switch them around. Ahh, the harsh restrictions inherant in hearing "the call."

The PCs carry out your instructions as soon as they are able. If they are currently in the middle of a "round", then they must wait until they have the opportunity to do something else. They can always walk around/change positions/run away and such, but a character that has begun to swing his weapon, cannot swing at a new target until his next round begins. A mage cannot start a second spell until the round in which he cast his first is over. This time delay between actions will still fly by if you are fighting multiple targets with multiple characters. You'll be pausing at least occasionally. Pausing does not reset the rounds, nor does changing their actions.

Fighters gain extra proficiency points ay 1st, 3rd, 6th, etc. It is every three levels.
Double Specialize - level 3 Fighter
Master - level 6 Fighter
Grand Master - level 9 Fighter

Yes, you can specialize in more than one weapon group.

You cannot leave the character creation screen without spending all your weapon proficiency points.

The mission pack is a plug-in for the existing game. Your character will not change.

Each character is a separate creature. It's not like Might and Magic wherein you are a four-headed beastie that is never without your fellows. Your characters can separate as far as the current area allows (as much as 8 screens by 8 screens, bigger than it sounds). You can send lone characters into most buildings without the rest of the party. Some areas are party required, but these are usually major encounter areas or entrances to larger areas (dungeons, etc.). The guys you have left alone are always just a click away. Select their portrait and you are centered on them. If you do leave them to fend for themselves (ignoring/phone/potty break...tsk, shoulda paused) and a monster happens by, they will act according to the script you have selected for them.

Basically, when you first create a character and when your first get an NPC in your party, they have the basic "respond if attacked" script by default. If directly assaulted they will do something, not always the best thing, but something. However, on everyone's character page there is a customize button, which allows you to change their scripts. There are plenty to choose from and they are grouped according to class. You can have your fighters attack anything hostile that they see and follow it until either they or their target is dead, or you can have them draw their bow and attack hostiles until teh enemie closes, at which point he will switch to his sword and go toe to toe. You could also have him only fight creatures that are attacking the weakest party member, or never go far from the party leader (there are more as well). Thieves can do this as well, plus automatically try to backstab (and more as well). Clerics can be told to fight as best they can, but if anyone drops below 1/2 hit-points they will heal them. They can also automatically dish out the offensive spells. Same for mages (and more). Basically, the party is as effective on it's own as you wish it to be. The scripts could likely take you through a lot of simple fights, though the tougher ones would overwhelm them.

Major encounters are placed including their items. Smaller encounters are randomized somewhat, though you will never get that superduper sword of doom from a wandering monster. You gotta work for the good stuff.

Lots of us are hardcore AD&D fans, but Lead Designer James knows it backwards and forwards. We all have to to some extent. Without it, we couldn't spot the subtle bugs (like a completely bitched THACO GRRRRRRERSRAFRDRE...fixed though. Smiles everyone, smiles).

Boxes are done, manuals are not. But don't benchmark against the Fallout2 materials. Baldur's Gate is very different (The manual is 160 pages, for example).

U:A daily dose - Sacrates
In what is becoming commonplace, we have a ton of U:A info flooding in from Sith once again. This is an absolute beautifull thing seeing the programmers starting to give feedback on the game! Here is Bill Randolf (Lead Programmer and mack daddy) laying some smack down for us.

I cannot say whether a specific numerical quotation would be possible. It would be, of course, loaded with risk, if we were to add a feature later to the engine that would slow it down, for example. Right now, we're in a phase where we're speeding things up to as fast as they can possibly go. But, as we add more and more features, the engine will runmarginally slower. Another factor that makes frame rate quotes difficult is the huge amount of scalability in our engine. Our lighting model has many levels of scalability. You can also adjust your far clipping plane in & out to change the number of objects & polygons in a scene. And, we're adding Levels of Detail. We're doing everything we can to allow you to crank up the fidelity, if speed is your gig, or crank up detail, with some performance degradation.

And, still another factor is the variability of our world. Frame rate varies depending on your location in the world. And, it also varies depending upon how many creatures are around you, and how much stuff you've carried from other locations in the world and dumped around you. There's little to stop you from collecting a huge amount of items & slowing the game way down, if that's your choice...

And, finally, I'd have to avoid saying anything that might cause us to be compared to games of completely different genres, such as Quake or Tomb Raider. Those products address completely different play styles from Ascension, and have inherently different performance issues.

So, about all I can say is that, if you play on the "recommended platform" with hardware acceleration, your frame rate will be fast enough for it to not detract from the immersiveness of the 3D world of Britannia!

And if that's not enough here is Audball bitch slapping us with some goods on the sound! This gets my Willy Wonka Wonkavating for sure since I recently purchased the SB Live as most of you know.

It should still sound good and immersive (you'll still get DirectSound3D). To offset the lack of EAX support in non-CL products, our Audio Engineers are pre-processing some environmental effects with reverb and whatnot. We will play them as 2D sounds (EAX only works with 3D sounds, so playing them 2D will ensure they sound the same on every system, with or without EAX) and they should help the game's ambiance quite a bit.

We might also use a second set of pre-processed audio samples for those sounds which would benefit the most - things like footsteps or events that you hear often. If we detect that you don't have EAX, we could play sounds from that special set of SFX; if you do have EAX, we would just play the "normal"/non-processes FX and let EAX do it's thing.

And one more from Bill on the Physics model in the game.

Things DO fall at precisely 9.81 m/2^2 in our world! Although, visually, it looks like they're moving too slow. But I've checked & rechecked the math. It's just a visual illusion. Perhaps other games modeled a stronger gravity to make it look right...

We do support moments of inertia in our physics system; objects do have a center of gravity, different from the geometric center. Objects have mass.

Our physics system does support buoyancy. And, crates float lower if they're full. But, I don't know if the design will incorporate this feature.

Signs, hanging herbs, and other objects swing in the wind in our world; but, surface-area forces aren't modeled.

Our engine does not support having an axe stick in a door & letting the Avatar pull it out. And, not just any item can be picked up--only those that make sense to pick up.

BG bundle of joy - Sacrates
Luke and the whole Baldur's Gate were over on the boards spewing more BG love. First is from Luke letting it loose.

on memorization of spells: You rememorize your spells each time you rest. You can rest as often as you like, but 8 hours passes each time, and many areas have chances for your sleep being interupted by enemies. If you are attacked while resting you do not get your spells.

on time: 24hours in-game = 2 hours real-time

on friendly fire: You won't have to worry about accidentally hitting your own guys with arrows. Area effect spells on the other hand...

on charisma: Charisma will have an effect on many NPCs, whether you are in multiplayer or not. They won't join if they don't like you. You can use NPC's that HAVE joined you as your "frontman." Ie. found the paladin? take advantage of that 17 charisma minimun he has. Use him to talk and barter. The main character is "you," but "you" are also "the party."

on factors that influence a retreat by NPCs: all of the above and more. number of opponents, damage taken, fellows fallen, enemies fallen, etc.

on whether high dexterity allows mages a better chance of executing spells: definitly

on THACO (To Hit Armor Class 0): In multiclass, your THACO is determined by whichever class is the best. In Dual-class, it is your active class until it is one level higher than your old class. Then it is whichever is better.

on intelligence: It varies. Mostly they will respond better. ie. two different characters could say the same thing and get different responses.

on in-party interaction: They will comment to you occasionally. Depends on the character. Some have agendas, some are easy to please.

Let me check into it this and I'll get back to you...

on Druids and Rangers: First of all, lest you feel too abused, we're really talking about half-elves here... no other demi-human race can multi-class with Ranger or Druid abilities. (Consistent with AD&D rules)

There are 5 possible multi-class half elf combinations that concern this conversation:

Fighter / Druid

Cleric / Ranger

Mage / Druid

Fighter / Mage / Druid (not applicable for BG)

and the infamous

Ranger / Druid

So if you REALLY wanted to play multi-class with a Druid or Ranger these are your BG choices.

Now I am departing from 'official' AD&D rules and commenting generally upon the decision to not allow Druid or Ranger dual classing in Baldur's Gate. (These are my personal opinions) Note also that I am saying Baldur's Gate only (in terms of the original game). No one has decided ANYTHING relative to additional BG products (expansion packs or whatever).

Given that a Druid or Ranger is really only interesting at higher levels (where their special abilities come into play), I would NOT recommend ANY of these multi-classed characters. If you multi-class with a Druid, for example, you are writing off the ability to shape-change in the game (you will never reach level 7) and you will have significant weapon restrictions (Druid weapon restrictions take precedence (as Priests) over all other weapon restrictions). So what would be the point of being a Fighter/Druid, or a Ranger/Druid? (Imagine being a Ranger/Druid and not being able to use a bow, for example). The only combination that would seem to make sense to me would be some sort of Mage/Druid... but why would you want to do this when a Mage/Cleric would offer so much more flexibility? Likewise a Cleric/Ranger... I really have an issue (personally) with the idea of Rangers that can't use bows... just remember that any Druid class comes with significant weapon restrictions and any Ranger class comes with significant armor restrictions and you'll see what I mean...

So I guess while I COULD see a very cool dual-class 7th level Mage / 8th level Druid, it simply isn't going to happen in BG... if you REALLY think through the AD&D rules, and think through the allowed possibilities, a BG dual-class character along these lines doesn't make a lot of sense.

You'd be better off developing a 7th level Ranger and dual classing in a BG expansion...

(Curious to see what other people think though....)
