Third World: The Interview
By: Madbard
With: Adam Maxwell

In an Industry filled with Diablo clones, and Baldur's Gate wannabe's, it's always good to get a breathe of fresh air. Third World looks to be the game to give us just that. Without further ado, sit back, relax, and let Madbard take you on an Altered journey into Third World!

MB: First off tell us a little about your personal background in the computer gaming industry. Also talk a little on the Third World design team.

Adam: Well, it’s a pretty standard story really, I got my start in the industry as an unpaid beta tester. By the time I had graduated from high school I had tested games for most of the major game publishers of the time. From there I managed to get a job as a technical support representative for Blizzard, where I met Ron Millar, Chris Metzen, and the rest of Blizzard’s amazingly talented team. I worked at Blizzard doing various things for almost 3 years. During that last year Ron Millar left to found Redline Games with James Anhalt. Ron offered me my big "break" as a an assistant designer shortly after that, and I left Blizzard to come work with Redline on Third World.

Coming to work for Redline was an amazing experience. The other companies I had dealt with were all established, large companies that had set procedures and hierarchies. Here at Redline I was introduced to a newly assembled team with no set rules, no set hierarchies, and no history. What Redline has is a team of passionate talented people dedicated to making the best possible games we can. We’re writing our own rules and history as we go along. Add the fact that we’re a small, very tightly knit, team to that sense of adventure and you’ve got one helluva cool place to work.

The team here is definitely the best combination of talent and personality I’ve seen in a long time. We all get along damned well, and our talents and skills compliment each other greatly. James Anhalt is the president of Redline, and the lead programmer for Third World. He’s a quiet guy, but he always knows what’s going on and how to fix a problem. A lot of the time he’ll come up with a simple answer to stuff I had considered a "design dilemma". Derek Frost is our lead 2D artist and Creative Director. That means he’s responsible for making sure the game and the game universe work together and that the overall feel of the game fits the mood we’re shooting for. Derek and I work pretty closely together on the design of the game, and we came up with the story together. I’m the designer on the game right now, my responsibility is to the playability of the game. My job is to make sure this game is fun to play and easy to grasp. Carl Chimes is the senior programmer working on Third World. He comes to us from working on Dark Reign. He’s a technical genius and is responsible for a lot of the technology and "eye candy" behind the game engine. In fact James and Carl are the guys that have built the Third World engine into the powerhouse it is now. Martin Jajam is programming the map editor and the scripts for the game. He came in and created a fully functional 3D environment editor in a matter of months. Then he dove right into the game engine and brought the objects of the world to life. The last programmer on the team is Joseph Lee. Joe is programming the AI for the NPCs of the world. Thanks to him the workers in the city are actually working, the prostitutes are… prostituting, and the enemy squads are working against you to stop you. Steve Pabst is our sound designer and one of the composers responsible for Warp Digital, the company that we’ve contracted to do the sound track for Third World. I consider working with Steve a true privilege. Only once before have I seen someone so dedicated to music and sound. His sounds, and the Third World sound track are instrumental (pardon the pun) to bringing Third World to life.

Of course, unless you can see the world it doesn’t matter, and that’s where our art team comes in. Paul Lee and Chris "Guzzy" Guzman are our 3D artists. Thanks to their efforts the characters in this game actually have character, and the special effects are something to be proud of. They work closely with Derek and Tony Carrillo, both of whom fill the role of 2D artists admirably. Derek and Tony did the textures for the map, the items, and just about everything else in the game. They also did all of the concept artwork that helped us all visualize Third World the same way. Without our 4 artists, I shudder to think of what Third World would look like.

The newest member of the team is Justin Pacuska, our level designer. Justin and I built all of the locations in the game using the editor that Martin programmed. Let me tell you that without Justin’s help, I’d still be building. I’ve never seen anyone build worlds as quickly, or as realistically as Justin does. His first task here was to build a mining complex that figures into the story. He was done in 3 days, and the quality, especially considering that it was his first serious work in 3D, was amazing. Justin was a welcome addition to the team.

MB: Now unlike most CRPGs Third World is set in the Sci-fi future which will be a welcome change for many gamers. Altered World's own Sacrates and millions of other Star Wars, Shadowrun and Blade Runner refugees are going to be popping out of the wood work for this game. Give us a little background on the story behind Third World and its Cyber-punk environment.

Adam: Third World takes places on another planet. Third world is, itself, a botched corporate colonization effort. Years after colonization the world is in a state of barely veiled chaos and perpetual decay. The corporations control the people and fight an eternal cold war amongst themselves. The streets are filled with gangs and hired guns, all looking for ways to make a quick buck and move up in the world. That’s where the player comes in. The player is the leader of a mercenary squad taking various covert and "Wet" missions from the corporations, building a bankroll for retirement and a chance to live life as one of Third World’s elite, or perhaps as a Warlord with a small empire all their own. How it turns out for each squad is up to them and is determined by their skills as warriors, diplomats, and gamblers.

MB: Now making a CRPG always opens developers to criticism. It seems that RPG fans are always the hardest to please when it comes to game detail and what a player can or can't do in the game. How much freedom will there be in Third World? And how much of the game world can the characters affect?

Adam: That’s definitely something we’re aware of. We’re all CRPG fans here, and we have our list of pet peeves that we’re trying to address. One of the major problems, as you mentioned, is how much the player can really influence the world they play in. When you make an RPG you have to decide what you’re primary goal is going to be. Is it going to be story, or is going to be player freedom. For the most part, the two are exclusive. That doesn’t mean you can’t make a game with a great story and a lot of freedom for the player, but often to get one you have to sacrifice the other. We didn’t want to make that sacrifice at all so we had to find a different solution. The Single player game is a very definite story. The player has a lot of influence at each individual location, but the story directs a lot of the player’s movements (not controls the player, but directs what goals should be accomplished, etc.). We wanted more freedom then that in the game as well, which is why the multiplayer aspect is more of a true RPG. In the multiplayer system, the player’s goals and motivations are up the player. If the player wants to be a merciless killer, so be it. If the player wants to fight for the side of right and justice, so be it. The player should be able to do pretty much anything they want to do. Of course there are consequences for every action, both in single and multiplayer. Break the law and the law will come looking for you, cross someone the wrong the way, and what happens depends on how fast you think and how badly you crossed the other person or NPC.

In both single player and multiplayer we wanted to give the player as much freedom as we possibly could. Our motto here is, "if the player thinks they can do it, they can." There is a lot of interactivity in the world that has nothing to do with anything, it’s there because it makes sense to be there. Break into a chemical refinery and you’ll be able to raid their labs, search their storage areas, and talk to their employees. It has nothing to do with your mission or goal there, but you can do it. That’s true of every location in the game. We hope that when the players get a chance to build their own locations with the included editor that they follow the same guidelines. After all, our goal here was to build a world, not just a collection of levels.

MB: The foundation of any RPG or CRPG is character development. How does the character creation system and the character advancement system work in Third World? And what are the classes and races players can choose from?

Adam: The character creation system was designed with customized characters in mind. There are restrictions on which races can be which character classes, but that’s about it. When you create a character you choose it’s race, it’s class, and customize the character’s features to suit your image of the character. That doesn’t seem like a lot of freedom at first, but allow me to explain how character advancement works. Once you’ve created a basic character, it’s up to you to decide how that character progresses. Advancement isn’t an arbitrary level based thing. Instead the advancement is based on how much and how well you use, the 5 stats and 5 skills available to each character. If you choose to play a human commando, for example, but use that commando primarily as a hacker, you’ll end up with a commando that can’t really fight, but can hack a mainframe without breaking a sweat. Your characters also age as the game progresses, and every year they age changes the character, in some cases for the better, but as with any of us, as we get older we get a little slower, perhaps a little weaker. Each race ages differently though, if you’re in it for the long haul, how a race ages will influence your choices. Wardroids, for example, are artificially intelligent military droids. They don’t exactly age, but the law has placed strict regulations on them. They have a 45 year lifespan. After 45 years they have to turn themselves in for mandatory recycling, if they don’t they’re considered rogue, and the laws that protect sentient beings on Third World don’t apply to them.

As for the character classes and races in Third World… There are 5 race, Humans, Greks, Dendrites, Ferox, and Wardroids. Humans we all know, so I won’t go into too much detail there. The Greks are a deeply spiritual race that have a reverence for nature and an understanding of the world is unparalleled. Their faith and ties to the land make them powerful hunters and shamans. Their strength and speed make them some of the best hand to hand fighters on the planet. The Ferox are a race of brutish, savage, thugs. They aren’t too bright, but they are strong, fierce, and quick to brawl. This makes the few Ferox on Third World highly sought after by corporate security and street gangs everywhere. The Dendrites are about as opposite from the Ferox as you can get. Dendrite are small, frail, and possess massive intellects and psionic abilities. They’re known for finding a solution to any problem, and make excellent researchers. Finally we come to the Wardroids. Wardroids were created in Third World’s largest war, the Ferox-Human war. When humanity first met the Ferox, the Ferox were not interested in coexisting peacefully. In fact they pounded the snot out of the human defense forces for years. Humanity was running out of soldiers and they needed to find a solution quickly. A pretty small robotics firm at the time came up with the idea of using robotic soldiers, the Wardroids. The results of the first few wardroid companies in the war were so positive that they’ve become a household word in security. In fact the planetary law enforcement agency is controlled by a central AI that orders modified Wardroids called Enforcers to patrol and keep the peace. The awesome power of the Wardroid, and the abuses they were used for after the Ferox-Human war, are the primary reasons for the strict controls on Wardroid lifespans. Street gangs and dissidents were salvaging old wardroids from the wreckage of the Ferox-Human wars, and using them as unstoppable armies to gain territory and power. The corporations found this… Distasteful and moved to regulate damned near every aspect of Wardroid usage.

MB: How does economics come into play in Third World? Do characters have and spend money?

Adam: Absolutely. Money is what makes any world go around, Third World is no different. The currency is different, sure, but the accumulation of wealth, and its applications towards gaining power are what it’s all about. After all, nothing is worth killing or dying for like cold hard credits if you’re the captain of a cold-hearted, well armed mercenary squad looking to live the good life.

MB: The modifications you can make to a character in Third World may not be for the faint of heart. Of course it's common knowledge that mutagenics and cybernetic technology is what puts the Altered in Altered Worlds. Tell us how characters can "improve" themselves with modifications in Third World.

Adam: The mods in third world run the standard c-punk gauntlet, but there are a few… surprises. I don’t want to give anything away, but lets just say that genetic research is a good thing.

MB: There are psionics in Third World. Are these mind powers used the way spells are in fantasy based CRPGs? And what types of things can a character do with psionics?

Adam: Psionics in third world are used differently by the races that possess them. The Dendrites, for example, channel their psionic power into the manipulation of perception. They can make you see things that aren’t there, slow down you perception of time, etc. In general I would have to say that psionics and their use are similar to spells in fantasy CRPGs, but a large effort has been put into making every possible effect unique, and viable given the universe the game is set in.

MB: There seems to be a lot of different environments in the game. Could you give us a few examples?

Adam: Sure, in the single player story you’ll encounter a lot of the different terrain on Third world. In the first half of the story alone you’ll see barren wastelands, subterranean caverns, the city streets, and more. I don’t want to give all of the different locations away, since some of them pretty amazing, but you’ll travel all over the planet during the game, so if it’s on Third World, you’ll probably see it.

MB: Many gamers complain about combat in CRPGs. How will combat take place in Third World? And how do tactics come into play with real-time combat?

Adam: Tactics and real-time combat go hand in hand to me. In a real-time environment, you’re pretty much forced to think tactically as opposed to strategically. However, there are very few games out there that really handle squad-based tactics well. Unfortunately none of the shining examples of squad based combat are role-playing games, and up until recently, none of them were real-time either. Our first goal with Third World is to create a world, and gaming system that promoted the use of squad based tactics. How you deploy, and employ, every single member of your squad matters, but because it’s real-time, your success is also dependant on your sense of timing. The feeling you get when you successfully pull of an ambush in real-time is totally different from the feeling you get in a turn-based game. Granted there isn’t as much time to plot and plan, but that’s one of the conditions you have to overcome or work around in real-time, just as visibility, positioning, timing, and equipment choices are. We’ve made every effort to factor in as many conditions and variables into the combat system as possible, while at the same time keep it fun to play and easy to control.

There are over 60 different weapons in the game, as well as over 45 items. In many cases those items and weapons work together. The benefit of all of this is that all of these factors change the tactics you use. A lot of times combat in CRPGs is severely restricted or simplified. Since combat is one of the strong focuses in the game we wanted to make sure the players had as much freedom as possible.

MB: Third World seems to be tailored made for multiplayer. What types of multiplayer games will be available in Third World? And what types of connections can we use to link up with our buddies? Also what's this I hear of players being able to set up their own servers and customize their own worlds? The Devil you say!!!

Adam: We feel that Third World’s multiplayer options are really what makes the game shine. If you log-on to sec-net through the internet, you’ll be presented with all of the missions currently running on the internet, and be able to talk to the other people playing the game. That’s all pretty familiar, but on-top of that are the items that help build the sense of an on-going world and a community. If you play a squad that has no respect for the law, or one that breaks contracts with corporations, you’ll be faced with bounties on your heads. The game is more then just combat and missions, although that plays a key role in the game. The game is also dealing with the other people on-line experiencing this world.

A great example of that is the mission system itself. When you accept a mission from a corporation and enter the world, there are events going on around you that you have nothing to do with. For example, you’re mission may be to destroy a Tubeway car as it pulls into a station, to send a message to the corporation running the train. At the same time another gang may have the mission of picking up a corporate executive and escorting him to a safehouse elsewhere in the city. While your two missions are unrelated, they take place on the same map at the same time. The other player is either going to have to delay you long enough to get their executive, or stop you from destroying the train. It promotes a secondary interaction with other players that a lot of the internet games out there lack.

The missions themselves are all different. You may be on an escort mission one day, only to be fighting an arena battle for cash prizes another. Another thing to note is that most missions have a counter-mission as well. Your mission may be to raid a lab and steal a sample of some new technology, as soon as you accept that mission, a mission for garrison duty at the same lab may be issued (or vice versa) for anyone on Sec-Net to take. Suddenly you’re not playing against the computer or the lab’s standard security forces, instead you are playing against the lab’s standard security and another mercenary team intent of destroying you.

MB: Sacrates will kill me if I don't get into some eye-candy specifics. You will be using a true 3D engine. Did Redline develop this engine? And what perspectives can we expect in Third World?

Adam: The Third World engine was built from scratch with the idea of tactical squad based combat in mind. As the game progresses the engine is continually refined to take advantage of new hardware and new refinements in special FX and interface. The game itself uses a ¾ perspective, but you have control of the panning, zooming, and tilt of the camera, for the most part. The game uses an intelligent camera positioning system to keep the camera unobstructed, and to keep the things in view that you want to see. Of course, we don’t lock you into anything, if you want to take a look around, you have that level of control.

MB: What type of accelerated support will be offered with Third World?

Adam: The engine is built to take advantage of most 3D accelerated video cards via D3D, while the editor uses OpenGL for it’s hardware acceleration. The engine will also take advantage of Creative’s EAX if you have drivers that support it.

MB: Now the requirements for Third World are listed at 133Mhz with 16mb of ram. That seems kind of low now days. Do you expect this to change in the future?

Adam: Umm, I’d definitely have to say that the requirements will most like raise before the game ships. Every layer of complexity we add the game takes its toll in processor and memory usage, unfortunately.

MB: How much of Third World is completed right now?

Adam: A large portion of the production work is finished at this point. We’re mainly tweaking, tuning, and cramming in a lot of the features we feared we weren’t going to get into the game.

MB: Is there anything else you want our readers to know about Third World?

Adam: There’s a lot I’d love to spill, especially about how all of the various game system interact with each other and influence the game’s universe, but I think it would be better if they were discovered by each player as they experience this game. After all, uncovering secrets and discovering intricacies are part of the game playing experience. :)
