System Shock update + - Sacrates
The Official System Shock page updated with a whole bunch of goodies for you SS freaks. First they've tossed up an AVI, then a few screenshots, and finally a Developers Update. Here's some of the smack, for the rest you know where to go.

Robert Caminos Last week I plowed though the easy motions and got about 120 processed. They will be entered into the game once Laura finishes the schemas. She has got about 50 or so done all ready which is marked in the Database. This week I will finish the priority 1 & 2 motions that remain. I will be working out a schedule for Joe listing the projections as to when the motions will enter the game.I will be nicer to Ken Levine.

Ian Vogel A former news reporter and photographer, "Old McVogel", as the team calls him, spends time after arduous shock 2 days on an 80 acre farm with his girlfriend...tending cows and horses and building fences. Then...he travels 2000 years into the future to build spaceship levels for a computer game. Ian has lately been busting hump to bring the Recreation deck to life. It is here you may find a swimming pool, theater, and casino, among other places. Very cool.

Scott Blinn I’ve been busy getting into System Shock workflow. Learning our editor and other technologies. I’m almost going full speed now and have gotten my currently assigned level (Command Deck) roughed out. My current project is to polish Command into a working, playable level. After that’s done, I will move onto the final level of the game. In my spare time (ya right!) I have been working on a total overhaul of my website and for those interested in Game/Level Design should stop by (www.skarz.com). I hope to have some articles about level and game design written soon by myself, Matt (another level designer on SS2), and other people. Have a good one!

Mike Ryan Mike (the other one, also known as "Ryan" or "Hey, you") has been working on Hydroponics as of late. Mikey likes it. He needs more textures and objects and sleep. Mike needs food badly. If he can't have that, he can have some more coffee. Yeah, that'll work.

Matt Boynton Hey folks, I'm still filling up engineering (it's a LARGE level) with monsters, crates, bodies (yup, dead ones), pipes, grating, robots, traps, security cameras, computers, logs, email, yadda, yadda, yadda. Just another day at the office. It's starting to come together and look like a real level.

Ken Levine Re-writing the logs, working with Dan Thron on storyboarding the cutscenes. Doing fun microsoft excel spreadhsheets with titles such as “Nanite distribution per level.”. Auditioning actors for the upcoming log recordings. Answering questions on TTLG, on of our cool fan pages. Illegally eading my Quotable Homer ’99 calendar in ’98.

Robert Fermier I've been reworking the interface in line with some new interface conventions, to streamline it and to make the system more flexible for reconfiguration. Also putting the finishing touches on some game systems like radiation, biohazard, energy stations, and the like. In the next few weeks we're going to be re-integrating our code base with the great stuff the guys over on Thief have been doing, now that they are basically done.

Mark Waks Worked on the details of avatars: getting players to look reasonable on each others' machines. This included fixing up physics models so that avatars work much like players, and adding "smoothing" code so that discrepancies between projected and actual player movement looks natural.

Jon Chey The last 3 weeks have been mainly concerned with integrating our new monster models and motion captures. We now have at least 400 motions that are to be used for cut-scenes & in game-animations as well as plenty of scary monsters. As well, we now have the majority of the weapons in the game and are waiting for the artists to generate effects. The weapons range from your standard military issue items to a variety of more exotic high-tech items & other pieces that must remain a secret for now. Most of my work over the next couple of weeks will be co-ordinating the continuing integration of these resources and writing any custom AI or effects code that is required.

Mike Rowley We just finished our initial version of networking for the dark engine. It is designed to send as little information as possible in order to keep the machines in synch. When we know that all the machines will simulate the same high level behavior in the same way, we don't send low level events for it. This should keep the bandwidth requirement very low. There are still a lot of bugs and known efficiency problems, but it is now basically playable with multiple players.

4th Coming update - Sacrates
Noticed over at Lorien's Love Shack (Gagames) That Vircom has made an update to the 4th Coming page on the beta release etc. I took a look at this game and the graphics don't look bad at all. Are there any chimps out there playing this at the moment? I would like to get a little feedback on that.

After carefully studying the feedback we've been receiving from both licensees and players, we've decided to stagger our release process into two stages. The first stage, called Base Release, is immiment and will feature the game in its actual stages, with few modifications and increased stability. The second stage, called Official Release, is scheduled at some point in the future (date to be determined) and will include several additional features which could not be included in the Base Release. These features tentatively include: the female puppet (actually, that one's certain and urgent), a chat box (with full scrollback/recall), a secure trading box, character shadows, additional skills, spells, NPCs & items, a *lot* more space to play in, etc. Even after the Official Release, however, you can expect more improvements on the game. Unlike standard "commercial" games, T4C is and will always be a work in progress. We will constantly improve it to keep everyone's interest alive. So the Base Release is the first of many release stages and you can expect that we'll add as much stuff as possible to the game, as soon as possible.

When the Base Release occurs, that will mean there will be a good number of T4C servers outside of Vircom's and the few existing beta servers currently available. At that point, you will have to contact the one nearest you (we'll give you their locations on a world map) and check out how much they charge for their service. Vircom does not interfere with its licensees in this regard.

Gamespot UK on BG - Sacrates
Looks like Gamespot UK has a nice little Preview of Baldur's Gate up with an Interview with Ray also. Hopefully all these Previews will be converting over to Reviews very soon! Thanks Desslock for that.

Baldur's Gate is one of several roleplaying epics in the making, and also the most ambitious. Unlike Diablo, Baldur's Gate is a more traditional game, albeit one with state-of-the-art graphics and a friendly real-time combat system. Underneath, however, it has all the hallmarks of a classic roleplaying game. Indeed, here is a game to rival the Ultima series ­ it has all the depth (an intricate storyline with over 100 sub-plots), the epic size (a 10,000-screen area to adventure in), the character interaction and, with hundreds of weapons, spells, items and monsters, a lot to get to grips with.

Even more Review goodness - Sacrates
Just one more Thief Review done by the guys over at NGO. So without further ado i'll toss the blurb your way.

Sound is also an important element. Not only can guards see the thief, they can hear him. Different surfaces make different amounts of noise. As the thief, the player can walk softly over resonant surfaces like metal plating and move swiftly over carpeted floors. Of course, sound works both ways; aspiring thieves can also hear the guards come and whip out the black jack. Thief has perhaps one of the best implementations of hardware accelerated sound design. Players will definitely want one of the many DirectSound3D cards to get the most out of the game.

More Thiefly Reviews - Sacrates
Woohoo, the full reviews are coming in on this bad boy. As soon as our copy arrives here at AW you can be sure we'll be smackin this bastard on the ass too. Gamespot is dishin out some lovin...check it.

You'll quickly learn to love the darkness in Thief, even if that darkness makes everything look a little dreary. At other times you'll find some wall textures that are a little too loud, while some of the level architecture feels a bit too plain. But those moments are the exception: For the most part, the environments in Thief are enormous, fantastic, and yet plausible. Ruined cities, lost temples, gaudy mansions, almost-high-tech factories, foreboding cathedrals and much more help maintain a cohesive and creative visual design. And with its smooth frame rate, subtly beautiful lighting effects, and a host of foes whose movements are startlingly lifelike thanks to motion capture, Thief's look will quickly grow on you.

Redguard love - Sacrates
Talking about Yin and Yang with this game?! Some Review chimps want to bitch slap it, others want to make love to it. Either way, Psychogamer got their paws on it and enjoyed it.

Redguard has truly lived up to its billing. It is a unique role play adventure game that combines a great storyline with good graphics and excellent CD quality sound and music.

The games main storyline puts the gamer in the role of Cyrus, a man who is searching for his lost sister. Although this may not sound like much of a story, it is. There are many plots and subplots that develop and reveal themselves throughout the game and there are a number of diverse and interesting characters. You are also challenged by various different puzzles which vary in difficulty.

Graphically, Thief isn't quite on par with games like Unreal or Half-Life. Although the game does still look good, it's actually a nice change to see developers putting new control methods into an engine before throwing in effects that only hardware manufactures and the most vehement fans of eye-candy demand
