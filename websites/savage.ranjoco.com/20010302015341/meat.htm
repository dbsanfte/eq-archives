Beef - Beef is well, Beef.  You can get different cuts of beef for different type of dish you are going to make.  

Kobe beef is well known worldwide as one of the most expensive beef you can buy.  Kobe Beef is a legendary delicacy of Japan, a type of beef that is so well marbled that it goes right off the charts for Prime grading in any other country. The meat ends up looking like it has been left out in the snow because of the intensiveness of the white fat marbling, rivals foie gras for richness and caloric content, and costs an obscene amount, often $300 a pound or more for the real thing from Japan.  Kobe beef comes from a breed of cattle called Wagyu. In order to earn the designation/appellation of "Kobe Beef", the Wagyu beef must come from Kobe, Japan, and meet rigid production standards imposed in that prefecture.  The "Wagyu beef" designation can legally be applied to the meat from any cattle of the Wagyu breed; it's a genetic thing, not a place appellation or a reference to how the cattle were raised and fed. This breed is genetically predisposed to intense marbling, and produces a higher percentage of oleaginous, unsaturated fat than any other breed of cattle known in the world.  How does Wagyu beef taste? If it's cooked wrong, lousy. Bland. Not too flavorful. Kind of boring. If you cook it right? Awesome. Beef foie gras. Smooth, velvety, incomparably sweet with a subtle tang of savor that lingers on the palate like a rare perfume. It's a Japanese thang, I guess, and a Westerner used to eating a huge plate of aged beef (which is also something I adore) might not be able to fully appreciate the subtlety of Wagyu.  And true Kobe beef? Overrated. At ten times the price because the cow was raised on expensive land eating expensive Japanese grain and beer, the quality is not noticeably better than ordinary Wagyu beef that grades out to super-prime. About those legendary cow massages? It's in part because they don't have enough room to exercise in a normal paddock. American and Australian raised Wagyu cattle that get the oleaginous feed and a well designed exercise program grade out just fine, and I doubt even the most discerning palate could tell the difference if the grade was the same. 
Mishima beef is from the small island of Mishima in Japan.  The beef is supposedly even more tastier then Kobe Beef, and costs even more.  I think Mishima beef is the root of Wagyu beef.
Veal comes from calves that's three weeks to six months old.  The best type is the special fed veal which is also known as milk-fed that's about eight weeks old.  The meat is usually light in color and is a nutrient dense meat which is low in fat and calories.
Beef cheek meat is very tough because of all the chewing cows do.  The meat needs to be cooked for a long time, but the result is an extremely tender and sweet tasting meat.
Oxtail is one of the most economical and most flavorful cuts of meat, and one that takes well to marinating for days in a hearty mixture of red wine, herbs, and vegetables. The longer you marinate the mixture, the more flavorful it will be, but be sure it marinates at least 3 days. Oxtail is also a fatty cut -- give yourself plenty of time to allow the stew to cook and then cool, so all the fat can be skimmed off. Serve this with thick noodles in warmed soup bowls, accompanied by a tossed salad, and of course, a robust red wine.
Beef tongue is an acquired taste, which I hope I will never acquire.  I think they also serve cured tongue in a deli.  The fresh tongue is used in other cuisine.  I've seen recipes for Indonesian, Hungarian, and Mexican dishes.

Pork - Pork, aka the other white meat is not really a white meat.  But I don't really care.  Here's a list of some pork products featured on Iron Chef.

Tokyo X is a breed of pig with marbled meat.  The hog has the combined features of three breeds from China, England and the United States.  It is also quite expensive, perhaps it's like Kobe Beef but for people who don't eat beef.  The actual pig itself looks like a breed of Chinese black pig which probably contributes the fatness to the Tokyo X breed. 
Black Pig, I believe originated from Asia.  They are very large and high in fat content .  With its fantastic lop ears it is often known as the ‘elephant pig’ because of the similarity the newly born piglets have to a very small black elephant. Viewed from behind, after just being born, their huge ears and little straight tail certainly make one think of a baby elephant.  Despite its size it is both docile and an excellent mother capable of rearing large litters and producing excellent bacon for the table. Really much more of a bacon pig than the smaller rounder and much chunkier pork pigs.
Suckling Pig is usually used for roasting.  And usually the whole pig too.  Personally, I've not had any.  I believe they cost about $165 each, for a 40 lbs. size.  The meat is probably quite tender.
Kinka (Ching Wah) Pig is a breed of pig from China and It is small in size, which produce a good textured pork.  The famous Ching Wah ham is named after the pork and is very expensive if it is made authentically. 
Spare Ribs, Pork Belly, Ham, and Bacon are other pork products used in Iron Chef.
