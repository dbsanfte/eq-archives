Fruits - Fruits are good for you and taste good too.  Many of my favorite fruits are not available here.  But recently, New York has proven to be a good source of exotic tropical fruits.  Fruits are used in some Iron chef battles.  Common fruits used include apple, banana, melon, peach, pear, pineapple, orange, strawberry, and watermelon.  
Mango, member of the cashew family (Anacardiaceae), one of the most important and widely cultivated fruits of the tropical world, considered indigenous to eastern Asia.  This is one of the favorite fruits from my childhood.  There are a couple of different types with various sizes available.  The Mexican mango is the type that is available here in the states.  It was hard to find mango ten years ago, so we are very lucky.  The fruit is very sweet and juicy with a large hairy seed.  If you never had mango before, go out to your local grocery store and buy a case.  
Papaya is also a tropical fruit.  The tree looks like a palm tree, but due to its shorter stature, the papaya is harvested more easily. Papaya is harder to find in the supermarkets here.  The ones I've seen in the states are really small compared to ones that I've seen in Taiwan.  The seeds look like Salmon Roe, but are dark brown in color.  The fruit has a scent that I do not like much, but I really  enjoy the special Papaya drink.  Just blend papaya, milk and sugar together and it is delicious.
Pickled Plum (umeboshi in Japanese) is a traditional Japanese item.  Plum is eaten every day in Japan and it is used to make sweet plum wine and liquor.  An umeboshi is a sour, pickled plum, soaked in vinegar. Many Japanese believe the umeboshi is a healthy and sustaining food. During Japan's feudal period, samurai warriors would eat an umeboshi each day to maintain their energy during a battle.

Vegetable - vegetables are a good source of vitamins if cooked properly.  There are quite a few colorful vegetables available in Southeast Asia.  Many has appeared in Ironchef.  There are also more common type of vegetables used which includes asparagus, bean sprouts, bell pepper, broccoli, cabbage, carrot, chestnut, corn, cucumber, eggplant, green onion, leeks, lettuce, mushroom, potato, pumpkin, rice, soy beans, spinach, sweet potato, tomato, turnip, and yam.
Avocado is also known as alligator pear, a tree native to the Western Hemisphere from Mexico south to the Andean regions.  The fruit is exceedingly variable in shape, size, and color, with certain Mexican races no larger than hen's eggs and those of other races sometimes 1 to 2 kg (2 to 4 pounds) in weight. The form varies from round to pear-shaped with a long, slender neck, and the color ranges from green to dark purple. The single large seed, with two cotyledons, is round to conical. The fruit's outer skin is sometimes no thicker than that of an apple and sometimes is coarse and woody in texture. It protects the greenish or yellowish flesh, buttery in consistency and with a rich, nutty flavor. In some varieties the flesh contains as much as 25 percent of unsaturated oil. It is often eaten in salads, whence the name salad fruit. Mashed avocado is the principal ingredient of guacamole, a characteristic sauce in Mexican cuisine. Avocados provide thiamine, riboflavin, and vitamin A.
Bamboo shoots came from the young sprout of bamboo trees.  The inner soft tissue is a delicacy and often used in soups or stew.  Bamboo can be eaten fresh or processed by canning and freezing. Fresh bamboo has a crisp, sweet flavor. It is used in Oriental cooking as an extender because it will take on the flavor of whatever it is cooked with. A bamboo shoot has about the same nutritional value as an onion and is a good source of fiber.  Most of the bamboo in the U.S. is canned although some fresh bamboo is beginning to reach the public. Gourmets report that once a person has tasted fresh bamboo it is difficult to switch back to canned even though fresh is much more expensive. Generally the U.S. public has indicated a preference for fresh foods--asparagus--for example, and is willing to pay a good deal more to get fresh vegetables. The price for fresh bamboo on the West Coast is about $3 per pound. It is considerably more expensive on the East Coast.  I remembered when I was younger in Taiwan, we would drive for hours to pick fresh "winter bamboo shoots" .  The taste is of course unmatched by canned variety.  I haven't had fresh bamboo shoots since then.  I hope I can get some next time I go to New York. 
Daikon in Japanese means "big root", also known as a type of Japanese white radish.  In Taiwan, we call it "white carrot".  I believe there are several different types of daikon in Japan that comes in various color and sizes.  The taste is a combination of sweetness and bitterness.  It is very refreshing in soups.  
Lotus root is strangely shaped as you can see from the picture.  It is less consumed than lotus seeds (hmmm, I think I should go buy a can now).  The inside of the root has many holes that may resemble Swiss cheese.  The root is also quite fibrous, as if it is made of thousands of strands.  You can make a  delicious drink with this.  Boil sliced lotus root in water and then add brown sugar to taste.  It has some medicinal properties and is used as a Chinese herb like the lotus seeds.
Mushroom is in a different section due to the number of different mushrooms used. 
Taro is a yummy root vegetable.  I believe it causes itchiness if you handle the root without wearing gloves.  The outside of the root is brown while inside is usually purple or white with purple dots.  The texture is like potato while it has some sweet taste to it.  It is a very versatile item, and can be used in soup, stew, or ice cream.  Taro ice cream is actually very good and you might be able to get them from Asian grocers.  I thing a type of Hawaiian dish called Poi uses taro as the main ingredients.  One of the famous Chinese dish I remember eating was made to look like Ice cream except it is actually very hot, with steams that gives the appearance of coldness.  
Wakame seaweed, not to be mistaken with nori seaweeds used in sushi, usually comes dried in a plastic bag.  They are usually hard and thick as opposed to soft nori.  I usually combine this and bonito flakes to make a light stock for Miso soup.  I believe it is also used to make light appetizers and soupy dishes.  Sauteed wakame seaweed can also be served as one of the dishes in Asian cuisine.
Winter melon is the actual translation from Chinese and Japanese characters.  I don't know if the harvest is indeed in the Winter, but they do grow to be large like pumpkins.  It is usually used in soup or as the soup container itself (frequently in Cantonese cooking)!  In Taiwan, they combine blocks winter melon and brown sugar as a way of brewing sweet "Winter melon tea".  Also one of my favorite drink.
