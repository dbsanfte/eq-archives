Fish (part 1)
Best Viewed in 800 X 600

     
(left to right) - ayu (sweetfish), fresh carp, 500 gram of tarako (cod roe) at 3700 Yen

Ayu (sweetfish) - A river fish, sometimes also known as "aroma fish" in Chinese.  In Japan, the fishermen use a type of bird to catch these fishes.  They would tie a knot on the bird's neck so that the birds can't eat what they caught.  Somewhat opposite of foie gras.  Here's a picture of an  expensive looking Ayu themed meal in Japan.
Carp - Another river fish, sometimes also known as "grass fish" in Chinese.  Correct me if I'm wrong, but I believe they are in the same family as catfish.  I do not care much for this fish as it does have some fishy taste.  This is a common river fish that can be purchased in any Asian region.
Cod Roe (Tarako) - Basically eggs extracted from a cod during the right season.  It is usually dried and packed in a pine box.  I believe in that form, the most popular way to cook it is to roast it and then sliced and serve with draft beer.  My favorite roe will always be sliced shark's roe with sweet mayo.

            
(left to right) - grilled teriyaki anago (Conger eel), unagi (Freshwater eel), big eel, and plain grilled unagi (freshwater eel)

Eel - Of the order Anguilliformes, There are about 500 species of eel.  Eels are slender, elongated, usually scaleless fish with long dorsal and anal fins that are continuous around the tail tip. Some, the snipe eels, have long jaws; and others, the snake eels, have pointed tails that enable them to burrow backward into sand or mud. Young eels are transparent, leaflike larvae totally unlike the adults. Eels are found in all seas, from coastal regions to the mid-depths. One group, the freshwater eels of the family Anguillidae, live in freshwater but return to the sea to breed. Congers  or anago are scaleless eels with large heads, large gill slits, wide mouths, and strong teeth. They are usually grayish to blackish, with paler bellies and black-edged fins. Carnivorous fish found in all oceans, sometimes in deep water, conger eels may grow to a length of about 1.8 meters (6 feet).  Personally, I never had this before, but I imagine this would taste the same as freshwater eel.  Freshwater eel or unagi is very common in any Japanese restaurants here in the states.  It is usually pre-grilled with teriyaki sauce and used in sushi or unadon (eel over rice).  I recommend trying this if you never had it before.  It is delicious well worth the price.

           
(left to right) - frogfish in the tank, top view of frogfish, cleaning a frogfish, and frogfish stew

Frogfish - They are quite interesting and not pleasant to look at for most people.  I believe they are also called angler fish.  The thingy on its forehead is used to lure other creatures close to its striking area.  The proper way to clean this fish is to hang it on the hook, gut it, and let the gravity remove all the innards.  This is to prevent a bitter aftertaste of the flesh that can result for gutting it on the cutting board.  I've never had this before nor have I ever seen it in any restaurant.  I'd rather own it as a pet instead of eating it.

       
(left to right) - fugu (blowfish), hamo eel, and managatsuo

Fugu (blowfish, puffer fish) - The Chinese name for this fish is "river pig" because they make pig sounds.  This is an infamous fish that carries a poisonous toxin.  This fish has long been praised in Japan as the most delicious of all fishes, it has also been feared, as improper preparation will kill in a short time.  There is no known cure to this toxin.  I believe the Japanese royal family is not allowed to eat any dish made with fugu.  A fugu can fetch about $200 in a restaurant.  
Hamo eel - Hamo eel is not really a eel, but a very long fish.  The name is actually referring to the ones produced in a small region in Japan.  There are many other similar fish from other regions that is available in some Asian grocery stores.  I had this before, they are very easy to clean and they are very meaty.  It is quite good cut into sections and steamed or grilled with soy sauce.
Managatsuo - Also known as "harvest fish" in Japan, but better known as pomfret.  They have a light texture, thus often used as sashimi or stewed.
