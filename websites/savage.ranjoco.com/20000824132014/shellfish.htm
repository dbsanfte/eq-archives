Shellfish & Misc. Seafood

Abalone - Marine snails of the subclass Prosobranchia (class Gastropoda).  The shell has a row of holes on its outer surface.  Abalones are one-shelled snails found in warm seas worldwide.  The dishlike shell is perforated near one edge by a single row of small holes that become progressively filled during the animal's growth; the last five to nine holes remain open to serve as outlets for the snail's waste products.  The shell's lustrous, iridescent interior is used in the manufacture of ornaments.  The large muscular foot of the abalone is eaten as a delicacy in several countries.  They are quite expensive; canned abalone can run you about $50 per can.  Fresh abalones are even more expensive.

Aori Squid - Squid is a cephalopod.  They may be swift swimmers or part of the drifting sea life.  They range in size from about 1.5cm to more then 20m.  There are many different breeds and many different ways to cook them.  Dried squid is something I always remember eating.  They are usually roasted on top of an open flame and then pulled into strips before consuming.  They also sell squid snacks in pouches that can be eaten immediately.  This is a somewhat expensive snack.  visit the page at www.taiwansnacks.com if you want to buy some.  Squid ink is known to be used in the making of pasta.  A less known usage, in Iron Chef, is Squid ink ice cream.  Hmmmmm, I don't know about that.

Asyura Oyster - They are named after Asyura, a deity with many faces. The oysters naturally cluster together and hence appear to have multiple faces. Thus, the oyster's name.  Since the oysters grow in a mix of fresh and salt water, they are sweet and delicate and do not possess an overly fishy smell.  

Black Tiger Prawn - The prawns are black and have tiger stripes although they cook up to a bright red.  They are bigger than regular shrimp and have thicker shells.  They are harder to peel, too.  Tiger prawns feed primarily at night.  Their diet consists of mollusks, crustaceans, and polychaete worms.

Botan Shrimp - The botan shrimp is a fairly sweet shrimp and is good when eaten raw.  The product usually comes from Hokkaido, the northern Japanese province.  The scientific name seems to be Decapoda Pandalidae.  

Crayfish - Also called crawfish or crawdad.  They are closely related to the lobster. Over half of the more than 500 species occur in North America.  Nearly all live in fresh water, a few in brackish water or salt water.  It is also known as Ecrevisse in France.

Escargot - Famous French snail appetizers that can cost from $6 to $18  for half dozen.  The United States imports $300 million worth of snails annually. 98% of these come in cans. Until recently, the Europeans have monopolized the snail industry. Presently, the raising of snails has become an increasingly lucrative and popular business.

Hamaguri Clam - also known as Venus clam or Common Orient Clam, is found on nearly all Japan's shores.  These clams are at their best from winter to spring, and are eaten as sushi, or grilled.  They look like regular clams. 


Kenagi Crab - Another breed of crab from Japan.  I think "Kenagi" means hairy.  The pictured crab looks like it is meaty.  Perhaps when I go to Japan I can get a taste of all the different crabs they have to offer.  

Lobster - any of numerous marine crustaceans (order Decapoda) constituting the families Homaridae (or Nephropsidae), true lobsters; Palinuridae, spiny lobsters, or sea crayfish; Scyllaridae, slipper, Spanish, or shovel lobsters; and Polychelidae, deep-sea lobsters. All are marine and benthic (bottom-dwelling), and most are nocturnal. Females are ready to lay eggs when about five years old. Sperm are transferred from males to females in summer, but fertilization does not occur until spring. A female lays 3,000 or more eggs, which remain attached to her swimmerets until they hatch several months later. Unlike adults, the young, about 1 cm (0.4 in.) long, swim freely for about 12 days and then descend to the bottom, where they remain. Some lobsters may live for 50 years. Young lobsters are preyed upon especially by dogfish, skates, and cod. The principal enemy of the adult lobster is man.  Unlike true lobsters,  spiny lobsters(Palinuridae, pictured), so called because of their very spiny bodies, do not have large claws. Usually only the abdomen is eaten and is marketed as lobster tail. The antennae are strongly developed. Most species live in tropical waters.

Matsuba Crab - The Matsuba crabs are in fact a species of spider crab. Caught in the Japan Sea using dragnets, these crabs can be boiled, grilled or eaten raw, but they are best boiled plain to make the most of their natural flavor.  A Winter delicacy in Japan.  The long-legged crabs, are considered the king of winter dishes. A gourmet is content by eating just this crab with a glass of sake.

Mantis Shrimp - Stomatopods are predatory crustaceans that live in the shallow waters of tropical and subtropical seas. They are not closely related to shrimps or the other decapod crustaceans, although they are commonly known as "mantis shrimps" due to the raptorial appendages that they use to efficiently capture and subdue prey. These animals range in size from 1-2 cm to more than 30 cm in some Lysiosquilla sp., and are one of the most aggressive and pugnacious of all creatures. Some mantis shrimps are feared and loathed as notorious pests by reef aquarists, and some species may be so abundant in commercial shrimp beds that they become major predators of the shrimps.  Divers call mantis shrimp "thumb splitters." The impact from a blow from the claws and head is only slightly less than the impact of a .22 caliber bullet.  Mantis shrimp can grow up to about ten inches in length and are fast moving. They eat crabs, anemones, and fish, among other things, and if kept in an aquarium with other fish, they will eventually eat them all. They dig burrows and live in crevices in reefs and rocks.

Octopuss - Eight-armed cephalopod (octopod) mollusk of the order Octopoda.  One of my favorite sushi, which is one of the sushi that's usually cooked when served.  The typical octopus has a saccular body: the head is only slightly demarcated from the body and has large, complex eyes and eight contractile arms. Each arm bears two rows of fleshy suckers that are capable of great holding power. The arms are joined at their bases by a web of tissue known as the skirt, at the centre of which lies the mouth. The latter organ has a pair of sharp, horny beaks and a filelike organ, the radula, for drilling shells and rasping away flesh.  They also have ink sac that can be used for cooking.

Pen Shell Clam - Also known as razor shell clam, it supposedly has the texture of scallops and the taste of abalone.  The size of pen shell is about as large as an open palm.  It is usually prepared raw as sashimi or grilled plain.  They cost about 500 Yen a piece last time I checked.

Rock Crab - The Featured Theme for the Special New York Battle between Morimoto and Bobby Flay of Food Network.  I think Rock Crab is also another product from Maine.

Scallop - Also known as fan shell, it is a marine bivalve mollusk.  The large white muscular part of the shell is actually the part being eaten, as the rest of the body spoils really easily when harvest.  I think the name is also referring to that part of the fan shell.  Dried scallops are a gourmet item used in Asian cooking and can fetch high price.  I saw them for sale in Washington D.C. for about $100 a pound.

Sea Cucumber - one of the marine invertebrates, with soft cylindrical body and often warty, thus resembling a cucumber.  A number of sea cucumbers exude a toxin that is lethal to small animals but not to humans; South Sea islanders place sea cucumber juices in water to kill or stupefy fish.  Dishes that I've seen, usually have it stewed in a hot pot with other seafood.

Sea Urchin - The spiny critter doesn't really look edible.  But at any rate, it is the inside that's being used.  Also known as Uni in Japanese,  it has the consistency of yellow mud, and probably taste like it too.  Usually comes in a small wooden box and is very expensive depending on the grade.

Takaashi Crab - The largest crab in the world, which, when its legs are extended, measures more than three meters long!  I've never saw the episode that used this crab, but I heard that they had to roll the crabs out in a cart due to its massive size.  I've seen a smaller size crab that runs about 10,000 Yen (about $100) each.  A Japanese restaurant in Hokkaido that serves Takaashi crab charges 15,000 Yen per person (three people minimum).

Taraba Crab - Also known as king crab.  Probably similar to Alaskan King Crab. 

Watari Crab - Similar to blue crabs from the east coast here.  Except it is not blue.  Also knows as "swimming crab".
