Warwulfe's Shaman Guide

 

Welcome to my new Shaman guide.  I was once known as Dirtforke the Troll Shaman, but after a long hiatus from EQ I decided to try my hand at a Barbarian Shaman.  I'd like to share with people what I've learned, having played with Shaman of two of the races more or less exclusively since launch. 

 

I. What Race should I choose?

 

A.  Troll

 

Trolls kick arse.  I love em.  However, playing an Ogre or a Troll is truly a labor of love because you will be killed on sight all over the place.  (Truth to tell, though, this is not a major inconvenience, since you will have the option at later levels to kill those who formerly oppressed you with little consequence...)  Trolls have significant experience point penalties, the lowest wisdom possible overall, and terrible fire resistance.  This is offset by their regenerative abilities, infravision, and their good looks.  Trolls in my opinion look very sharp.  Big floppy ears, long pointy noses, and lots of chest detail down to that pot-belly.  Their facial expressions are often exotic and pure evil, and they look great when they run.  Their newbie area, the Innothule Swamp, (For levels 1-8) is extremely well laid out (Grobb is in an easy-to-find corner of the zone) and has very nice loot.

 

B.  Ogre

 

Ogres are also KOS all over.  They are huge and start with insanely high strength and stamina.  They also have good starting wisdom, and infravision.  It is rumored, and I tend to believe this from Ogres I have spoken with, that Ogres are immune to being stunned when they are facing their foe.  If this is so, then this is a very nice benefit as well.  If you want to get up close and personal with your foe, this is probably the class to choose. The experience penalty for Ogres is moderate, somewhere between Troll and Barbarian.  Their newbie area is fairly poor; the mobs spawn too far apart and infrequently, and Feerrot is fairly easy to get lost in, in my opinion.  I see more newbie Ogres in Innothule than in Feerrot. 

 

C.  Barbarian

 

This is probably the most popular of choices for beginning Shaman simply because they are not KOS in a majority of locations.  Their Wisdom is the highest of all the primitive races, and their other statistics are well rounded.  They also are not hideously ugly (er, from a humanoid standpoint) and can get decent prices from vendors when they shop around.  Barbarians are blind as bats - they have no infravision, and life in the dark is tough going till level 9 when the Shaman gets Serpent Sight.  Their experience penalty is very low, perhaps some 5 or 10% in comparison with the equivalent classed human (if humans could be Shamans).  The Barbarian newbie area, Everfrost, is a maze just waiting to get new Shaman lost.  The beasts here are plentiful, however, and carry excellent loot.  Also, Everfrost is a massive zone with a wide variety of creatures.  It is possible for a Barb Shaman to stay here for a very long time, even into their 20's and 30's. 

 

II. Places to Fight

 

Each Shaman of each race will often take their own path of convenience till level 10 or so.  From there, there are countless places to gain experience in the world of Norrath.  It's not really until level 30 or so where one starts to see the overcrowding.  Yes, Oasis and South Karana always have 80+ people in them, but one of the reasons for this is that people don't know where else to hunt.  Many also consider dungeons too dangerous and trainy to be worth their time.  This, in my opinion, is a big mistake. Two of the best, relatively safest dungeons to hunt in are Unrest and Cazic Thule.  They are both outdoor dungeons, so a Shaman can keep Spirit of Wolf on themselves to make a quick escape if things go bad.  Other players in the zones know this, and so you will find yourself valuable if only for that reason alone.  Most Druids are too busy trying to kite their way to glory in the Karanas, so take advantage of this and hang out in these dungeons.  Here is the path that I have taken with Warwulfe, now a 37th Barbarian Shaman:

 

Level 1-5:  Everfrost newbie area.

Level 6-9:  Everfrost plains.

Level 9-14:  Misty Thicket and Beholder Gorge (Gorge of King Xorbb)

Level 14-19:  Some Oasis, but mostly East Karana and the Gorge.

Level 19-29:  Unrest, Totemic Armour quests.

Level 29-37:  More Totemic Armour quests, Cyclops, Giant Skeletons, and Hill Giants in Rathe Mountains, and Cazic Thule dungeon.

 

III.  Armour Class vs. Wisdom, and Shaman melee.

 

Good looking, high AC armor such as Totemic is extremely nice, but should a Shaman even wear it when better Wisdom items abound?  Your play style will affect how much of it you choose to acquire and wear, and that is certainly an individual decision.  However, I have found AC to be extremely important in many situations, whether soloing or in a group. 

 

With or without an Enchanter in the group, I can cast a slow-spell on a mob the group isn't currently concentrating on and let it beat on me till the Enchanter mesmerizes it, or the tanks finish off the current mob and switch to it.  This generally only works well with blue mobs, but it still keeps pressure off the tank and can ease the Cleric's healing load.  Don't get me wrong:  high AC isn't some miraculous life-saver, but it does and can make all the difference when it's down to you, the mob, and your big stick. 

 

Wisdom, at least for me, comes in a very close second.  Many Wisdom equipment pieces simply can't be justified due to their extremely low AC and relatively negligible Wis increase.  Also, there is an absolute deluge of different kinds of Wisdom equipment out there.  If you only wear the Totemic pieces that can be seen (I.E., skip the cloak), you still have more than 10 slots to equip nice Wisdom items in.  It takes time, but 150+ Wisdom can easily be attained before level 40 by any Shaman without sacrificing much AC at all.  Enchanter-made earrings, rings, and veils can account for up to 25 Wis by themselves.  In any case, ask yourself how much AC you are willing to sacrifice for a little extra mana here and there.  Most Shaman can afford to be a little conservative in their mana usage in a group combat situation, so with or without Wisdom items, there will be few times where you should be "OOM". 

 

Many people say that Shaman are the best, out of all the casting classes, at pure melee.  This is correct in a sense, but only because our spell line after 29 becomes increasingly well suited for close-quarters combat.  We can slow the mob down, and enhance the speed of our own swings, as well as the speed of our pets.  We can cast DoTs, and we can buff our stats and hit points.  Finally, we can wear plate armor and even stun or interrupt our opponent with the Slam ability.  All of this means that we are perfectly capable of standing up to a creature that most would flee in terror from or try to kite.  We're not super-beings by any stretch, it's just a style of combat that we are best at.  We're not the best kiters, we're not the best tanks, we're not the best healers, and we're not the best nukers.  We are the best at besting a single opponent, up close and personal, in prolonged combat. 

 

IV. Dang, I'm buff!

 

Some people never buff.  They say buffs aren't significant.  I think that in some scenarios they are correct, but tell me:  What does one do in a group when there is a Magician casting Malise, and an Enchanter casting Shiftless?  The fact is, there are other classes that can do many of our group-related jobs almost as well, if not better, than we can.  The only uniqueness that a Shaman can really cling to in a group is their buffs.  For this reason alone, they are significant, but there are other reasons as well:

 

Strength increases Attack, which in turn increases overall damage (by 1-2 points or more depending on how much str), and increases the tendency to hit for maximum damage.

 

Dexterity basically makes things proc more. (NOTE:  Proc is short for Process.  Some weapons and special abilities have a built-in effect to them that PROCESSES randomly;  Proc is short for this.)  It makes weapons proc, and it makes critical hits and other special abilities proc more often.  It may have other effects in combat as well, but Verant is not forthcoming as to what those other effects might or might not be.  Put it on the Warriors and/or others with weapons that proc.  Rumors abound that this affects Dual Wield abilities also, but I don't lend credence to them just yet. 

 

Stamina may seem worthless, but it adds hit points, and they add up. At higher levels it does confer a significant hit point bonus, and it stacks on all other hit point buffs. Sure, Clerics have the best hit point buffs in the form of the Center/Holy Armor line, but your Stamina line will add to these, and at level 34 you get the Talisman line of spells. These are very nice HP buffs. At level 34 your Stamina buff will usually average a 50-75 HP increase. At level 49, 100+ hp can be added by using your best Stamina buff.  At level 35, my best Stamina buff gave me exactly 50 hit points.

 

Agility is ok.  It adds AC, if nothing else.  I have to admit it's hard to say if this buff really helps people avoid hits.  Perhaps it has a greater effect on Monks.  I cast this much less often than other stat buffs simply because a person can get more AC from my AC buff, or a Cleric's AC buff, than from an Agility buff. 

 

Some buffing tips:

 

1.  Use discretion with buffs, and work out a buffing pattern.  This way you can work out a buff 'cycle'.  Always start with a full bar of mana.  I usually only buff myself and other casters with Spirit of Wolf.  Then, I buff everyone else.  When my Spirit of Wolf starts to blink, I know that all other buffs are going to fade.  Time to rebuff everyone.  This prevents my teammates from having to tell me they need a buff.  This makes them happy, and it makes me happy because I have a handle on when I will have mana, and when I will not.  Always tell your party that you are 'refreshing' their buffs, so a brief break from combat can be taken. 

 

2.  Some people want certain buffs all the time - like Quickness.  Quickness is a buff that simply cannot be cast at all times because of its mana drain and short lifespan.  It is obviously meant to be cast during combat, because it casts quickly and has practically no recovery time.  Politely explain this, and they will usually understand.  :-)

 

3.  There are times when you will have to cut back on certain buffs.  The best and most indispensable buffs for tanks are Quickness, Strength, and Dexterity.  Stamina and Agility are important, but are not nearly as effective.  If you are the only healer or caster in a group, you will need to prioritize certain buffs and let others fall by the wayside. 

 

V.  Shaman Summary

 

Shamans are a powerful, versatile class.  They can be become a very specialized class in higher-level group situations, similar to Enchanters in many respects.  While Enchanters can buff the mind and meditative powers of the casting class, Shaman buff the physique and add regenerative powers to the melee class.  Play your Shaman as such, and you will be very successful.  Much like real life, your reputation and desirability hang in the balance of your contribution to a party.  Direct damage is an incredible waste of mana for a Shaman, and you should always regret having to use it.  Melee classes need every boost they can get, as it is their lives, not their mana, that is at stake.  Should they loose their lives, yours will almost surely follow with them. Go now, make them invincible!
