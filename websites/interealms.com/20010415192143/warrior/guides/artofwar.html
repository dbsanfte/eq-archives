Tactical Art of War

By Tsebach Silverwolf

 

Fellow travelers of Norrath, hail and well met!

Long and hard do we quest the lands of our world, searching out adventure, treasure, and fame. Though glory is what we seek, the most important of tasks are often not glorious in the least. With that thought in mind I present to you the Warrior: simple, strong, a master of a select few disciplines; always the first in, and usually the one that never comes out. However, it is this staple occupation that can provide victory or ruin to a campaigning party.

My objective with this guide is not to provide a be-all-end-all path towards becoming a lean mean fighting machine. I will not discuss strategies on how to level quicker, what monsters to fight, what race to choose in creating a warrior, or what statistics are most important. If this information is what you seek, there are plenty of other guides out there that deal with these subjects.

My humble goal is to simply share some of the observations and conclusions that I have stumbled over in my year or so of playing this wonderful class. Almost all of the suggestions that I will make are to improve the skills of the "player", not the numeric "stats" of the character. (An obvious exception being when I discuss equipment choices). Another trend that the reader may notice is that I almost exclusively refer to the Warrior as being in a group. This is because I feel that this is exactly where a Warrior should be. Warriors do not solo past the newbie levels (which I deem as around 1-7, in which there really is no differentiation between the classes). Once the specific traits of each class start to become apparent, it is time for the Warrior to settle into his role in the group.

Thus the Warrior’s role in said group is essentially divided into three sections: Pulling, dealing meelee damage, and taking any damage. So, I divide this writing as such, with an additional beginning section on setting up the User Interface to give the Warrior an added edge. That being said, let’s begin!

The User Interface:

Most people take the interface for granted, accepting what is given to them, occasionally changing one or two key mappings for ease of use. However, how you set up your access to the various abilities you own can drastically effect your prowess in battle. In my two years of exploring Norrath I have divised a keyboard layout that allows me to keep my eyes on the battle, with every key accessible from one position on the keyboard.

First of all: the Auto Attack button. Everyone (I can’t stress this enough) should remap this button. Every time you see a newbie corpse laying on the ground in front of a guard, you can pretty much be assured that they went to talk to the guard but didn’t hit the <enter> key first. Soon as they pressed that <A> they attacked that guard, and guards don’t like that. Which key do you use? Well, that’s really up to you. My strategy for remapping my keys was to arrange them as close to the directional arrows as much as possible. When using a mouse with the right hand, the left hand as almost always at those arrows, for walking forward, backward, and sidestepping (when the right mouse button is held down). I chose to remap the Auto Attack to <Numpad 0/INS>. Keeping my middle finger to hit the forward and back arrows, I can trigger my attack by simply using my first finger to hit the key.

Next are the user assignable hotbuttons. Once I realized the value of having the Auto Attack so close the the directional arrows, these became self-evident. I remapped my six hotbuttons from the keys <1> - <6> to <Numpad 1> - <Numpad 6>. So, again, everything I need is within easy reach of a finger while I am at the directional keys. When Verant added four more buttons to each bank, I just added <Numpad 7> - <Numpad 9> as well as the <foreslash> key that sits at the top of the number pad. To switch from bank to bank is a simple task of holding the right <shift> key with my pinkie and hitting the number on the number pad of the bank I want.

While this may seem obvious and trivial, the end result is that my right hand is always on my mouse, my eyes are always on the screen, and I never have to search for a key during combat.

"Come Hither Yon Beastie!" (a.k.a. Pulling)

Until your Warrior reaches the mid-high levels, you will find yourself often nominated for the task of running out there, kicking some evil-nasty in the shins, and bringing him back to the rest of the group to finish off. This makes sense: You have the most hitpoints and, unless you have been neglecting to equip yourself properly, the highest Armor Class. In short, you stand the best chance of making it back alive. As I said before, this holds true until the innate abilities of some other classes make them a better candidate, such as Monks (the famous Feign Death pull), Rogues (because of their thiefly abilites of sneak, hide, and the anti-taunt), and Rangers (due to their prowess with a bow as well as the ability to snare and track). Also, the Warrior’s pulling efficacy is less noticeable outdoors, where perhaps a speedy Druid or Bard would be better suited to the task.

So, you mainly have to deal with bringing back creatures through the labyrinthine corridors of Norrath’s many dungeons. This can prove to be a very daunting task, and really the only way to get good at it (I still only consider myself mildy profficient) is to practice, practice, practice. However, I may be able to provide you with some useful tips...

The very first thing you should do is become familiar with the territory. If you know your way around, that only improves your chances of getting back to the group intact. Every wrong turn means more precious seconds by yourself with a hostile monster at your heels. When you head out from the staging area to pull a mob, try to remember which way you’ve turned, look for landmarks, and try to replicate your path on the way back. When in an unfamiliar place, try only making left turns for the first few pulls (making only right turns on the way back). Then reverse it: right turns out, left turns coming back. Once you are more comfortable, try alternating every turn (first take a left, then a right, the a left, etc.). Try to remember what the last turn was, then do the opposite on the way back. Of course, soon you should be able to simply know where you are by visual recognition, so having to follow your "path of bread crumbs" back should not be an issue after the first few pulls or so.

Now, once you have found your victim, the next thing you should do is let your party know that a mob is incoming (remember: "Life is great when we communicate!"). This gives them time to prepare, and a Cleric can have you targetted and a heal on the way as soon as you arrive.

You are very likely to take a lot of damage while pulling a mob, especially once you get past the mid levels, where the monsters start getting disproportionally more difficult compared to your level. One way to decrease the amount of damage is to pull with a bow. Any distance you put between yourself and your target when you initiate the encounter gives you a head start, which means fewer smacks in the back of the head. Also, I don’t know if this is true or not, but when pulling with a bow, you seem to bring less surrounding mobs with your target. For example, while pulling from the first room in Infected Paw (where three gnolls spawn), if I went right up to one of them to pull, I invariably brought all three. However, when pulling with a bow from a short distance away, about half the time I only brought two. Again, I haven’t clinically proven this yet, but it seems to be a valid conclusion from my personal experiences.

Another way to put a little distance between you and the mob you are pulling is to jump occasionally as you run. Jumping gives you a short burst of speed, and every blow that is avoided by distance helps. Just remember to ask for that Invigor when you get back to the group, as jumping eats away stamina almost as fast as a hastened Wurmslayer. J

Sometimes, as a puller, you are required to make a sacrifice of yourself to save the group. The pathing in some of the dungeons is atrocious, and you will often find yourself bringing far more than your group can handle. In these occasions, it is best to try to run in the opposite direction of your party, towards a zone if at all possible (be sure to shout that Train warning). You will probably die, but if you are far enough away the baddies will return to their spawn points and leave the rest of your group alone. You will be awarded with your comrades admiration, but it also leaves you in a better position to get your corpse back, since there are up to 5 other alive and ready people in a position to help. The worst thing that can happen is for a full group to have to break in to a zone, naked and helpless, to retrieve their corpses, so try to avoid that at all costs.

Now that you have brought that monster back to your group, the real fun begins...

"It Slices, Dices, and Juliennes..." (or, dealing damage the Warrior way)

It is a common misconception (usually within the Warrior circles themselves) that Warriors are veritable whirlwinds of death in combat. While we are no slouches in the martial arts, we are difinitely not on top. Strictly talking about meelee, Monks surpass us in raw damage, and Rogues are about on par with the copious amounts of damage done by backstabbing. Throw in the Wizard’s jawdropping direct-damage spells, and a Necromancer’s DoT’s, and we are pushed even farther down the line. This does not mean that we are useless as attackers, far from it (what we do still goes a long way towards dropping that bad guy), because the damage a Warrior does is for a specific purpose... which I’ll get in to later. First off, let’s look at how a Warrior can maximize his/her effectiveness.

It was once thought that a Warrior should concentrate his or her time and energy into one or two types of weapons (the Ruins of Kunark Official Strategy Guide even supports this). Having learned the hard way, I can attest that this is just plain wrong. We are meant to be masters of weaponry, ALL weaponry, evinced by the fact that we are one of the few classes that can use every weapon skill. Work on each and every skill as you rise through the ranks (yes, even 2-hand blunt, as horrible as it is...). As I said, I learned the hard way. I focused on two-handed slashing, as did many Warriors. After all, when weapons such as the mighty Mithril Two-handed Sword exist, what more could you need? Enter the Wurmslayer: here I am in posession of a one-handed weapon that outpaces many of the two-handers out there, and my skill is just horrible. So, I began the long trek towards advancing the skill. I have finally maxed it for my level, but it was not easy, and in many cases not fun. I have also begun working on every other skill as well. Right now the Wurmslayer is my weapon of choice, but you never know what will be discovered next, and I refuse to be left in the dust again. Just talk to any high level monk who didn’t practice thier blunt skills, and ask them how they felt when Kunark came out...

By the way, on the topic of two-handed blunt... This is commonly known as the worst skill to work on, since the weapons are so incredibly slow. However, there is a solution: the Ketchata Koro Mis. It is a two-handed blunt staff that drops in Kunark, and has a delay of 30, making it the second fastest two-handed blunt out there. You can find them everywhere, as they are a common drop. I bought one for 5gp (yes, gold pieces) in Greater Faydark in order to start practicing.

Being able to use the latest and greatest weaponry is always nice, however there are more reasons to be able to wield a wide variety of weapons. For example, taunting is much easier when you can dish out damage quickly and consistently, calling for Duel Wielding two swift swords. On the other end of the spectrum, a high damage/high delay weapon is more desireable when dealing with a mob that has a damage shield, since you want to dole out the most damage with the least amount of contacts.

Another "weapon" not be discarded is the humble shield. For smaller races, using a shield is the only way a Warrior can bash an opponent. The stun component of the bash skill is invaluable when dealing with casters, especially those that can heal themselves. Also, bash becomes a (less effective) version of Snare in the absence of a Druid or Ranger, stopping or slowing a running mob from reaching reinforcements.

In order to speed up the time it takes bring a skill up to max, get a few items to increase your dexterity. The combination of a Grotesque Mask and an Executioner’s Hood adds a total of 16 to dexterity, and (at least on the Innoruuk server) can be bought for less than 200pp for both.

A final topic I would like to address is the choosing of a proper off-hand weapon. There has been much debate on the subject, and I have tested the many different theories and found which one works best. The central idea to it all is that the two weapons do not act independently. The only time you use your secondary weapon is when a successful "roll" for Duel Wield occurs. The only time that Duel Wield is "rolled" is when you make an attack with your primary weapon. And, of course, the delay on that primary weapon is what determines how often you attack with it. Follow that line of thinking back, and you realize that the delay on the secondary weapon is meaningless unless it is greater than the delay on your primary. Each time a weapon is used, it becomes inactive for an amount of time, denoted by the delay of the weapon. As long as your secondary weapon recycles quicker (or at the same time) as your primary weapon, it will always be available when Duel Wield succeeds. If your secondary weapon is slower, there is a chance that Duel Wield will roll successfully, but that weapon hasn’t recycled yet, resulting in a lost attack. So, to implement this theory, basically you want a secondary weapon with as high a damage as you can find, and with a delay no greater than your primary weapon.

So, you will recall that earlier I said a Warrior’s damage output is really for one specific task. Basically a Warrior is meant to take the brunt of the assault, but this cannot be done if the enemy has other targets in mind. The Warrior must present himself as the most imminent threat to a mob in order to do his job right, thus the need for weapons mastery. This brings me to the final section of this guide...

"Bah, ‘tis but a flesh wound! Come here, I’ll bite your ankles, I will!" (Tanking):

Here we are at the most important of the Warrior’s roles, our very reason for existance: getting hurt. And most of the time, getting hurt bad. We are the ones who stare that beast in the eye(s), attempt to ignore its foul breath, and keep it busy while the others do their thing.

One skill is central to this: Taunt. Keeping it maxed at all times is a must, and knowing when to use it is just as important. First of all, let’s look at how taunt works. Each mob has its Aggression (or Aggro) Table... basically a list of all the things it currently hates. It will always go for the target on the top of that list. Now, a person’s placement on that list is determined by many things: how close they are to the mob, how much damage they have done to the mob, how vulnerable of a target they are, and faction standing are just a few of what I am sure are many factors in evaluating aggro. What taunt does, if successful, is to take the Warrior and place him or her just a tad bit higher on the list than the person who was previously at the top. Taunt does nothing if you are currently at the top of that list. And, unless you back that taunt up with some damage, the next aggressive action by the person you just replaced will bring the mob right back to him or her.

Your responsibility as a Warrior is to keep yourself at the top of that list, for all mobs, at all times. This requires a little effort, because you in your gleaming metal armor are a far less enticing treat than that frail looking caster in a flimsy robe. The fact that casters often do so much damage at once just increases the difficulty. So here are a few tips...

First of all, insist to your party, particularly the nukers, that they hold off for around 15-20 seconds before blasting. Stand toe to toe with the mob right from the start, so that by the time they start blasting, you have already done enough damage to keep its attention. Ask that a Druid or Wizard cast a damage shield on you just before the start of the battle. Damage shields are incredible for keeping up aggro, and will work on all monsters that are attacking you.

The problem is further confused when you have a Druid or Ranger pulling with Snare. Snare tends to really tick off creatures, and it can be very difficult to taunt them off. When you see the Ranger or Druid come in with the pull, stand directly in line with them, so the mob has to run past you to get to the puller. Time your Auto Attack so that you take a swipe at it just as it is running past. This takes a little practise, but after a while can be done with ease. Firing a few arrows at the mob while it is being reeled in also goes a long way to ensure it sticks to you.

Another non-aggressive spell that carries a huge taunt component is Healing. Any Cleric (or Druid or Shaman) that casts a heal spell risks pulling a mob onto them. As a Warrior, the last thing you want is your healer distracted by a creature pounding on them. Keep an eye on the health bars of your healers (as well as other casters) when dealing with multiple creatures. As soon as you see one of those bars drop, immediately leave your current target alone and taunt the mob off your healer. Smack it around a bit to make sure it stays with you, then go back to the first one and finish it off. In a group with more than one tank, one should be designated as the "bodyguard" for the casters, while the other one stays with the current target. The tank with the highest taunt skill and fastest weapons should be chosen as the bodyguard.

It is often stated that the only thing more difficult than playing an Enchanter is playing with an Enchanter. Enchanters require tanks to pay special attention to what is going on and to communicate their target to the Enchanter, so that he or she knows which ones to mesmerize. Always stick with your first target (unless the above situation arises), that way you won’t accidentally break a well placed "mez" (this also means putting that Painbringer or Sword of Skyfire away. Besides, Area Effects are best left in the capable hands of finger-twiddlers anyway.) Once your target is dead, agree with your group which one you will take next. But, before you break that mez, there is one thing that you must do: taunt! Go to each mezzed mob and taunt it. Mesmerization really makes creatures angry, and they will beeline right to the Enchanter soon as it is broken... unless you make them even more angry at you. With mobs that verbally respond, wait until you see the "I’ll teach you..." indication that your taunt has succeeded. Otherwise, 3 taunts or so should do it. By the way, huge thanks go out to Kaiytlyn of the Spirit of the Moon for opening my eyes to this very simple yet very effective tactic! Do it, it works.
