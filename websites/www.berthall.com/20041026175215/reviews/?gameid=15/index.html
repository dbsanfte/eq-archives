As you may or may not know, the NDA has been lifted from the Horizons Beta, in preparation of its European December 5 and North American December 9 launch.

I thought it might be appropriate to give you something of a Beta Tester’s review on the game. I can’t stress enough that this is a BETA review; Artifact Entertainment has purposely held back quite a bit of the content from us, to prevent the Beta Testers from having the huge amount of knowledge that is typical of most MMORPGs.

This review is of the v6.6.6 Beta build, and may not reflect the final release build. The last version of beta was v6.7.6, but this did not include any client-side changes, only server-side.

THE GAME

If you read the background information several years ago on the Horizons web site, put that out of your mind right now. The only thing that is the same is the name.

In Horizons: Empires of Istaria, there has been a war waging for some time, between the forces of the Living, and the undead and ensorcelled slaves of the Withered Aegis. The AI that runs the Withered Aegis is rumored to be very good; unfortunately, we have yet to see it. The AI’s objective is to conquer the remaining territories and the Living Races of Istaria. Your objective, and the one thing that all player characters work towards, is to defeat the Withered Aegis and free the subjugated races, such as the Dryads and the Orcs.

The game is, as usual, based on the concept of classes, called "schools". There are two different types of schools, Adventuring and Crafting. Adventuring schools include your clerics, warriors, mages, and scouts. Crafters are armorers, weaponsmiths, jewelers, etc. As a player, you can be an Adventurer and a Crafter at the same time. In order to prevent you from getting bored with your school, you have the ability to change on the fly. That’s right. Tired of being a level 3 mage? Just go to the warrior trainer and join that school. Now, you’re a level 1 warrior. A level 1 warrior with level 3 hit points.

Prestige schools are also available. These require that you reach certain levels in the standard classes or another prestige class before you can become one. For example, to become a Paladin, you will need to be a level 15 cleric and a level 20 warrior.

In order to craft, you have to join one of the crafting schools. You level up in these schools with experience, which is earned through crafting. The crafting system is very intricate, and after a short while becomes very tedious. You have to go out and collect your resources. For a very basic item, say, a bronze shield, you will need to go out and collect ore. This requires you to go out with a pick, find a vein of ore, and mine it. You’ll get an average of about 5 units of ore per vein. Now, loaded down with ore, you need to smelt it into bronze bars. Takes around 10 units of ore to make one bronze bar. Say 15 bars to make a shield, and you can see how this goes. Of course, you have to take the ore to the furnace and equip the smelting tongs to smelt it, then take the bars to the anvil and equip the hammer to create the actual item. You have to manually change these tools around in your inventory to accomplish this.

Ore is heavy, and it could take you several trips to get enough ore to make that shield. You’ll have to smelt it into bars to get both the weight and the physical amount down to a manageable size in order to get more. However, if you have enough money or skill, you can either make or buy a cargo disk. This is a flat disk that follows along behind you, and allows you to carry a lot more weight than you normally could. Now, to me, this seemed a bit too science fiction. A horse pulling a cart would have accomplished the same thing, and kept it within the mythos Artifact Entertainment was trying to portray.

All useful items in the game will be made by the player characters, on the Galaxies model. You can loot item components off of the mobs you kill, but you will not see your Sword +1 or whatever. You’ll have to make it yourself or buy it from someone else.

All characters have the ability to Recall. Think of this as EQ’s Gate spell, and you’ll basically have it. Every school of character has it, and it can be used whenever wanted in order to return you to your bind point. All bind points are shrines in whatever town you choose. To bind, you right click the shrine and select “Bind” from the list.

This brings up another point. Almost everything has a right-click context menu. Other players, items, NPCs, even the teleport gates. Don’t be afraid to try it out.

GRAPHICS

Much has been made of the graphics in Horizons. To be honest, I’ve never been impressed. The landscapes are definitely better than EverQuest, and in some ways may be better than Star Wars Galaxies. Artifact Entertainment has the ability to change landscapes on the fly. This will become important down the road, as the Withered Aegis is driven back, or the players lose ground to the AI. The land changes depending on who owns it, and is a visible indicator to the player characters of how well or poorly they are doing in this conflict.

The characters are another matter, however. When you start with character customization, you have more options than the original EverQuest, but certainly far less than more modern games, especially Galaxies. Characters are blocky, textures are very blurry, and half of the options are bizarre. Purple, green, and orange hair? Well, at least you have multiple skin tone options.

Animations are poorly done. When running, they look like they’re hopping from one foot to the other. Sitting and standing are accomplished so quickly that you would bite your tongues and get nosebleeds if you tried to do it this way in real life.

The cities and towns of the various races all have their own racial quality to them. Tazoon, the capital, is a magnificent and huge city, though it is somewhat reminiscent of the cities on Naboo in Galaxies. The textures have a washed-out look to them.

Swords, hammers, shields, and armor all look fairly well done. They are shiny where they should be shiny, and dull elsewhere. Unfortunately, Beta only provided us with limited options as far as appearance goes.

Arrows arch towards their target beautifully… sometimes. At other times, they appear to be stuck on your bow, and you can collect at least four arrows there, all pointing in different directions.

All in all, I am pretty disappointed with what I have seen graphics-wise. This would have been fine 4 years ago, but it doesn’t stack up today. Some of the bugs will be ironed out for release, but the others, well, I can only hope (dubiously) that the rumors of better graphics in the release version are true.

SOUND

The sound effects in this game are about average. Nothing stunning or spectacular, but solid. You’ll hear the clash of weapons. You’ll hear your character cry out in pain when hit. You’ll hear some sounds coming from your opponent. You’ll hear the patter of your feet running along the road, and a different sound when you run through grass.

The music. Ah, the music. In virtually every game I’ve played before, I’ve turned the music off because it just gets irritating after a while. Not so in this game. If there is a shining beacon in Horizons, this is it. The music is amazing, moving, and sets the proper tone no matter where you go.

COMBAT

Well, this will be the bread and butter for most of you.

There are several different methods of combat. The first, naturally, is melee combat. There are a number of different weapons available. Unfortunately, the game is hard-coded to prevent you from using them until you are a certain level. Want to use a longsword instead of a shortsword? Tough, wait ‘till level 6. Want to use an iron longsword instead of a bronze one? Wait till level 11. Fine steel? You know the drill. This applies to all weapons and armor. Artifact Entertainment wants to promote this as a skill-based game, but they neglect to point out that your skills are dependent on both your level and your class.

As you progress in level, you gain special attacks, special abilities, and other abilities. These are all based on timers, and in some cases those timers are linked, so that using one special ability will also activate the timer on another ability, preventing its use. Among the special abilities is the concept of “stances”. A stance is more of a fighting style than anything else. Anyone that has read the Wheel of Time series would be familiar with the different fighting styles that Rand Al’thor used. Moving from “Stork standing in the swamp” to “Broken teapot” to “Bull charging gate” in an effort to catch the enemy off-guard. Oh, how this could have been something special. Instead, they decided to use the Rock-Paper-Scissors method. Rock Stance defeats Scissors Stance defeats Paper Stance defeats Rock Stance. They even named them this way! When in a stance, a colored disk appears under the character’s and opponent’s feet. Blue to Rock, Red to Scissors, and Green to Paper. Stances are all level/skill based as well, so it will be a while before you have all three.

Combat itself consists of pressing the attack button, pressing whatever occasional special attack button you wish when it is available, and changing stances if appropriate. Pretty standard fare for most MMORPGs. Yawn.

Ranged and Spell combat pretty much run the same. Thank God there aren’t any pets… yet.

STABILITY

Well, this is a Beta. Lag has sucked, and badly so. This has been a constant throughout the entire duration of my experience with this game. All you EQ players have seen this lag before. Mostly, you saw it in raids. You know, you’re fighting, trying to attack, trying to cast, whatever. But nothing happens on the screen for 10, 20, even 30 seconds. Often, the lag meter on the screen would turn red.

Well, same thing here. Except you aren’t in a large group of people. You’re by yourself, or near one or two other people. Makes trying to fight, trying to heal, or anything else extremely difficult.

Disappearing NPCs have been the bane of my existence in this game, though. The pawn broker is missing, so you can’t sell off the excessive amount of item components that is weighing you down and reducing your movement rate. The consigner is missing, so you can’t buy anyone else’s goods. Even the vaultkeeper is missing at times, preventing you from storing anything in the bank. Want to change classes? Better hope that trainer hasn’t decided to take a vacation.

OVERALL

I’ve been in a few betas. I’d have to say that this one isn’t the worst I’ve been in, but it certainly hasn’t been the best. Artifact Entertainment doesn’t have its own message boards; for Beta, we’re using boards run at Tazoon.com. For release, they’ve already contracted Horizons Vault. And that means that if you hate the moderating we do over here, you're going to absolutely despise it over there..

They have a poor ability to talk with their beta participants, and don’t expect to see that improve with their regular customers after release. If customer service is your bag, don’t look for it here.

Quests are rudimentary, and quite often broken. Now, Artifact Entertainment has stated that they’re holding back most of this content for release, but if we’re even seeing a tenth of what will be in release, there will still be a dearth of content.

To sum it up, this game looks like it was supposed to have been released years ago, but wasn’t, and never had the graphics updated. Interest-wise, it sits below Star Wars Galaxies on the Boredom Scale, but not by a lot. The draw of this game is going to be for people that want to fight the long war against the AI. I haven’t seen anything in the way of an Ubermob, so I don’t know if there will be much of a draw for those that enjoy raids. The lack of droppable items would seem to put a nail in that coffin anyway.

I’m going to wait to purchase this one.

Moiraine
