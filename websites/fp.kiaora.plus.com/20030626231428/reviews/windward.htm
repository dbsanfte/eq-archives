Look to Windward
Iain M. Banks

Look to Windward is the latest of Iain Bank's science fiction novels, and it lives up to everything I have come to expect from this highly talented author. Previous Culture novels have included The Player of Games (a personal favourite) and Excession, he is also critically acclaimed for his non science fiction work such as The Wasp Factory and The Crow Road.

Set in a distant galactic future where the dominant civilisation is the Culture, humans have overcome even death and live out near Utopic lives, basic life needs having been met millennia before. The culture is benevolently "ruled" by near godlike artificial Intelligences, which reside in spaceships or control whole worlds.

In Look to Windward a widowed survivor of a civil war is sent to the Culture world of Masaq, apparently to persuade a composer to return to his home world from his self-imposed exile and help heal his people. But the mission, as the composer Ziller suspects, is not all it seems to be. Iain Banks seems to delight in surprising the reader, building assumptions and expectations only to shatter them with a sudden revelation. Often a character easily assumed to be human suddenly turns out to be something truly alien, in shape at least. The plot itself twists and turns, part thriller, part mystery, and leaves you in suspense right to the final pages. If you have read Iain Banks before you will know that it is not  a foregone conclusion that the main protagonists shall win, or even live!

As usual the exploration of character motivation is detailed and complex, and the discussions of comparative culture held by some of the characters are thoughtful, provocative and sometimes humorous. Even the Minds themselves have a somewhat bizarre sense of humour, shown in their chosen names; examples such as All the same I saw it first, Resistance is Character-Forming, Inappropriate Response and Lasting Damage,  spring to mind. The non-culture (and non human) civilisations he depicts often seem to have more in common with the present than the culture itself does, a fact he also uses to force the reader to question their assumptions.

As you may have guessed, though a thriller, a mystery and space opera, Look to Windward is not the lightest of reads, so if a throw-away piece of entertainment is what you are looking for, this most certainly is not it. If on the other hand you prefer a more thoughtful and challenging read, it most certainly is.

No review is complete without a small taster, so here you are:

The General System Vehicle Sanctioned Parts List appeared on the screen in the Superlifter's lounge as another point of light in the starfield. It became a silver dot and grew quickly to fill the screen, though there was no sign of detail on the shining surface.
~That'll be it.
~I suppose so.
~We've probably passed near several escort craft, though they wouldn't be making their presence so obvious. What the Navy called a High Value Unit; you never send them out alone.
~ I thought it might look a little more grand.
~They always look pretty unimposing from the outside.

The Superlifter plunged into the centre of the silver surface. Within it was like looking from an aircraft inside a cloud, then there was the impression of plunging through another surface, then another, then dozens more in quick succession, flicking past like thumbed paper pages in an antique book.

They burst from the last membrane into a great hazy space lit by a yellow-white line burning high above, beyond layers of wispy cloud. They were above and aft of the craft's stern. The ship was twenty-five kilometres long and ten wide. The top surface was parkland; wooded hills and ridges separated by and studded with rivers and lakes.

Bracketed by colossal ribbed and buttressed outriggers chevroned in red and blue, the GSV's sheer sides were a golden, tawny colour, scattered with a motley confusion of foliage-covered platforms and balconies and punctured by a bewildering variety of brightly lit openings, like a glowing vertical city set into sandstone cliffs three kilometres high. The air swarmed with craft of every type Quilan had ever seen or heard of, and more besides. Some were tiny, some were the size of a Superlifter. Still smaller dots were individual people, floating in the air.

Two other giant vessels, each barely an eighth of the size of the Sanctioned Parts List, shared the envelope of the GSV's surrounding field enclosure. Riding a few kilometres off each side, plainer and more dense-looking, they were surrounded with their own little concentrations of smaller flying craft.

~It is a little more impressive on the inside, isn't it?
Hadesh Hurler remained silent.
