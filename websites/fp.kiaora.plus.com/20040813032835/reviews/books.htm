The Truth

Terry Pratchett

I don't know how he does it. You would think that after about 25 Discworld novels they would start to feel a little old hat, but he just keeps coming up with the goods.

"The Truth" takes journalism, and more specifically newspaper journalism, as the subject, or perhaps victim, for Terry Pratchett's wry humor this time round. If you have ever read those "about the author" type paragraphs you will remember that Pratchett was himself once in the field of journalism, so he is certainly writing about an area he is familiar with.

The dwarves have developed a method of turning lead into gold... namely a printing press. It isn't long before William de Word, a born muckraker, find's himself starting Ankh-Morpork's first newspaper, and learning the pitfalls of investigative journalism the hard way. Many exaggerated headlines, talking dogs and humorous vegetables soon follow.

The villains of the piece, a couple of hit men from out of town, are both well developed. I particularly liked touches such as one of them being a "wannabe" drug addict, who keeps trying to mainline oven cleaner.

If you are already a Pratchett fan you will be pleased to hear that a few regular characters do make an appearance, namely the Patrician, Vimes, Gaspode (the wonder dog), and of course Death.

Here is a little taster to whet your appetite:

"It was curry tonight. And one of the strange things about eating at Mrs Arcanum's was that you got more leftovers than you got original meals. That is, there were far more meals made up from what were traditionally considered the prudently usable remains of earlier meals - stews, bubble-and-squeak, curry - than there were meals at which those remains could have originated.

The curry was particularly strange, since Mrs Arcanum considered foreign parts only marginally less unspeakable than private parts and therefore added the curious yellow curry powder with a very small spoon, lest everyone should suddenly tear their clothes off and do foreign things."

Note that this excerpt demonstrates the positive aspects of being a professional author, namely you are allowed to start sentences with an "And" without some English teacher performing unspeakables with a red biro.
