The Fresco

Sheri S Tepper

 

15 years ago an unusually thoughtful parent bought his daughter a book by an author she had never heard of before. The Chronicles of Marvin Manyshaped was my introduction to the work of Sheri S Tepper, who I thereafter  named for quite some time as my favourite author. She may even have been instrumental in my character formation given that I was 16 at the time and very willing to absorb quite a few of her feminist and radical ideas. I have known her work to be read and enjoyed by people usually utterly averse to science fiction or fantasy of any kind.

Tepper can create, with enviable depth and clarity, incredibly complex, utterly alien worlds. Her characters are deeply engaging and her narrative smooth and readable. Perhaps that's enough praise singing for now though, as I must now insert a note of caution. The Fresco was a very enjoyable read, but this time I felt she had gone too far, that her bad guys were inhumanly evil caricatures and this seriously detracted from the points she was trying to make. Senator Morse in particular, is a woman hating, pro-gun, pro-life cartoon of a man, who is prepared to barter human lives to aliens for personal gain. Pretty much all his views are seen as stemming from a pathological hatred of women and pregnancy, and he is held up as a character of hate, with no effort made to explore reasons for his views.

The plot follows Benita, a Hispanic woman abused by her drunken husband and generally put upon and under appreciated by her family. She is chosen as an intermediary by aliens choosing to make contact with humanity, and this she does, travelling to Washington, and eventually to  other worlds. The aliens in question are a well meaning race who want to help humanity achieve the level of social advancement required for admission to a galactic federation, which would protect earth from some of that Confederation's more predatory members.  I do not want to give away too much of the plot so I will simply end this with an excerpt.

 

She threw herself down on the bed, sprawled every which-a-way like cooked spaghetti, muscles letting go all at once, mind switching from angelica to the bookstore, back to General Wallace, and then to the creature that had called itself an athyco. Whatever did they really look like? And how could they have gone out of her mind even for a moment? So strange, so wonderful, yet hard to think about. Well, strangeness was hard to think about. Wonder grazes you like a bullet, it zips by and is gone, and all you really perceive is the zing as it goes past, or maybe the pain if it comes too close. It does no good to search for whatever it was, for it never lodges anywhere you can get a good look at it. The truly strange has no hooks of familiarity that one can catch hold of.

It had happened though. It wasn't a dream. She really had met weird aliens, Chiddy and Vess, who had done her as good turn, who had to have done it, because it was the only way she could explain how well she'd been doing. She hadn't cried once. She hadn't lain awake, worried over what she might have done or said wrong. She hadn't been concerned about running back home because it was her duty. Somehow, it seemed, Chiddy and Vess had unquirked her.
