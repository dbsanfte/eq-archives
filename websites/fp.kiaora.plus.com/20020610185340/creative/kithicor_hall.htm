Kithicor Hall

The sun was well up in the sky by the time they dared enter the forest. Stories of the region by night had even filtered back home, now so unbearably distant.  They had camped in the western commonlands overnight, keeping a fire going strong to ward off roaming beasts, but neither felt refreshed.

They were dressed for the road, strong boots with thick soles, caked in mud, and long warm woollen capes, only slightly frayed about the edges. Their clothes had born up well to the punishment the journey had subjected them to. Though dirty, these two were certainly no peasants.  Both moved with an easy sinuous lope, the larger of the two leading the way into the mists of Kithicor forest.

The smaller traveller, though weary, felt excitement rise as they neared their goal at last. She would be able to be herself in this place, her father had told her so. No one would spit on her, afraid of her gift, ridiculing her for her differences. Her green eyes gleamed and her handsome face glowed; even the lack of light beneath the dense distant forest canopy failed to chill her, as it should. She began to hum under her breath, an alien but joyful air, until her companion stopped her with a respectful but quelling backwards glance.

-Just because the undead are not around does not mean these woods are completely safe My Lady, please try not to make more noise than necessary.

- I’m thorry.

She whispered the reply, flushing deeply. Strictly speaking her companion was a servant, but this ancient family retainer had known and cared for her since her hatching. She adjusted her pack and reached a hand to her lute, the familiar feel of its smooth surface reassuring her, and she ran on, eagerness once again rising as she willed the last miles past.

 The trees thinned suddenly after an hour or more’s run, and both of the travellers stopped suddenly as they finally saw their destination before them. A huge edifice of grey stone smothered in ivy stood in a wide forest clearing. Turrets and crenulations aplenty topped the thickly buttressed walls. It was evidently an ancient building, that had been added to over the centuries by a variety of owners of eclectic and sometimes questionable taste, the fashions of eons echoed in the multitude of gargoyles, icons and carvings; the windows speckled the façade like a demented rash, here arched, here perfectly rectangular, interspersed with arrow slits and a crazy paving of down pipes and guttering.

The two iksar simply stared. They had both looked, and laughed a little, at the woodcuts they had been sent of this foreign building, but it seemed with hindsight that a little artistic license had been used on the part of the craftsman. For the first time Thespa felt a qualm concerning her new life, the setting for which now loomed before them. She shrank a little towards her companion, who gathered himself at last and took her hand in his. Gripping it reassuringly, he spoke in his usual measured tones,

-It will be all right small one; your father took great care to find out all he could about this place. The Lady will have been expecting us for a week or so by now, so let us go and greet her shall we?

Thespa nodded, her iridescent facial markings glowing beneath her hood. She stepped a little more confidently towards the alarming building, tail lifted slightly behind her, a sign of alarm or wariness in her kind.
