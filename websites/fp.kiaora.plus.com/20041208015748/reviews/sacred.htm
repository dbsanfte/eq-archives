Sacred

Introduction

Sacred (from Ascaron Entertainment) is a fantasy role playing game in the style of Diablo II, which can be played online or over a LAN, or as a single player game.

Classes and Character Development

Sacred has a novel take on the traditional roleplaying classes. While at first glance some of the classes seem familiar, the implementation adds a fresh dimension to the usual, and adds a good sprinkling of the brand-new to complete the mixture.

In brief, the classes are Gladiator, Seraphim, Vampiress, Wood-elf, Dark-Elf and Battle-Mage. Gladiator, Wood-Elf and Battle-Mage are perhaps the closest to traditional RPG archetypes (warrior, ranger and magician). Dark-Elf can perhaps be best described as a combination of the roles of monk and thief, while the Seraphim and Vampiress could be thought of as somewhat similar to a paladin and shadowknight, but they feel quite different to play. 

I tried every class myself, but the vampiress was very much my style of play - a do-gooding knight by day, a bloodsucking whirling ball of claws by night... though with the right skills and combat arts I found I could be a blood-sucking ball of claws all the time. What fun.

 

Every class has different basic stats, which increase as a percentage of that base per level. You do get a point or so to spend, but it's not enough to make a wood-elf into a gladiator, for example. You also receive an increasing number of skill-points per level, and this is where the character building really comes into its' own. In the case of the wood-elf, you have the option of creating a bow reliant ranger-type, a knife or sword wielding dervish,  a druid-like spell caster.. or a hybrid of all three. With a limited number of skill points, it is obviously possible to seriously gimp your character. Suggested "builds" with recommended skill point distribution would have been a welcome addition to the level-up process for those learning to play. Ascaron's message boards are strewn with the stories of  those forced to re-roll because their choices had rendered the later game or difficulty settings un-playable.

Combat and Spells

Combat is basically point and click based. You left click and hold to attack at full speed, and right click to use your currently selected special attack (which can be a special combat move, or a spell-like ability). Combat is all real-time - there is no means of pausing the game and stacking up actions as you could in say Neverwinter Nights or Baldur's Gate (or even Diablo to some extent). If you are playing a class with low hit-points, it therefore pays to either always be prepared (buffing before moving on, for example), or save frequently!

The number of special attacks you have available increases every now and then as you level. By level 8 or so you will have 3 special attack slots, into which you place the "combat arts" or spells you wish to use. With no means to pause, your choices need to be good ones! It is possible to stack up to four arts or spells into one slot by use of Combos, which you pay for at a special merchant called a Combo Master. These can be different attacks, or four instances of the same attack, but  take a fair amount of time to charge up compared with the use of a single art.

The combat arts are acquired through the memorisation of runes, which drop from monsters or are given as quest rewards. You will find you are collecting far more runes belonging to other classes, but luckily you may trade them at a Combo Master for runes of your choice. (see screenshots). The power of a combat art increases if you memorise another of the same type, but the recharge time will also increase. For some attacks, such as a multi-hit combat art, it is actually better to stay with a fairly low recharge time rather than build the power of the attack to the highest you can, so you can use it more frequently when besieged by hordes of creatures at once.
