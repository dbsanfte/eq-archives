    Re: "DieselGate" possible huge fall of VW Group?

    Originally Posted by Bonlainy
    So you're ok with this I guess.
    Ford estimated 180 deaths from this issue. Ford recalled 1.5 million Pintos according to the link (I have no idea what their sales estimate was). Using the estimated deaths and actual sales, and rounding up for a nice number assuming Ford expected to sell more of the cars, call that an one in ten thousand chance of catastrophic failure at some point in the product's lifespan. The actual number was 27 deaths, so closer to one in fifty thousand, but if Ford felt it would be 1 in 10 K they should be held to that.

    What failure rate is acceptable?

    Zero isn't going to happen. This is engineering, everything has a chance to fail over time, and some of those failures will be catastrophic. What is the acceptable number at which you can say, "Ok, you can ship this"? I don't know, and I wouldn't want to be part of any exercise that involved putting a dollar value on human life to perform a cost-benefit analysis. But that is the question: what is an acceptable fail rate for a consumer good under stress (a rear-end collision is certainly a stress condition)? I guess I'm just happy that the legal system has inflated the dollar value of a human life to such a degree that companies are taking pains to minimize catastrophic failures.

    Ford's decision was an acceptance of limited power by an engineer. You can't make something safe from everything. The problem is here, that decision was the incorrect one - because it was solvable. Here's the problem - engineers are required to court things like this to get the job done. Technically, the LHC had a small, but calculable chance to destroy the world. We fire it anyway, because the chance was minimal. So am I happy that the pinto problem existed? No. And does Ford deserve the reputation hit it took for that? Yes. But it's just the most extreme example of a fairly normal engineering dilemma, and an example of when such a dilemma was resolved incorrectly. I can forgive bad decision making. Also, the Ford decision is clearly the effort of a bean counter fucking with the engineering team. Most engineers involved in real projects empathize with it.

    VW's decision is a flagrant disregard for design specification and regulation. As an engineer - VW has fucked with the equivalent of holy scripture. You do not cheat specification. This wasn't some bean counter making this decision. An engineer had to willingly design a device to get around the spec. This is a specific thing in the somewhat universal code of engineering ethics. You quit jobs rather than do this stuff. VW Engineers are tainted members of the community for the next several decades.
