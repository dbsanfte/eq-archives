    Plane, not simple: On set with 'Agents of S.H.I.E.L.D.'

    CULVER CITY, Calif. — Ming-Na Wen is about to show off the fancy digs of Marvel's Agents of S.H.I.E.L.D. when duty calls.

    "I've got to go fly the plane, people," she says, heading off to film a scene in which her character, martial-arts expert and pilot Melinda May, takes the team's flying headquarters, affectionately known as "the Bus," to Sweden to investigate a case.

    Later, Wen, outfitted in May's black flight suit, returns to offer a tour of the swanky living quarters, the middle level of the airplane set that is both home and workplace to the skilled agents at the heart of ABC's action series, premiering Sept. 24, (8 p.m. ET/PT).

    She takes a sense of ownership by representing her character, part of a six-member team brought together by Agent Phil Coulson (Clark Gregg), a popular character in recent Marvel films, including The Avengers.

    "May knows all the ins and outs of the Bus. I don't think she's out there detailing it, but she definitely makes sure that everything is taken care of and in working order. This is our headquarters," she says.

    The plane has special capabilities that serve the agents, who investigate the strange and unknown in a world where superpowers exist. However, Wen can't reveal them. "That's Level 7 (clearance). But I will say, there will be some surprises."

    The actress (ER,Stargate Universe), starts her tour in a stylish living room, where modern white chairs and sofa — all with seat belts — contrast with the brown wall and shelves. There's even a bar. A gray-and-silver S.H.I.E.L.D. eagle emblem adorns the back wall.

    "This is our beautiful lounge area, where we don't really lounge very much. There's no time for lounging," she says.

    The state-of-the-art command center, a darker, glassed-in area lit by monitors and computer screens, divides the long interior.

    "Any time we have any sort of investigation we have to deal with or if we're locating our S.H.I.E.L.D. agents, it's all here," she says. "If there is an emergency where a satellite is blown out, we have our own capabilities to communicate with the people in the chain of command. It's almost like Air Force One in that sense."

    Another small area features in-flight reading material, including 16 volumes of The History of S.H.I.E.L.D. and a manual that requires top-secret clearance. Wen has a favorite: A Concise History of Super-, Hyper- and Ultra-Human Abilities. "I love that one."

    Near the command center, a circular staircase leads up to Agent Coulson's office, a sky-lighted aerie and virtual museum. It contains a mix of new and old, including a model of the plane, a S.H.I.E.L.D. badge, a needle gun, a wireless radio, a telescope, binoculars, pilot goggles and a compass.

    "It really reveals a lot about who Coulson is because of how he's decorated it," Wen says.

    The bottom floor of the team's plane houses a science lab filled with microscopes, flasks and test-tube samples, along with vehicles, including Coulson's beloved red Corvette.

    Back on the middle level, Wen shows off the white-and-silver kitchen ("I would love it if May did some cooking.") An eating area nearby is flanked by five narrow sleeping modules, each with a wall monitor and one-way mirrored glass. "I absolutely love the beds. Isn't it cozy? It's like the womb, right?" she says.

    Overall, Wen is pleased with the agents' airborne home.

    "I love the sleekness and the color and if I didn't have kids, I would go with white furniture, too. A kid, a dog, a hubby, no," she says. "That is the real world. It's not the S.H.I.E.L.D. world."

    ‘Marvel’s Agents of S.H.I.E.L.D.’ Begins on ABC

    There may never have been a television show as hard to address on its own terms as “Marvel’s Agents of S.H.I.E.L.D.,” beginning on Tuesday night on ABC. That’s what happens when you’re a cog in the Marvel Cinematic Universe, the interlocking entertainment machine that encompasses comic books and megabudget movies. “Agents,” the franchise’s first TV property, picks up the story after the superheroes-versus-aliens battle that leveled much of Midtown Manhattan in the $1.5 billion-grossing film “Marvel’s The Avengers.”

    That’s a lot of baggage, and fairly or not, the first thing you may notice about “Agents” is that it looks kind of puny in the context of the Marvel universe, even though it’s a reasonably lavish production, by TV standards. It’s not a superhero series: it’s a less expensive secret-agent series, with just one anonymous paranormal in the pilot who climbs a wall and throws some heavy objects.

    You’ll also notice that the first episode is polished, fast-moving and at least moderately entertaining, which is no surprise, given that it was directed by the gifted storyteller Joss Whedon, who created the show along with Jed Whedon (his brother) and Maurissa Tancharoen (who is married to Jed). But there’s still an inevitable residue of disappointment.

    Whatever you think of Mr. Whedon’s “Avengers” and the other Marvel films, they take place in Imax-size worlds, with big characters played by actors who can give correspondingly large performances. “Agents” does what it can, puffing up the dialogue (“They’re giants; we’re what they step on”) and cuing the orchestra when a shiny new piece of hardware is introduced. But in Week 1, the series doesn’t demonstrate the imagination or verve it needs to break its comic-book narrative out of the bonds of budget and weekly format.

    The one character who makes the move from the films to the TV series is Agent Phil Coulson (Clark Gregg), whose earlier, inconvenient death — a major plot point in “The Avengers” — is explained away. (Another performer from the Marvel films, Cobie Smulders, as Agent Maria Hill, makes a guest appearance.) Coulson has assembled a typical assortment of experts in hacking, science, seduction, combat and so forth, to keep tabs on new superheroes.

    Mr. Gregg hits the same appealing note of wry authority that he struck in “The Avengers” (it’s not yet clear whether he has any others), and the newcomer Brett Dalton (“Killing Lincoln”) shows some charm as a Bond-style operative. Joss Whedon, meanwhile, has fun with the show’s obligatory jabs of self-awareness. The mishmash nature of the story, strained together from decades of comics, movies and TV shows, is acknowledged when a team member says that the agency’s latest nemesis seems to have been created by throwing into a blender every superhero origin — 1940s serum, gamma radiation, alien artifacts.

    The first week’s adventure feels perfunctory, though, even given the constraint of introducing characters and back story, and most of the team members are still strictly two-dimensional. “Agents of S.H.I.E.L.D.” doesn’t need to emulate the Marvel movies, but it will need to show pretty quickly that it has at least as much wit and style as its TV comparables: shows like “Heroes” (the first season) and “Alphas,” on the superhero side, or “Alias” and “Covert Affairs,” on the spy side. Of course, there’s always the chance that Iron Man or at least Nick Fury will drop by to lend a hand.

    Marvel’s Agents of S.H.I.E.L.D.

    ABC, Tuesday nights at 8, Eastern and Pacific times; 7, Central time.

    Produced by ABC Studios and Marvel Television. Created by Joss Whedon, Jed Whedon and Maurissa Tancharoen. Joss Whedon, Jed Whedon, Ms. Tancharoen, Jeph Loeb and Jeffrey Bell, executive producers.

    WITH: Clark Gregg (Agent Phil Coulson), Brett Dalton (Agent Grant Ward), Ming-Na Wen (Agent Melinda May), Iain De Caestecker (Agent Leo Fitz), Elizabeth Henstridge (Agent Jemma Simmons) and Chloe Bennet (Skye).
