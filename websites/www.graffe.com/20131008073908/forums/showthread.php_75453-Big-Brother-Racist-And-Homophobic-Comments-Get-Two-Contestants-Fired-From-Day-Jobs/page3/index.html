    Texas State student leaders call for discipline of 'Big Brother' contestant

    July 17, 2013

    SAN MARCOS — A student group at Texas State University is calling on administrators to punish and possibly expel student Aaryn Gries, a contestant on “Big Brother” who was recorded making derogatory remarks on the reality TV show.

    “I feel like she has tarnished the reputation of the university,” said J.J. Franco, president of the school-funded University Ambassadors, which recruits prospective students and provides campus tours.

    Franco said he is disappointed by Gries, especially because she identifies herself as a Texas State student and wears clothing with the school's logo on the series.

    Gries, a psychology junior, was shown making racial comments and using offensive language to describe gay people on the show. She's been dropped by an Austin modeling agency over the remarks and received national criticism, including a petition sent to CBS to remove her from the show. Two other contestants also have lost their jobs because of similar comments.

    JoAnne Smith, vice president of student affairs, said no disciplinary action will be taken against Gries.

    Franco cited a provision in the school's student handbook that states, “persons who racially or sexually harass another person are subject to disciplinary sanctions up to and including suspension from the university.”

    But Smith, citing the First Amendment, said, “It's a stretch.”

    She said the school “values the diversity of people and ideas and the spirit of inclusiveness on the campus.”

    The CBS reality show, in its 15th season, continuously films participants who are sequestered inside a house and pitted against each other in competitions. Every week a housemate is voted off of the show by the other contestants. The last one in the house wins a large cash prize.

    Gries' modeling agency has dropped her, and two others have lost their jobs.

    Numerous contestants on the show have expressed disapproval of Gries' comments. Some warned her she was being offensive. But Gries seemed to brush it off.

    She was nominated for eviction Sunday but will get a chance to save herself Wednesday if she wins the Power of Veto competition. If she doesn't, she faces possible eviction Thursday.

    Gries was born in San Angelo and raised on her family's ranch. In her initial interview on the show, Gries said she would not “jeopardize her values and morals” to win the competition, and said she doesn't think her family “would be surprised” by her actions on the show.

    Her father, George Gries, declined to comment, saying his daughter “can comment for herself.” Gries' Twitter and Facebook profiles are private.

    She has competed in beauty pageants and appeared in local television commercials in San Marcos for an apartment locating service.

    The owner of the company, Great Locations, did not return a request for comment.

    Gries has also been a member of several dance teams, including the Texas State squad, the Strutters. She also was a member of the Zeta Tau Alpha chapter at Texas State in 2009-10. The sorority chapter's president did not return requests for comment.

    Tami Johnson, president of the student group Hip Hop Congress, a diversity driven organization that she says, “strives to unite cultures through hip-hop,” said she watches the show “religiously” and is “ashamed” of Gries and “shocked” by her comments.

    “It's very unfortunate for someone from my school, who says they represent my school, to go on the show using racial slurs and homophobic language,” said Johnson, 21.

    Xander Peters, an English major who sat next to Gries in an anthropology class in 2011, said he was surprised by the negative attention and comments made by Gries.

    “She was actually a really nice girl,” said Peters, 22. “From what I took from our class a few years ago, she wasn't racist or homophobic at all.”

    Franco said what bothers him the most is Gries' lack of response to her housemates' warnings.

    “Even if she thinks these comments are made in good fun or made as a joke, she's still hurting peoples' feelings,” Franco said. “And it's not just people in the house but people in the entire country.”

    Big Brother 15 Controversy Continues: Spencer Clawson Makes Child Pornography Jokes

    Big Brother 15 is shaping up to be the series' most controversial season ever.

    The CBS reality hit has been making headlines all summer after houseguests were overheard using racial and homophobic slurs during the program's 24-hour live feeds. And on Monday, Aug. 5, contestant Spencer Clawson made the season's most appalling comments yet.

    Many live-feed watchers have noted that it looked like Spencer was speaking directly into houseguest McCrae Olson's mic while he was in the shower; Spencer made several references to Minnesota, where McCrae is from. Still, even if Spencer's remarks were meant to be jokes on McCrae, they are this season's most worrisome yet.

    On the live feed, Spencer was overheard talking about child pornography, joking to his fellow contestants: "I like to beat off to child porn. Did I ever tell y'all about that?" he said.

    And Spencer wasn't done yet, saying, "I love it. Beating off to child porn is my favorite thing there is."

    "I love it when they're around 3 or 4 years old," he continued. "My favorite ones are when you can tell they're in a basement." Spencer was speaking with Andy and Amanda Zuckerman, who both look uncomfortable, with Andy Herren saying, "Oh God," before changing the subject.

    CBS said in a statement to E! News regarding the remarks: "Big Brother is a reality show following a group of people who have no privacy 24/7, and seeing every moment of their lives. At times, the Houseguests reveal prejudices and other beliefs that we do not condone. We certainly find the statements made by Spencer on the live Internet feed to be offensive. Any views or opinions expressed in personal commentary by a Houseguest appearing on Big Brother, either on any live feed from the House or during the broadcast, are those of the individual(s) speaking, and do not represent the views or opinions of CBS or the producers of the program."

    Houseguests Aaryn Gries and GinaMarie Zimmerman made headlines (and lost their jobs) after using racial slurs overheard on the live feed, with CBS later airing their comments during an episode.

    CBS CEO Les Moonves recently addressed the controversy surrounding Big Brother, admitting he is "absolutely appalled" by some of the contestants' behavior.

    "Big Brother obviously is a social experiment, it always was. It was established as a social experiment and clearly that's what's happening this year. I find some of the behavior absolutely appalling personally. What you see there unfortunately is a reflection of how certain people feel in America. It's what our show is," Moonves said. "I think we handled it properly. A lot of it makes us uncomfortable. I've watched every episode of the show; obviously my wife (Big Brother host Julie Chen) would kill me if I didn't. So I do know what's going on there and we do discuss it quite a bit. I think we are handling it quite appropriately. We did not comment on some of the racial things being said until it started affecting what was going on in the household. There was a lot of chatter about it on the Internet and I think we've handled it the way we should have."

    The site BigBrotherSpice was the first to note the child pornography remarks.

    Big Brother fans, is this the worst season yet?

    Originally Posted by Dru
    Is Big Brother just survivor in a house? Do they have challenges, etc?
    Kind of. Sort of.

    The premise behind the game is that it's a social experiment. You take a number of people (12 to 16ish) and lock them into a small house together and they get no contact with the outside world. This creates a source of drama since you're forced to deal with and interact with these people 24/7 with no other contact from outsiders other than the show's host (Julie Chen). The other catch is that the house is rigged with cameras and microphones so everything that is said and done can be viewed by people who pay for the Live Feed subscription and highlights are shown on the actual shows. Each week there is a competition to determine the Head of Household. This person is safe from eviction and nominates two other players who can be evicted. The next challenge is a Veto competition where the Head of Household, the eviction nominees and a couple of "random" house guests compete for a Veto Power which will remove an eviction nominee and forces the Head of Household to make a renomination (and the Veto user is also safe as well). Finally on Thursday nights the safe house guests do a secret vote to determine who is evicted. Last person standing win's the game (though at the end there's a few other twists to determine the winner). As I said, it's a social experiment.

    Each year they add a bunch of twists and there are also a lot of allegations that the show's producers have too much control and influence over outcomes. The general consensus is that the house guests, ultimately, get to make their own decisions but production can, and will, step in to influence decisions behind the scenes or tailor challenges (or game twists) to favor certain house guests over others (particularly to save some high profile guests from eviction to keep the show entertaining).

    And, yes, this was a very weak cast that needed to be screened better. A lot of these contestants have no business being on the show and I don't like how CBS is, essentially, capitalizing off of the racist and homophobic remarks to draw viewers. It's turned into something like "Hey, look! It's a train wreck! Stop the car I want to watch!"
    There are other worlds than these.
