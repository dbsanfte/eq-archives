This system is geared for more exciting and realistic offline AD&D combat and replaces the standard initiative system. It employs a "battlemat" which is generally a reusable map that is graphed in one (1) inch squares. The ratio of the combat map is 1" equals 5 feet. Each character should use a miniature that fits within one square UNLESS the character is enlarged or naturally giant sized. The "chits" mentioned under this system are readily available as poker chits. The "chit container" can be anything, but we recommend something of the quart size. The container is filled with about 60% white chits, 30% red chits, and 10% blue chits.

Please note that throughout this discourse, the container is occasionally referred to as the "soup bowl."

Combat Rounds

When combat occurs, the standard one-minute rounds are replaced with 10-second combat rounds. A combat round is made up of three passes, each about three seconds long. Each time a player hands off the chit container (see below), he or she has completed a pass.

The "Magic Soup Bowl"

A player may resolve his or her character(s) actions only when he or she has the chit container. The chit container is a physical object that is passed from one player to the next. Only the player with the chit container can speak, move his or her character, or perform an action. The only exceptions to this rule are:

  • If a player is replying to the DM (for example, when the DM requires a Perception check from the character).

  • The character declares that he or she is attempting to parry or block an attack.

  • The character can make an attack of opportunity (described below).

Passing the "Soup Bowl"

The chit container is always passed from the DM’s left, around the table, and eventually back to the DM. Therefore players who sit to the immediate left of the DM will be able to resolved their actions before other players (instead of rolling for initiative every round).

Hot Soup!

A player must hastily resolve his or her actions when he/she receives the chit container, or pass the chit container without acting. While this makes combat more difficult and intense by forcing players to pay attention to the situation and think of their feet, this adds an important level of realism and seriousness to combat situations.

In order to improve combat speed, you are required to do the following:

  • Have attack modifiers (THAC0 and damage) calculated before game play.
    Know your Combat Movement Rate (CMR).
    If a character is casting, the player must have the effects of the spell ready. If a character casts a wall of fire but the player needs to look up the effect (instead of having the book open and ready to use), the player is wasting the group’s time and the spell automatically fails.

Initiative Chits

Every time a player receives the chit container, each of the characters under his or her control receive TWO WHITE initiative chits. White initiative chits are used to "pay for" various actions, such as making an attack, using an item, or casting a spell. A character can have a maximum of six initiative chits of any color when he or she hands off the chit container to the next person.

White initiative chits are used for the characters actions and primary weapon. Red and Blue can be used for a secondary weapon, special ability, or to mark spell duration. If a character is using the red or blue chits, he or she may take 2 chits of the appropriate color per pass in addition to white chits. However, a character can only spend the red and blue chits to perform that specific action and nothing else.

What a Player Can Do in One Combat Round

When the player receives the chit container, each of his or her characters can do any and all of the following three things:

  • Make a Standard Move (equal to his combat movement rating, CMR) or a Withdraw Move (always 1 square in any direction on the battlemat.)

  • Speak for a maximum of three seconds (unless casting). Note that you must have the chit container in order to reply to someone else!

  • Perform One Action that the character has enough initiative chits to perform.

Movement and Combat Movement Rate (CMR)

Characters either have a base movement of 6 or 12, as per the Player’s Handbook. This movement is modified by adding both your "to hit" bonus for Strength (if any) and your Reaction Adjustment for Dexterity (if any).  For example, a human character (base 12) with 17 strength (+1 "to hit") and 17 Dexterity (+2 reaction adjustment) has a base movement of 15 (12 + 1 + 2 = 15).

A character’s Combat Movement Rate (CMR) is 1/3 of his or her standard movement (see the accompanying chart). The movement rate is equal to the number of 5’ squares the character may move through on the battlemat (diagonal moves are equal to 1.5 squares). It is also the standard miles per hour the character travels while walking (useful for long distance speed calculations).

Attacks of Opportunity

If a character is engaged with an enemy (they are within melee striking distance range, usually meaning the enemy is in an adjacent square) and makes a standard moves that distances himself from the enemy (by running past the enemy or retreating), the enemy gains an immediate Attack of Opportunity.

An Attack of Opportunity gives the enemy an immediate free attack. This attack can be performed even if the enemy does not have the chit container. There is no cost associated with this attack. The enemy must have a weapon ready to make the attack; a character with a sheathed blade, a casting character, or a completely unprepared character does not gain an attack of opportunity (as determined by the DM).

The attack is one standard melee weapon attack regardless of how many attacks or weapons the enemy has ready and is resolved like any other attack.

The Withdraw Move

A Withdraw Move is a one step move in any direction (even diagonal) that prevents any attacks of opportunity. This can be a fighting retreat or defending oneself while moving through a group of enemies.

Actions

An Action requires that the character have the minimum number of initiative chits to "pay" for the actions (as listed in the accompanying chart). The cost of an attack depends on how many attacks per round your character can make. For NPCs, to calculate the number of attacks, add any attacks made with a secondary weapon to the total and manually keep track of when the secondary attack takes place (usually the last attack). For PCs, use red chits for a secondary weapon and attacks are always one per round (6 chits). Note that the character can perform one action per acceptance of the chit container, even if he or she has initiative chits left over PER color of chits used. For example, it is possible to attack with a primary weapon (white chits) and secondary weapon (red chits in the same pass of the chit container.

All actions have a cost. Even those actions which only require a thought or are "at will" require the use of an action and cost 1 or more white initiative chits. Automatic results (a feather fall that activates whenever the character drops more than 5’) do not require an action and do not have a cost.

Also, characters will need to pay attention to what they are holding due to the draw and replace costs (see chart). For example, to use a wand (an action), a character must first spend 1 chit to ready the weapon (an action and another 6 to use it (second action).

Action -- Attacking

Attacking is always done one blow at a time (unless indicated on the chart). The chart lists the cost for a character to make one attack based on how many attacks the character receives per round. The more attacks per round a character has, the less expensive the cost to make a single attack.

If a character is using two weapons, he or she uses the number of attacks with the primary weapon to determine the cost for one attack. The secondary weapon should be kept track of with different colored chits: this weapon always makes one attack per round (cost of 6 chits to attack). The character gains 2 chits per pass for the primary weapon or action and another 2 chits per pass for the secondary weapon only (these chits can not be used for anything else). A character does not gain secondary weapon chits until the secondary weapon is drawn.

Action -- Parrying

A parry costs the same number of chits as an attack and can be made at any time. A parry successfully blocks an attack if the defender hits the attacker’s armor class with a penalty as indicated below. Only melee attacks may be blocked without the use of a shield. Shield parries may also block missile attacks.

Any attempts to parry with a weapon or block an attack with a shield are made against the attacker’s Armor Class with a -4 penalty to the "to hit" roll. The following chart expands these penalties to allow characters to attempt to block attacks from creatures of any size (such as the thrown boulders of a giant). Note that a natural 20 roll is always successful on a parry (or attack) attempt.

Action -- Blocking

For one initiative chit, a character may declare that he or she is blocking for another character as an action (note that actually blocking or parrying an attack will cost additional chits; see below). The Blocker must be adjacent to the Protected character. Any attacks against the protected character that pass through the Blocker’s five front squares or originate from the Blocker’s five front squares (see chart) can be parried/blocked or absorbed.

These attacks may be parried/blocked or absorbed in one of the following three ways:

  • The Blocker can attempt to parry/block the attack. This is resolved normally; the Blocker must have the chits to make the parry/block and make a successful roll with the standard blocking/parry penalty to stop the attack. If the Blocker fails the roll, the attack succeeds against the protected character normally.

  • The Blocker can absorb the attack. The Blocker can attempt a parry/block roll without the standard penalty to his/her "to hit" roll against the attacker’s Armor Class for 1 chit. If successful, the attack is applied to the Blocker instead of the Protected character (i.e. the Blocker takes the damage from the attack.) If the Blocker’s attempt fails, the attack is applied to the Protected character normally.

  • The Blocker can absorb a magical attack (ie. "Throw yourself on the grenade"). Only magical attacks which have a physical effect and a limited area of effect (such as Burning Hands or Magic Missile) can be absorbed for a cost of 1 chit. The Blocker can absorb these attacks, suffering full damage (no saving throw allowed), if he or shee makes a successful Dexterity check. Not that the Blocker must have declared blocking and the spell must be directed at the Protected character or must pass through the Blocker’s five front squares, if another target other than the Protected on the battlefield. Incorporeal spells (such as Slow or Charm Person) or spells with a larger area of effect (such as a Lightning Bolt or Cone of Cold) can not be blocked. Note that a Fireball could be blocked; however, the spell would detonate on the Blocking character (as per the spell description in the Player’s Handbook).

Surprise attacks (such as those from an invisible character) can not normally be blocked. A DM may grant a free Perception check (i.e. "sixth sense") for the Blocker and allow him or her to try and stop the attack, if the perception check is successful.

Action -- Casting

For two initiative chits, a character can declare that he or she is casting. While casting, the character can not speak (even with the chit container). The next time the character receives the chit container, he or she must pay two more chits to complete a spell with a casting time of 2 or less. If the character choose not to spend the chits, the spell fails and is wasted. If the character is wounded during the cycle of the chit container, he or she loses the current spell.

When a character declares casting, he or she must immediately record which spell is being cast. The can be recorded on the character sheet or a note, but if the spell is not written down, it automatically fails.

Example of Casting: Basal declares casting and pays 2 chits the first time he receives the initiative chit container. He immediately records the spell he is casting (Fireball). The second time he receives the container, he declares "still casting" and pays another 2 chits. The third time he receives the container, he pays 2 more chits and the spell goes off.

Surprise!

At the beginning of a combat a character may be fully surprised, partially surprised, or not surprised. A character initiating a battle (the attacker) is not surprised. The victims of the attacker generally are required to make Perception checks; a successful check gives the character partial surprise, a failed check results in the character being fully surprised. The DM will determine situational modifiers to this check, if any.

  • A fully surprised character begins with no initiative chits.

  • A partially surprised character begins with d6 chits, plus a number of chits equal to his or her Reaction Adjustment (determined by Dexterity). A character may have no more than six initiative chits regardless of the roll and reaction adjustment. If the character is using secondary chits (non-white), he or she begins with none (unless these are used for keeping track of a once per round free ability).

  • A not surprised character begins with six initiative chits.

Death and Dying

A character reduced to zero or fewer Hit Points loses all of his or her chits and 1/3 of a Hit Point every time the player receives the chit container. The character is not dead until -11 is reached: a character with -10 2/3 Hit Points is alive. At the end of the combat, any fractional Hit Points are considered healed (-10 2/3 becomes -10).

Dying or unconscious characters do not gain any chits. Characters only gain chits if conscious (they gain chits even if held). Stunned characters do not gain chits while stunned, but do not lose their current chits.
