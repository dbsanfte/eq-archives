RELEASED TITLES:

ULTIMA ONLINE
Released in 1997 by Electronic Arts/Origin Systems Incorporated (OSI)

While some disagree on the title, I feel Ultima Online was the first true MMOG to ever be released, and the most defining online game ever produced. In over 3 years with millions of copies sold, 200,000+ active users UO has shown longevity and features that have yet to be equaled by the competition.

Arguably the most defining characteristic of Ultima Online was the ability of players to shape their own place in the world, to chose to fight, explore, or support others through crafts. The utter complexity of the game, and depth allowed for diverse communities to form, for players to come together for mutual defense and friendship.

Quick advancement, ease of acquiring items, and relatively painless acquisition of wealth allowed the player-base to spend more game time on other efforts such as social gatherings, hunts, Player Versus Player, or whatever took their liking. Ownership of buildings also further strengthened the feeling of community or tie to the area one called "home". The activity of guilds banding together to form large communities, buying out or fighting for possession of small islands was not unheard of.

Technically the 3D isometric display giving a birds eye view of the action in some ways captured the very essence of UO, putting the emphasis on the area or group, not the individual. Players being instantly aware of their surroundings and the action rather than focused on only what lay in front of them.

Conclusion: While the final credit goes towards the great members of guilds like ours, Ultima Online's excellent game design is as much to credit for building the large thriving guilds and communities that exist today.

As a footnote, Crimson Sky Empire closed the Ultima Online Faction in late 2000, after suffering tremendous churn in membership and changes in expectations that did not live up to the original code and spirit the guild was created in. These changes were brought about by fundamental game design changes by the Origin staff as they moved towards a more mainstream market and split all the servers (Shards as they called them) into two separate worlds. These separate worlds, one affectionately called Carebear land or Barneyland was for the Non-PvP players, or those merely looking for a safe haven. The other world more sinister and now featuring sort of a "capture the flag" type system contained the Player versus Player enthusiasts, including those of the old players that still refused to move to "Barneyland". It was this split that caused a significant amount of harm to anti-pvp and conquest guilds such as CSE that founded themselves on protecting the weak, and conquering enemies in a dynamic community of good and evil alike. The change that overtook Ultima Online gradually and then immediately after the split was the final nail in the coffin for a global dynamic roleplaying community. At our peak, the Crimson Sky Empire was the #2 Guild established on the Pacific Shard of Ultima Online with over 150 members (Our highest membership recorded was over 200).

 

EVERQUEST
Released in 1999 by Sony Online Entertainment/Verant

Released among the controversy of constant fundamental changes in Ultima Online, Everquest managed to take the MMOG community by storm eventually to overtake to membership base of the once darling of the industry, Ultima Online.

If you could call 3 years of development, simple, that's exactly how the gameplay could be described. Based upon a fundamental multiplayer text based design known as DIKU MUD, Everquest added a 3D client over the top of a character advancement system, driven by gameplay of killing server controlled monsters (MOBs) and NPC assigned quests. While the gameplay wasn't considered advanced to those familiar with MUD's that have existed for over a decade, the 3D engine was another leap forward in the industry, and as many envious publishers soon found the gameplay was 100% addictive. The addictive gameplay is much more akin to the craze of Pokemon' card trading where it's simple gameplay and viral aspects such as acquisition of levels/items caused players to devote unheard of amounts of time (average players often spending 4-8hrs+ a day event for comparatively small events/gains).

Much of the challenge to players comes in the form of organizing their groups with the necessary character classes, and the strategy of efficiently using those characters abilities against various monsters.

Even today Everquest continues to be improved upon, the developers adding many positive technical changes to the client, and some progressive changes to their customer support which are often considered draconian in their methods. The latest upcoming add-on called Everquest: Shadows of Luclin features graphics rivaling that of many single player 3D games, changes in the player race model graphics, and much more. There is even talk of a totally new method of handling visible graphics and network data, which when implemented will cause servers to only process and send 3D data that a character can actually see. This change will significantly cut down on exploits due to packet sniffing, and the large amounts of data being sent thus making it possible to have more dense playing areas.

Crimson Sky had a very naive and rough start in Everquest. It was attacked with the same presumption and convictions that built the guild in Ultima Online, the mistake being the fundamental difference in both games emphasis on community and gameplay. The first sign of trouble was the ousting of the CSE/EQ Faction Leader who was blamed for the problems affecting the guild. Whether he was actually at fault, or these were but symptoms of a deeper problem in game design were to be discovered much later in CSE's history in Everquest.

Due much to the design of Everquest and the unchanging nature of CSE's codes and spirit, the guild never thrived as it did in UO, even to this date within the game. Membership stagnated in the mid hundreds compared to the several hundred that flourished in UO. This is not to say that the CSE/EQ community had any undue hardship, for they enjoyed the game, and the community built, however the personal and outside peer pressure to continue to advance to higher levels, equipment, and uber camping prove to be a constant enemy of the guild. If it weren't for the undying effort of many CSE members in Everquest, the Faction would have been closed long ago like many of our allies and guilds such as KGB or Krieger who experienced similar success in Ultima Online but also found Everquest a unforgiving MMOG when it came to expanding their guilds. The guilds that didn't leave Everquest, yielded to the design dropping their ideals in favor of systems that favored the Uber powerleveling numbers game. An interesting fact of the design is it's very black and white appeal to gamers, roughly breaking down to 50% who absolutely hated the gameplay, to the 50% who absolutely loved and played it constantly (affectionately calling it EverCrack).

ANARCHY ONLINE
Released in 2001 by Funcom

The Company Line:

Anarchy Online is set almost 30,000 years into the future, in a science-fiction world created uniquely for the game. Players will participate in the ongoing conflict between revolutionary rebels and the mighty Omni-Tek corporation, and ultimately play a key role in deciding the fate of the planet, Rubi-Ka.

Anarchy Online is promised to be story-driven, with a four year story arc already developed (although it's said players would have some say in how it goes) and faction-based PvP between the Rebels and the evil Omni-Tek corporation. One of the new features of Anarchy Online and MMORPG's is the ability for players to build their own quests, and offer rewards for other people to do their dirty work for them. Anarchy Online also spawns randomly generated "dungeons" for each adventuring party, thus solving overcrowding and camping problems experienced in games like Everquest.

Anarchy Online promised a LOT, and as they suffer from the typical launch problems it still remains to be seen if the issues faced in the Beta test continue to thwart the game into the next couple of years. While on paper much of the game design looks fresh and new, the majority of it has been borrowed from Verants Everquest style of design leaving little original improvement other than the sci-fi theme.

Some aspects like PvP which were hyped quickly lost their luster as players found combat boring and lacking any finesse' (How much challenge is there in selecting an enemy and pressing your attack key?), The pseudo PK switch in cities, and limited amount of PvP+ areas where combat can actually occur just leads to the PK gang bangs of past only seen in Ultima Online - leaving much to be desired on the R-O-L-E-P-L-A-Y-I-N-G front of the games so-called 4 year storyline.

One questionable aspect of private player/group quests is their relevance in a MMORPG title. At what point do you decide to segregate your players from the rest of the gaming populace in order to improve their quest experience? In other games such as Ultima Online, dungeon crawls were in public areas with risk and reward from the benefits or perils of friendly or hostile players. This fundamental change from social global interaction to private group interaction in what's claimed to be "Massively Multiplayer" brings to mind the term oxymoron. If gamers and companies are headed towards this trend, it could be a driving force of success for Non-MMOG's such as Neverwinter Nights that offer smaller 64 player run games and NO MONTHLY FEE.

This game will appeal to Everquest refugees who are looking for a change which I will guarantee to be an improvement over the EQ gameplay in some aspects. I expect Anarchy Online to suffer from a higher than normal churn rate of customers moving to future competitors as they're released due to the lack of overall originality in their title and high ratio of hype to game reality.

CSE remains uncommitted to this title due to the limited percieved lifespan and guild friendly features.

 

WORLD WAR II ONLINE
Released in 2001 by Playnet/Cornered Rat

World War II Online represents an entirely new category in the world of massively multiplayer online games. It brings air, ground, and sea combat of World War II to life in an online, real-time, persistent, and massively multiplayer environment. Now you can command and crew a variety of accurately modeled vehicles, aircraft, and ships or fight as a foot soldier in first person- combat with and against thousands of others players!

Another game I had the opportunity to beta test, and a very welcome change at that for an old time online Flight Simulation player (Warbirds, etc.) like myself. The graphics won't win any awards but the vehicles are well detailed functional, and don't detract from the fun factor of cruising around in a Panzer III with your allies engaging the enemy.

Even with the sub-par graphics, immersion is very good, especially when using voice communication programs to coordinate attacks and information. It's pretty common to actually see player morale raise or drop as they gain air support or suffer increased enemy straffing runs.

In small engagements this game is very fun, I'd say around 50-100 players per area in near visibility. When you get to areas with higher that level as experienced in the games launch in retail lag, bugs, inability to communicate effectively, general chaos, swarms of allies/enemies, and crashes make you wonder how much is too much in a MMOG attempting to recreate a real war. Whether they sacrificed fun for realism is something you'll have to ask yourself if you play this title.

 

JUMPGATE
To Be Released in late 2001 by Netdevil

The Company Line:

The player is the owner and pilot of a spacecraft, with the objective being to acquire wealth and status by engaging in trade, mining, and/or combat with that ship, and via roleplaying. Players may work on their own or together as members of a collaborative team. Players can use the funds they acquire to improve their craft in various ways, including weapons, engines, shields, and other modifications and upgrades.

With significant beta test experience in this title I will admit there is some addictive gameplay, mostly in the acquisition of levels and wealth, however long-term play and the diverse nature of the playerbase point towards some disturbing trends. One of the big issues this game suffers is trying to appeal to multiple gaming styles such as space combat simulation, trading, and exploration. The problem with this is balancing the Player Versus Player group and the non-combat group who want nothing to do with the PvP Players. In typical MMOG fashion you can't have one or the other, and a PvP switch instantly destroys the credibility of a dynamic space simulation. What solution NetDevil comes up for this problem remains to be seen.

Another issue with the game is long term playability due to their inability to add effective money-sinks other than new ships, especially for players who are able to avoid combat and the loss of their investment. This quickly leads to players trading their way into billionaire status, and then either going on a PvP rampage or giving away their wealth and quitting after "winning" the game.

There have always been plans to implement Capital Ships, an Expanded Galaxy, true Pirate/Smuggling, and a number other features considered *must-have* by the beta testers. As the release day is but months away and development as slow as ever, I'd estimate any improvements to be no less than a year away if they aren't already under severe pressure by their better developed competition (Eve Online, Earth & Beyond).

As of 10.01.01 3DO announced the release of JumpGate as it hits the retail market as the first space-sim MMORPG.

STARPEACE
Released in 2000

What do you get when you combine SimCity 2000 with a MMOG, a Criminal Underworld Simulation, and Player Governed Cities? You get Starpeace, one of what's probably many future Massively Multiplayer Strategy Simulations to be attempted. This project also suffered a number of setbacks after release forcing it's publisher to turn over control back to its developer for day to day management. A release date for the US is set for sometime in late 2001.

After being part of this games alpha testing over more than a year ago, I can attest to it's fun factor for the ability to play a SimCity(tm) style game with the community of a handful of other gamers. The drawback being the chaotic and progressively lower return of enjoyment as additional players are thrown into the mix ala MMOG's are known for. The question is, how many players are enough, and how many players are too much? Games such as this are starting to make it clear that not every game is MMOG material just because you can build it, doesn't mean they will come.

HORIZONS
To Be Released in 2001/2002 by Artifact Entertainment

In one of the seven empires or kingdoms, you will be able to play one of the widest range of different Characters and Races ever offered in a MMORPG. This immense 3D world, where land, sky and even air are fully used to play.

Become the well known Human, Elf or Dwarf, chose one of the less played characters like Ssslik (lizardmen), Giants, Dryads or Goblins, or take on a totally new experience by becoming a Vampire, Angel, Demon, or even a Dragon!

Horizons will include a various range of Deities, where worshipping each of these religions will grant you different ranges of knowledge, strength, weaknesses, protection levels and much much more. It's up to you to decide if you will worship Good, Neutral or Evil gods (there are 19 in total). The game will track your loyalty to the gods and they will either reward you for your loyalty, or punish you for disobeying their motivations.

One concept in Horizons, is the ability to create items yourself. Magical weapons, spellscrolls, Armour and much more. The creation of such things will exist out of several processes based on the complexity of the item to be created. Depending on the primary skill you have, you will be able to craft these items based on experience. This should prove to be a great boon to the craftsmen that thrived in Ultima Online, and help the game economy.

Another unique concept in the game is character age. It will be impossible to create an Ancient dragon in a bare three months, since your character will simply be too young to be so. Growing with age, your skills and abilities will refine and expand, putting powergaming in a whole other light. If you think Everquest was too addictive, or the fanaticism seen by UO players refreshing their houses weekly was too much, welcome to character aging!

Another feature will be the ability to own a house of yourself, as this has only been seen in Ultima Online to date with mixed results. Owning a house will be very expensive, a very rare occurrence, and players will have to purchase a piece of land from a real estate broker. The application of this feature will remain to be seen, hopefully overcrowding will be guarded against, however this is very promising as a money-sink to drive the game economy.

This is a VERY promising title, with some very ambitious goals. At a basic level it takes some of the best features found in Ultima Online, adds a second generation 3D client, and throws in everything but the kitchen sink to offer a MMORPG to be taken very seriously. A MMORPG of this scope is unlikely to be seen other than it's closest competitor Star Wars Galaxies (Rumor is SOE/Verant made an offer to publish Horizons). SWG however being in a totally different genre and with a very expensive license. All in all this is one to watch, curiously it has had very little press and could be the sleeper hit of 2002.

As of 10.01.02 theres has been yet another exodus of two key employees from Horizons, and a lockdown on the release of all information related to the game. Announcements from the company allude to a possible restructure of the entire game, and scaling back of the very game design that defined it. While the impact remains to be seen, this definately cannot bode well for such a once promising title.

 

DARK AGE OF CAMELOT
To Be Released in 2001/2002 by Mythic Entertainment

The Company Line:

Dark Age of Camelot is a massively multi-player online role-playing game conceived and developed by Mythic Entertainment. Set in the Kingdom of Albion in the years immediately following the death of King Arthur, players of the game enter a world in chaos, where Arthur’s peace has been shattered and dark forces threaten the Kingdom. Unlike traditional role-playing games in which a player’s greatest challenge is fighting computer-controlled monsters, players in Camelot will come face-to-face with their greatest challenge yet, other players. In Camelot, players must choose to be members of one of the three Realms that are striving for mastery in this chaotic world: The Britons, the Celts, or the Norse. The Britons are the former kingdom of Albion, once ruled by the great King Arthur. The Celts are from the wild and magical western island of Hibernia. The Norse are the large barbarians from the lands to the north of Albion.

Building upon the foundation laid by such great games as EverQuest, Asheron’s Call, and Ultima Online, the game features 3D-accelerated graphics with movable camera, multiple class and race combinations, and has built in and balanced team Player vs. Player (PvP). Unlike its predecessors, in Camelot, PvP conflict is an integral part of a player’s experience in the world, not an afterthought.

I had a chance to see this game live at E3, the graphics were as expected, and the gameplay look like typical EQ faire'. The problem DAoC is likely to encounter is it's stance on PvP, and the players it's trying to appeal to. It's simplistic faction based PvP may not be enough to separate itself what what appears to be more significant competition such as Shadowbane who also offer the ability to capture territory, siege cities, and alternative player professions such as merchants. It also faces significant competition from games such as Horizons which also promise a degree of flexible PvP PLUS a complex simulated world, the likes of which haven't been seen since UO's initial release. If it weren't for the fact there's nowhere else to go for the band of disgruntled American PK/PvP'ers after their issues with Shadowbane changing it's tune, it's doubtfull this title would have gained the large following it currently boasts.

SHADOWBANE
To Be Released in 2001/2002 by Wolfpack

Coming soon.

Shadowbane is a very unique title that's managed to carve itself out a rare niche for itself in a very crowded marked. Initially sold as a no-holds-barred PvP MMORPG after the fiasco of nerfed PvP changes in UO, and non-existant PvP in EQ, Shadowbane managed to catch the attention of the PvP community.

After some time in development the game and it's design have matured considerably, however the byline of "Crushing your enemies" is now replaced by "Build an Empire", and the focus moves from PvP to a more strategic but open ended game design straddling the fence between the PvPers and PvM group.

In some ways this game will compete with the likes of Horizons, Star Wars Galaxies, or systems similar to UO's original design, however the features allowing guilds to conquest over territory will ultimately make or break it's success in the market.

Due to it's distinct published features that compliment the conquest nature of guilds such as CSE, our guild has already sanctioned a Faction for Shadowbane at it's release. This well designed aspect of the game will lend very well to the building of guilds, communities, and a loyal playerbase..

 

NEOCRON
To Be Released in 2002 by Reakktor Media

Neocron is an action packed 3D MMORPG (Massively Multiplayer Online Roleplaying Game) set in a post apocalyptic metropolis.Your experience as a player is billed to be whatever you want, be it non-combat roleplaying or PvP.

Despite Planetside's claims otherwise, Neocron is actually the first massively multiplayer first-person shooter. It's the 27th century and the Earth isn't looking too good, so we've colonized other planets. The cities should be safe most of the time, as it's patrolled by powerful non-player characters (police and robots), but the surrounding outlying areas known as the outzone will be where most of the first-person shooter action takes place. Neocron will use a skill-class system that will allow players to be hackers, fighters, and Psi-masters, among other things.

An interesting title that has the possibility to bridge the gap between PvP and TRUE Roleplaying if they can only decide how to implement it in a manner that doesn't make one or the other an ignored aspect of the game.

EVE ONLINE
To Be Released in 2002 by Crowd Control Productions

The Company Line:

The game is set in an unknown portion of space, spanning thousands of solar systems, many of which are settled. Players begin by creating an in-game character, which starts the game equipped with a basic spaceship ready to explore the world. To begin with, players can trade goods between systems or conduct other money making activities, such as mining asteroids, gathering scientific data on unmanned systems or even cleaning up space debris. Gains made from such activity can be used to upgrade the ship with weapons and equipment, and also to develop the character by training him in various skills.

When a player has mastered the basics of the game, amassed some money and equipment and advanced his/her character through basic skills, the possibilities become almost endless. Players who wish to explore peaceful paths can continue to upgrade their ships to bigger and better-defended cargo vessels, purchase advanced mining or research equipment, and continue to develop their character by specializing in their preferred skills. Others will choose to explore more dangerous path, such as piracy, smuggling or bounty hunting. There will even be ways of making money through such activities as spying on other players and reporting illegal activity.

Eve Online is probably one of the most successful looking Space MMORPG to be released in what may be a semi-crowded market. From my beta testing experience in JumpGate I highly doubt Eve will have any problem stealing the market.
