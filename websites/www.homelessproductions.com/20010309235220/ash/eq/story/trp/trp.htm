The Rugged Plains

The great spirits must be toying with me, thought Kiedatar, as he trudged wearily through the rain. It always rained in the Karanas. For a moment, he felt a great yearning of homesickness, longing for the frozen north. But the Great Spirits had set him forth a challenge and he must obey.

"Seems a bit damp out," his companion, Lenhaj the wizard said. Kiedatar cast him a sour look, but the cheerful grin on the wizard's face caused him to give a slight smile. "I suppose you're right. I, too, detect a bit of moisture in the air."

The piercing cry of a lioness on the prowl disturbed their banter. Kiedatar gripped the haft of his axe tightly and looked around for the lioness. She was not to be seen. A clever beast.

"This way," Lenhaj said, heading toward a nearby rocky hill. "This will give us a vantage point from which to spy her out."

Kiedatar followed his friend and did his best to keep his already rusting axe from getting any wetter. It didn't seem to be working.

As they neared the crest of the hill, the sudden smell of lion spoor filled the air. A low throaty cough, a flash of movement, and the lioness was upon them. Kiedatar swung his axe, but the beast jumped nimbly out of the way. For several moments, the beast dodged his attacks leaving the barbarian with several deep wounds in return. Lenhaj's voice whispered sibilant words that Kiedatar did not understand nor did he care to, but the spell did the trick. The lioness attempted to lunge, but found that the earth had bound her back paws. Kiedatar stepped forward and hammered the axe into the beast.

Even trapped it put up a ferocious fight, managing to hold the barbarian off until a blast of frost from the wizard's hands stunned it long enough for Kiedatar to step in and deliver the death blow.

"Whew, that was close, my barbarian friend," Lenhaj said, leaning on his staff. "I was about to get ready to start running until ... just what are you doing?" Kiedatar looked up from where he was cutting the heart from the lioness.

"It is a custom of my people. Eating the heart of a lion will bring you it's courage." He pulled the aforementioned organ from the beast's chest and brought it to his teeth, tearing off a bite. He held it out to the wizard. "Want some?" "Er," Lenhaj said, looking slightly greenish, "I think I'll pass this time." He looked away to the south. "You always do this?"

"Only with certain animals. A lion's heart will grant you courage. A bear's strength. And a wolf's will bring you intelligence and cunning. They are manifestations of the Great Spirit. He sends them to us to challenge us so that we will be ready when the darkness comes."

"What darkness?"

"An ancient myth of my people. There will come a time when an unholy darkness sweeps across the land, crushing all before it. Unless ... unless we can somehow stop it."

"And you believe this is darkness is coming soon?" Lenhaj asked, looking around. "Whether or not it will actually come in my lifetime does not matter, friend. What matters is that we are prepared."

Kiedatar popped the last bit of lion heart into his mouth, chewing it slowly. "Besides, I think the time is coming."

"You do?"

"Aye. How else do you explain the increasing numbers of undead that walk the land? The ever more organized tribes of gnolls and orcs and such? Everywhere you turn, you see a necromancer or a shadow knight. The darkness is coming, friend Lenhaj, and I don't mean to be unprepared."

Kiedatar gave the corpse an appraising look. "Hmm, don't think we're going to be able to salvage that pelt."

"It's okay," Lenhaj said inclining his head down the slope, "I see a bear that will do instead."

"Ah, the Great Spirit is kind today."
