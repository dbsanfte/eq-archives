Solutions

 

In the course of the last two months, I have participated in one of today's phenomenon's: EverQuest. Oh...I played Ultima Online as well, but I wouldn't really consider it a phenomenon...It was more a test run I suppose since this genre of games is so new...but it was lacking (at least for me) any real potential for roleplay, or even more importantly (again for me) fun. In fact, one of the biggest problems I saw with UO, I also see with EverQuest. It seems the designers of these games want the players to write their own story, and create their own adventures....The problem is most people are not used to such freedom, and they don't know what to do with it.

This, if read wrong, might make me sound like a dungeon master who controls his players...controlling your player base is a horrid idea. Indeed, Everquest has done a wonderful job in creating a world in which we can create our own fantasy lives....but do they not seem pointless? How often have you just stood around, and wondered what to do? Sometimes, as humans, we need a goal set for us, and if we never have goals, we feel like we aren't getting anywhere...much is the same in EverQuest.

What could fix this? How about a basic story-line...Faction ratings are wonderful...but WHO are some of the factions that are listed. Why don't we ever hear of these factions doing anything. Why do armies never attack Freeport, and what are all these monoliths and ruins? Why can't we go to the libraries in the world and discover the origins of these? Why are there no NPC's in taverns or anywhere that could clue us in on grand quests...perhaps even quests that could only be completed once, and which might even be failed?

And thus, it brings me to the Quest Counter. If a PC approaches an NPC and ACCEPTS a quest (perhaps it would bring up a window with a YES box and a NO box) the PC gets a flag, and can no longer accept any quests until the one he accepted is completed. If there is a set amount of time for him to get the quest completed, and he fails, maybe he would set off a chain of events...If he did complete the quest, since it could only be done once, he might even get a good reward. And in the same way that completed quests raise faction, perhaps failed ones lower it.

Another problem with EQ (as seen on the newsgroup) is the economic system. Verant wants players to rely on each other. This is a great concept, and since there is a PVP switch, I think players would be much more inclined to work together than in UO. Buying and selling merchandise from players can be very tedious at times, but still it is a good system. What is not good, however, is the fact that low level characters are becoming more and more able to buy higher level equipment. This is due to the extremely low prices that vendor's will give for a particular item. Case in point, the Minotaur Axe. It's not a really high level item, but initially the selling price (to PC's) was around 15 - 20 platinum. Now it is down to 4 platinum (on Cazic-Thule, at least). If you try to sell the same item to a vendor, it will sell for 3 platinum, 8 gold.

So, it looks like the PC market has fallen to vendor prices. You can check with other items, as well...such as a Greater Lightstone. They also sell for 4 platinum pieces, and a vendor will buy them for around the same. What this means for our market in EverQuest, is that eventually all prices will fall to vendor prices. Verant should increase the buying cost of the vendors to whatever they want an items base price to be. And another thing they should have realized, is that the lower the price, the earlier the levels will be for an item.

I only bring this up because Verant has designed the game to take a year for the average player to reach level 50. In doing so, they designed the game with the concept that there would always be something new to buy, try, or find. If the market gets saturated with high level items for little cost, this goal will not be met. In fact, if they don't invent new items, they will lose accounts of high level players who have nothing left to do. (Or even mid-level characters who can just buy anything they want).

Another problem that is there is a never ending supply of items and money. This has been touched upon heavily in the newsgroups. The obvious solution would be to begin making items wear out. This would make the blacksmith skill useful as well. Most people would complain about this, in the same way that they complain about getting a "bind" in a city. So, the way around this would be to make NPC blacksmiths who will fix items for a set price. The price should be a little above what a normal PC blacksmith would charge. Eventually, an item would not be able to be repaired (unless it is magical), and thus, would have to be replaced.

Along with all this equipment we've been talking about comes the dreaded word: Camping. The new Lore item tag is a great start. In fact, it's a really nice addition. BUT, it only helps the problem a little. And it encourages having mule characters to hold these valuable items. One thing they could add would be a random equipment generator. It would make alternate equipment (which spawns on appropriate creatures, of course) with similar stats. So you can't dual wield PGT's anymore? Fine. Now you found a Pristine Longsword with X delay, and Y damage. Something comparable to a really good sword, but perhaps, not magical. If enough variables were thrown in, there could be an infinite amount of different weapons in the game. Maybe even some better than a PGT or a BLW, or demon claw...you get the picture. You could have an extra tough Orc Warrior who wields this weapon...perhaps he is only tougher because he is wielding a better weapon than the others...

Camping is also caused by another factor. I just noticed this last night when I was in Befallen. If they don't want campers in their game, why do you have to wait 15 minutes for the creature to spawn that holds the key to get to the next section of a dungeon? If you're a higher level character, you don't want to run around on the top level. Everything there is green, and is the same on the second level. They are sending our some mixed signals, to say the least. I do like the fact that the doors are locked....but maybe it would be a good idea to spread locked doors and containers around, so that, perhaps, we might have a rogue (of appropriate level) around to crack the door for us.

I have a lot of gripes about the rogue class...and I haven't even played one before. I realize that their skills are broken and that Verant is fixing them. But, typically, no game does this class justice. They should be able to sneak and hide at the same time, and disguise themselves to the point of assuming a new identity...perhaps a human rogue might walk into Neriak, and the people not even think twice about it. The strength of this class is their cunning, and stealth: Why not let the class live up to what it is?

About the only other real problem that I am going to address in this essay, are Enchanters. These poor people can't enchant anything. Perhaps the people at Verant didn't have time to do everything that they wanted. I don't know. If I was an enchanter, I would want to make magical weapons and magical armor. Enchanting metal is only part of it. Here is a neat system that could be used for this (AND it makes players work together, in the way that Verant wanted).

An enchanter must enchant a piece of armor or a weapon, so that the metal can take a spell (either offensive or defensive from a select set of spells). . A wizard (or cleric) would cast a spell upon the piece. Different spells might make different effects, as Haste on a sword might make the delay a bit faster. At any rate, with each enchantment, the armor/weapon would undergo a check to see if the spell holds or not. Failure destroys the piece. As a final step, a magician must come and cast a spell to make the enchantment permanate. And this would require a check as well. So, in this way, magic weapons would still be rare, hard to get, and would be expensive, but it would give high level players a lot to do. It might also diminish camping somewhat, if they could get a PC made sword. You might even add that different quality metals react different to spells. Even better might be a random element that might make swords react better to certain creatures, or maybe not even have the correct spells place upon it.

This system would require extremely high level characters. In this way you keep them from being too common (until further on in the games development). The system could be balanced in such a way that no weapon gets too powerful.

Lastly, I would like to address gained experience. I have seen a lot of suggestions on ways to gain experience, both good and bad. Killing monsters should defiantly not be the only way to gain it, but neither should walking a long distance give you any. How about experience for discovering locations people have not been? Just like in pen and paper RPG's you can gain experience for stumbling upon some treasure hidden away. Why not in EQ? The world is big enough to hide many items, bags, loot, etc. These would encourage exploration.

Another way to gain exp besides monsters, and exploration, would be a little for increasing skills. Perhaps every five or ten points a skill goes up, you receive a bit of experience. If items wore out, and a blacksmith had to repair it, he would get experience for every several pieces he fixed. In this way, he does not feel like he's wasting his time, and his character even grows from it...and all he's doing is helping the player run economy.

Success needs to be rewarded, and although I feel that EQ is one of the greatest games I have ever played, I find myself inventing my own reasons to play. I love the graphics, and the atmosphere that I and my friends put into the game, but the NPC's, factions, and even the gods do very little. It is very easy to break the game down into levels, exp, and the next camp to....well, camp...but a level based game does not need to be this way--especailly as close as EQ comes to creating a wonderful deep world.

These are some of the things that would help to make EQ a better place to play. I think these ideas would help the world in general, from the roleplayers to the DoOdz. I can't see where any of this would actually hurt anybody.

Not to be misunderstood, I think Verant and all involved have done a wonderful job with EverQuest. It's highly addictive, and it's fun to play. But since it's really only the second game of it's type, it still needs to be critiqued. Things need to be adjusted. It's a new animal to say the least, but it's a great improvement overall than the first one, and as long as they keep taking these kinds of steps, perhaps the game we all seek is on the horizon.

 

Anonymous

Cazic - Thule
