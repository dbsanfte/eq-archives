Alan Discusses EverQuest Adventures PS2 Game
- Aurum @ 7:10 PM PDT
One of the most active topics on the Developer's Corner today was that of the upcoming version of EverQuest for PS2. Alan first spoke on this topic here:

    For those of you that haven't seen this plastered all over the fan sites this morning…

    We have announced EverQuest Adventures for the PlayStation 2.

    We've got a placeholder site up right now for the game itself. Check back there for more information.

    It looks like I'll have to break down and buy a PS2…

    Alan
More on this topic right here:
    Obviously there has been a lot of talk on the net and through the Developer's Corner about EverQuest Adventures. Rod Humble, Executive Producer for both EverQuest and EverQuest Adventures, has been reading your comments and has some answers for you all. So I'll just get out of the way…

    In answer to your questions:

    "Is it a port?"
    No. The product started mid 2000 with the explicit goal of being designed from the ground up as a PS2 game. A port simply would not have worked. Adventures is designed to be played in the living room which is a totally different environment from the study or bedroom where PC games are played.

    "Will I be able to visit all my favorite places?"
    Yes and No. EQOA is set 500 years before the original EverQuest so Antonious Bayle is not yet alive. The migration to Odus has not yet been completed and there are still Elves on Tunaria (it is not yet known as Antonica). We have built the world from scratch, you can visit many places which are familiar in EQ but don't expect them to look the same or even have the same inhabitants.... :)

    "How do I play without a keyboard?"
    Very easily as it happens. Last week I got to level ten around Moradhin just doing quests from the comfort of my couch. Certainly chat is a lot easier with a keyboard but not mandatory. I don't know how others will want to play but for me lying back on my couch with both feet up controller in one hand beverage in the other as my friends and I go dungeon delving is the preferred method.

    "Does the game have the same classes, abilities and races?"
    No. 500 years ago on Norrath races and classes were different (some didn't even exist yet!). While there will be familiarities there are many new spells and abilities that existed back in Norraths age of adventure. We will post more details on this in the upcoming months.

    "Is the EQOA the same team that works on EQ Live?"
    No. The Adventures team has been separate for nearly two years now working in the dark and in secret :)

    "Will I be able to transfer my own character to EQOA or see PC players from the original game?"
    No. Your characters wouldn't have been born yet! (Unless they were Elves possibly). The games run on totally different servers.

    "How many players does EQOA support?"
    More than the original EQ per server. Each world should support at least 3000 people simultaneously who can go wherever they want. There are no zones or restrictions on players going anywhere its quite possible to wander by hundreds of other players as you journey across Norrath.

    "What about Fippy????"
    Clan Darkpaw is alive and well in the Age of Adventure :)

    Hope this helps!

    Rod Humble
    Executive Producer Studio 1


    There you have it.

    Thanks Rod!

    Er… I mean Mr. Humble! :p

    Alan


Patch Follow-ups
- Aurum @ 6:22 PM PDT
Alan "Absor" VanCouvering posted two patch follow-ups this afternoon on the Developer's Corner:

    What about taunt in the new cazic thule?

    What about the complete heal bug?

    What about the extra aggro from shaman slow?

    Actually, most of that stuff has been fixed on Test. Unfortunately we weren't able to push those code changes Live this time around because of some other errors that the code would have caused. We're making some changes to the server architecture (including character files), and we didn't want any problems to crop up. So a bunch of the fixes that we've got on Test didn't get to the Live servers this time around.

    Taunt isn't, technically, broken in Cazic. The rules for taunt in old-world zones make most of the NPCs in that zone too high level to taunt. We'll be fiddling with that rule when we get a chance to patch code.

    The bug with complete heal was/is related to filters. I don't fully understand it, but we think we'll have that fixed the next time we get to patch code.

    We plan to make changes to the way slow spells give agro. The plan is to return them to their old aggravating levels. But we need to gather more data before we can make the fix properly. We weren't able to get to that before the patch.

    Alan
And now follow #2:
    Two items of note that were missing or wrong in the patch message that I wanted to clear up:

    The proc on the Iksar Skyiron Cudgel of the Arisen has been fixed. You'll need to trade ask your GM to trade you a new one for your busted version.

    - Fixed a 'safe spot' in Chardok - wasn't what happened. What we fixed was the zone out of Chardok. Zoning out should put you in the proper place in the Burning Woods now.

    Alan

    Sony Online Entertainment Brings Best-Selling EverQuest® Brand Exclusively to Playstation®2 Computer Entertainment System

But like the original EverQuest, it will feature intricate character development, continuous player interaction and a strong sense of community, which is sure to create a phenomenon that PlayStation 2 gamers have never experienced before. With an installed base of more than 8.8 million units in the U.S., the PlayStation 2 represents an opportunity to expand the massively multiplayer online role-playing game onto new platforms, delivering a whole new genre to the console gamer.

This is truly a new experience for the console gamer."

By connecting directly to dedicated servers, SOE can help to ensure low latency gameplay and fast connection times. Gamers simply insert the disc and the world of Norrath is just a few button pushes away. By 2005, Gartner Group estimates that 18 percent of American households will own an online enabled game console.

MMORPG Documentary Film
- Aurum @ 7:40 PM PDT
From Brimestone:

MMORPGs go to Hollywood

Real People Virtual Worlds is an upcoming documentary film about MMORPGs and the communities that have grown up around them. Through interviews with players, game developers, and observers of cyberculture, as well as footage shot directly from the game environments themselves, filmmaker Tracy Spaight explores how people interact with one another in the textually and graphically mediated settings of on-line worlds.

Production began in January 2002, thanks in part to a grant from the Texas Council for the Humanities. "Our first filming expeditions took us to FanFaires in Austin and Dallas," Tracy says. "Hundreds of people come out for these events to meet their online friends - to meet the person behind the keyboard." In the coming months, the crew will shoot interviews with game developers and academics, to capture different perspectives on these environments and their inhabitants. "When we begin editing later this year, we'll put all these different groups together in a conversation," Tracy explains.

The Project Goals

The film will 1) explore how people use technology to create novel forms of community. 2) examine what people find so compelling about life on the screen. 3) foster informed public discussion of internet culture. "If our world is to be inhabited by people who spend their free time inventing themselves anew through role-playing and fantasy," Tracy says, "then we should study this phenomenon and begin to make sense of it. Who's playing these games? What do they find in these environments? Do on-line worlds bring people together or do they detract from real life relationships?"

Tracy points out that the MMORPG genre will be a $1.8 billion industry in 2004. "In the coming decades, we may see the emergence of five-sense utilizing artificial environments, which are as real as the world around us," Tracy says. "Very possibly, MMORPGs will emerge as the entertainment medium of the twenty-first century. I think it's important to have a conversation as a society about these environments."
