Project "M" Delayed Indefinitely
- Aurum @ 8:29 PM PST
'Tis a sad day in the land of Norrath. Alan announced on the Developer's Corner what we've suspected for months now; the project allowing players to play as an NPC is now on hold:

    Hey, i was just thinking back to the day you guys implemented the "Monster Button." I was just wondering if you have any plans of re-implementing it on the live servers, or atleast the test servers again. I ask this because the day that button got implemented was seriously the most enjoyable time i ever had playing Everquest. I cant even begin to describe how fun it was to actually take control of monsters and be able to engage PCs and interact with other monsters. Not only was it fun, but it also added an entirly new level of play to the game. Everyone else i know in real life who plays everquest agrees with this as well. I was just wondering if there is a chance we will ever see it again any time soon. Thanks for reading this! heh hope i submitted it in the right area

    Charles Rojo (Sogar Gofin, 47 druid of Solusek Ro)

    It was fun, wasn't it?

    Unfortunately that project is very much on hold right now. Many of the changes and updates we've been making lately have made the old functionality unworkable. There isn't any time in our schedules for fixing it, there are too many priority concerns right now. There were also some concerns from players (and us) that players being monsters is rather disruptive to play on servers.

    Playing an NPC was indeed a blast. But I wouldn't expect it to return in the foreseeable future.

    Alan


Protecting Your EQ Account
- Aurum @ 8:26 PM PST
As its been stated a million times over, never give out your EQ password. For further elaboration check out Alan VanCouvering's latest post:

    Just an idea...
    You might want to put together a little disclaimer (Verant will never ask for your username or password by mail or in game... etc.. etc...) and have it auto added to all your email correspondance (especially the mass mailing type emails). We (an ISP) do something similar with our monthly system status/server maintenance/features emails, except ours has to do with viruses.

    --
    Tyas

    Well, we don't send out a lot of mass email (though we do have that neato newsletter…).

    But it is certainly worth pointing out again, we will never ask for your password. We don't need to know it. We only need it when you log in and on the guide application. We will never ask for it by email.

    You should never give out your password and you should change it frequently. Don't pick a simple password. Don't make your password the same as your character name or your login ID. Don’t use EQ words (don't use a class or race name for a password).

    I don't mean to sound like I'm being harsh, but protecting your password is important. I spent a few years working in a government office. I can't tell you how many times I was able to locate the users password just by glancing around their cubicle, either in written form (heck, sometimes sticky-noted to the monitor), by noticing that they had an affinity for a certain animal or by learning the name of their kids by reading the signature from their art projects. The same can happen using the Internet. If you choose a password that is easily guessed, someone will guess it. Use your kids name and then spend some time chatting on a message board and you just might mention it.

    The simple solution is to use a non-word for a password. Make it something you can remember, but that isn't a word.

    /em sigh.

    Sorry about that… too many years dealing with password security. :)

    The important point here is, don't tell anyone your password, no matter how official the email looks.

    We won't ask for your password.

    A virtual cookie to the first person to guess the most common place I've found a 'hidden' password in an office situation.

    Alan

EverQuest Player Commits Suicide, Family Plans to Sue
- Aurum @ 9:00 PM PST
The death of an EverQuest player has made headlines in Milwaukee Journal Sentinel yesterday. Shawn Woolley commited suicide after allegedly living a life that consisted of playing EverQuest for upwards of 12 hours a day. The family is now planning to sue Sony Online Entertainment. Here's an excerpt from the article:

    On Thanksgiving morning last year, Shawn Woolley shot himself to death at his apartment in Hudson. His mother blames the game for her son's suicide. She is angry that Sony Online Entertainment, which owns EverQuest, won't give her the answers she desires. She has hired an attorney who plans to sue the company in an effort to get warning labels put on the games.
What she intends to accomplish with this suit is explained a bit here:
    Woolley has tried tracing her son's EverQuest identity to discover what might have pushed him over the edge. Sony Online cites its privacy policy in refusing to unlock the secrets held in her son's account.

    She has a list of names her son scrawled while playing the game: "Phargun." "Occuler." "Cybernine." But Woolley is not sure if they are names of online friends, places he explored in the game or treasures his character may have captured in quests.
Our condolences go out to Shawn's family and friends. While there are doubts surrounding the lawsuit’s legitimacy, most notably concerning the desire to place "warning" labels on MMORPGs, it seems understandable that the family wishes to answer the many questions surrounding Shawn's death.

EQ Third Anniversary State of the Game, Part 2
- Aurum @ 3:08 PM PST
Alan unveiled another installment of the State of the Game updates this afternoon on the Developer's Corner. In this announcement the EverQuest Team elaborates on changes that will soon be coming to the lands of Norrath:

    As promised, we would like to explain in more detail some of the changes that you'll be seeing in EverQuest.

    Some of these changes are already available on the Test Server and will be arriving on Live servers just in time for EverQuest's third anniversary. Others you may expect to find on Test in the coming weeks, which we hope to make available to everyone soon afterward.


    For those new to EverQuest, as well as our mid-level players:

    o The need to sit down and stare at the spellbook to regain mana has been removed at all levels. (Sitting down and having Meditation skill are still required.)

    o When starting a new character for the first time, it can take quite some time to get into that class' core abilities. In some cases, the style of gameplay needs to shift dramatically. That being the case, we've accelerated the rate at which people gain experience up to level ten.

    o In addition, undue frustration can occur during these youthful times. Traumatic events such as losing a corpse and not knowing where to find it or how to retrieve it can be quite the ordeal. With that in mind, we've changed the way that death works below level ten. Characters level nine and under who die will appear at their bind point with all of their equipment intact. (A short-lived corpse will still be left at the site of the disaster to allow the player to receive a resurrection.)

    o Experience gained in dungeons designed for players level 30 and under will be increased. For many people, there is nothing more satisfying in EverQuest than adventuring in dungeons. By giving people the tools to help them succeed earlier, and rewards to match, we hope that more players will discover why many people feel this way.

    o In the spirit of getting characters into more dangerous and rewarding situations, earlier, the following spell lines have been altered to make them easier to use and more widely available, across more level ranges.

    * Resurrection, for Clerics and Paladins.
    * Summon Corpse, for Necromancers and Shadowknights.
    * Succor/Evacuate, for Druids and Wizards

    o The bind points of starting characters will more closely match the areas they are already familiar with. (No more getting lost as new characters die in town, only to show up someplace outside.)

    o New characters now start with considerably more food and drink.

    o After applying advanced mathemagics, the commonly known "hell-level" experience gain effect in levels 30, 35, 40, and 45 has been largely done away with. Further, the "post-hell-level" experience loss in levels 31, 36, 41, and 46 was driven from the face of Norrath as well.


    For all EverQuest players:

    o Rumors abound that Bristlebane, God of Mischief, for reasons of his own design, has infused the Gnome and Halfling races with new potential professions. (Whether this is a blessing or a curse has yet to be decided.)

    o The long-requested global chat channels are well on their way. We'll be supporting player-created chat channels for raids, for friends, for total strangers - Anything you'd like to use them for. Expect a documentation update (eqmanual_supplement.doc in your EverQuest directory) explaining how to use these soon.

    o We've cleaned up what happens while you're zoning. The days of a frozen screen full of blurry text may well be over, forever. (The days of losing /tells, /guild chat, and group messages while zoning will hopefully be heading out the door behind them.)

    o While we have aimed to support armor/skin layer tinting on the majority of video cards, some of the abandoned video cards that are still used to play EverQuest unfortunately do not have the power to handle this feature. With that in mind, we will soon be implementing a switch that will allow players to enable armor/skin tinting at their discretion. (Stay tuned to a patch message, coming soon.)


    The last item we'd like to mention is a brief update on caster and priest rebalancing. (Please note that the balancing act is still going on, across the board. We wanted to update you on the progress we've made so far.)

    o Expect to see the first of the Focus Items for spell casters and spell-casting hybrids soon. We will leave the specifics to you to discover, but rest assured that useful and exciting focus items are making their way into the game as we speak.

    o Enchanters' Memory Blur line, and aspects of Rapture and Glamour of Kintaz have been improved.

    o The grouping requirement for the Translocate series of spells has been removed.

    o Clerics' Smite line has been improved.

    o The Post-50 Lich line for Necromancers has been redistributed.

    o Necromancers' Levant and Skin of the Shadow have been changed to allow them to be used any time of the day.

    o Necromancers' Quivering Veil of Xarn has been improved.

    Many of the caster balance changes to date have been made with feedback from the dedicated players of these classes, both from EMails and message boards. We're grateful for the time and effort that has been put into many well-reasoned comments we've received.


    Thank you for helping us make our world, yours.

    - The EverQuest Team

Alan on Making Tough Choices
- Aurum @ 6:48 PM PST
On the Developer's Corner, Alan VanCouvering posted a new update focusing on the EverQuest team's ability to make hard choices, mostly in the form of a set of player-based observations. Here's the scoop:

    Before you read this, I want to point out that I'm posting this because it's a nice positive comment, and it's nice to post those some times. Yes, I'm trying to make us look good. That's part of what I do. But I also think that the analysis of these changes is well done, and shows some of the decisions that we've had to make over the last year or so.

    Alan:

    I appreciated the comments on the manastone. Having played with parties that had one I realized how difficult it would be for the designers to accomodate both parties that had a manastone and parties that did not.

    This led me to comment on a number of very positive changes that represent change in position for Verant. Each of these changes in position have their own story, but taken together they show a company committed to providing the best possible product for its customers:

    1. Manastones--Clerics without manastones sometimes had trouble getting into raid groups. The ability of a cleric to manastone down and then do a complete heal was clearly and visibly unbalancing. It also diminished the class-defining Cannibalize and Dark Empathy lines. Tough call to restrict usage to the Old World...but right call.

    2. Donal's Breastplate of Mourning--Mana free complete heals used strategically mean that the players always win all single fights with a main tank plus complete heal cycle...and the clerics are at full mana at the end. Again notice that clerics without the breastplate were often left out of raid groups. Tough call to remove the complete heal...right for the game.

    3. Soulbinders--This game is designed for interclass dependence. You assemble the parts to make a stronger whole than you can make with just parts. But late at night and particularly for overseas players, bind affinity wasn't always easy to come by. Compromise? Soulbinders are not always in the most convenient locations, but they give you a bind when you need one. Excellent choice.

    4. Rangers and defense--Rangers by design are offensive melees with spells. They are based off of a chain-armor class 'template' that they share with Rogues and Shaman. The chain disadvantage first becomes apparent around 39th level when the Ranger's Defense skill starts falling behind Warrior's. Rangers were finding themselves unable to be 'light tanks' and actually take damage the way they were used to. First Verant implemented Jolt to mimic Rogue evade and permit the Ranger with personal aggro control.

    Then after letting that change sink in they came back and added defense to the Rangers. Result is a class that is VERY powerful in melee but can still take an appropriate licking (and continue ticking). Kudoes for the principle to implement the vision of the design and for tweaking it where appropriate.

    5. Removing teleport restrictions for Emerald Jungle, Skyfire, Great Divide, Wakening Lands, and Cobalt Scar--When the Kunark and Velious expansions came out, the designers decided there would be three ways to get to Kunark (Firiona Vie, The Overthere, and the Combines/Dreadlands portals). You had to port from inside a Kunark dungeon to use Emerald Jungle or Skyshrine evacs. Similarly when Velious came out you could either take the boat to Iceclad or get a port to the Iceclad Portal--you had to get a tooth to go to the other portals. These restrictions were sensible storytelling limitations that controlled how players progressively saw Kunark and then Velious.

    When Shadows of Luclin came out, Verant properly felt that the world was 'big enough' and removed the Kunark Dungeon and Velious 'Tooth' limitations. Tough choice, good for the game...allows the teleporting classes to get near fellow guild members and friends so they can be moved to a spire so they CAN spire up to Luclin.

    Each of these decisions took time to make. Some of them were accompanied by cries of "Nerf". Others were generally well accepted by the playing community. But each of them demonstrates the character of the company to do the right thing for the game and for the players. Congratulations on making tough choices and sticking to your guns.

    Please continue to give us the big picture perspective as you make these kind of tough decisions. It helps us players see that what seems right or fun for an individual player may be very harmful when the whole game is considered.

    Saeul


    Thanks Saeul. If I didn't know better I'd think you worked here. :)

    We really are trying to look out for the game as a whole, and sometimes that means making decisions that don't always please the majority of our customers in the short term. We can only do what we think is best and adapt where needed.

    Alan
