Petition Discussion
- Aurum @ 8:28 PM PDT
A thoughtful topic was started by Mr. VanCouvering on the Developer's Corner this afternoon:

    For as long as I’ve been involved with EverQuest, as a player and as an employee, petitions have been a popular practice. Someone will write out a list of ideas that they think should be implemented. They’ll post those ideas to a message board, usually one that caters to members of a certain class. Usually these ideas will be discussed and refined. Eventually someone starts a new thread and asks people to post to the thread in order to ‘sign’ the petition. Then someone (or everyone) sends a copy to us.

    This is a natural thing for a community to do. And often this is a very good way to generate conversation and can result in some good ideas.

    However…

    For us as a team there are several problems with ‘petitions’. Unfortunately these problems make it so that petitions often end up being harmful to the production of useful ideas and they hamper good communication between the players and the company. A good idea is a good idea. Having four hundred people send in a good idea doesn’t make that idea a better idea. Yes, we’ve missed a few good ideas in the past, but there are better methods for getting those ideas across. We think that the Dev Corner is one of those methods.

    We’d much rather have one person send in a good idea than a thousand people send in a list of ideas. The single idea is easier to recognize, easier to discuss, easier to reply to. It’s going to be clearer and far less cluttered. A laundry list of ideas is very hard to respond to. A petition that has a dozen suggestions basically requires a reasoned argument in response to each of those suggestions, even if some of the suggestions are obviously bad. The problem here is two-fold. First, we have to spend the time replying to the whole petition rather than just the good ideas. So just about any reply to a petition is going to sound negative, and this makes it generally a bad idea to reply to them. It makes us look mean, when we’re really a bunch of teddy bears.

    Also, by their very nature these petitions are extremely long. It’s not uncommon to see a petition with a few thousand words or more. A very time-consuming thing to read. I know, I’ve read every petition written about EverQuest over the last 16 months or so. Petitions end up being bloated because of their create-by-committee nature.

    It’s also hard to believe that a petition accurately represents the desires of a community. It’s probably fair to say that for most of these petitions any given member of the community will agree with some part of it and not other parts. But because they can only agree or disagree with the entire petition they will often sign it as a nod in agreement to the parts that they like. By it’s very nature, a petition can’t represent the individuals of a community very well.

    And then there’s the “well they did it, so should we” problem. The moment we reply to one petition it gives people the impression that they need to use petitions to get an idea across. The truth is that ideas get across much more readily by just about any other method. And despite the fact that we respond to far more individual ideas than to petitions, any response to a petition seems to strengthen the perception that an idea has to flood into our email boxes in order for us to see it.

    So here’s the problem. The discussions that take place during the creation of a petition are great. Some very interesting ideas come from those discussions, and we really don’t want to discourage them. But I do want to discourage the use of petitions as a tool for sending us ideas (for the reasons given in wordy form above).

    All this leads to one comment, and one question.

    Comment: Please, if you have an idea about EverQuest that you think will make the game better, send it to us through the Dev Corner.

    Question: How can the community foster the discussion that produces good ideas without also creating the petitions that seem to be the natural conclusion from those discussions?

    Alan

More EverQuest 2 Details
- Aurum @ 9:47 PM PDT
Mirk sent some additional details regarding the massive EverQuest 2 article found in June's PC Gamer:

    The June 2002 PCGamer just arrived in the mail today. It has the first look
    at Everquest 2. This is an entirely new game from scratch, currently
    targeted for third quarter of 2003, it is 30% complete. This is not the
    same thing as the Planes Expansion that was announced for EQ1 some time ago;
    it is not the same thing as the SOE-developed Star Wars Galaxies arriving
    later this year. It is a whole new game based on new technology. The game
    is set on the first three continents of the original Norrath, far into the
    future. The other continents are going to be explained away somehow.

    Some things mentioned in the article:
    - Half new content, half "reimagined EQ1 content" with similar monsters,
    themes, etc.
    - The minimum hardware reqs are going up to a 733MHzP3 with 512MB RAM, and a
    GeForce3 video cards. (min, not recommended)
    - They are eliminating corpse runs
    - They are trying to reduce camping, but some will ultimately exist
    - There will still be zones, but zoning will be faster and smoother. Some
    mobs will follow through zones, others will wander off.
    - Characters level faster, lvl 6 can be reached in 30 min of play
    - There are 100 levels in debut, probably will go up to 200 in expansions
    - All chars begin the same until level 6, when a base class is picked
    (fighter, mage, thief, priest, tradesman). Specialize at level 15. (e.g.
    fighter -> warrior/crusader/brawler). Specialize again at level 30. (e.g.
    crusader -> paladin/shadow knight/ranger)
    - Most places are within 5 min of a bind spot... but no bind spots in
    dungeons.
    - Items will now break with use
    - They have reduced twinking by making items break faster when used by the
    inexperienced
    - There is a small karma attack bonus for destroying low level items, to
    help clean the world of junk
    - Local banks, guarded caravans to transport stuff
    - Ownable land/bldgs w/ a fixed number of available large structures
    - Vehicles
    - Experience debt instead of loss
    - Highest quality items will be trade-created
    - Combat is generally the same with the addition of hit-location and special
    attacks that avoid certain defenses or specific body-armor pieces

    There are some very high resolution screen shots, it looks pretty good, if
    not a little sparse. The game is described by the article editor: “EQII is
    likely to be the greatest-looking, most complete, deepest, and most
    satisfying MMORPG experience ever.”
Thanks!

EverQuest 2 To Be Unveiled
- Aurum @ 9:27 PM PDT
After years of speculation, rumors and mystery, it is now confirmed that EverQuest 2 is going to be a reality. The June 2002 issue of PC Gamer will contain an article detailing the features of the sequel to the most succesful MMORPG to date.

Speaking of which, Casters Realm was able to obtain a good in-depth summary of the article as well. I'll provide a brief excerpt here:

    The world of EQ2 is set some time into the future from the current EverQuest world. There will be only three continents however at release. 50% of the zones will be entirely new, and 50% will be rebuilt areas of the traditional Norrath. But the world has changed. The article describes how Freeport has been taken over by the corrupt Freeport militia and the remnant paladins isolated in a new area known as Marr. The Iksar still roam, but the Vah Shir have disappeared (Kerrans are however playable).

    Banks will no longer be global. Rather each town will have a unique bank to be accessed, and if large amounts of goods need to be moved from one bank to another then a horse and cart or boat can be acquired for the journey.

    Players will be able to purchase houses. As towns develop from the placement of houses the world will spawn guard towers and NPCs to man them to protect the towns.
Definitely some exciting news, although we can only begin to guess what its impact will be in such a fierce market.

More on Fan Faire
- Aurum @ 9:09 PM PDT
Back at the Developer's Corner, Alan VanCouvering responded to feedback generated from the recent Fan Faire event in Washington this past weekend:

    Here's my feedback: It was great! If you did it again in this area, I'd definately sign up again.

    My biggest complaint: No microphones at the workshop sessions. I was struggling to hear sometimes. Bring a boombox for each room along next time, just in case the same thing happens...

    Another thing that I disliked a bit: All sorts of people were introduced at the beginning (e.g., a GM for our server, an eqdruids.com person, the castersrealm.com guy, etc.). I would've loved to meet them, but I never found them after they were introduced. Maybe you should give them a table? Or a workshop? Or something like that.

    The most amazing thing in my mind: I'm pretty shy. I didn't really hang out with anyone I knew previously (in-game or out), but I still had a bunch of fun and met new people. One was from a brother guild, several were from another guild I've seen around, and several were in my group in the real-life quest. Thinking about it now, I'm just stunned. Usually I'm the guy standing in the corner that nobody talks to. Maybe a significant portion of the Fan Faire attendees were like that, and something "clicked." Or maybe the whole "judge a person by his looks" concept didn't exist. I just don't know...


    There were supposed to be mikes, both for the panel and for the players. The hotel failed to provide them. I’m sorry about that.

    You know, that’s an interesting idea… maybe we could host a panel with the fan sites, let them field questions about what it’s like to run a site. As you might have noticed, our table space is often limited. But it sure wasn’t hard to find most of those guys, just head to the loudest party.

    There certainly is something to be said about the dynamics of the Fan Faire groups. For me the shocking thing is that, despite my usual hatred of conga dancing, I too had to do a brief stint with the Bixie Brenlo…

    I suspect it has something to do with the fact that all of us have something in common, and as weird as we might think we are, we know everyone else there is weird in many of the same ways. :)

    Alan

Fan Faire Seattle Report
- Aurum @ 5:38 PM PDT
Alan kicked this up on the Developer's Corner regarding this past weekend's Fan Faire:

    Wow, was that a blast or what!

    Our flight arrived pretty late last night, so I’m still struggling to keep my eyelids open.
