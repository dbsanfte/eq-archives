IN TESTING FOR NEXT UPDATE: MORE GROUND WORK FOR NECRO AND FACTIONS
Thursday, July 20, 2000 - 8:42 AM [Filed by arcadian del sol]
The following was posted today to In Testing at uo.com, and while I'm sure many of you will say, "Arcadian you're an idiot. This has nothing to do with necromancy or factions!", I defend my assumptions by saying, "Well, what else can explain these changes?"

In testing for next update:
The circumstances under which lockdowns may be "used" will be updated with the following changes:
1.Resources that are used by "targeting" will no longer be useable when locked down. These items include ingots, boards, logs, and shafts.
2.All items that are locked down will be useable only by the owner, co-owner, or friends of the house in which they are locked down, the only exception being that items which have another locked down item on top of them will not be able to be used, or released until the item above them is released.
3.Items which can be used by visitors when locked down are:
4.Purple Books will only be able to be read from when locked down. All others will be able to be edited by visitors.
5.Runebooks will allow visitors to add recall scrolls to them, otherwise they will work as before.
6.Visitors will be able to use locked down dyes in a house.
7.Visitors will be able to use locked down dye tubs is a house. Dye tubs must be unlocked before the color of the dye can be changed.
It will no longer be possible to lock down a "stacked" item unless all items beneath it are locked down. Additionally, it will no longer be possible to lock down "valueless" items such as new player clothing and resurrection robes.


A mixed bag this time. Some of the updates have been asking to be implemented since UO went live (such as read only books and "use only" dye tubs). Some of them I could live without, such as not being able to lock down an item stacked on an item that isn't locked down. So all you new home owners - get your decorations done NOW. Granted, most players don't give a rat's ass about whether or not they have dried flowers hanging on their walls. Maybe it's just in my neighborhood. My guess is that this is the fix for the "newbie bag" exploit. If that's the case, they need to take another look because some locked items still decay (don't bother asking me for a list, k?).

Between this and the new vendor changes, I'm all giddy like a schoolgirl. Hopefully, these changes will allow for a balanced necromancy system and will enable them to better implement the faction system. I'm positive that these recent updates are laying the groundwork for something gigantic!

Current MMORPGing Sucks
Tuesday, July 18, 2000 - 6:46 AM [Filed by hedron]
Yep, it sucks.

There’s this whole multiple causation (nods to Aristotle) thing going on that seems to contain just a few too many variables for the designers to decipher the mix that will foster a self-perpetuating game. And each individual piece of the right solution gets blown off because without being able to see the whole picture, the importance of the individual pieces gets missed.

Look, here’s an example.

I can’t tell you how much it pissed me off a while back to read that post from Brad what’s-his-face yammering on about game burn out as though it’s an inevitable and natural phenomenon. Guess what, Brad? It is - in a freaking single player game, and in a co-operative player game.

Why? Because in your game the challenges are a stale pattern of repetition - because your world is not a backdrop for people interacting, it’s a pre-occupation. It’s a focus. And as a focus, any relatively static and repetitive series of activities will ultimately fail to entertain. That is why environments that are conducive to PVP and RP are the only truly self-sustaining game environments, because PEOPLE aren’t static.

I’m so damn tired of jumping into the next cool game and mucking around with leveling and blah blah blah only to burn out because true PVP and RP are never supported. And the prospect of doing it again only to find out that some new company got it wrong again really chaps my ass. I miss having a real community to beat down.

The game designers out there are SO stuck on directing their games down some specific path – The Vision – that they never quite catch on to a singularly important concept.

Here’s the concept. Ready? “Vision” sucks.

A vision is a useful mental model of how you intend for something should work. It guides decisions that are made in building a functioning system that will fulfill some specific purpose. The problem with building an MRPG for an OVERLY specific single purpose is that you are automatically building in burn out. Forcing the player base to play in some specific way or towards some specific goal – whether it’s a single over-arching plot, a ridiculous moral lesson, a continuous treadmill of leveling, a constant state of battle, or WHATEVER – will eventually get boring.

I’m renaming “The Vision” for ALL of you designers out there. From now on it’s called “The Tunnel Vision”.

MMORPGs should be backdrops. They should be a contextual universe in which people can share a common setting and then chaotically evolve from that point into whatever happens to amuse them at the moment.

Some strong measure of chaos as opposed to vision leads to diversity. It leads to mutation and evolution along a myriad of paths, opening up game play choices that leave room for everyone to find what he or she will like. The free market! Laissez-faire, you blind humps, laissez-faire!

YOUR job, you visionary little hamsters, is to promote some measure of chaos within this contextual BACKDROP of a universe. You must preserve the freedom in the environment that allows people to innovate, and compete, and co-operate, and basically do whatever floats their boat at any given them. That means carving out enclaves for play styles, not penalizing them.

It boggles my mind how lost this concept has been in MMORPGs. The whole thing really just kind of pisses me off. It’s a freaking crotch cricket infestation.

Here’s part of why it happens. Let’s go back to the whole multiple causation thing. Designers think they need some overriding vision of how people should play, because the idea of creating a game in which the PVE aspect ENDS seems suicidal to your average designer. It seems suicidal because to them, it’s a given that once the PVE ends, the game ends. After you have all the stuff, and have gotten all the levels, the only thing to do is to stand around and be bored. But why do they think that? Because in order for players to not be bored after PVE ends, many things have to happen. And if you can’t quite get your brain around how all of the factors interact, then you can’t quite see your way clear to implementing any single one of them, because without all of the other supporting issues in place it doesn’t look like that one measure would really solve the problem.

In order to have something to do after PVE, you need to have an environment that encourages PVP and RP, because that’s what’s left after PVE is over. In order to understand how necessary it is for PVP and RP to flourish, you have to understand that PVE eventually = burn out. In order for PVP and RP to flourish sufficiently such that if PVE ended, people would stay involved… well, you have to end PVE, because PVP and RP don’t flourish when everyone is distracted by playing PVE. Right there that requires a big leap of faith.

But it doesn’t stop there. Let’s say you can see the above pattern, but even after you think about ending PVE in order to end the distraction that prevents PVP and RP from developing, you still think that it won’t work because PVPers and RPers hate each other. At least if there’s PVE going on, they have something to do besides drive each other from the game.

You have to understand that in order to encourage both PVP and RP you have to provide separate environments tailored around each of them, that will protect their style of play. But in order to prevent either one from becoming stale, you need to understand that there has to be motivation for the two player types to mix, while still leaving enough room for them to NOT mix when they don’t want to. Still, it doesn’t seem like quite enough of a working pattern. Why not?

Well, let’s take these one at a time. Let’s start with PVP.

In order for PVP to work, you have to understand the various factors that encourage involving PVP. You need to understand that looting of ANY significant nature discourages PVP, because in PVP somebody always loses. If you PVP a lot, assuming that most people want to fight other people that are a challenge to beat, then you lose at least 50% of those fights, and if you lose valuable items or gold as a result… well, then you aren’t happy. So in order to minimize this unhappy result, you look for fights you can’t lose. And eventually everyone does this. So ultimately what you have are a bunch of people finding and killing other people that have no hope of winning against them. This destroys PVP and converts it all to PK.

So you think about eliminating looting. But once you do that, PVP seems so pointless… so flavorless. It’s lost its thrill. Why? Because PVP as designed in the current MMORPGs doesn’t require the player to exhibit and refine his or her own human skill beyond the most basic of learning curves. You can’t eliminate looting unless you can create a PVP system that is rewarding in and of itself. In other words, PVP has to be FUN. But in order to make PVP fun you have to understand what makes it fun. You have to understand the truism that any truly engaging game (that doesn’t rely on the monkey-trick of behavioral positive reinforcement through leveling, and pretty graphics) is engaging because of the mental or physical SKILL that it requires of its players.

And THAT, my friends is where the buck REALLY stops. Because designing an engaging and human skilled combat system is no easy task. Especially not when you’re doing it in an MMORPG environment, because there are so many unbalancing factors that it’s really easy to put your head up your own design ass. There’s an entirely new set of layers to deal with. And if you’re actually going to try and keep PVE going, allowing continuous level and item advancement, then may the gods help you with the balancing act, because no one else can. Not and actually make PVP WORK.

Multiple causation. Heh. Miss a turn? Then you’re screwed. And it’s not like I’m trying to be exhaustive here. I’m just making a point.

We haven’t scratched the surface of designing PVP. And we haven’t even TOUCHED RP yet.

Sometimes I just want to go flow chart the whole damn thing. Ah well, screw it. Let ‘em keep guessing. They’re getting payed to figure it out, and I’m not. ;)

Go ahead, yell at me for not interpreting the word “vision” broadly enough. But you missed the point.

As you know, from time to time, I dip into my mailbag to help out game companies. Recently, my mail has been overflowing with desperate letters asking me "how the hell we can save Ultima Online?". With that in mind, I will use my amazing powers of observation and a big strong cup of common sense to save your sorry asses.

Q&A With the Great Bob: The Ultima Letters

Q. Parents are now using my name to scare their children into doing their chores. I've had to practice some real tough love in order to get rid of the scoundrels in my game. I'm wondering, is it better to be feared or loved? -Tyrannical in Austin

A. Machievelli was wrong. You see, the best rulers are those who are ignored. You are doing your job correctly if nobody bothers to think about what you're actually doing. James K. Polk was America's greatest president, but do you think he ever gets any attention? Of course not. People love Kennedy and hate Nixon, and they were both lousy leaders. You've made a great deal of noise stepping on the insects. Too much noise. Let the people know that there is a dark, malevolent force working for good. Don't give them a name though. You're mailbox will thank me for this.

Q. I'm the president of one of America's largest entertainment software firms. We publish numerous titles, including Ultima Online. Our collective has been assimilating developers left and right. Recently, however, I noticed that we no longer dominate the MMORPG field, and our iron grasp on the sports game market is also slipping. I know who our main competition is. Should I hire thugs to break all 989 kneecaps of those who dare to question the collective?

A. To badly mangle Shakespeare, "The fault, Dear Electronic Arts, lies not in our competitors but within ourselves". Despite what you may think, Sony did not get where it is by bombing one of your pacific naval bases. Instead they used the power of the pocketbook to drain away your best and brightest. Remember when your company was founded? You recruited programming talent by telling them they were the new movie stars. You would treat them like rock stars. Your first recruiting drive featured scantily clad babes and a room full of arcade games. You gave people freedom, and they gave you an empire. You take away the brand name, take away the freedom to create, an then wonder companies like Verant Austin start up. Electronic Entertainment is the new movie business. Don't force Orson Wells to make movies about giant space aliens. You do that, and you may wind up with Ed Wood working for you instead.

Q. If a player volunteers in the middle of a forest, and nobody is there to hear him, does he make a sound? -Cal in Austin

A. The only sound he makes is the sound of him canceling his account. You have a really, really, really difficult job ahead of you. Others have been in your position and have failed. Where are the thousands of seers? Where is the volunteer staff making the world of Sossaria a better place? Same place they've always been. Sitting behind a computer, growing more bitter and angry. Your war is in the boardroom. If you don't make the case for the importance of the volunteer program, nobody else will. You must get the tools you need from the dev team, and the backing of higher-ups to allow you to make fundamental changes to the way fiction is viewed in the game. No other MMORPG comes close to UO is the flexibility to transform it from a game to a virtual world. You need to act now. Let others read the message boards putting out fires. You got world building to do, bub.

Q. Me and the boys in marketing are wondering how we can make the game better. Why are we only number 2? We have as many scantily clad women as your competitors do! -Emily in Austin

A. I would be happier answering this letter if it hadn't been sent over and over again in the form of mass mailing spam. Damn you and your marketing scoundrels to hell. However, this is important enough to comment upon. You are not selling shoes. You are not selling a television show. This is a new market, a new medium, and the requirements are different. Focus groups mean nothing. UO is still kicking after three years not because of advertising, but because of ambitious design work. Let the development team do their job. Stay out of the kitchen. Your chefs must decide the menu. Feel free to change the tableclothes, or the prices on the menu. Just don't try to recommend to the chefs that they add more pepper.

Q. I'm a lead developer for the second largest MMORPG in the world. Why do people laugh at me when I show them my business card?

A. I'm as guilty about this as the next person. You guys are the third stringers on the team right now. You're the team the coach sends in during the ninth inning when the team is ahead by 30 runs. The fans are leaving the stadium. Now, some of you might be great players. Someday kids might collect your cards. You want to know how to turn this impression around? Show us what you can do. (my inner child of the eighties just slipped through!) Why do we still have a usage base skilled system? Is that something you think should be changed? If not, why? Don't be hampered by the past. Is there a way through code to make a new players experience better? What about us vets? You see what I'm getting at. Try to keep everyone out of your kitchen, and show us the goods.

MY THREE NIGHTS IN JAIL
Friday, July 14, 2000 - 5:08 AM [Filed by arcadian del sol]
I was driving home from a late night study session in college (yes, I actually went and yes I actually studied. Just don't tell my parents) when I see flashing blue and red lights in my rear view mirror. I knew the routine. You see, I have one of the dumbest hobbies you can imagine: I collect traffic warnings. No, not tickets - warnings. I've always been a big of a charmer and a smooth talker, and of course, I always have those "I paid twenty bucks to the fraternal police order and they gave me this nifty sticker" logos all over my car (note: Don't put them on your bumper, put them on the window of the driver's side door so the officer has to look through it to see you). So I hand him my registration and he calmly request that I step out of the vehicle. I had about 25 warnings and 10 overturned tickets by that time, and never had I ever been asked to get out of the car. I knew something was wrong.

We did some sobriety games and when he asked me to count backwards from twenty, I offered to do it in any of 4 languages. I breeze his tests (because I'm like, sober and all), and he begins to place handcuffs on me. Without being told why, I find myself in a jail cell for three days. All they would tell me is that they are waiting for a witness to show up from out of town. I ask to speak to my attorney, and they said, "Tell us his name and we'll get in touch with him."
Cops like calling your bluff.

Anyway, the kindly old woman shows up, looks at me, and says, "He's not Mexican." They wheel her out, open the door, and say "We are terribly sorry for the inconvenience. Your car is at the Northwest impound lot. They don't accept charge cards or personal checks, though." (Yes, they violated all kinds of procedures here. Par for the course for the Anne Arundel County Police Department, unfortunately. Don't worry, I was happily compensated at a later date.)

As it turns out, someone else (Mexican, I would have to assume) had sideswiped the lil lady and she got a partial tag number and a general concept of the color of the car that hit her. I happened to drive the same color car and had the same three numbers on my plate. As my lawyer later informed me (this was where the happy compensations took place), if police have a reason to think you are a suspect, they can detain you until that suspicion is either confirmed or denied. An honest, law abiding citizen can be held in jail for up to four days while they wait for some lil old lady to say "He's not a Mexican." That's just the way the system works. This has something to do with Ultima Online, I swear it.

Whenever law enforcement rounds up mobs of people, there is the chance that some dumb schmuck in the same color pants is going to get wrongly detained. It doesn't happen every day, but it does happen - and we cannot simply stop arresting criminals for fear that the wrong person might be detained and questioned. We just have to accept that this will happen from time to time, and trust that the system of justice in place will ultimately exonerate those who are wrongfully accused. Ultima Online has such a system. It has a court of appeals that while it will not entertain the rabid babblings of the guilty, does hear the outcries of the innocent. Calandryll and Melantus are but two of many people at OSI who do actually have the interests of the players in mind. Ignoring the cadre of disgruntled former UO players who like to show up in drag and dance a jig whenever there is some sort of UO controversy, I think the majority of level-headed people out there understand that these kinds of things can happen, will trust OSI to work them out properly, and they, like me, do not think the solution is to stop bannings altogether.

JUST A GAME
Thursday, July 6, 2000 - 10:46 AM [Filed by myschyf]
And so castles made of sand
slip into the sea
eventually.
-- Jimi Hendrix

I've written about this before and I'll no doubt write about this again. It seems to come up over and over again in discussions that usually center around bannings or some sort of other administrative action that takes place in an MMORPG. Are these games we play 'just a game' or are they something larger?

Many of those who engage in non-consensual PKing (similar to the story about CoJ that I posted last night) or what we've come to call 'grief tactics' often fall back on the argument that 'its just a game' so anything they do in the game really has no lasting effect. They argue that of course they are wonderfully, polite, upstanding well-mannered people in real-life and of course they would never equate what they do in the game to anything they do outside of the game.

Unfortunately what this equates to for me is saying that one might be ordinarily decent but that being in an online game gives one the freedom to say and do things that would never be said or done to my face. Forgive me if I find that abhorrent. It's as if the anonymous saftey of being in an online game lowers ones inhibitions and gives one the 'freedom' to say and do the loathesome, repugnant things one would never dream of doing or saying otherwise. This is freedom? I don't know -- my sights and goals for freedom are so much more loftier than this. I never imagined freedom from inhibition as the ability to engage in egotistical rudeness and agression.

For me, it is more than 'just a game.' For me, real life values, morals and ethics apply. That is why you won't see me robbing people in the game. That is why you won't see me doing non-consensual pking (although you might see me doing consensual pvp). You won't see me scamming anyone. You won't see me doing anything in the game that I wouldn't do in real life (in an abstract sense -- obviously I don't run around killing orcs or pvping in real life). For me the choice is clear, I would no sooner gate an unsuspecting victim into a sure-death trap than I would have a bunch of friends hold down someone I didn't like so I could punch the person's lights out. And I don't go looking for weaker people than me to punch. For me, the time I spend online is as valid and as real a part of my life as the time I spend offline. My actions online reflect my actions offline -- and vice versa.

Now this is not to say that I don't find those of you that play an evil whatever (demons, undead, murderers and the like) the same outside of the game. I think the difference is that most of you are not looking to go make someone miserable. You aren't really looking to be loathesome and repugnant. I can hear some of my friends in those evil guilds right now insisting that they really are loathesome and repugnant -- just shut the fuck up for a moment alright? Because you aren't. You are there to have fun and to do so with other people. The difference is between the game being, for you, making other people miserable and 'winning' no matter what and playing to have fun with other people that are also having fun and sure winning is a part of it but not the primary goal. Looked at from another view -- how many of 'good guys' come over the Kaaos boards 10 years later just to say hi, and how many of CoJ's victims have ever posted on their board? In the former case its two groups of people who have chosen 'sides' or teams, if you will, in order to set up some competition which can be fun and, to further the fun, its enjoyable to envision that one team are the good guys out to smite evil and the other team is the bad guys out to cause evil and wreck havoc. If everyone plays their part then you get to do the RP thing and that can be fun too.

But even then, for you, I submit it is more than a game. Even though you are pretending to be evil demon god thingies your purpose in the game isn't to make other people miserable. You don't go around scamming people. You don't go around gating unsuspecting victims into a sure death trap simply to laugh and belittle them. Your values and morals generally carry over into the game with you.

It doesn't have to be that way though. I suppose it can be 'just a game.' In that case, it should not be a big deal when a GM appears and says you can't do something. Because its just a game. And if you get banned for whatever reason -- fair or unfair -- it should be no big deal. Because its just a game. And if the game has stat loss it should be no big deal. Because its just a game. If the developers change the direction of the game and turn it into something that is different than what it began as, it should be no big deal. Because its just a game.

But you can't have it both ways. Either it is just a game and nothing matters, not your actions or the actions of the game company or the people around you -- or it is more than a just a game and your actions, the actions of the game company, and the actions of the people around you matter a great deal. But it is not both.

THE PLAY'S THE THING
Tuesday, July 4, 2000 - 9:35 AM [Filed by great bob]
During the Edo period in Japan, a new form of drama came into existence called the Kabuki play. Kabuki plays are highly formalized dramas that typically deal with the relationship between man and feudalism. Actors in Kabuki plays study generations of past actors and emulate their acting style. Over the centuries, the roles of the players have become set in stone and form a kind of formalistic beauty.

MMORPG rant sites are developing their own form of the Kabuki play called the "player banning story". Like the Kabuki play, symbolization has been carried to the extent of abstraction, so that the formalized action of the character is no longer relevant to, or even in direct contact with any rational interpretation of the players involved. Let's go through an example from this week.

The News is Delivered: On the Pacific shard this week, the guild Hell Hounds was banned for duping items inside the game. Despite having no treasure hunting skills, this guild always seemed to have an unlimited number of magic weapons for sale.

Chorus sings praise of OSI: At this point in the play, a message thread is created. Since most players understand how destructive cheating is in a MMORPG there is a period o rejoicing where most players say "right on".

Enter the Rebels: No drama is complete without the token villian. In this drama, the villians are played as comic figures. In every performance, the following lines will be spoken; "What about innocent untill proven guilty?" or "OSI just baned them because they were pks, and osi is unfair to pks and whatnot!". Of course, these characters are not to be taken seriously. They are symbolic characters that we are meant to ridicule. They represent the mindset of the morally bankrupt twit who feels that destroying the game is his right.

Falling for the Trolls: This is where the heros fall for the scheme of the vilians and try to present a rational case why cheating is bad, and why cheaters should be wiped off the face of the planet. The protaginist will try to patiently explain that OSI is in the buisness of making money, and they wouldn't lose money by banning accounts unless they had evidence. The heroes in this drama will then sit back thinking that logic and reason has won the day.

Trolls Never Die: Like Kabuki, interprative dance plays an important part in the drama. The comic villians, now exposed for the brain dead miscreants that they are, will now do a dance known as the "twistie".
