THE SINGLE MOST FUN THING YOU CAN DO WITH HUNDREDS OF OTHER PEOPLE WITHOUT WEARING ANYTHING MADE OF LATEX or THINGS TO DO WHILE WAITING FOR A GM IN UO
Friday, June 30, 2000 - 3:34 PM [Filed by myschyf]
How weird is this? I'm updating Lum's with an essay by Damion 'Ubiq' Schubert (Lead Designer for UO2) about M59 while waiting for a GM in UO. Anyway Ubiq was Lead Designer for M59 before he was Lead Designer for UO2. I have a whole bunch of M59 stories that I'll be compiling into a one large read but as this is rather humorous I thought I'd post it while I wait. It also gives me something to do while I wait. I am currently 146 in the queue. Woo woo!

This is the lighter, more amusing side of Meridian history. One might read this and assume that we were troubled, or screw-ups or clueless. I suppose, to some degree, that this is true, but for the most part, this was the golden age of Massively Multiplayer games - everyone was feeling their way through the dark, trying to figure out every aspect of the business, from technology to design to management to marketing to customer service and quality assurance. Answers that seem obvious now were still waiting to be discovered. I do know that the team that worked on Meridian 59 was, while I was there, the most passionate and hardworking team that I knew of.

Meridian 59 benefitted mostly from being in the right place at the right time. It had most of its network code done, in order to be a BBS door game, at the same time that the ability to use the internet escaped the college campuses and entered the homes of thousands of Americans. As a result, we were able to get to market very, very quickly. Perhaps too quickly, as we never achieved nearly the success that UO recognized.

Possibly the most interesting thing about reading the timeline below is that many of the odd problems, scenarios and conflicts that are now thought of as being unique to UO and EQ have been around for a very long time. Now that I've worked with former members of Simutronics (Dragonrealms, Gemstone) and Kesmai (Legends of Kesmai, Air Warrior), I know that these problems are somewhat endemic to the industry. Perhaps realizing this we can think of better global solutions instead of band-aids.

Getting Meridian 59 to ship and supporting it selflessly for 2 years in the role of lead designer is probably the greatest and most satisfying achievement of my life. It also gave me the majority of the knowledge, experience and maturity that I brought to take on leadership of the Ultima: Origin project. And while I may not agree with every decision that management ever made (a common enough event in any industry and company), I do feel enormously thankful to the 3DO Company for taking the risk to buy a fledgling company and invest so much into making it happen.

As for the game, I do not lament Meridian's death but praise it's life. Meridian had a great ride for something that started in a garage and had the muscle of EA, Sony and Microsoft waiting to crush it like a grape.
  • Zaphod and Zandramas write the core of the game systems at home. They form a company with a pair of brothers (one calls himself Meridian) to build a graphical MUD.
  • A friend and former player on a MUD I helped to run, Raph Koster, turns down a job at Archetype to go do something at Origin, and recommends me to Meridian-the-designer. I send him 20 pages of ideas, most of which I now recognize as complete and total crack. I get the job.
  • Archetype is a virtual company. I have never met any of these people before, in person. I have only interviewed with one person over the telephone. Everything is surreal and dreamy. I live in total terror that maybe this is all a joke and I will be on the curb come rent time. Miraculously, a paycheck actually appears in my mailbox at the beginning of the month.
  • I spend many, many, many hours downloading the latest builds of the game over a 14.4 modem.
    I discover the hard, grueling, stay up all night way that the Meridian editor will only compile rooms that have walls at 90 degree angles. Our lead programmer’s response is “Hmmm, that’s odd.”
  • I convince the team to hire my brother, Mocker. He proves to be a much better world-builder than I am.
  • The company start arguing about the name of the game. One member of the team passionately believes that the game should be called TOS, short for ‘The Other Side’. Attempting to placate him, I say, “Don’t like the name for the game, but hey, wouldn’t that make a great city name?” The city of Tos would be our first city, and always be our most popular one.
  • We settle on the name ‘Meridian’ for the game that we are working on, based primarily on two reasons: (1) we’re sick of arguing about it and (2) everything else we came up with was already taken or trademarked.
  • The name ‘Meridian’ turns out to be trademarked – it is the name of a printer driver somewhere. Rather than fight that battle, we change the name to ‘Meridian 59’, figuring we’d write some backstory that would explain what 59 meant later.
  • Meridian 59 hits alpha. It can hold a scant 35 people. It has 7 ‘rooms’, 2 spells, no skills, three monsters (all giant insects), 1 quest, and no form of PK control whatsoever. The newbie spawn point is a sea of bodies. The game is so full that we have to (randomly and without warning) kick people off the server to debug our own game.
  • Trying to get investors into the game, we want to show off all of our 1337 art. Problem, all of our customers are playing men, and our women look much better. I somehow get volunteered to have a female moniker in game. Mostly through laziness, I never bother to change it. Oddly enough (and thankfully), never as an immortal was I hit on, except by women.
  • In a time when we are desperately trying to impress investors, the original NeverWinter Nights closes down. Without warning, we are flooded with MMPRPG-starved fans. And they all want every single NWN feature.
  • One of my co-designers chooses a bad time to bug me. I haven’t slept in 36 hours, and I’m acting as the combination QA/CS department for our new alpha, frantically taking calls and bug reports from the fans when the designer sends me a completely random, out of the blue idea. I send him an extremely angry, confrontational /tell laced with profanity, press enter – and then realized I had sent a /shout, not a /tell. The entire game goes silent. I quickly log off and get some sleep.
  • Working remotely, we realize we haven’t heard from one of our contract artists in about a month, much less gotten any art from him. Given that he lives in Arizona, there is no way to check up on him. He later returns, mentioning mostly that he needed a ‘break’.
  • Broke, we hire a guy to build levels for free, some guy named Q. He turns out to be worth a hundred times that much. Well, more, anyway.
  • Running on fumes, we convince 3DO to buy us. They may still hate us for that. My salary was tripled overnight – and I was still grossly underpaid.
  • I meet my coworkers in person for the first time. We are all a lot less intimidating in person.
  • 3DO doesn’t have art resources to spare, so we hire an outside art house to replace some of our art for us. For some reason, this house seemed to be under the impression that we were a Sierra-like game. As a result, a small segment of our art looked like it belonged in a Hanna Barbara cartoon.
  • In what may be the single worst ad in gaming history, we tells PCGamer readers that Meridian 59 is “The single most fun thing you can do with hundreds of other people without wearing anything made of latex.”
  • One of our new Guardians chooses the very original name ‘Gandalf’. When told by his coworkers that they were all going to start a tradition of having names that start with ‘Z’, he sullenly changes his name to Zgandalf.
  • Setting a time-honored precedent for the genre, we ship off our gold master off to duplication whilst still fixing server-side bugs.
  • 3DO ships Meridian 59 on September 27th, 1996. Our marketing guy tells us that he spent lots of marketing cash to get the product on the endcaps of every CompUSA in the country. We pile into Q’s mustang to go and see our product on the shelves for the first time. It is not on the endcap. It is hidden behind a pole.
  • At launch, we have something like 20 different monster models, a full third of which are giant insects of some form.
  • Within one day of being live, Meridian has its first virtual prostitute, making that Meridian’s oldest profession.
  • I smite my first player, an annoying person named ‘Sexy’ who emoted ‘wraps her legs around you’ and similar phrases as I was attempting to tell her (in front of an audience) that she needed to stop killing newbies and doing odd things with their corpses.
  • We ban a guild for duping, as we finally catch on that the 10 bucks a jerk gives you is NOT worth as much as the 10 bucks a peaceful player gives you. The guild en masse writes to PCGamer.com, which promptly reports of our vindicative, evil Customer Service department on the front page. Our policy on not commenting on account cancellation prevents us from responding.
  • Our first major hack program goes into existence. I forget the name of it (“Meridian Extreme”?) Neatest feature – someone discovered that there was no room check on shopkeepers, so you could sell your loot to the shopkeeper from anywhere in the room.
  • http://www.3dosucks.com goes live. Just as good cannot exist without evil, and UO cannot exist without Lum, we feel our existence is justified. Far better than if they didn’t care enough to hate us.
  • Meridian Extreme is improved. It’s interface is now much, much better than that of the game.
  • Perhaps trying to compete with ourselves for worst ad ever for a computer game, we tells readers “The next time someone tells you to get a life, tell them you’ve already got one. A virtual one. Meridian 59”
  • Meridian wins GameCenter’s award for RPG of the Year, beating out Daggerfall.
  • In a round of layoffs, I am forced to lay off my own brother. We rehire him a month later.
  • Starting another fine tradition of MMPRPGs, in a desperate attempt at self defense, someone releases a version of Meridian Extreme which will log in your character, suicide your character, and log out. Our Guardians actually take great pleasure at fielding calls from cheaters begging to have their accounts reinstated.
  • The program ‘EZ Macro’ convinces me that I hate use-based systems.
  • We perform our first major ‘nerf’. Meridian 59 has a political system which gives bonuses to you based on what faction you are in and how powerful it is. The duke is too powerful, and so we (accidentally) overcompensate by making the princess too powerful. However, the term ‘nerf’ has not been popularized by UO and EQ yet, so instead I get an angry email complaining that we ‘castrated the duke’. I often wonder what the mail would have read if we had nerfed the princess.
  • We ship the ‘Vale of Sorrows’ expansion pack, after a long discussion with marketing about whether or not that is an appropriate name for a product released two weeks after major layoffs. Our new producer, a guy named Rich Vogel, defends the dev team’s choice in names, and wins.
  • A dupe bug renders money useless overnight. In an interesting economic development, the players almost immediately fall upon Dark Angel Feathers as a replacement currency until things return to normal. DAFs are valuable because they are rare-dropped yet stackable items and required to cast player-killing spells. Pkers have an interest in getting them, and non-pkers have an interest in keeping them out of pker’s hands.
  • We change the palette of all of the art in the game. The only art excluded is some of the very old textures used for the forests way back in beta. These rooms still exist, and the textures that were formally green are now orange, red and yellow. Mischievous Guides enjoy teleporting players to these old zones, and telling them that it was a new area we were working on that was half done, the land of the burning trees. We never successfully combat these rumors.
  • One of our Guardians parts ways with the company, muscled out for giving favors to his favorite customers. He is so popular that dozens of people write messages on boards that cover hot tech stock tips saying that 3DO is going down because they can’t keep their best CS people. Our stock actually dips that day.
  • The infamous GuideWozzle episode: a player volunteer Guide goes crazy. He goes to the Ghost room, and uses a bug to spawn several ghosts (by far the hardest monsters in the game). He then marches down the who list, teleporting people in the room one at a time. They have enough time to say ‘wtf’ before being cut down like weeds to a weedwhacker. I teleport into a room full of hundreds of corpses. I ban GuideWozzle. The last two people teleported into the room (who were saved by my intervention) immediately begin looting the corpses of all the other players.
  • Starting a time-honored tradition in MMPRPGs, a Guardian is banned for fraternizing with the counselors.
  • Not to be outdone by 3dosucks, a new website goes live, detailing how Q will give personal favors in the game for oral gratification. This is accompanied by doctored images of his in-game character. The entire team is quite amused. Except for Q.
  • In another round of layoffs, I am forced to lay off my own brother. We rehire him two months later.
  • We release Revelations, the second expansion for Meridian 59. It is a sizeable, tropical island that is hard to get to and full of sentient yet primitive races, and setting a precedent for the genre.
  • Setting a time-honored precedent for the genre, the advertising for the Revelations product has a scantily clad female that literally has nothing to do with the game. Given our previous advertising, we are delighted by this.
  • The stupidest thing the design team ever did: in order to get to the island, you needed to cast ‘dispel illusion’ to open an illusionary wall and enter the caves that led to the island of Ko’catan. Unfortunately, the only place to get the reagents for dispel illusion at launch were on the island. We quickly add a quest which teleports users to a new room which contains a new monster (hued version of an old monster) which drops the reagents as loot. The fans wildly applaud the brilliance and convoluted nature of the first opening of the passage to Ko’catan – except for a couple of players who said, “Funny, I tried doing exactly that quest yesterday, and nothing happened.”
  • The entirety of server 109 gets bored, and raids server 108. They don’t player kill. Instead, they all choose names starting with clone, as in ‘clone1’, ‘clone2’, ‘clone3’, ‘clone4’, etc. They all use the same character model and they only speak in binary. One person, the master, does all the talking for the group. Server 108 freaks the hell out. They blame us. We try to explain that we aren’t that smart.
  • In an admirable example of the community defending itself, a player on server 108 starts a new character, calls it ‘Clone16’, and joins the game. Immediately, the other clones include him in the clone’s in-game chat channel, and start talking normally, saying things like ‘ha ha ha! I can’t believe they R so freakd!’ He quietly listens. The next day, he posts a log on the message board complete with a listing of which clone is which 109 player, and which clone guy is cheating on which clone woman with which other clone woman.
  • I drop a rare artifact on the ground by accident. A nearby thief snags the item and runs off with it. When I found out what happened, I delete his arm. The team is stunned that this is even possible.
  • In a cunning grasp of the economics of the internet (and over the dev team’s strong objections), the pricing model of Meridian 59 is changed from $10 a month to $2.49 a day, but never more than $10 a week, and never more than $30 a month. This effectively tripled the price of the service, with the added benefit that it confused the people it didn’t insult. Hundreds of accounts are cancelled. Tragically, the move proved to be profitable in the short term because 2/3rds of the population did not cancel their accounts. However, the damage to the community was very, very deep, and Meridian never fully recovered.
  • I leave the Meridian design team to work on 3DO’s new project, Might and Magic Online.
  • Ultima Online goes live. Surprisingly, few people leave, partially because the gameplay is so different, but largely also due to the fact that most people who were going to leave already did so in the Price Change Defection.
  • 3DO cancels Might and Magic Online, citing the ‘failure’ of Ultima Online. (Note: Origin kept their numbers close to their vest for quite some time).
  • The Renaissance update is released. I had little to do with this update at all, other than helping with initial designs. It introduced the Hunters and the Necromancers. The Necromancers got neat buffs, but had to feed their hunger by player killing. The Hunters got neat buffs, but had to kill Necromancers. Players promptly proceeded to powergame the system (using mules for necromancer fuel, thus allowing them to get the buffs without cost). They then complained that we never added any cool PvP scenarios for elder players.
  • I do a summary of all the magazine and web reviews that UO and M59 got. Meridian 59 almost nearly always had the better review.
  • I quit my job to take a position of greater responsibility at Origin. Before I do, I implement what is in my opinion the coolest easter egg ever – a room full of statues that pay tribute to the original design and support staff of Meridian 59.
  • At my new place of employment, a key member of the UO design team tells me that they were, at the time, ‘terrified’ of Meridian’s impending launch.
  • I log into the EQ chat rooms with my Meridian 59 name, while waiting for the servers to open for phase 4 beta. Humorously, enough people recognize me and start chatting Meridian old times that the volunteer in the room has trouble answering EQ questions.
  • The Necromancers had a safety valve built in. They had an achilles heel – the lich queen – who, if killed, would kill all the necromancers and therefore reset the scenario (removing the buffs). Since the necromancers and the hunters were conspiring to powergame the buffs, this never happened. Until finally, one player got bored. He killed the lich, with one stroke killing 50+ necromancers, possibly the single most impressive player killing action in MUD history – 50 death messages in a row, interspersed with occasional ‘LOL’s and “WTF”s does make for highly amusing reading. It had been so long since the lich had died that the necromancers actually argued that the Lich killer was a grief player.
  • Noted Meridian fanatic and self-admitted troublemaker convinces her company, World Fusion, to begin work on a massively multiplayer game called Atriarch.
  • A player, Ixit, achieves immortality in my mind by performing a feat of spectacular powergaming - something that I thought was mathematically impossible – by becoming a master in every spell and skill school in the game (most players are masters of only 3 instead of 7). When he tells me how he did it (a process of meticulous pingponging of skills in the use-based system that literally took hours a day for months), I say to myself, “wow, yeah, I guess that would work.”
  • EverQuest goes live. Given that the gameplay is similar but much, much deeper, it devastates the Meridian community. The diehards cling to the notion that EQ sux and M59 will rule forever, but numbers dwindle quickly.
  • While strolling through EQ with my Meridian name, someone gives me free healing, SoW and buffs because “your chat system didn’t suck like this one does.”
  • Meridian enters the new millenium with no 3D Graphics Card support and no hope of ever getting it.
  • Meridian 59 is #91 in GameSpot UK’s most important games of the Millenium.
  • A noted player, Dementia, is banned from Meridian 59. He attempts to get someone in trouble by doctoring the logs of a chat session, and sending it to a GM. Little does he know that the other player was that GM’s mortal character.
  • I log into Meridian for the first time in a year . “Oh my dear god,” I think to myself. “We never put mouselook in this stupid game.”
  • A key member of the AC design team tells me that they were ‘terrified’ of the speed that we could make Meridian happen.
  • Horizons core members Dave Allen and James Jones mention in passing that they were both M59 addicts, and that it came up frequently during their design sessions.
  • A general amnesty is declared for all players banned from Meridian 59. The exceptions are those "banned for credit card fraud, those banned for racist and sexist activities, and Dementia".
  • A key member of the EQ design team tells me that they were ‘very worried’ about competition against Meridian.
  • I tell a former member of the Underlight team that we were ‘terrified’ about their impending launch.
  • 3DO announces that Meridian will be brought down on August 31st, 2000.

    ON THE UP SIDE, UNLIKE BLIZZARD, YOU CAN ACTUALLY USE VERANT'S SERVERS
    Friday, June 30, 2000 - 12:33 PM [Filed by lum]
    In the midst of everything else, a long-awaited upgrade for Rogues was patched in earlier this week in EQ. Unfortunately, as rogues and anyone following EQ should have known, "upgrade" for Verant means approximately the same thing as "not having sex with that woman" for President Clinton. Either way the other party ends up worked up, but ultimately really frustrated. For more, we take you to someone who actually plays a rogue for the analysis...

    Poison wasn't fixed.

    They did these things:

    1) Added more poisons (sounds good at first)
    2) Put vials on vendors (mmm another money sink)
    2) REDUCED the effects of all poisons, including existing ones. Thus the "nerf".

    Slight more detail, and an example:

    Before: Contact Poison
    Decrease Hit Points (HP) from 5 (L1) to 241 (L60) - Meaning if a lvl 1 Rogue used this poison, it would do 5 damage. A lvl 60 using it causes 241 damage.
    Took two snake poison sacks

    Now: Contact Poison I - Decrease Hit Points (HP) from 5 (L1) to 50 (L13) - This poison uses the same components as the old poison shown above.
    Contact Poison II - Decrease Hit Points (HP) from 5 (L1) to 81 (L20)
    Contact Poison III - Decrease Hit Points (HP) from 5 (L1) to 121 (L30)
    Contact Poison IV - Decrease Hit Points (HP) from 5 (L1) to 161 (L40) - Almost half the effectiveness of the original poison, at a cost of ~25 plat more. Takes other, more widespread and harder to obtain components.

    As you can see, they basically took an old poison and split it into four levels. As you can also see, the new highest level now does LESS damage than the original poison.

    In addition, if any rogues had the old poison in their possession, it was converted into the level I poison. I.E. If I had a pack full of 200 DD poisons in the bank when I logged out, when I logged in they were now all 50 DD.

    So... poison was not viable, at least at higher levels (>30) before, and even worse now. In addition, all of the annoyances are still there:

    1) Pottery requiring master skill (although you can opt to buy them for pp now).
    2) Farming of components off of greenies.
    3) Chance to fail "make poison"
    4) Chance to fail "apply poison" - You can now even exhaust your poison if you try applying before you are at an appropriate lvl to apply that strength of poison. Whatever that appropriate lvl is.
    5) Chance for mob to resist poison
    6) 7 second apply time for one charge
    7) Interruptability on your hit
    8) Poisons are not stackable
    9) One charge per vial

    All this for the slight chance to do 160 damage once per fight. In your 50s and even 40s, rogues backstab for that much easily every 8 seconds with a low level haste (FBSS), or even less, down to 4 seconds, with an enchanter along. Why go through all that trouble for something you can do every 4 seconds, and usually for more damage, for free.

    So:
    1) It was nerfed
    2) It is still not viable

    Which isn't really unexpected, I mean this IS Verant after all... but the huge disappointment is that they had been promising for NINE months to give the rogues a "poison upgrade", when in fact it was just more of the same plus a nerf.

    The majority of this information was compiled by Rohaise of the Druids
    Grove    . Most rogues are too upset to think about it this long without breaking something.

    The Safehouse, the rogue's primary message board/community, is in quite an uproar about it, as you can imagine.

    Cloauk N'Dger
    Soon to be retired
    47th Scout of the Haven
    Karana Server

    Abashi's response was as usual terribly informative and not at all patronizing, no sir:

    I commented a while back that rogues are already so powerful that we'd have to be careful with poison to keep from *overpowering* them. I should also mention that it's quite obvious that most of the assumptions about poisons are being based off of parsing the spdat.eff. Don't do that.

    The goal of this change was not to turn rogues into casters. They already do more damage than any other melee class in certain circumstances.

    The goal was to turn it into a viable skill to be practiced. It is that now. Try going out and playing with it for a while before deciding that it sucks.

    He then moved on to give Whineplay English lessons.

    You know, I really can't make this stuff up.


    OH WELL THERE'S ALWAYS SINGLE PLAYER
    Thursday, June 29, 2000 - 3:30 PM [Filed by lum]
    Some of you may wonder why there have been so many Diablo 2 stories here lately - some of which seem to be slavering at any opportunity to take a whip to the Second Coming of Online Gaming, even so much as (in the story below) jumping on a Starcraft cheater bust and proclaiming the imminent death of Battle.net. I don't know, call it hype fatigue. Anyway, I decided to be different and actually play the game before judging it online.

    I'm still waiting for the chance to play it online. Blizzard is discovering that persistent world servers are HARD. Connectivity is HARD. Latency is HARD. The battle.net world servers (USWest, USEast, Europe, and maybe others that never even made it above water this afternoon) were going up and down with an annoying frequency.     In my case, I began by waiting to develop my character, which was entirely my affair. This was followed by waiting for OSI to code in half of the features they had planned in the game. After all, they couldn't possibly call it a finished Ultima product until there was a virtue system, right?

    What we were given instead of a complex inter-related system of virtue scales was a good/evil teeter-totter. So with that "wait" over, though I wasn't much happy with the result, the next thing to wait for was the plot. Realizing that this was pretty slow in coming, several of us decided to make our own plots, with real Britannian characters with real Britannian backgrounds. We forged along, and one of us apparently had some GM connections. Then we waited for some support we were implied might be possible for our events. We didn't just wait, though, as we valued our independence.

    Then, after finishing the first cycle of our events, several of us were invited to apply for the Seer program. Some of us had no idea we'd been put in for it, including myself. So then we waited with anticipation of what could come of our humble beginnings. Eventually, the e-mail I was waiting for arrived, and several of my friends and I were officially accepted.

Submit a quest event, wait for the pitifully understaffed IGM department to respond. Eventually, you get sick of waiting impatiently. Waiting patiently sure is easier. The next thing I know, a year's gone by and it occurs to me that our independent role-play group accomplished more in three months than we had as Seers in nine months... a lot more.

    The IGM staff fluctuates but never gets substantially bigger. The "solution" to this - in other words, the solution to letting us actually do what we were invited to do in the first place - is to give us tools to do things without having to beg an IGM for attention. So then we wait for powers. And wait and wait and wait. It's audacious, we spent much of our time waiting for the program to become a priority for OSI. Clearly, it never quite got to that point. Everything's always coming "Real Soon now" (tm).

    Eventually, you stop "waiting", as such, because that gets really old. After a while, I'm just in it because I've bought the ticket, and by god no matter how bad the movie is, I'm going to see how it ends. The longer I watched it dispassionately, the more I started agreeing with Lum's proposal that maybe the entire system needs to be dismantled.

    It gets to the point where you're waiting for something to wait for. The last straw that made me quit was a small enough incident, but representative of that which was causing me dissatisfaction.

    And that's just one person's experience. I've heard countless others who were waiting for good/evil, waiting for necromancy, waiting for advanced alchemy, waiting for T2A housing, waiting for factions, waiting for UO:R housing.

    If you're still having fun in the game you're playing, more power to you. But if you're "waiting" for something, I whole-heartedly encourage you to re-examine your relationship with the MRPG of your choice, whatever it is.

    After all, it's a game - should you have to wait to have fun?

    YOU GOTTA KEEP EM SEPERATED
    Friday, June 16, 2000 - 8:56 AM [Filed by lum]
    Battle Vortex recently posted an article by Riprend on a zone-based solution for PvP-non PvP interaction. Take a look, then come back. (It's called "How MinerD00D_00 Saved the MMORPG")

    It, and similar solutions, are doomed to failure. Here's why.

    Ultima Online was unique in many ways; the virtue of being the first mass-market MUD. (Pause while Kesmai and Meridian 59 partisans throw rotten eggs.) One of it's hallmarks was that it was basically a PK mud (players being allowed and in fact encouraged by game mechanics to kill one another) yet was marketed as anything but a PK mud. If you look at the original UO box or pretty much any incarnation since, you won't see many screenshots featuring PvP combat. Everything is either socialization or exploration - two of Bartle's four groups of game players. To summarize for those unfamiliar with Bartle's work, almost all players can be defined as being a member of one of four groups - achievers, explorers, socializers and killers. In UO terms, "PvMers" were a mix of achievers and explorers, "roleplayers" were socializers and, of course, "PKers" and "Antis" were killers. Other folks have broken these groups down further, but the 4 groups work well for this piece.

    Now, in Ultima Online, we saw all four of those groups being thrown together by virtue of many things - the small size of the online world, the contest for limited resources, and the "chokepoints" that formed outside of safe zones, such as the area outside of Trinsic gates and the crossroads west of Britain.

    Now, no matter what side of the fence you were on then, the results were undeniable. As the wise men of Smash TV said, "TOTAL CARNAGE! I LOVE IT!" The killers won. Whether they wore red or blue, the killers won. They dominated the game, owned the game's resources, and killed other players at will, with the game rules serving as only minimal restraint. "Oh no! I'm a Dread Lord! Aagh!" UO was rapidly devolving towards Bartle's social model of what happens to a MUD with too many killers - no one is left, because the killers have killed everyone else.

    UO's solution was to penalize killers (according to rumors I've heard, PKs were actually outright targeted for harassment and removal from the game by purposely "flawed" game mechanics at one point in UO's development) and cull their numbers down so that they would no longer dominate the game. This struggle continued throughout UO's existence, with further and further penalties levied against killers until finally, with UO:R, the world was split and player killing was prohibited in one half of the game. Ultima Online is basically two games right now - one, Felucca, aimed at killers (once the promised "Faction" political system is introduced), and one, Trammel, aimed at achievers and explorers, and by default, socializers, who tend to despise killers. (Thanks to spectacularly poor support in UO's early years, political conflict between killers and socializers - "PKers" and "roleplayers" - has reached the level of jihad, both inside and outside the game itself.) UO's final solution for its killer problem, then, was one of complete seperation.

    Everquest's solution was to prohibit playerkilling except among playerkillers. This was an interesting theory, but was a complete failure in practice. To prevent interference in PK battles by non-PKs (healing, buffing, etc -- what in UO were called "blue healers") Everquest forbid nonPKs from casting any beneficial spell whatsoever on PKs. Since the vast majority of players in Everquest were nonPKs (many of them in fact refugees from Ultima Online and violently opposed to the PK concept) PKers thus found themselves unable to even change their bind point or ever be healed. You will almost never find a PKer in Everquest on the standard servers now, save those newbies who were unaware of the consequence of their actions.

    Instead, EQ's PKs gravitated to the PK=only server, "Rallos Zek", which was set up almost as an afterthought, where everyone defaulted to PK status. This "PK Everquest" had many problems of its own, such as looting disparity creating an overabundance of "naked mages", or players without any difficult-to-obtain equipment on them. "Racewar PK" servers were established early in EQ's development, but suffered the same problems as the standard PK server. They still have a cadre of loyal users - at any time there are usually about 2000 people on all the EQ PK servers - and they in fact enjoyed a brief renaissance when due to massive overcrowding on other EQ servers players were actually willing to endure PKs to play on a less crowded server.

    Still, Everquest's model for dealing with killers is, again, one of complete seperation - de facto, completely seperate servers. Everquest is skewed towards achievers in Bartle's model, with a literally insane amount of time and effort required to reach the pinnacle of achievment. There isn't much place for the killer in EQ.

    Asheron's Call sought to modify Everquest's basically successful model to appeal to more killers while still protecting most of the playerbase from them. The regular servers still have a small community of PKs on them, since the game system is not as punitive towards PK/nonPK interaction, but their numbers are small and they cannot attack any other than other PKs. Instead, most PKs play on Darktide, AC's PK server. AC's PvP system, although flawed, is still superior to EQ's, and monarchies in AC have carved up the world into their own defended territories. In fact, Darktide at the moment is in a sort of stasis, as new players find it difficult to gain a foothold (many Darktide players actively hunt and kill new players, seeing it as some sort of "rite of passage" or, more simply, they make easier targets) yet older players are unable to really affect the status quo of entrenched monarchies.

    So every major online RPG has dealt with PKs in the same way - complete seperation. Why? Well, it works. Ultima Online's early history shows that, given free reign, killers will dominate the game and drive away every other customer. It's the nature of the PK to hunt those weaker then themselves - and those are precisely the people who have no interest in being PK'd. The non-PKs will then kill the PKs - by leaving the game, by insisting that the game be changed, by petitioning the support system with example of the PK's antisocial behavior (examples most PKs are usually only too willing to provide) - thus enforcing the seperation themselves. UO's development, in large part, is the story of the non-PKs PKing the PKs. What PKs are left in UO now are usually bitter, anti-social, and to a man averring that the moment a better game for them is released, they are gone. Thus completing the seperation.

    Apartheid. It's a dirty word. But in this case, probably the only word that works. Going back to Riprend's essay, I read and find basically a blueprint for victimization. PKs can attack non-PKs here for this penalty, there for that penalty, etc. Unfortunatly, as long as the risk-reward ratio is skewed in the PK's favor, they will attack those weaker than themselves. It's the nature of the PK. No one wants to lose, after all. And if the risk-reward ratio is skewed against the PK, they simply won't play. An example is the "red PK", or PKs who ply their trade in UO despite the threat of stat loss should they lose. Very few PKs play red PKs for long - and those that do generally ensure that they attack only in numbers so overwhelming that their death is mathematically impossible.

    Why, then, must PKs and their prey be kept apart? To do that, we must look at the nature of the PK. They are usually (not always) violently opposed to the thought of any play style other than their own.     MENTORS, THE STANDARDS MAKERS
    Wednesday, June 14, 2000 - 9:26 AM [Filed by delusion]
    If you think the primary difference between traditional pen and paper RPGs and MRPGs has anything to do with computers, think again.

    The primary difference, of course, is the players - and not just the amount of them.

    Those of you who learned RPGs from the words of Gary Gygax (or his contemporaries), think back. Remember the first campaign you played with players who actually knew the game? If you were lucky, this happened the first time you played, otherwise you may have just futzed through the rules before playing in a "normal" campaign.

    You were playing with real people. They knew who you were, and at least some of them knew where you lived. They gave you a brief overveiw of the rules, probably as they talked you through creating a character.

    They explained to you why you couldn't start with a level 10 character in their fledgling campaign. They talked you through the concept of having to make decisions about your character's stats. They made it clear that fudging dice rolls wasn't cool with them. They showed you, by example, what role-playing was.

    You couldn't play a chaotic evil half-drow-elf multi-class assassin/paladin/magic-user/cleric with 18/00 strength, 19 intelligence, and 18 dexterity in their campagin... The Dungeon Master had his own world for you to adventure in, and "drow elves" didn't exist in it. He was flexible alignment with but not completely dismissive of the issue as he'd have to be for that particular combination. He didn't give experience points out like candy, which would be required to actually attain any level with such an absurd array of concurrent classes. He followed the most "player-friendly" method of character stat dice rolling in the manuals, but even so, you weren't going to get stats like those. Besides, he didn't allow anything over 18 in any skill for any reason, with the exception of Strength, which he maxxed at 18/50.

    Perhaps just as importantly, you were playing with other people who had characters of their own. Your "super character" wouldn't have fit in with any of the goals of the characters in your party, and your character would have either been at odds with theirs all the time, or you would have had to play it in a ridiculously out-of-character fashion.

    You decided to play the dwarven cleric instead. Not because the DM was a hard-ass, or because your fellow players were whiners. You played it because you were being taught how to play this game in a way that was compatible with the group.

    You had a mentor.

    Your mentor explained why certain actions weren't allowed, helped you through the more unusual rules, and helped you figure out how the group played. Maybe they expected you (and everyone else) to bring $5 to cover pizza halfway through the game session. They might have had house rules that weren't covered in the manuals. They let you get a feel for how the DM wanted you to declare your character's actions, and what you could expect from him in return.

    In MRPGs, it's possible to purchase a game, have no idea about any of its in-game or out-of-game social structures, have no concept of the standards that are expected of you, and play.

    You don't automatically have someone looking over your shoulder for the first couple weeks to explain to you why a certain character creation choice might unnecessarily weaken your character later on. You don't have a group of your peers whom you must deal with after the session by default. You don't really have any accountability at all beyond not getting caught doing something that is blatantly in violation of the game's license or usage agreement.

    What's worse, finding the wrong mentor in an MRPG can be worse for you and the game than not having one at all. What if the "community standards" of your group of peers thinks it's perfectly acceptable to do anything as long as you don't get caught? What if you end up playing with people who actively thrive on causing grief to other players? What if your "community" binds together simply on the basis that you all hate "role-playing" despite none of you ever having actually tried it?

    There are new groups that will be joining us in the greater MRPG community. MRPGs that are based on science fiction will bring in new crowds. MRPGs based on successful film franchises are on their way, and have installed fan bases. MRPGs for the console markets are in development. MRPGs designed for the strategy and action gamer are inevitable.

    Looking at these new groups in dismay and retreating to our "safe" cliques full of people who always agree with us is the best way we can ensure that none of our "community values" will ever rub off on them. And they will out-number us, no matter who you think of as "us".

    Looking at them as new gamers in need of mentors is one of the best ways to raise the bar of MRPG community behavior. Giving in to the lowest common denominator is our other alternative, and that's none too appealing.

    So let me just start by saying that I derive a certain amount of joy from the fact that in UO Raph “never found a way to have the good guys win.”

    I consider it poetic justice.

    UO was developed as a game that had somewhere near its roots this idea of “diversity”. PVPers and PKers were allowed into the world under the banner that the diversity was what would bring life and excitement to the game play. That was part of the general idea, anyway.

    But Raph has pretty much confirmed here what every PVPer and PKer in the game already knew. There was from almost day one an undeclared war on the “diverse” PVP and PK segments of the audience.

    Fine. But there’s something here that I find slimy. Pay attention to the social dynamics of what was really going on. First, player hunters are empowered in the game to follow their play style. Then they’re classified as the “enemy” of society. In fact, the game mechanics are set up in such a way as to forcibly classify player hunters as not only the foe, but as a truly damaging foe – they can loot, and loot hard. Not only that, but it’s hands-down the most profitable way to exist in the game.

    Then, having created the player hunter menace, UO proceeds to try and control this new animal. Why? And how? Well, the why is easy. Raph tells us.

    “Simply put, I think that the things that drive community are: shared interest to get everyone in the same place; limited resources that you need to cooperate over so everyone gets enough; and an enemy you have to fight to keep out.”-Raph


    So what were the player hunters? They were the enemy, the terrible soulless scourge that was to forge a community by bathing it in fire. But not too much fire. Not too much opposition. Not too big of an enemy.

    What Raph wanted was a controlled tool. A segment of society that he could set up to be the “victims” so-to-speak of a social experiment whose out come was SUPPOSEDLY already pre-determined. Here was the script: anarchy rules, then all good and decent folk react, communities form as civilized people band together and fight off the menace of immoral player hunters, over time player hunters realize the evil of their ways and of the futility of fighting against the valiant spirit of cooperative humanity. Oh how gushingly sweet. Happiness rules, lasting friendships form, and everyone settles down to a nice cozy knitting session at their hearths while sharing a warm cup of tea among their warmly regarded companions.

    Except the player hunters. They’re either dead, powerless, quit or were forcibly converted.

    But things didn’t quite work out as planned. Instead, the player hunters basically beat on everyone mercilessly. It was at this point that something HAD to be done. The war machine went into full tilt. A new alliance was formed: 90% of the player base, plus the designers, plus the game code. This coalition would muster its troops and smash the player hunter menace into the tame little community building tool that it was supposed to be.

    BAM! Visible marking of the “enemy”, notoriety, banishment from towns, constant nerfing of powerful player killing tactics, stat loss, bounty systems, blah blah blah. Whoooo!!!!!!!!!!!!!!!! GIT ‘EM!!!!!! SICK THE CROTCH CRICKETS ON ‘EM!!

    (slow smile) To repeat:

    “One of my biggest disappointments in UO is that we never found a way to have the good guys win.” – Raph


    A moment of silence for the effort? Sure. Why not. (pausing a few beats in a solemn hush)

    Why did it fail? What happened? How could the mighty forces arrayed against the player hunters fail to achieve victory? We could speculate on a thousand reasons. Accountability, instant travel, better and smarter players preferring the challenge of player hunting - whatever your favorite explanation is, you can have it.

    I’ll say it again. Poetic justice.

    Think about what was actually being done from a social perspective. One portion of the gaming community was being hurled down on the Tarpeian rocks of the game mechanics for the moral edification of another part.

    Of course, you could argue that the player hunters deserved whatever they got due to their antagonistic behavior. But I would argue that the point of a game like this is to conflict just as much as it is to cooperate. I’d argue that it was only bad decision making about death penalties that made player hunters such a rabid issue instead of the generally welcome added thrill that it should have been. I’d argue that it was the decision to try and set up the conflict as “All of Society” vs. “The Aggressive Player Hunters” that screwed things up, instead of setting it up as Community A vs. Community B, each with their own band of merry ultra-efficient player hunters.

    The game took players who signed up thinking that rising up and smashing down their enemies (or at least trying) would be some damn good fun, and demonized them. Not that all of us minded being demonized. But the mere desire to attack and defeat other players in A GAME is not inherently evil. Part of the fun of a game is that you have a harmless forum in which to conflict. It was the choice of the designers to give the act of player killing the sting that converted it from harmless conflict to “damn that pissed me off”.

    Although there was more to it all than simply teaching players that X action is good and approved, and Y action is evil and penalized. There was the purpose of forming the populace into a community. But it needed to be more than a community, it needed to be a PLANNED community, one where spoon-fed morals are enforced. Sort of Brave New World-ish, isn’t it? Raph Huxley? What do ya think?

    Or maybe there’s a better parallel. (Trying to think of an historical example where the law makers/government singled out a distinct group of people and used their presence as a menace to encourage the unification of the majority of the population under a new order, eventually even visibly marking that small group for easier identification.)

    Anyway, it’s an interesting irony to have tried to teach ethical behavior through the device of setting up a group of people as an expendable teaching resource. And then to have that expendable resource become a sort of anti-lesson? Now that’s rich.

    Damn. I just can’t think of that better parallel. Can you?

    WHAT IS THIS? HOOKED ON ETHICS?
    Monday, June 5, 2000 - 8:30 PM [Filed by hedron]
    Well here I am in an airport again. So you know what that means, don’t ya? Yap, another load of crap for you all to peruse.

    The subject of today’s false prophecy is Raph’s recent post that was cited somewhere down page of here.

    I had quite a few comments to make on his post, but for the sake of all of our attention spans (mine included) I'm focusing on just one area for now.

    You know what I thought when I read that post by Raph? I thought, “Now here’s a nice guy, who’s clearly intelligent and well read on this topic, deeply experienced in the issues (perhaps more than anyone else), and who is just trying way too hard.”

    Somewhere in the course of designing games, he crossed the border from creating entertainment to creating some kind of combination of a teaching experience and a model of real life. He knows that. He thinks it’s a good idea. But I can’t really say that I agree with him.

    “I can't think of any better experience to have in ANY game of ANY sort than for real people to work together against antisocial activity, selfish people, and other forms of creeping insidious evil, and WIN, and build something lasting and good. To work together and have fun together with types of people they never would have considered worth speaking to otherwise. And yes, to convert a few selfish jerks into better people along the way. If having this experience in a game means that they are more likely to dare to do it in real life instead of living in passivity, then I'll feel like something really important has been accomplished.”-Raph


    Raph believes that forming an online community centered on the vanquishing of evil is the ultimate gaming experience. That putting people in a position where they can build something which is lasting and good delivers the most satisfying gaming experience ever. But there’s a flaw in that concept. Building something lasting and good is a lot of work. And it requires the development of a complex system of social consequences for actions that are deemed unacceptable. It also requires functionaries to carry out those consequences.

    This is how real life works. And it’s not a coincidence, or something that can magically become Not-Work when transposed to a game setting based on similar rules. We maintain a civilized and orderly existence through paid police forces, courts, laws, elections, fire departments, insurance companies, taxis, room service, manicurists, message therapy, mental therapy, free gift wrapping, and blue light specials in aisle 5. Blah blah blah.

    But orderly and civilized existence is boring. Now in real life, it has lots of rewards. No one in real life wants to be killed for the sake of a more exciting existence. No one wants to have the freedom to raise his or her children in safety screwed with. No one wants to have their house broken into, their bank account raided, or their dog killed. Because in real life all of those things mean something. Sure it would be exciting to face a sword-carrying raider attacking your town, but what the hell? Die with a sword in your belly for entertainment value? No.

    Thus, in real life, all the tedium and boredom of maintaining a happy and peaceful society makes all the sense in the world.

    So we invented books, and radio, and TV to entertain us with spectator versions of what we didn’t want to risk doing ourselves. And eventually we invented computer games, and moved from there to massively multi-player computer games.

    What on earth could drive a designer to assume that the true goal of computer games of this ilk is to duplicate the social structure that we’ve already formed in real life? I don’t know about you, but I get up every morning and go to work. Why would I want to do that AGAIN in my spare time? Raph seems to think you can turn the work of building society into a “meta-game” that’s fun. I think that’s not workable. People don’t do work-like tasks on a consistent basis purely for their own moral satisfaction. Capitalism proves that. And they don’t do it for the community either. The failure of communism proved that.

    Here, in my (cough) humble opinion, is the task of a game: to amuse as many participants as possible, to as high a degree as is possible, for as long as possible, and on as deep a level as is possible within the paradigm. It’s not brain surgery to make that statement. It seems relatively obvious. And yet everyone seems to over complicate this task.

    Words like morality, ethics, realism, physics and immersion should all be secondary considerations. They are scales on which to measure various human activities. They are models of rule-sets that can be used as a starting point for the construction of a game like this. They are tools to enhance the flavor of the basic experience. But they are NOT the goal. Making a game more real or more ethical doesn’t make it a more playable and enjoyable game. And if it’s not playable, it will eventually fall short of its potential, regardless of the laudable nature of its other goals.

    What do we all really want out of games? I’ll tell you what. We want to log out, lean back, and each and every session say, “Damn that was a fuck of a good time.” We want to have that dumb ass smile plastered on our faces that says we enjoyed ourselves immensely. Isn’t that why we bought the game in the first place? Think back to the times you actually DID log out feeling like that. Do you really think that all the less-than-exciting game play in between those times was wholly necessary? Or was it just poor planning and execution of “fun delivery” by the game designers, dooming you to miles and miles of dry deserts and crotch cricket infestations before reaching each oasis of amusement?

    So tossing complicated rule sets back and forth to encourage ethical development, or laboring over whether a mage can wear armor, or a thief can wield a two handed sword or WHATEVER is not smart. Not unless you’ve first asked the following question:

    “What’s the fun way to do this?”

    Now don’t misunderstand me. I don’t mean the totally shortsighted version of fun – give everything to everyone because that’s what he or she wants. I mean a standard of fun that takes in the longer-range view of creating a lasting fun experience, not a death-orgy for a month before everyone gets bored and quits. (Although of course, I can’t fully disapprove of death orgies. I mean… come on! Death orgies?! WWHHEEEE!!!!)

    But really, it’s a great litmus test. “Should we make banks interconnected, or force people to walk back to where they banked stuff to get the items?” Easy. The question isn’t “What most closely resembles a medieval banking system?” It’s “What’s the fun way to do this? What maximizes the fun?” I don’t think there’s a game mechanic in the world that shouldn’t be exposed to that test at the beginning, middle, and end of conceptualization.

    Want to know whether you should allow teleporting around the world? What’s more fun? Getting where you’re going through a recall in UO, or trudging across empty landscape, like travelling in EQ with a warrior?

    Wondering if stat loss is a good solution for curbing PKs? Is it fun? Hell no. Why not make PVP low risk, so that those who die aren’t quite so pissed off? “But that’s not realistic! Death is death! It should hurt!” Ah HA! Caught you ignoring fun for the sake of meaningless realism again, didn’t I?

    It’s just like any other business. Game makers are fun merchants. Just like a restaurant is designed to deliver the pleasures of food and ambiance to its customers quickly and easily, a game should likewise find the most direct route to bring fun to its players. Its designers should work with that concept in mind.

    This piece isn’t meant to be an outline of how to build the perfect game, or even to make definitive statements about what should or shouldn’t be in a game. I know it’s not the easiest of tasks to design a game, and limiting the analysis process to one simple “Is it fun?” litmus is an intense oversimplification. But no matter how many additional criteria you can throw on top, that single question should remain paramount. And I’d maybe throw in as #2 the KISS principle (keep it simple stupid – just in case there’s anyone alive who doesn’t already know that).

    If you don’t shoot at the right target, you’ll never hit it. Give me some fun. Don’t try and be my mom, Raph. You missed that chance by a couple of decades and a sex change operation.

    And if the rest of you insist on trying to be my mom, you can try this dress on and come yell at me.

    THE EVIL THAT MEN DO LIVES AFTER THEM; THE GOOD IS OFT INTERRED WITH THEIR BONES
    Saturday, June 3, 2000 - 8:48 AM [Filed by great bob]
    I'm a sucker for the idea of the alternative universe. You know, what if the Spanish Armada triumphed, what if the battle of Hastings went the other way, what if Grant had been drinking at Appomattox? That sort of thing. I like to think of an alternative universe, too, that involves gaming. One where Tyrant was on board to handle the managment end, and Raph Koster handled the desgin end of the equation. As we enter into the new age of Ultima, where managment is on track and the devlopment team in chaos, thinking about what might have been becomes a rather pleasent diversion. What could that game have been like? We will never know for sure. What we do have is this. A recent post from Raph Koster on the newsgroup rec.games.computer.ultima.online. It's a very interesting piece by Raph on what he would have done different in UO. In EQ the Vision(tm) never changes, but then again it was never as vast or as interesting as Raph's.

    So this (really long) thread really made me think tonight (and not just because Zaphkiel made me look like a math-impaired idiot, either. :) It made me question a lot of assumptions. Forgive this rambling post as I dissect what my thinking is on all this--I welcome the feedback.

    First off, I have no patience for harassers, assholes, etc. They make my physically very angry when I see them, and during my whole time at OSI I was continually frustrated by the lack of action against them. I know many of you will not believe this statement, or will think it hypocritical, but I ask you to take it at face value.

    When we started up UO, we were very naive about some things. For one thing, the game design was originally for a MUCH smaller world. We were asked to change it from a 300 player game to a 3000 player game around nine months before ship. All of our expectations were for not only a smaller simulatenous population, but also for a smaller playerbase in general--forecasts for sales were not very high, and the most successful online game to date at that time was Meridian 59. We expected to do better, but not by an order of magnitude.

    A lot of the things we wanted to do were different from how muds had done things. We had both played and worked on muds with switches, and our experiences were universally lousy. Loopholes, ways to 'pk' without actually pk'ing, artificial restrictions on grouping and equipment use--all sorts of things that damaged immersion and physics and led to a lot of special-case code. We wanted to make a game that was more immersive, and that meant putting in a lot more freedoms into the game. We wanted to challenge players to act ethically, in the spirit of the Ultimas previous, without making it a set of quests that would be 'gamed' and up on a cheat website within a couple of weeks--and we didn't want that to happen not because it meant extra work making new quests all the time, but because it meant that ethics themselves were being "gamed" and were therefore meaningless. We were not prepared for the audience we got--this was evident not only in the game design, but if you recall, in the hardware infrastructure we had at launch.

    We also wanted to get away from levels, believing that levels were a) an addictive but shallow game mechanic and therefore not good for overall game longevity, b) divisive of players in that you have to create level restrictions on stuff all over the place, c) poorly suited for other forms of gameplay, such as questing, social achievement, etc, and therefore restrictive of player experimentation with other things to do. There were other things we wanted to do to change things from the typical mud pattern (which is today best exemplified for all of you by EverQuest, since its design is *extremely* close to that of a standard well-evolved Diku mud).

    Things that I have changed my mind on:

    - I used to think that a richer, more challenging game would be rewarded. I am no longer sure that is the case. I think that had we just made the same game we had made previously, only bigger, that UO would probably have done much better. The market, and more particularly the players, don't reward experimentation very much. More people are willing to do the same repetitive activity over and over again for the sake of getting a red polkadotted item to replace the green striped one, than are willing to engage in a broader range of activity. This is evident industry-wide, to my mind, and I am not saying to slam on EQ (especially not given that I work for Verant now). More as a comment on the audience in general--most people want mere entertainment, stuff that is easy to cope with. Stuff that doesn't make them ask questions of themselves. Witness TV and movies and books, all of which are mostly affirmations that "you're doing the right thing" or "whatever you do is normal compared to THIS."

    - I like safe and wild zoning now. I really, really didn't. I have never seen an arena embedded in an otherwise safe game that was at all popular after three months, and the boundary conditions are truly a pain in the ass to deal with, leading to tons of exploits and problems. On top of that, safe zones tend to be way the heck more profitable for everyone including the people who really ought to be playing in the danger zones, so the danger zones tend to tank, just like separate PvP servers do.

    - I used to think that you could reform bad apples, and argue with hard cases. I'm more cynical these days.

    - Closed economies can't work.

    - A sandbox is not enough.

    - I used to think that people were willing to act communally for the good of the community. Now I know more about the Tragedy of the Commons and the Prisoner's Dilemma and think that people are mostly selfish. This isn't Ivory Tower theory gone looking for empirical evidence. It's experience gone looking for explanations.

    Things that I still believe today:

    - Related to the last one--what I now know more about regarding how the Tragedy of the Commons and the Prisoner's Dilemma are reflected in the lack of communal action, has just reinforced my thoughts on the importance of the Other and so on. Simply put, I think that the things that drive community are: shared interest to get everyone in the same place; limited resources that you need to cooperate over so everyone gets enough; and an enemy you have to fight to keep out (and often, I think that I have served the role of said enemy in this newsgroup). Yes, communities form without the enemy, but they seem to fragment into cliques and manufacture an enemy within themselves (again, like this ng many times!). Shared interest by itself doesn't really drive community. It drives acquaintanceships. And acquaintanceships are easy to come by, there's no need to make a whole honkin' game for them. As far as limited resources--we were stumbling about in the dark on this issue when we set up the game economy, when we did the size map we did... none of the games out there limit resources enough because they are all hung up on "having enough for everybody." Well, a game with enough for everybody is a game where you don't need other people very much. But on top of that, even though UO did limit resources more than most, it didn't provide any benefit for sharing them or working together to extract them. Miners were penalized for working near each other, for example, rather than encouraged to do so.

    - Switches still suck. They damage immersion badly by requiring either the exclusion of or the fictional incoherence of all area-based effects; they do not handle many of the indirect forms of possible conflict (often fatal things, like blocking for example), leading to ways to get around the switch; they don't work very well as a way of mixing PvP and -PvP styles within one game because the grief players will just move on to alternate forms of causing grief while the PvPers will feel forced out of the game by all the restrictions...

    - Levels still suck. I don't know what the complete replacement is, but I am troubled by how addictive the experiences we're making are (like, seriously addictive, ruin-your-life addictive) and I think levels are a large part of that. Plus I still find them divisive of players and a forced limit on interaction, however convenient they may be for advancement ladders. They are a bad model in terms of adding ongoing content to your game, in that you always have to add at the top end, and you have database deflation problems. Lastly, I have trouble fitting in many of the mechanics we were successful in putting into UO, such as crafting, onto standard level systems.

    - There is a substantial amount of people out there who enjoy player vs player conflict of all sorts, who get crowded out of a game when it is completely safe, and go play elsewhere. And these people aren't necessarily assholes. But it is easy for them to become assholes if they feel put upon enough or if they think they can get away with it. I'm gonna disergard Bartle's Four, gonna ignore all statistics--this is just my opinion and my sense of things based on everything I have
    seen.

    - I still believe that running servers themed around PvP or not is also a bit of a waste of time. The amount of wolves who want to play on a wolf-only server is way smaller than the total amount of wolves, and generally speaking, wolf-only servers are extremely underpopulated. You might as well devote those resources elsewhere.

    - Someday we WILL be able to hand over the reins of policing to players. It will be seen as just a meta-game for those who are interested in it (and what's more, I bet the cops will be the same people we're currently turning into grief players with our limited mechanics). But right now, neither players nor developers are ready. I can't tell you how much I wish that in UO we had found a way to make the players able to do this, actually able to win against the bad guys--because I do regard those grief players and those rampant PKers (be they the "good" ones or the griefy ones) as the bad guys in this virtual world, far more so than the monsters. One of my biggest
    disappointments in UO is that we never found a way to have the good guys win.

    - Thinking crazily into the future--the above point matters a lot to me because I do think that we will have virtual spaces where there's no admins to call. And it's a good idea to tackle the problems of not having admins while we still have admins to fall back on. As it is, the FBI can't do diddlysquat about most hacking cases, and it's gonna get worse when you do, say, remote medical monitoring via a virtual environment, and you get the future equivalent of PKed somehow. I can't even predict what shape this will take, but it will happen... I also know that a lot of players might even agree that this is something that needs tackled, just not in their backyard.

    - Lastly, and feel free to call me a stupid idealist on this: I still believe we need to get all kinds of people into one game. That niche products are all well and good, but we already KNOW how to make those, and they aren't going to teach us anything interesting about ourselves. It's so easy to fall into ruts and niches in our real lives, and I want online worlds to offer us exotic experiences and interaction with people we wouldn't interact with otherwise, and a chance to try out lifestyles and worldviews we otherwise wouldn't have, a chance to try to solve problems that we find difficult to tackle in the real world. Otherwise, why bother making them? I am not that interested in them solely as games--games are all over the place, and there are plenty of narrowly focused communities out there. You can find a support group or hobbyist club for just about anything you want, but you're mostly going to find other people like you there. And I am not nearly as interested in how people interact with likeminded souls as I am in how to bridge gaps between people.

    In an odd sort of summary... Being safe from evil is, in my mind, an uneven tradeoff for the fact that you don't get to be heroes anymore, in that you can just opt out of fighting evil. It may be nobody wants to be heroes except when it doesn't count, when it isn't challenging, that people would rather fight "pretend evil" than the real thing, but I don't personally believe that. I still think people are better than that. I know this is an odd and probably controversial (perhaps even stupid) position to take, but it's how I feel. I think that the
    greatest value of interactive entertainment is when it engages you for real, and teaches you things for real. It is what made the Ultima series great. For me, the struggle to be good, to be one of the good guys, is where people were really challenged in UO, and it's not really a challenge that exists elsewhere. Sure, you can choose not to use ShowEQ, or choose not to auction spawn points in AC on eBay, but these are not as immediate and direct as dealing with people "virtually" face to face. Being safe from the only real evil in the game, and choosing not to fight it is, well, just fine, but it's also nothing that is going to teach you about where you stand. It's the difference between living the Virtues and, well, playing them in a computer game.

    It kinda saddens me and scares me to write the above paragraph, because I know that many will misread my intent in writing it, will take bits out of context, will feel insulted. But I don't mean any of that by it. The failure was ours in setting up the game, for not making it possible enough to live the Virtues and establish by consensus a better place, a better society. This is why I proposed elsewhere in the thread letting people fall back on the code crutch of a safe zone once they had done it once, at least. You still get the experience of actually building a society, but after that the hard part (keeping it going) is handled for you. (Yes, townstones are at the top of my "wish we had gotten it into the game" list).

    I can't think of any better experience to have in ANY game of ANY sort
    than for real people to work together against antisocial activity, selfish people, and other forms of creeping insidious evil, and WIN, and build something lasting and good. To work together and have fun together with types of people they never would have considered worth speaking to otherwise. And yes, to convert a few selfish jerks into better people along the way. If having this experience in a game means that they are more likely to dare to do it in real life instead of living in passivity, then I'll feel like something really important
    has been accomplished.

    My greatest worry is that instead, we've inadvertantly taught people to be bad as far as they can get away with. Or, far more troublesome, that Daddy will solve it for you and you can feel free to just complain about the problem from time to time, and ignore it. Right now, I have to believe that enough people learned the right lesson, because I DO see it every day in players of these games, and *especially* in players of UO, whom I have watched grow to a much greater awareness of social issues and community formation issues over the years, and become far better able to engage in high level discourse about the tough questions in MMORPGs.

    But there's no doubt that it can be done better, and though giving up and just entertaining the "good people" may be better business or may be enough to bring in the money, it's not enough to make me feel like this is a field worth being in. It's about the other people. Dani Berry said, "At the end of the day, nobody ever regrets not having spent more time on the computer." That's why she made multiplayer games, so that the computers would be other people. That's why I want to make this sort of game too.

    Hopefully this lengthy post puts some of my replies in this thread into context. Flame away.

    NEWS FLASH: UO'S TOO EASY!
    Saturday, June 3, 2000 - 3:11 AM [Filed by lietgardis]
    I observed an interchange on Tuesday in Trammel's West Britain. A "Famed" fighter, apparently still in that "pure black is really, really KEWL and I should wear lots of it!" phase, walked up to a trader and asked him if he could buy the kryss of might advertised on his profile. The trader asked the fighter to make an offer, and the fighter said that he wasn’t really sure how much things like that were worth. He paused a moment, and then gave the trader an honest offer of 8k.

    There are a few things to keep in mind here. First, a first character becoming the "Famed" generally indicates that one has achieved a certain level of proficiency in Ultima Online. Not the best, not the worst, but on the way out of the worst and into the best, as far as monster fighters are concerned. Second, items of might are about equal to weapons crafted by grandmaster smiths, and since everybody has his or her own GM smith, they’re worth next to nothing -- say 500, maybe 1000 gold for the magic label from a fair trader. I also assume the fighter was observing the most basic rule of trade –- when making an initial offer, give the lowest amount you’d be willing to pay.

    Wait a minute -- I promise there's a point. You've seen the asshole "new generation" tamers who didn't have the trial-by-fire of an excruciating advancement period; a big red dragon or two stealing your lich lord is a little hard to miss, especially when they're controlled by a gentleman named "BlAcKsAbBaTH." And you've seen the people you introduced to the game a month ago buying castle deeds. (Forsee yourself planning bodily injury to them when they manage to place before you next Friday.) One guy making an uneducated offer on a magic weapon means nothing alone, but in the context we cat-abusing UO players understand, it's quite significant.

    *drum roll* The bigger problem is this: people are advancing too quickly with the advent of Trammel and burst hours. It's not elitism on the veterans' part -- the character advancement rate has outstripped the player advancement rate, and this is bad. The character is good, but the player is still a newbie; it's like martial arts. You know how people who have achieved high levels in martial arts never abuse their abilities to break blocks and cars and stuff? They learned during all that training. You know how unsupervised little kids do stupid things like, oh, shoot each other, with guns placed in conspicuous places around the house? No training, no learning. Overkill metaphors, I know, but they get the point across. It's not elitism, it's just concern.

    Trammel makes it possible for people to get to high levels quickly; there's nothing to stop people from farming liches to riches, titles, and multiple grandmasterhood without learning anything about the game on the way. I didn't go to Deceit until I'd been playing for a year and a half and had a good mage to take there. Now it's the next stop after the character creation screen and the Sweet Dreams Inn. Player killing and slow skill gain rates helped pre-Renaissance players become familiar with Britannia before joining the high title-orc mask-black sandals-roof of Britain bank club. Trammel has neither, and that very large club is full of people who couldn't walk from Britain to Shame if their account depended on it, and couldn't tell you where the ancient wyrm resides, and aren't aware that anatomy can be used on people and creatures for a brief physical description, and don't know the tradional values of low-level magic weapons.

    Let me refute elitism again. There's nothing wrong with people having gold. I've never understood those who put "balance" in their bank macros; I suppose they're attempting to impress the masses, but I don't care about it. It doesn't bother me if newer players have better skills either; I'm proud of what I've achieved without macroing, and have accepted that I'll never be the best. I'm fully aware that titles don't mean anything (although I know you're going to say it over and over and over again in the message board thread linked at the bottom here). I do have a problem when people abuse skills and gold because they don't know how to use them, because they got too much too fast and didn't have the time to learn. The titles are merely additional evidence. Wouldn't you think it strange to see a Glorious Lady buying ruin crossbows for 5k a piece?

    You have to learn something about the game before making it to the top, and this takes time, no matter how much good Companion advice a player gets. The less time it takes a player to achieve material wealth and glory, the less he or she learns about the game, and the sooner he or she gets bored and quits. Everybody tells me EverQuest is on one end of the advancement curve spectrum; apparently Ultima Online, in the wake of the Renaissance, overcorrected to the other side.

    Toilet Scrubbing Burn Out
    Friday, June 2, 2000 - 3:50 PM [Filed by hedron]
    So I’m sitting here in an airport in New Jersey, and I’m thinking about game design. Why? Because my damn flight was delayed, and the earliest flight I can be confirmed on is 3 hours later, and I’ve got nothing better to do.

    Oh yah, and because I’m a freak and I think about crap like this anyway. Throw on top of that the fact that I’m now set up to post articles on this site regarding that very topic, and there you have it.

    Here’s my pissed-off-in-a-dumpy-airport-while-thinking-about-game-design article.

    I hate leveling. Yep. I hate it. Go ahead and tell me to go back to Quake. See if I care. I still hate it. Leveling is the most mechanical, moronic, plodding, and boring part of playing MRPGs. It’s a necessary evil. It serves a purpose and a vital one when employed correctly. But make no mistake. When it’s done wrong, it’s evil.

    See I’m a veteran of all this crap. That makes me old, bitter, and impatient. I’ve leveled my way through Neverwinter Nights, Ultima Online, Diablo, Everquest, and now (reluctantly) I’m plunking around AC. And it’s killing me with boredom. To top it off, I’m waiting anxiously for Shadowbane, and you know what that means. More leveling.

    It’s boring. B-O-R-I-N-G. I mean, if somebody walked up to you and told you pre-MRPG-addiction that you were going to spend hours and hours of your life fighting with AI that could be out-thought by your average hamster… would you have believed them?

    If they’d told you that for the next 6 months of your life, you would be repetitively hitting F3/F4/F1 (or whatever) on your computer to accomplish a virtual task that challenged you and your human faculties about as much as scrubbing a toilet. Would you have volunteered to get started right away?

    Hell no.

    But wait! What if they told you that it was cool because:

    A) For every toilet you scrubbed, you’d get a bigger scrub brush, and a bigger toilet to scrub, and
    B) They’d decorate the bathroom like a medieval castle, pipe in lame and repetitive music, and let you invite a few buddies over to help you scrub some REALLY BIG toilets as a team?

    Ya know what this is like? It’s like water torture. Only instead of drops of water splashing on my forehead, it’s a slow trickle of experience bouncing off my CPU. What a load. What a crock. What a pain in the ass. It’s the leveling treadmill. Bleh.

    But I keep slogging through it. Why? Why you ask? YOU DARE ASK A PISSED-OFF-IN-A-NEW-JERSEY-AIRPORT-PVP-FANATIC WHY?! Ok, then I’ll tell you.

    Because I’m after the pot o’ freakin’ gold at the end of the rainbow, man. I want to have fun. I want to immerse myself in the fantasy of the classic RPG world. I want to dive into challenging combat, and interesting conversations. I want to get to know some people in a game and then slaughter them relentlessly. Hell, maybe even spare a few.

    BUT I DON’T LIKE LEVELING.

    Maybe you do. But ya know what? I bet you won’t when you’ve been knocking around as long as I have. This type of game is a relatively new genre for many people, and the intricacies of MRPG kindergarten (PVE play) are still interesting to people learning to color inside the lines. But the reward of a nifty looking graphic wrapped around a mathematical description of a bigger toilet brush can only go so far.

    Ask yourself how long you can last in a genre that keeps asking you to restart a long and tedious process of leveling. And it’s not as though we’re burning out at the rate of the release of games. Thanks to the constant efforts at balancing, we’re obliged to go through multiple characters in each and every game. The sad part is, when a game does come out that gets everything right, the veterans of this process will be so disgusted that they’ll blow it off.

    Don’t believe me? Look to the limited popularity of AC. Why is AC performing so much less well than EQ and UO did? Well, partially, of course, it’s just fractured markets and some admittedly poor PVE in AC. But EQ did quite well in getting its flood of UO users who were sick of things in the Origin camp. So what’s the difference?

    Here’s my theory. It’s at least due in part to the intense burn out that EQ caused among people who had to level level level. I’ve talked to a number of guilds that didn’t bother with AC and cited as one of their first reasons the fact that EQ burned them out for a while. Level Quest. Heh.

    Oh, and then there was the coin-only loot test that EQ did on one of their servers. And when people didn’t flock to that server en masse they declared it a failure. As though people could stomach starting over in a game that demands that so much time and frustration be sacrificed on the alter of leveling!

    These are clear indications (to me anyway) of the waning interest in hamster-wheel-powered AI, and PVE, and the building frustration with the leveling process. Now that’s not to say that the genre is doomed! It’s not. It’s the post-max game that really harbors the fascination of this genre. It's the post-max game that (IF done correctly) can support a player community that stays in the game for 5 years or more.

    Of course, there’s more to the formula of what makes a good post-max game than the scope of this update allows me to cover. Things like reasonable level caps, low risk PVP, human skilled combat, player-run competition, NPC manipulation, geographically separated PVP/NON-PVP areas, etc. And the tough part is that unless you get the whole picture, it’s hard to understand how this goes together. I mean, if you take away leveling in a game like EQ, you’re left with nothing. So at first glance, it seems like a bad idea – UNLESS you put the other things into place.

    But the point is this: the leveling treadmill eventually = burn out. It’s inevitable. Ask yourself how you’re going to feel about playing and leveling in the NEXT MRPG (which you WILL do because the post-max game in everything that’s out now blows goats or doesn’t exist). Are you going to have the same feeling of fun and excitement that you had when you jumped into UO? Or when you moved from UO to EQ? Or when you grudgingly decided to try out AC? Or are you gonna be pissed off in an airport somewhere musing over how annoying scrubbing virtual toilets is, like me?

    You’ll get there too.

    You just don’t know it yet.
