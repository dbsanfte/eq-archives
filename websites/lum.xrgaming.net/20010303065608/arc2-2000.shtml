COMMUNITY INVOLVEMENT AT ITS BEST HAS LEFT THE BUILDING
Friday, March 31, 2000 - 6:57 PM [Filed by lietgardis]
Richard Garriott isn't the only person leaving Origin. We're told twenty other members of his court have been asked to leave as well, twenty other faceless developers, twenty whose names we don't know and whose situation we're almost ignoring, unless we're circling vultures from Verant, Wolfpack, and Reakktor. Make that nineteen unknowns. We know we're losing Firedog.

Firedog didn't frequent the Dev Board, and was thus one of the lesser-known UO developers. But while he was staying away from the chaos of CoB, he was helping smaller groups -- tamers and alchemists in particular. I can't itemize what he did for tamers, since I was never involved in that group, but he was the alchemy community's greatest benefactor. He posted regularly on our message board, looking for input on ideas and for information on problems. He visited our IRC channel during many UOHoCs, not only for the same reasons as he read the board, but to simply get to know us. He set up a whole special testing area for us on Test Center, and he visited us there as well. And to this day, he's the only developer I've ever seen attend in-game events as himself (the Lake Superior Alchemy Festival, specifically).

This implies a lot of dedication to me. No other developer has worked so hard for any single part of the Ultima Online community. And even though we're not all tamers and alchemists, we're still collectively losing out. We're losing because we no longer have a developer working so hard on any single facet of the game. We don't have one working so closely with any facet of the community. We're missing Firedog's example of community interaction for the whole team.

I hope somebody took notes. I want to see other developers acting as representatives for small groups; I want to know that such groups are getting attention at all. I don't want a dev team who gets its daily Dev Board digest from Calandryll and calls it "community involvement." I want one that gets its own hands dirty and actually communicates and builds relationships with the players themselves. We've lost the part of it that did.

Goodbye Firedog, and good luck with your future. I hope you find opportunities in the MMORPG industry; a single-player game wouldn't allow the virtues of yours I've seen to come out. Remember that gaming is infinitely more rewarding than making word processors.

Let's move forward.

STEPS ON THE ROAD
Friday, March 31, 2000 - 6:57 AM [Filed by lum]
I have something to confess here: I am a terminal ubergeek. (No, really?) And it's all Richard Garriott's fault.

When I was growing up in the late 70's, the science lab in our high school had a TRS-80 Model 1. I managed to singlehandedly piss off everyone else in the school who wanted to play Asteroids or Space Invaders on it by convincing the teacher that I wanted to, you know, learn how to program. So after school, every day, I sat at it, for months, with a dogeared BASIC reference in one hand and some RPG rules in the other, trying to craft the Ultimate Game.

So did Richard Garriott. Only he sold his. (Probably as well I didn't, since most of the time it didn't work.)

In 1984 I graduated from high school. My graduation present was a shiny new Atari 800, complete with huge whirring floppy drive. One of the first programs I got for it was Ultima 2. I think I played it and its sequel for around 3 years or so. Sure, there was no plot whatsoever to speak of and the combat mechanics were unbelievably dorky (you basically got on a boat and parked outside Europe and blew away anything that came toward you). Ultima 2 and 3 is probably a large part of why I dropped out of college. (I mean, come on, COBOL punch cards or sitting in a forest waiting for Dawn to show up. You make the call.)

Most people thought Ultima 4 was the bomb, but truth be told I didn't care for it much. (The fact that I was still only halfway through Ultima 3 when it came out probably helped.) No.. for me, it was Ultima 5. That I can still say is probably one of the most unbelievable games I had ever played. Y'see, it was the first one that actually had plot... had things happen for a reason. The kid in Yew who pleads with you as you leave not to leave him. The death of Iolo (Hey! I can't res him! He's... DEAD!). And yes, I blew up the world using Mondain's skull. Probably not the best of omens.

Things changed. Origin's corporate philosophy became "don't just push the envelope of PC hardware, rip the envelope into small tiny bits" around Ultima 6. My idea of upgrading PCs meant trading in the Atari 800 for a Commodore 64, so I was left behind for the next few years. In the interim I got into the local BBS scene, wrote a lot, pissed people off, the usual.

Around the time I finally updated to a PC (in 1995 or so -- don't laugh) Ultima Online was being talked up pretty hot and heavy on Usenet (being an ubergeek, I called a BBS in California long distance to read Usenet, and got fired from my job for it). Seemed pretty cool. In fact, since BBSs were busily being shredded by the juggernaut called "Internet" at the time, it actually seemed like, well, a virtual community.

When UO finally came out, I actually bought it first for my new wife, because I felt guilty about always buying myself games. "Look, hon, here's a game where you can bake bread instead of making things go boom." A week later I got my own copy. A year later I started a website. (No, not this one. Believe it or not this is actually the second ORPG website I've done.)

I'm sure we'll see a new gaming company pop up somewhere shortly with someone who may or may not be called Lord British still at the helm. So, yeah, Ultima 9 was a big pile of goo, and Garriott had almost nothing to do with UO aside from the rare token appearance, and his latest interviews had the air of someone who really needed to get out and breathe actual oxygen, but you know, I think he's earned being weird. I mean, looking back at my life, the degree to which it has been shaped by his work is, well, frightening. And I'm not even that much of an Ultima fan.

Good luck, and good fortune to everyone else busily sending out resumes today.

ULTIMA ONLINE: REFORMATION
Player vs Player Enhancement Specifications

Changes to combat skills and weapons to increase the diversity and tactics in player vs. player combat (PvP) and combat as a whole. Note that many of these changes are related to each other. It is important to consider all of the changes together, rather than looking at one change by itself. Be sure to read the entire document carefully.


Archery
The archery skill will be significantly resturctured to make it more realistic and more effective in Player vs. Player combat.
  • Current archery weapons will be replaced. Their replacements will be Handgun, Shotgun, and Plasma Rifle.
  • The damage and rate of fire of these weapons will be increased significantly.
  • Archery will be renamed "Gunnery" and Bowcrafting will be renamed "Gunsmithing".
  • Smithing of guns will require iron ingots. Creation of ammunition for guns will require sulfurous ash for handguns and shotguns, and valorite ingots (which will be renamed "plasma core") for plasma rifles. This will counterbalance the insta-kill nature of plasma rifles. We think.
  • All NPC rogues, wizards and paladins will carry shotguns and plasma rifles. All NPC beggars and seekers of adventure will carry handguns.
  • Players who continue to use bows and arrows in this new environment will be laughed at upon logging in (the laughter will be implemented in a future sound patch)


Poisoning
  • You will not be able to heal a poisoned character. You can, however, encourage the character and give them moral support as they die painfully.
  • A new poison, anthrax, can be found on sheep when slain. Anthrax may be dropped in a fountain in any town. All NPCs and PCs within that town will die within the next ten minutes.
  • Plasma rifles may not be poisoned.


Parrying

The chance to successfully parry a melee attack and the amount of damage absorbed upon a successful parry will now depend on the type of shield the character is using, his/her parry skill, his/her intelligence and dexterity, the clothing he or she is wearing, the length of hair selected upon character creation, whether or not that character is in a guild, how much food the player has eaten recently, and the mood of the character (this is a server-side value).

The rumors that damage being parried is simply a random value from 0 to 100 are false.

Lumberjacking and Axes

The lumberjacking skill will provide a special move, "Off With His Head", when using the executioner's axe. This special move will instantly kill the lumberjack's opponent, unless the character in question is red (since it is the opinion of the dev team that red characters rarely require the use of their heads). The higher the characters lumberjacking skill the more chance of the move taking effect, up to a 15% chance for 99.9 lumberjacking. At Grandmaster lumberjacking, the chance is 20%.

In the future we may implement the continuation of play as a headless, with similar stats and skills to other headlesses.

Two-handed Weapons

Any melee weapon that requires two hands to wield will gain a special attack. The type of special attack will depend on the type of weapon used. These special attacks will only work against player characters, not against monsters or animals.
  • Mace Weapons: "Skull Crusher" - the head of the opponent is caved in, resulting in 50-90 points of damage and a permanent loss of 50-90 Intelligence.
  • Sword Weapons: "Lionheart" - the opponent is beaten down by approximately 32,000 sword strikes. We hope to be able license the Final Fantasy 8 animation for "Lionheart" for this special move. It is hoped that lag will not be increased by 7 or 8 "Lionheart" animations on screen at once.
  • Fencing Weapons: "Gay Blade" - the opponent is paralyzed by laughter at your bringing such a useless weapon class into PvP. This paralyzation is non-resistable and non-interruptable. Especially by those damned trapped boxes.
Upon a successful hit, there will be a small chance to inflict one of the special attacks. The base chance to inflict one of the special attacks is 20%. A high intelligence will give a small bonus towards the chance to execute a special attack up to a total chance of 30%. If you are using a plasma rifle, none of these apply, since you can pretty much kill anyone onscreen at will. We like plasma rifles.

Hitting and Intelligence

Currently, melee weapons check for whether a hit is successful or not in the beginning of the swing animation. This will now be scaled based on intelligence. The higher your intelligence, the earlier in your swing you hit. At very low intelligence, you will never swing at all, but simply stand there like a worthless simp and ask "George, can we see the rabbits now?" over and over.

Armor Changes
Armor ratings and dexterity penalties will be modified. The table below illustrates the changes:

Power Armor 40 AC/-22 DEX
Kewl Mail 28/-12
Bilbo's Ring 22/-6
Studded Leather Bondage Suit 16/0
Leather Bondage Suit 13/0
Bone Thugs N' Harmony Outfit 30/-15 (additional absorption of Gun damage)

Additionally, leather armor will not cause any meditation or stealth penalty, although occasionally large women with whips may take an unhealthy interest in you.

Recall

Recall is gay and will be removed. [Young] players will be allowed to cast recall, but a sound clip of an annoying child yelling "Thou Art Gay!" will play whenever recall is cast.

Ride-able Animals

The exploit that allows a ride-able animal to be refreshed after becoming fatigued will be addressed. Animals will now tire based on the weight of the person riding them. Additionally, animals will eventually require food, attention and love. No, not that kind of love.

Wrestling Moves

New attacks with the wrestling skill.
  • "The Claw" - Player picks up a chair from his backpack and slams it over the head of his opponent, stunning them for 1-5 seconds.
  • "The People's Elbow" - Player prances gaily about the screen, pointing out his inherent superiority to all passers-by. Since he is prancing gaily, he may now cast Recall.
  • "The Twist" - Player marries Vince McMahon's daughter in a stunning reversal of fortune. Opponent is dumbfounded for 8-12 minutes.
  • "The Scrivener" - Player hires a scribe to write a best-selling book about his or her life. Fight is halted as the player walks around rubbing his chin trying to remember what in his life anyone would remotely care about.

SHADOWBANE

Developed by Wolfpack Studios, publisher to be announced. An ORPG where you play to crush, not bake bread like those other sissies.

Still without a publisher (they were supposed to announce one last week), and a recent interview published by Dr. Twister's site made some wonder if the core game design wasn't still up for grabs (one week there's combat-related trade skills, the next week there isn't, one week it's a deep diplomatic/military sim, the next week its a fragfest of 0wnage). Still, this PvP-friendly ORPG has a rabid fan base and plenty of hype.

Assuming a publisher is found, Wolfpack is targeting an August 2000 release date. Both Windows and Macintosh versions are planned.

ANARCHY ONLINE

Developed and published by Funcom. A persistent mission-based RPG set in the far future.

Anarchy Online is set 30,000 years in the future (not 30,004 or 29,998, 30,000) where a struggle between the evil Omni-Tek megacorp and the brave Rebel Alliance is set to determine the future of the next 30,000 years or so. AO is a 3D 1st person game, and those who have seen its engine rave about its detail and how it blows away everything else on the market. Of course, that engine hasn't actually supported thousands online in the same room yet, either.

AO's developers promise both a PvP element to the game (since players choose a "team" when creating a character) and a robust mission-based quest system. Looks pretty interesting and should appeal to a lot of folks who so far haven't been interested in medieval fantasy ORPGs.

Anarchy Online is planning Windows and Linux versions, and is set for a Fall 2000 release.

ULTIMA ONLINE 2

Developed and published by Origin Systems, a "Persistent State World", or complete world simulation akin to Ultima Online 1 set 200 years after the first game.

The UO2 dev team has been under a Cone of Silence ever since the game's announcement last year, which no doubt was embarassing when the only information the community could gleam of the game was from some really bitchin' dolls. UO2 will be a 3D 1st person/3rd person over-the-shoulder hybrid akin to Asheron's Call's default view, with 3 seperate player races representing the past, present and future of Britannia. (Pause while every "reputable" web site pretends to be discovering these details for the first time.) As with the new UO:R expansion, instead of a PvP switch play styles will be segregated geographically. The skill system will be based loosely on Ultima Online's skill system, although far more tree-based implying that although there will be no player classes, specialized character archetypes will be possible. Player housing and a fully working in-game economy are also promised. The whole project is really quite ambitious (we've never seen player-placeable housing in a 3D ORPG environment yet, for example) and it'll be interesting to see how much of what's promised can be pulled off.

No official release date for UO2 has been announced; some press releases have mentioned Winter 2000.

DARK AGES OF CAMELOT

Developed by Mythic Entertainment. No publisher yet announced although Mythic has many games you've never heard of, such as "Godzilla Online" and "Starship Troopers Online" already on Kesmai and AOL.

Set shortly after King Arthur's death, players can be either the stalwart British, the wild Norseman, and the, um, Celtic Celts. These teams cannot attack one another but can band together and invade the other two. (A newbie area safe from all that sturm und drang is promised, as well.) Looks to be a fairly deep, combat heavy ORPG, although little is really known at this point aside from a few impressive screenshots and an exclusive relationship with the Vault Network, which I guess means I can't talk about this game any more. In fact, until now they've been supporters of stat loss - they understand that PKers have to be rare and with a risk to the PK. When they stand up and say, "It's time for a change", I listen. The reason stat loss has worked up until this point is that it is actually thinkable to recover from, yet not so trivial as to be meaningless as a penalty. The UO:R changes escalate the stat loss penalty to make recovery unthinkable. If you see a red fighting a grizzly bear or some such, well you pretty much have a neon light over his head saying "Kill me and destroy my character even further!"

First, for this sort of thing to be implemented, the ambitiousness needs to be ramped downward. There aren't that many stat loss reds, and Origin isn't going to devote resources to designing UO:R version 2 for them. Any proposal that works needs to be accomplished with as little coding as possible. This sort of elegance also keeps possible exploits to a minimum. (Reds? Exploiters? Say it isn't so!)

However, I like the concept. The idea of stat loss is to take the offending character out of circulation for a not inconsequential period, whether through waiting out the red count as a ghost or taking the stat loss and rebuilding the character. The initial problem with this is that the waiting process isn't very fun for the waiting person; although it is a consequence of the player's actions, the fact is that player is paying $10.00 a month for the privilege of suffering punishment. The current problem, of course, is that with the ever escalating antimacro jihad, waiting out stat loss is no longer a solution. Origin's response, to work off the counts during regular play, isn't an option, unless you like dying to waves of opportunistic notoPKs for some reason.

You would also have to disable access to boats for offending characters. Once you do that -- well, there really is No Escape. Buc's Den is a pretty big place. Now there's nothing stopping you from hitting your bank box, or from others to meet up with you and equip you. But you aren't going anywhere. And there's lots of folks who probably have a few bones to pick with you. And I'm sure antiPK guilds would love to have some scores to settle. And if you're a kewldewd, hell, most of the inmates will probably start to kill you over and over on principle alone.

Something even cooler... just disable recalls and gates ENTIRELY within Bucs Den. All access via boat (which remember, the inmates can't access). So if those Antis want to raid Bucs? They do it the old fashioned way. Storm the boats!

Now see? You take the PKs off the streets, and create another dimension to the game, and have some fun in the process.

Fun is good. We like fun. Even for reds.

Excellent article posted on Gamasutra: Constructive Politics in a Massively Multiplayer Online Roleplaying Game. This has always been a subject near and dear to my heart; once you get to 7x GM/Level 50 the REAL game begins.

The first and most important thing that you must consider when creating a political system is that it must have meaning. It's not enough to, for instance, set up an electoral system and create some offices to be run for. If the system has no meaning to the player-citizens, then any power that officeholders have over the citizens will serve no purpose except irritating and angering the citizens. You must create perceived benefit for the player-citizens.

And again, in a note that will carry some weight for those playing UO:

There are really two emotions that we want to arouse in the players to accomplish the goal of comradeship: fear and the elation that comes from overcoming challenges. It's important to understand that by fear I don't simply mean the fear one feels when one looks out ones window in the morning to see Mongol hordes thundering towards that lovely little picket fence in your front yard that you spent last weekend painting. I also mean the fear of humiliation; the fear that comes when your hated rival bests you at something you both care about.

The most obvious way to create this sort of unifying threat, which has numerous real-life parallels, is hostile foreign militaries. Creating a military system for your statist governments is a topic too complicated to go into here, but suffice to say that as long as your military system has potentially real negative effects for the citizenry of the victim political organization, and provided that the victim citizenry doesn't feel totally powerless to combat the threat, and further provided that there are perceived positive benefits to being a citizen (public services, etc), external threats can be a fantastic way to gel a citizen base into a viable sub-community within your MMORPG. Fear is a powerful motivator. This fear can be useful, even when the object of the fear triumphs. Providing that a certain hope of eventual success remains among the citizenry, the siege mentality that can come from being beaten can really bind people together.

I hope all you Ultima Online and Shadowbane developers are taking copious notes. There WILL be a test later. Tune in later, when I present an objective implementation of the ideas presented in this thesis. I like to call it "Ultima Online Faction System 2.0".

STAND AND DELIVER... YOUR HONOR OR YOUR LIFE
Thursday, March 9, 2000 - 9:10 PM [Filed by lum]

Reading the boards and checking my email, one would think that the new UO expansion is some sort of horrible punishment being visited upon the UO player base. With the clean sweep of one patch session, PKs will be savagely neutered, and player-run towns will be savagely torn apart by those same neutered PKs. You’d think those two thoughts couldn’t co-exist, but you’d be wrong.

Contrary to popular belief, we here at LumCorp do have opinions on all this. Hell, we have at last count 6 megabytes of opinions online. Just search through them in that happy little box over to the right. And last time I checked, we haven’t been at all shy about sharing them with you, the viewing audience, which last time I checked my statistics, made up about 1% of all current UO customers. So here are my opinions, once again, on the topics everyone seem so worked up about. In fact, since I’m in such an expansive mood, I’ll even solve everyone’s problems, hopefully in time to adjourn for a flagon of fake ale.

An epiphany struck me as I thought about this today. The PKs and the player run towns – both in an uproar. They really are all the same topic. That topic is control.

At first glance, you would see the self-proclaimed Mayor of Moonglow and the Murderer Muffaletta as diametrically opposed creatures, but they’re really not. They both have the same goal – that of controlling your game. They want you to play the way they have decided is the correct way to play the game. And they are oh, so unholy pissed that what little control they have is going away.

The Murderer Muffaletta is an easier case. No one likes murderers. They are rude, uncouth, and speak in words of three letters or less. So it’s easy to just kiss them off. And, in large part, this is justified. Being a murderer is not a lifestyle option. Being a murderer is not being a member of an oppressed minority. Being a murderer is not a character class you choose when making a new character. It is the result of choices that the player behind the character makes. One of UO’s main problems in the first year of its life was that there were no consequences for those actions.

Adrick over at Battlevortex has been a very vocal advocate of those murderers. His response is that stat loss is unfairly punitive. Well, duh. Punishment is punitive. The entire point of the exercise is to dissuade the players from randomly murdering each other. Adrick and the rest of Those Darn PKs have been very adamant that stat loss is unfair, but have been curiously silent as to what the replacement for that penalty should be.

“Well,” someone else might say, “setting foot in the Enhanced PvP lands” (as opposed to the Gimped PvP lands) “is tantamount to consenting to be killed.” No. The old lands are not turning into Abyss, and they are not turning into Siege Perilous. This no doubt disappoints many of you. But the fact of the matter is that, with a revamping of the Order/Chaos/Guildwar system that everyone involved sees fit to ignore completely for some reason, the rules in the old lands are the same as they always have been. There will still be PKs, and they will still be in numbers too large to ignore, yet too small to make playing the game impossible. The sky is not falling, the world is not coming to an end, and Siege Perilous and Cool Test Center (is it still called that?) are still there for those who don’t want to bake bread, but play games to crush.

But that’s a problem for many reds. They don’t want responsibility. They don’t want it to be difficult. That’s why they’re PKs, for God’s sake. Being a PK is the quickest way to wealth in the game. Human beings have the best loot. It’s a simple thing to understand.

Which then makes a possible replacement for stat loss present itself – after a person becomes Red, they may no longer loot any grey or blue corpses. No loot, no PKs. This won’t stop minerkillers and it won’t halt Noto PKs, but it will control the PK for loot phenomenon. Will it make the PK problem worse or better? Who knows. The brutal fact is that stat loss, unfair as it is, does have the benefit of actually working for its intended purpose – keeping the PK population under control. It’s really doubtful that the UO dev team will disassemble something that works, especially considering the amount of things that don’t work. And frankly, operating under stat loss is probably a better deal for the PK then operating without loot. You know, without loot, those Corp Por regs become a bit rarer (well, except for the 99.99% of PKs with blue mules.)

Actually, what is really crippling PKs with the new changes aren’t so much the thought of stat loss as the fact that you can no longer safely spar up your skills in a house any more. No more safe Red macroing. That is a sea change for the Red, and many of them understand this already. The PKs are about to become the hunted, fleeing through the wilderness hoping that their pursuers never find them – oddly enough, very closely akin to the life of a murderer in reality.

The rumor floating around now is that Sunsword is hinting of a Red Amnesty – a one-time-only chance for reds to lose their murder counts, and rejoin the rest of humanity in Trammel fighting opposing factions, or Felucca (or as I like to term it, “Happy Fun Land” since I can’t spell Fellucca) slamming fake brews. Of course, very few would actually seriously take that chance and then remain blue, but it’s a gesture that in my opinion needs to be made.

You see, there’s this thing called the faction system. It hasn’t gotten much press with the usual suspects lately. Most blues think it’s part of that icky Enhanced PvP stuff they are running away from as fast as they can. Most reds think it’s, to use PK/junior high school argot, “really gay, dude”. But the faction system is the most exciting thing introduced into UO since… well, ever. It makes Order/Chaos look like a bunch of silly townfighters. It makes guildwars look like a pack of idiotic gang bangers. Screw fighting for gold and regs… try fighting for political power. You know, like the big boys do in real life. You think Reds should be allowed in Britain? Fine. Take over Britain. (You can have it, that place is a pit anyway.)

But so far an embarrassing amount of folks regard the faction system as anathema. The hard core PKs see it as a roleplaying thing. The hard core roleplayers see it as a PK thing (since most hard core roleplayers misspell PvP “PK” whenever they are able to utter the phrase in Olde Anglish) What’s really funny is that most of those PKs post on message boards “this is so gay! I can’t wait for Shadowbane dood!” when Shadowbane is built around a very similar faction system as its complete reason for existence. One can only imagine the Shadowbane message boards in 2001. “this game is so gay. Remember UO? We could r0x0rs whoever we wanted! This game sux my nadz!”

And PKs aren’t the only ones ignoring the faction system. The player run towns, with a few noble exceptions, are screaming bloody murder that they want to get the hell away from it as quickly as possible. They are demanding special exemptions, that their carefully constructed empires be moved magically en masse to the Happy Fun Land, or in one extreme case, just making the old lands Happy Fun Land with a flip of a PK switch. (I guess the Reds currently in Happy Fun Land get to fall down and migrate as ghosts or something. Hope they don’t own any houses.)

This sickens me. All of these player-run establishments claim to be run by role-players. Well, guess what. There’s a plot now. And it’s in your face. And people are going to die. And this is the best thing to happen to this game in, again… ever, and your collective response is to say “Hmph!”, pack up and leave.

Well, good riddance. I’m not planning on spending much time in Felucca anyway. I’ve got a career as a ruthless dictator now! “Today Occlo, Tomorrow Sosaria!” Minax wants YOU, babe. So if the thought of someone with an actual reason for being hostile and aggressive makes you piss your pants and run away, buh bye. I can understand player establishments wanting protection from random PK attacks, but there won’t be that many any more. Just ask the PKs, they’ll be glad to tell you exactly how far they are taking that crowbar up the butt.

So, the player run towns, again, with rare and noble exceptions, are demanding special treatment. This should surprise exactly three of you (and don’t worry, that [young] tag will wear off soon). They are insisting that, since they’ve, you know, given so much to the community and stuff, they should, you know, get to be grandfathered. As in having every single house they own be magically zapped to Happy Fun Land. I’m sure every GM in Austin right now is going “Uhh… we gotta do WHAT?”

Well, that’s just bullshit. I’m sorry to say this to such self-declared pillars of the player community, but you do not have an inherited hammerlock on property rights simply because you were the best at sucking up to a Green Robed Freak. There are HUNDREDS of player-run groups out there who are aching for the chance at running a player community – no doubt as a response to the political backbiting and corrupt double dealing that goes on in the ones already “blessed” (sounds of old-school Seers puking here) – and they’re to be told “Sorry, you can’t have a town in the new lands, either, because, you know, Happy Fun Land is already taken by the Bozarkian Mage Tower Incorporated. But you can still stay in Desolate Wasteland! We’re too good for it, but it should suit you nicely! Got to go bust it strictly for the players now! Bye bye!”

(If you can’t tell by the above, hypocrisy bothers me. A lot.)

Jinx has a lot to say about this as well. It’s recommended reading. He makes the not inconsequential point that a community is not defined by some sort of holy goodness, but merely by being a grouping of people. Everyone is in a community, whether that community be a tavern or a PK guild. Do PK guilds get to move their mules’ towers, too?

Now, I acknowledge that there are some folks who have been waiting for the One Holy Apostolic PK Switch since the dawning of time (or at least the dawning of UO) and really, really want to move. They don’t want to fight people, they don’t want to have to defend themselves, they just want to slam those fake ales. In Richard Bartle’s incredibly insightful paper that everyone including me keeps telling you to read over and over again, they are “hearts”, or socializers. Happy Fun Land is made for hearts. They should have a chance to go there.

But not as easy as they’d like it to be mind you. Here’s my suggestion.

Leaders of communities recognized by the people of the shard, as nominated by GMs, Seers, Elders, and news reporters of UO Vault, Crossroads of Britannia, and UO Stratics, will have the opportunity to detail which houses of their community may be transferred to the new lands. Each house so moved must be public, and open to all for the benefit of the community. There will only be a very limited number of communities that will be moved for each shard, and they must have a valid, sound reason for wishing to be transferred (such as being a place of socialization more suited for Felucca than Trammel.) and such a transfer must be viewed by all concerned as being in the best interest of the player community as a whole.

Those houses’ ownership are transferred to the lead Seer of that shard. The leaders of that community may be friended to each house so that they can make use of the banning and co-ownership functions of housing. They will no longer have ownership of that community, but stewardship. If the leadership of that community fails in its duties to the community, whether by active misuse or benign neglect, the houses will be taken from those leaders and placed in the hands of stewards who that Seer feels worthy of the responsibility of maintaining a community.

All communities in the future that seek IGM/Seer enhancement must also follow these guidelines. After UO: Renaissance is opened, no communities will be moved in the future, nor will GMs or Seers at any time allow houses to be placed illegally or at the expense of other players.

Again, it’s an imperfect solution, but save growing cojones and defending your home or trying to beat the land rush, it’s all we got, folks.

In the end, it’s all about control. Whether someone denies you the right to place your own house in the new lands because they have some unspoken prior right as the landed nobility of UO, or whether someone simply denies you the right to exist (or at least to see the game in color), there are an awful lot of self-appointed arbiters of how you should play UO.

And you know what? They’re all wrong. You go do what you want. Just stay the hell outta my yard. Which, as soon as the Faction System goes in, will be most of Occlo. And Nu’jelm is looking nice, too.

And if you are running a player community that has the vision and courage to stay in a changing, uncertain world, you have my respect. And if you’ve read this page for any length of time, you know that is a rare thing indeed.

I’m sure you all have something to say.

Stereotyping is when you try to fit all obviously recognizable members of a group into a mold with easily identifiable criteria - by sex, religion, color of skin, age etc. All men are testosterone laden cavemen - all women are weak, helpless, need protecting. Typing people is indeed necessary when dealing with mass numbers - but stereotypical classifications are an extremely poor database sort. Database sorting however IS crucial - the sort criteria are the problem.

In many instances, especially when talking about MMORPGs, which is what I intend to primarily focus on in this article - the criteria is motivations. Motivations can only be loosely associated with stereotypical models. Get down to archetyping by motivation - and everyone is served better. Archetyping crosses gender and age boundaries. Let's talk about penalties. Specifically, as our test case, stat loss for murderers in UO.

Ultima Online did not launch with stat loss, or even the concept of murderers. You were free to do whatever you wished, whenever you wished, to whomever you wished.

The result? Some would have you believe this was the golden age of Ultima Online, when Dread Lords and Great Lords battled the good fight in the streets and all was well with the world. Unfortunately, as Peter Smith wrote on cdmag.com in 1998:

The idea was a good one. Create a world, populate it with creatures, with an ecosystem, and with the skeleton of an economy. Then turn it over to the players to rule as they see fit. That was one of the basic tenets of the Ultima Online design team. A hands-off policy.

It failed, of course. To be sure UO is still in operation, but the vibrance is gone from the world and my guess is that, though it is less than a year old, it is already seeing the twilight of its existence. Oh, it will survive until one of its serious competitors (Microsoft's Asheron's Call or Sony's Everquest being the most likely) drives the final nail in its coffin. And even then, as Origin Systems Inc. pulls the plug a devout few will fume and curse at the loss of the world of which they've become so much a part.

It didn't have to be this way. OSI came so close to hitting the mark that it gave us a glimmer of what could...no, what will be, when someone gets it right. But they erred in their judgment of us, the gaming community. They gave us too much credit - assumed that we were honorable people who would play within the rules set forth, rather than strive so hard to twist them.

Luckily for Origin's bottom line, shortly after this article was written the Notoriety and Reputation patch was implemented. For those two of you unfamiliar with UO, this penalized player killers by implementing "stat loss"... player killers were defined as "murderers" using a reporting system, and if a player died with too many murders on his record, his character lost a significant portion of stats and skills. In a skill-based game like Ultima Online, this is one small step short of simply deleting the character in terms of the gravity of the penalty.

This redefined the style of grief players, and not necessarily for the better. While a small hardcore minority of players continued to play as "reds" subject to stat loss if they were unlucky enough to die, most "k3wld3wds" became that particularly vile subspecies of player known as the "Noto PK".
