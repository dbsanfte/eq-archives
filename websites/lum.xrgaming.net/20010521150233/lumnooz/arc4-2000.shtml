At least that's the thought process. Everyone's a network now. Everyone.

So should it surprise me that there's a UO2 HoC? Probably not. Maybe I'll see you there tonight. Let's watch yet another specific-purpose entity become a generalized diluted offering. Maybe next week I'll catch you at the Star Wars Online HoC and the Rune Conquest HoC the week after that.

THE WHIMPER OF WHIPPED DOGS
Monday, May 29, 2000 - 2:02 AM [Filed by lum]
You know, for being allegedly a coddled bunch of whiny babies, ORPG players are a remarkably forgiving bunch. Look at the current state of the two market leaders, Ultima Online and Everquest.

Ultima Online - well, I personally haven't played UO in a while, since shortly after UO:R's release, in fact. Whenever anyone asks, I tell them "I'm waiting for it to be debugged." It's a good joke, but one told while one's lip is being bit in frustration. Sure, PvPers are somewhat irked that warhammers now can do one-shot kills (I heard of one that did 91 points the other day), and summoned creatures such as demons are completely useless and can be killed by a headless or something, and the interplay between poison and pretty much everything else can get downright freaky, but those may well have been intended. It's hard to justify your client crashing when, say, you cast a heal spell on someone. Or when someone else casts a spell in your general vincinity. In fact, it's pretty damned hard to justify a client going fwoomf and dying, period.

Of course, in UO, your client rolling over and dying on an regular basis is new to UO:R. In Everquest, for me it's a somewhat daily occurence, and it's almost routine and no one really cares any more. So you die, and you lose a week's worth of experience, which Abashi helpfully notes is good for you even if you don't like it, because, you know, otherwise it wouldn't be fun. Fun is also defined, in Everquest's world, as losing all your equipment to zone pathing bugs, being attacked by invulnerable mobs that are running around "below the world", being unable to hit anything in combat past level 50 if you're a ranger, and dying to unstoppable, inescapable mobs and losing a week's worth of... you know... fun. It's nice in Everquest's world. They get the red pills.

I can't talk about Asheron's Call, because I don't play it. I know that the folks at Turbine are really cool guys who have really neat ideas and they have this really neat world with really neat PvP and stuff, but it's just blah to me. I guess I prefer being whacked by one-hit-kill warhammers while running from unstoppable, inescapable mobs and hoping no one casts a spell that will cause my client to crash, so that I would stop having fun. Call it a personal preference.

The fact is, the current state of the art sucks. It really, really hard-core sucks. You have
"UO Live" and "EQ Live" which each pull in millions and millions of dollars on a monthly basis, and those millions of dollars pay for a few guys who do coding 80 hours a week until their brains leak out of their ears, a few surly college dropouts who decide to be a GM until someone catches them having a god complex, and... well, not much else, really. Lots of volunteers, but, you know, volunteers don't actually get paid. Once you get past the internet connectivity (a known quantity, and in both EQ and UO underutilized) and the initial outlay for hardware (also a known quantity, and in EQ's case stretched to the breaking point), the money just goes into a bank account and a little bell on Smed or Tyrant's desk goes *ching*.

Because we put up with it. We've made friends in these little virtual outposts of hell, and the suffering we put up with is shared suffering, and it builds character. So we put up with snarling "Internet Relations Managers" telling us pain is fun, and we put up with the musical dev teams over at EA.COM (the company formerly known as Origin) and we say we're just marking time waiting for the next best thing. Which at this point could be, hell, anything. Massively Multiplayer Deer Hunter Online, I dunno. UO2 *cough* UWO:O has a cool music video. Shadowbane has a wild and crazy dev team that buys Roman armor and cheers on lesbian sex on Jumbotrons. Anarchy Online has a bunch of Europeans who are so goofily happy about their own game they actually showed it off to other developers (as opposed to the game of Whack-a-Raph the UWO:O and Shadowbane teams kept playing at E3 whenever he tried to see what was going on). Diablo 2 is being done by Blizzard, and as we all know they can do no wrong whatsoever. Jesus himself works at Blizzard. The stigmata gets in the way of typing sometimes, but being the Son of God, he makes do.

I dunno. I just wonder what the "Shadowbane Live" or "Diablo 2 Live" teams will be like. If they'll be a couple of guys gone mad from nonstop patchwork coding and a couple of dudes doing GM work as a summer project, and the rest of the money going to... the next best thing. Because, you know, we're just marking time waiting for the next best thing.

We whimper and we cower, because it's what we are trained to do

I could speculate on why they closed their doors (they ran out of money -- doh!) but what I'd rather do is talk about one of the finest single-player studios in gaming today closing their doors, while other studios with marketing power and big names continue to release dredge (*cough*daikatana*cough*forcecommander*cough*). I know this is an online multiplayer game site but I think this points to something that affects us all. Would that all the games we love be produced by LucasArts or Ion Storm but then again, perhaps it is the very fact that the games we love, that the games we find really very engrossing, are not produced by big names riding on a reputation that makes them what they are. Perhaps they are games we love because they are designed and created by people working at half the pay they could get elsewhere simply because they have a passion for playing and making games. No reputation, no big name... just some guy or gal in a cube at a console coding what s/he thinks will be a great game -- and it is because that person's and the whole development team's heart and soul has been poured into this game. Games made by people who love to play games and want to create great games.

So the next time you might be inclined to buy a game simply because Jon Romero has his name attached to it stop for a moment to think about the kind of industry you are funding.

The Bravery of Being Out of Range
Monday, May 22, 2000 - 1:42 PM [Filed by delusion]
Do we, as gamers, let the minority with the most at stake - the "vested interests" in political terms - determine the evolution of the games we play? Given the less than stellar performance of this model in the US political system, I certainly hope not.

Let's toss the analogy aside and dig in. The issue here is fair reward for fair risk in Asheron's Call. In order to accomplish this, you also need to institute a program of reducing the rewards for reduced risk.

I'll indent while making some notes about UO for those of you who want to skip back to the AC commentary in a few paragraphs.

In a game like UO that has more viable non-combat career choices, it gets a little more difficult - OSI certainly jerked around fishing during the "feast or famine" patches oh so long ago. I remember doing a statistical analysis on fishing based on the numbers OSI put out in its internal patch document (which was very similar to Turbine's Build Notes, but was SUPER SECRET and only supposed to go to interest and support volunteers and employees - it generally got to Dr. Twister within a day). This analysis clearly showed a MASSIVELY high amount of loot generation for a Master Fisherman. I sent it to the Dev Team, and gave it to other interested parties, but if anyone at OSI was paying attention, they were keeping it a better secret than the internal patch document ever was.

The response to the fishing patch aftermath was the infamous fishing nerf. 'Feast or famine' is a very appropriate metaphor when talking about fundamental changes in stock fish availability, perhaps less so when the goal was large pillows, paintings, and globes. Suddenly, a Master fisherman was all but worthless, ad it had been for most of UO's existence. A Grandmaster Fisherman could still get items, but to call it 'toned down' would be an understatement of criminal proportions. I again did the analysis according to OSI's own numbers. My experiences matched the statistics nearly perfectly. I tested for hours, and compared my results with others'. Be they novice, journeyman, or grandmaster, their numbers reflected my revised analysis numbers nearly perfectly. When I was audacious enough to tell a GM that my analysis did, in fact, hold true, I was assured that this wasn't the case, that I was doing something wrong, that I couldn't possibly disagree with official doctrine (which dictated that fishing was merely 'toned down a few percent') and be right, that quite frankly, I was causing trouble in #uo-council and being an ass by even suggesting it.

So, despite agreeing that the original numbers were too high and that some sort of moderation was needed, statistical analysis and testing the numbers in-game wasn't of interest. Apparently, neither was the fact that the overkill of the original fishing boost was obvious to anyone who tested it on Test Center... You know Test Center, that server they used to use for testing before it became a play shard for the elite.

As with many things, the changes were on Test Center for weeks, and any notes from players about the success of said patch were ignored before publishing to the regular servers, whereupon which the regular servers' players echoed the exact same sentiments the Test Center folks had. This was, of course, a big shock to OSI: If you don't fix something, it stays broke.


That's a model of how not to do things. Turbine's been good about testing code before it gets published. In fact, during the last patch, test code was accidentally published, but it was pretty well-tested at the time, so its inclusion was anything BUT a show-stopper. In fact, even though they removed it again after they realized what had happened, the could have left it in, quite frankly.

The code in question lowered XP gained by low-level characters slowly whittling away high-level creatures from the safety of a ledge or perch or wall. Cheap kills are still possible, but some are just not rewarded as well.

Of course, people complained.

As players, we need to ask ourselves whether or not we want a game by committee or whether we trust Turbine to make changes that are good for the long-term viability of the game. Trust doesn't come easy in this genre; it's difficult to earn, and easy to lose. Turbine's made mistakes, as do all online game companies. The key here is that they admit to them, and they resolve them quickly. Most importantly, they don't blame the players for the mistakes of programming. In other words, I trust Turbine, - they've earned it. If it comes to the vocal bitching minority and Turbine disagreeing about the future of Asheron's Call, Turbine's got a great record. The vocal minority are going to have to do better than bitch about free XP being unfairly stolen from them before I can take them seriously. If you want to be taken seriously, make constructive suggestions that amount to something other than "don't affect my favorite twink of the month!" Let the Dev Team do its job, it's good at it.

This month's "The Spin from Turbine" speaks volumes:

The intention was to prevent people from slaying things that are way too strong for them by wearing them down over time with no risk. Also, this did not stop those players from doing this, it just made it so that they didn't gain XP faster than those players who do play at a risk, with all the hardship and struggle that entails.


Honestly, if you're 10th level and you're camped out at WaiJhou or Fort Tethana or elsewhere in the Direlands, you forfeit any right to complain about what happens there. Your first clue should be that you're 10th level, and that drudge ravener that sent you careening back to the lifestone was over 57th level. What's more, at 10th level, when you inevitably start begging for a ride back to the eastlands (despite there being a convenient Sho Roadside Portal near WaiJhou, and a Hilltop portal at Tethana), you're going to annoy the hell out of everyone who is actually sufficiently prepared to be there.

We rationalized that by reducing the XP granted to people using exploits in the game, we were, in a sense, rewarding those who play heroically. Should the guy cowering behind a door and attacking a monster that is levels beyond his abilities get the same reward as the valiant hero who bravely charges in? Legends tell us of great warriors and magicians who stood toe-to-toe with monsters to do battle . . . Little is written about cowards who slew the dragons without any personal risk.


The risk-free game is an example of the bravery of being out of range. It's not the game that Turbine designed, so don't be surprised when they fix it. Again, let the Dev Team do its job, it's good at it.

Of course, some people don't want risk. Just ask anyone with a 30th level or higher character that's died less than 60 times.

Elsewhere, in the "Build Notes" from earlier this month...

Everquest players, pay close attention:

We had a problem with certain generous chests that gave players an incentive to wait in long lines to get their booty, usually at little or no risk. All you had to do was wait your turn. This is not very heroic, and unfair to those players who want to get a nice chest of loot but have better things to do than wait in a line for hours. It also created lots of complaints about players who did not follow the “etiquette” of how to wait their turn. Therefore, we made some changes. The good news is that these chests will regenerate their treasure much faster than before, so if you are waiting your turn, you will have to wait much less time. The bad news is that the chests are locked, so just waiting there won't do any good. The good news is that the keys to these chests are now part of monster loot throughout the world. So go out and fight powerful monsters anywhere you like, and if you are lucky, you may end up with a key that will unlock any one of these wonderful chests. You can then make it your quest to fight your way to such a chest and, without having to wait in a line, open it up and enjoy your booty. Though players who did not mind the wait are sure to be disappointed, we know that far more players will be pleased by this change, as it rewards true adventurers with mini-quests that yield great rewards.


The subtext in that paragraph is critical in understanding the key difference in how Trubine treats Asheron's Call versus the way Verant treats Everquest, and a wonderful illustration of an earlier point I made about programmers taking responsibility for mistakes, be they code bugs or unforseen design problems.

"Line etiquette" is a symptom of a broken game design. Turbine recognizes this, and repeatedly chooses to fix the problem instead of trying to fix the player by enforcing absurd behaviors like queueing up for loot. In Everquest, you get increasingly intricate looting rules enforced by the tattle-tale system instead of the game engine. The new Everquest player is thrust into a situation where there are complex (and artificial) rules of behavior that have very little basis in common sense. Not knowing them is dangerous, as many of them are bannable offenses. Turbine's come up with a solution that's apparently novel in this genre: fix the game instead.

Let's get that experimental code in-game again, and to the nay-sayers, let the Dev Team do its job, it's good at it.

TEN COMMANDMENTS FOR THE NEXT MMORPG

PURPOSE: I know you're out there. Who? People planning to make the "next" big MMORPG. Kindly incline your ear while I make some kindly ... who am I kidding. Do all 10 or don't expect me to buy it

#1: Thou Shalt Not Require Vulnerability to Other Players.

Consider the number of active EQ Subscribers (henceforth “customers”) complaining that their server is too crowded. Now take a good look at how many people are on the Player-Versus-Player (henceforth “PvP”) servers. Either they are being hypocrites, or as a whole the customers do not want to be vulnerable to harm from other players at all times. And yet I find it curious that practically every upcoming MMORPG I’ve looked into has Mandatory PVP – one (Atriarch) even goes so far as to have permanent PvP (if the character dies, it’s time to start a new character). Others (UO2, Anarky Online) skirt this by making some areas Non-PvP – and yet it takes something less than a rocket scientist to realize you won’t be able to stay in the “safe” areas all the time.

#2: Honor Thy Customers and Consider Their Suggestions, for Thy Game is **Not** Holy.

Commandment 2 is also known as Business Rule One – if you don’t take care of the customer, somebody else will. Both of the “headlining” MMORPG’s (EQ & UO) have suffered from massive customer-relations problems. In both cases, those behind the game *refused* to re-examine fundamental game issues “because [problem] is part of the game – learn to enjoy it or go play Chip’s Challenge,” to quote a statement made to me by a representative of Origin. And Verant has made similar if not *quite* as inflammatory statements regarding a number of issues (melee binding being the most egregious but certainly not solitary). Origins faced a gradual but considerable exodus due to this; I for one will never purchase an Origins product again, and this is coming from someone who has never eaten at KFC because of poor service his mother received 28 years ago. Verant finds numerous customers prepared to do the same (I would still buy from them at this time). Both companies got away with it for quite some time because there wasn’t a worthy “someone else” – this won’t be true for long.>Another way to say this: don’t tell us "Everything is subject to change" if Everything doesn’t mean ***Everything**.

#3: Thou Shalt Use All Means at Thy Disposal to Communicate With Thy Customers

I find this issue particularly curious. In a genre in which change is the norm, Verant hardly ever polls their customers for information – yet constantly say they know what how the majority of their players feel. Posts by various Verant personnel on their Official Board seem to indicate they have a low opinion of polling. Granted, the majority is not always right, but here’s a hint: the majority isn’t always wrong, either. As a nigh-complete idiot I once had for a boss told me, “If one person says it’s raining, that only means they say it’s raining. But if half the people coming in the door say it’s raining, you might want to at least look out the window before you go out without an umbrella.”

#4: Thou Shalt Not Lie To Thy Customers – And None of that Misrepresenting the Truth Stuff, Either

There’s no way around this. They can make all the PC “we were mistaken”, “we had no creative control”, “this was pre-release information”, and “we are not responsible for second-party statements” dodges they want – both Origins and Verant committed outright lies to their customers on various issues. And both companies had misleading statements in Official sources as well. Sure, “soloing is supported” – for maybe two classes, over maybe 2/3rds their careers. It is my personal opinion that Verant’s problem is not deliberate misinformation, but an utter lack of quality control. On that subject…

#5: Thou Shalt Test Thy Product Before Thy Releaseth It or Any Part of It

Should be assumed, and yet everybody but game manufacturers seems to know better. Here’s a clue – if it’s “your world”, you bloody better well be willing to sign your name to it. Now granted that the very nature of the MMORPG genre is such that updates will be constant, and that any changes that need to be made can be handled quickly. Nonetheless, there is NO EXCUSE WHATSOEVER for a game to be out for a year before the people responsible for it discover something along the lines of Wolf-form having the wrong faction. There is an equivalent amount of excuse for quests that appear to have been in the game from the beginning to still appear broken (which reminds me, I haven’t checked Kinool Goldslinger since the last “quest” patch). A recommendation? Absolutely NOTHING in the game should go Live until the person most responsible PERSONALLY confirms it is working on a Test Server, and this confirmation should be made the same way the player would be doing it.

#6: Thou Shalt Remember That This is a Game, and is Supposed to be Fun

Or, to quote the Ogre, “Fun good!” I myself am baffled by Verant’s view of doing anything except fighting in EverQuest. They create a high-level Magician spell that summons beer “for fun”, but disallow the Shrink spell outside of dungeons “because it would serve no useful purpose” and all but mock enchanters for wanting spells that serve no purpose but to “look cool.” An interesting thread about the Fishing skill gets ignored for three weeks while a post describing the prospect of X-Rated visuals in the game received an almost instant answer. Here’s a clue – players play games to have Fun. It is the role of those running the game to give them as many different ways to have Fun as possible. Is that really so hard to figure out? Here’s another clue: anything that represents a lengthy “do nothing” time (the boats in EQ being a hideous example) is bad for Fun and therefore bad for the Player – and what is bad for the Player is bad for the Game. By the same token, what is Fun for the Player is good for the Game.

#7: What Thou Expecteth Players to Have, Players Should Expect to Obtain

Go ahead and call this the Casual Commandment if want. Or, to quote the Ogre, “Camping Bad!” If “Crafted isn’t 50th level gear”, 50th level players should be able to obtain 50th level gear **REGARDLESS OF WHETHER THEY CAN PLAY 1 HOUR A WEEK OR 50**. In a Non PvP environment, there is NO reason for a player not to have the gear they are “expected” to have at a certain level. This is not to say that the player who can play 50 hours a week shouldn’t have better gear – quite the contrary, the 50-hour player **deserves** better equipment. The problem is that the 1-hour a week character shouldn’t still be carrying a basic Long Sword at 25th level (don’t laugh, I’ve seen it). There are several methods around this (viable trade skills come to mind, as does the “piecework” quest suggestion), and I strongly advise game makers to look into them.

#8: Problems, Not Puzzles
This is an old roleplaying GM rule I’ve followed for years when I run campaigns. The fact I heard a lead designer for the upcoming Deux Ex game say this in an interview is one of the reasons I *will* be buying that game. What it means is this: do not create situations that can only be solved one way. Create *problems*, and then create multiple ways to solve this problem. Reward players for using less-obvious methods. If the only way to get a treasure out of a room is to kill the monsters guarding it, what you have is a war game, not role-playing.

#9: If Thou Must Have Classes, Balance Them in *Quantifiable* Ways

Or, to quote the Ogre, “Me kan’t eben spel cum-bel-ink, let alone play it.” Another nice little sound bite: the Compelling Reason to play every class should be known, and should outweigh the Compelling Reason *not* to play the class.

#10: If Thou Must Have [Whatever], Have [Whatever].

Or, to quote that near-complete idiot I once had as a boss, “Swimming is not putting your feet in the water.” Is there Tinkering in EQ? Technically. Can enchanters make magic items in the game? Technically. Can rogues use poison? Technically. Are there religions in EQ? Technically. Are there quests in EQ? Technically. See where I’m going with this? First person to answer “Technically” is gonna be the next victim of my Kerran Fishingpole.

Interesting. I think this serves as a good guideline for developers. Of course, I can't agree with the first commandment, but overall a well done document trying to answer the question of "what the hell do players want, anyways?".

Why stop at 10? Add your thoughts and let make this the 175 commandments instead!

WELL, WE ALREADY FIXED SAMURAI KILLSTEALING AND RONIN CAMPING...
Thursday, May 18, 2000 - 4:57 AM [Filed by lum]
...so next up is ninja looting. Sensei at the Dojo, who obviously has better contacts at Verant than we do, is reporting that ninja looting is the next "behavior modification" to be patched in. To quote:

If confirmed by a Verant GM, ninja-looting will result in disciplinary action up to and including player suspension or banning. Ninja-Looting is when a player, disregarding the players or player who killed a mob, loots an item that is generally regarded as significant or valuable from the mob they did not kill. When a CS Rep receives a petition reporting ninja-looting, they are to contact the petitioner, gather preliminary information, and escalate to a GM for resolution. Determination of whether or not an item is 'generally regarded as significant or valuable' is left to the GM's discretion as directed by Verant Management.

OK, so what is "ninja looting" (or "yoinking", as I prefer to call it) in English? Here's the deal.

Most of the REALLY COOL LEWT in Everquest is what is called NO DROP. That means that you can't trade it, you can't give it to your level 4 friend, you can only use it if you loot it. No worries there, right? Well, the kicker is that as you increase in levels, your relative power in Everquest vs. monsters decreases consistently. At level 1, you can pretty much kick everything's butt that's your level or maybe even one or two higher. At level 25, you are going to have to group to get anything done. And at level 50 (taking everyone's word for it mind you, since level 50 characters to me are like minor godlings that move in more rarified planes of existence that I can only wonder at) the more important/interesting creatures require multiple groups of characters to kill. That's the kicker - because you can only loot something off of a monster that your group kills. There's no support for multiple-group kills in EQ.

So the upshot. You're a Shadowknight, and your guys just killed Erollsi Marr, who drops the Hairpin of Ungodly Might. It's a shadowknight only item. It's no drop. The only hope of your getting the Hairpin of Ungodly Might is to kill Erollsi Marr. Which you just did. Only, you were in the melee group, whose job it was to get hammered on while the group behind you, the finger-waggling caster brigade, actually got credit for the kill. No one in the caster group is a shadowknight. They don't want the hairpin. They can't use it. If they loot it, the only thing they can do with it is to throw it away.

So what do you do? Well, the only thing that groups do in this situation - sit and wait for the timer on the corpse to go off. You see, the limits on who can loot a corpse expire after 5 minutes or so. Then anyone can loot Erollsi Marr's steaming husk. So you sit around and wait 5 minutes so that you can get your rightful reward for helping to slay the Goddess of Love. So everyone's kicking back, cracking obscene jokes in /guildchat, waiting for the timer to expire, when some random stranger comes walking by and calmly loots the corpse. That's ninja looting. Needless to say, it pisses people off. A lot.

Now, there's a lot of things that can be done about ninja looting. You could code in support for multiple group credits for kills. You could set a timer for looting no drop items that allowed you to trade them, say, the first 3 minutes after you looted them. You could even allow the looting group to re-designate who has looting rights for the corpse. All these remedies would make sense, and would actually, you know, preserve the original intent of the loot timer - limiting who can loot a kill to who actually had something to do with it.

But, you know, coding remedies is HARD. Much easier to just write up an official sounding rule actually codifying what players are doing to work around a failure in the game system, saying "You can't ninja-loot" instead of fixing the game so that ninja looting isn't an option and make the guides enforce it. You know, since the guides in Everquest don't have enough to do already with flitting around the servers ensuring that everyone is playing nice under penalty of linkdeath.

You have dishonored my family! Prepare to have your kills ninja looted!

THE WARS OF THE ROSES: GRIEF PLAYERS THROUGH THE AGES
Tuesday, May 16, 2000 - 7:34 AM [Filed by myschyf]
The Wars of the Roses took place between 1452 and 1487. Simplistically put, it was a power play between two branches of the British royal family, the Lancasters and the Yorks. So far this has absolutely nothing to do with online gaming but bear with me -- I'm getting there and besides this is a nifty intro. (On the other hand, one could make a case that this was nothing more than a bunch of people with way too much free time on their hands [i.e. powergamers] fighting for ultimate power and control of a land [i.e. they wanted to "win"] and a whole bunch of innocent people got hurt in the process. The similarities to online gaming are frightening.)

The War of the Roses is also a mediocre movie starring Kathleen Turner and Michael Douglas. This, too, has nothing to do with online gaming. (On the other hand, the movie is about a married couple who fight and fight and fight without either side being willing to compromise [i.e. pkers and people who don't enjoy pvp combat in any form whatsoever] and they eventually kill each other and no one wins [pk switches and zones]. The similarities are a little less frightening but they are there nonetheless.

However, a long time ago (1990 to be exact), in a MUD called Islandia (we are now getting to the part that has to do with online gaming. I thought I'd tell you just in case you didn't pick up on it immediately.) there were two groups: the White Roses and the Black Roses. In an incident fairly famous in MUD and online gaming deveopment circles, (known, surprisingly enough, as the Black Rose incident) a GM tries to reason with the Black Roses, who, for all intents and purposes, are grief pkers.

The Black Roses didn't start as grief players. One player who was there at the time says they actually started as some of the better roleplayers in the land. As he relates it "the Black Roses started as a group of outspoken cynics who were tired of putting up with crap from younger, less experienced players, and formed their own little bitter clique. Before long they turned into an RP group, playing evil on Islandia. Originally the small core group were intelligent and skillful roleplayers, but they expanded a bit too much, and after awhile there were some rather unsavory people running around with the name. It started to earn a very bad reputation as a bunch of player killers, even though there were just a few that were performing 'random' kills."

The White Roses, from what I can piece together (and there is far less known about the White Roses than the Black Roses), was a group that was "at war with the Black Roses, with some people claiming to be a White Rose just because they hated the Black Roses, some role-playing a vicious campaign to save the younger players running around, and some even going so far as to actually hunt Black Roses actively." It is also thought that "a few members of the administration of Islandia were White Roses themselves, one from the very beginning, and a few others later on in the conflict."

Now there's a few things at work here. First, if you read the log of the Black Rose incident you'll notice that these grief players said the same exact things 10 years ago as they say today. So did the people who don't want to be bothered. The conversation is exactly the same. Two diverse groups of people at direct odds with each other, each demanding the right to play the game as they choose. Sound familiar?

It gets better. The other part of this conversation that is very interesting is the administrative part. The question being: How do we handle this?

Islandia's final solution? They went to being a complete social MUD -- no combat of any type allowed. The two players types were deemed incompatible and since everyone had pretty much stopped using combat in Islandia anyway they ported the database and went to a strictly social MUD.

Everyone playing UO:R in Trammel right now should find this familiar. In a way, it was UO's solution as well. Yes I'm aware there is still Felucca with the accompanying consequences for grief players there. Nonetheless what UO has effectively done is to separate the two sides. Just as there are still grief players in Trammel, however, bringing the grief to players through language, emoting, camping spawn points and being obnoxious about it, and basically doing everything in their power to be assholes, the Black Roses remained in Islandia after it went to being a social MUD. An Islandia player relates "they stuck around once Islandia went non-combat, and many migrated into various "Social MUDs" where they came as close to PvP combat as they could, by insulting and flaming anyone around that they didn't like. A very bitter, but occasionally witty, group."

UO is the only commercial game in recent memory (besides maybe M59?) and the only MMORPG that tried, with limited success, to merge the two play styles. Indeed it was a stated dream of the original designers. In the end they were defeated and they have, with the introduction of the PVP- new lands, introduced PVP/PK shards -- only they are zones instead of shards. EQ and AC didn't even try. They gave the PVPers/PKers their own shard, slapped a switch on the rest of the shards and washed their hands of the whole administrative nightmare. And it is a nightmare. This is something the UO Live team has struggled with for 2.5 years now.

But this doesn't actually deal with the grief player -- there are other ways to give grief than simply killing a person. Separating the two sides is not a successful solution. In UO, some of the more successful grief players have been scamming people out of houses for literally years now -- due to the lack of secure house trading in the game. No combat necessary. Even after Islandia had gone social, completely eliminating combat from the game, the Black Roses remained to humiliate anyone they felt was deserving of their special brand of attention, now using insults instead of combat to harass the hapless player. In EQ, a game with the noted 'switch,' the grief players just steal your kills and train mobs on you. Your only recourse in that game (should you not play on the PVP shards) is to call a GM and whine and if you are lucky, the GM will remove the bad players. So just separating out the PKers from those that don't wish to be killed in any way doesn't alleviate this problem. Indeed, it perpetuates it. No player retribution, in any form whatsoever, is possible.

"But Mys," you say, "this is old news. We know all this already." So you do. Why do I bring this up now? Well that interesting thread on the Shadowbane demo at E3 sort of sparked my interest in this area. This thread has a central theme running through it that I think has been largely ignored. People commenting on the preview that Lum wrote from E3 have sort of settled into two camps -- those that think the Wolfpack boys might have a trick or two up their sleeve and those that think the Wolfpack boys are in for a severe shock when the grief players take over and ruin their game. Nonetheless Shadowbane also has the distinction of being the only game in development that is trying to institute an in-game, player-run system to handle grief players which is not an artificial artifact (pk shards, gm interference). Once again a group of developers are saying that an online gaming community can police itself, if it wishes to, given the correct tools to do so. They also say their game will make it unprofitable for the grief player to... well, cause grief.

I'm not so sure that profitability or even being unable to build your character well figures into this equation though. There seems to be a larger motivation here for the grief player that transcends any reward found in the actual game. After all, the Black Roses were content just to sit around and hurl insults and humiliation. So lets look at another game that has instituted artificial tools which allow the player to effectively ignore anyone who decides to be a pest.

According to Delusion (yes the LumCorp Delusion -- he plays AC y'know?) "In Asheron's Call, on the regular servers (i.e. not Darktide), you can't kill anyone who hasn't flipped the big red switch, you can't bait monsters to people very easily, squelching (ignoring) people is trivially easy, and secure trading is coming." These artifical restraints have reduced the grief player population to minimal numbers. "It's the third tier grief players on AC. The pros stick to UO." So obviously the folks at Turbine have found ways to curb this behavior. Everyone I talked to about AC pretty much had the same opinion. Some of those that hadn't enjoyed the joy and rapture that were the early days of UO didn't even know what a grief player was. (I should note here that I did not talk to Darktide players and that the mileage may vary greatly on Darktide).

This is not to say that no grief players exist in AC. Del mentioned something called a shut-out where a relatively high-level player goes and kills everything in a lower level zone, leaving nothing for the players of that lower level and effectively 'shutting them out.' Additionally since there is no secure trading in AC theft and scamming happens pretty commonly to those players who are newer to both AC and to online gaming.

However, I think this brings up an interesting discussion. Certainly removing all possibility of delivering any sort of player-delivered retribution without some sort of artificial restraints does not work and never has. The question is, what sort of restraints? Separating the combatants from the noncoms is obviously not the answer. EQ either doesn't have the restraints or what they have doesn't work. UO just doesn't have them, at all. So would you rather work with a system of the sort that Shadowbane is planning -- where if someone does decide to be a jerk AND you have the muscle to do something about it you can? Or would you rather just be able to flip a switch, squelch this person and go on your merry way? I know a lot of times in UO when I've been attacked or stolen from or someone decides to start spamming I just recall away and forget about it. No big deal. On the other hand there's something inherently satsifying about getting a few friends together and 'handling that pesky little problem.' What do you think? The little tree, went exploring and immediately found the entrance to the netherworld of Kesmai. Down the dark and worn steps he went and stood looking at that damp underworld for a minute or so. As he was pondering his next move, up stepped two skeletons who began to beat on him roundly! "Fight!" "Kill" "Swing sword!!" commanded the little tree, but nothing seemed to happen and the the little tree was soon felled.

IOK always was special because the graphics were mere ASCII symbols and your mind supplied the details. At first Legends was a little disappointing because no graphic could compete with those images of dragons, trolls and other beasts that your mind had created. Also over the years things have changed as they remained the same. The hunt has ever been the same, but the prey changed as the game grew and developed. There was Thisson, lord of lava. Thisson went down to an intrepid group, not too long after he went up. There were horrors and terrors all over. The cloud dragon quest, and the Mama Katana quest. The OverLord Staff quest and the Three Weapon Quest. And for those who ever played IOK the Sword Of Light quests.

But of all the things that I have experienced in IoK and LOK, the people who became my friends and the people I met are the most important. Some of them I know beyond the realms of Kesmai, and some only through this medium of the computer. But all are cherished and will not be forgotten.I also know that this ending is not really an end, but a beginning. From chaos comes order. From ashes new life... The friends I have made and the people I have met because I played IOK and LOK will always be a part of me. And if you think that you will never see your friends again, think otherwise. They will be there in other games and other places. And you never know, some of them just seem to crop up when you least expect them!

But, players took it to the logical conclusion: if skills improve with use, then use it all the time, including times that don't make sense in game terms. Using simple tools (IE macros), players were able to gain large amounts of skills over short periods of time. Sounds like somebody has played a little Ultima Online, no? Granted, the hindsight vision of the problem is crystal clear, and at the time that Ultima Online was in development, there were no EverQuests or Asheron's Calls to look at and contrast against your own designs. It was Civil War surgery at it's best, and while a great many great ideas were not performed with expert precision, a great many lessons were learned and pathways forged for those showed up late to the dance.

But now we have the luxury of looking at several systems both in deployment and in development, and can compare them and contrast them. A "per use" skill advancement system has it's flaws, yes. But when you are discussion the potential for "fiction shattering" design, nothing breaks it apart faster than a system of kill kill kill kill *level* kill kill kill *level* kill kill...and so on. You cannot "fiction out" a player raising their carpentry, mining, tailoring, and fencing skills because they killed a certain number of Mahamoti Djinns (there's a little peek into my past, no?). "Per use" isn't perfect, but I cannot see it being replaced by the current "levels are good" systems being used. I'd like to see some kind of marriage of the two, a system that rewards both skill use as well as experience points, in order to handle character career advancement. Why? Well because the "per use" system just makes sense, and the leveling system is at least a minimal measure of goal-achievement for players.

GET YOUR MONEY FOR NOTHING AND YOUR TRAMMEL FOR FREE
Thursday, May 4, 2000 - 10:42 AM [Filed by lum]
It's been about a week since UO:R was deployed to all the shards. Overall, I like it, as I mentioned before. However, some problems are rapidly becoming clear as the players get their mitts on our Brave New World.

Trammel, the PK- side, is becoming popular among monster hunters. Actually "hunters" isn't a very good word. Let's call them "monster harvesters". A good group can make 100K of gold a day - a day - hanging at the Ophidian keep doing nothing but hiding and casting Blade Spirits (the new hunting tool of choice - cast one, watch it go!).

Fishermen, free of the threat of being ganked by "pirates", are crowding the oceans, merrily fishing up treasure maps and magic weapons for sale on their Felucca vendors.

And of course, you can always just go kill some NPC humans, which apparently weren't covered by Lord British's new "Play Nice" rules and can't even give murder counts now.

The upshot of this is that money is flooding into the already near-dead economy. Reagants are running short in Felucca (but oddly enough, not in Trammel, even with the Occlo mage shop closed to all but newbies) and castle deeds are being snapped up like popcorn. If you thought money was worthless now, just wait.

OK, Lum, this is bad, you're saying. How do we fix this?

Well, if UO is a persistant world, it sounds like it has a problem common in our world - an economy in free fall. You have too much money in the global supply. In the real world, when this happens, you get inflation. Why can't this happen in UO? A couple reasons, all of which probably need to be fixed.

First off, and most importantly, the economies of Trammel and Felucca are linked. Since money flows out of Trammel, it also flows into Felucca. Now, we can either continue this linkage - and apply whatever draconian economic penalties we levy on Felucca to Trammel as well - or we can sever the ability to move goods and money between Felucca and Trammel. Probably the only way to do this is to force everyone to go through the Trammel/Felucca moongate naked, and to have seperate bank boxes for Trammel and Felucca. That would neatly cut the economies of the facets in two. However, for obvious reasons, it probably won't happen. "What do you mean I can't move my new house deed to Trammel!!!" So we probably need to be resigned to the fact that Trammel and Felucca share the same economy.

One popular remedy proposed for this problem is to simply lower monster loot in Trammel. This also rewards players for taking extra risks by hunting in Felucca. However, Everquest has proven that players will gladly take the no-risk path with minor reward over the high-risk path with great reward. For example, there was a quest in game that involved getting a miniscule amount of experience for giving a certain dwarf a Tumpy Tonic. Sure enough, there grew an ingame economy solely around Tumpy Tonics, which were sold by the hundreds. It was in fact actually easier to just go out and bash orcs over the head for experience, but players preferred the no-risk method of forcefeeding tonics to dwarves. Given that, we can assume that even if loot drops in Trammel dropped dramatically, people would simply harvest more monsters to make up the shortfall.

Another possible remedy is to make monsters in Trammel more of a challenge. This is good in theory. Have the Ophidians start slinging Dispels, for example, to get around the I Love Blade Spirits "strategy". However, it is a good rule that no AI will ever conquer a human in battle (well, unless it's Deep Blue vs. Kasparov). While making monsters in Trammel harder is a good thing, there will always be "cheese" tactics and they will always be used. Count on it.

Well, people say, gold sinks are always a good idea. If we have lots of money, give us something to spend it on. Again, gold sinks are good, and with the Coming-Really-Soon-Now-We-PROMISE-PROMISE-PROMISE Faction System, presumably that will provide an avenue for gold to leave the economy. Of course, that assumes people join the faction system. And spend money on it. And don't then run out and make another 100K of gold a day. OK, so gold sinks probably aren't the answer here.

Now. We have monetary inflation in this economy, but goods and services are remaining static. The combination makes money worthless. How to fix this? Simple - tie vendor prices to the total money supply. Note that this implies that UO's servers actually track the total amount of money circulating in game. If this is not in fact the case then probably that could be simulated, say by keeping track of how much money NPC vendors take in. Once we have those figures, by either means, as the money supply increases - so do prices. Your stash of 10 million gold suddenly seems a bit less trivial when nightshade costs 1000 gold each. Or 10,000.

One negative aspect to this is its impact on newbies - if a sword costs 5,000 gold, it gets somewhat difficult getting started in the world. One way around this is to simply allow all new characters to start in Haven, not just [young] ones, and keep the prices in Haven fixed (its own little microeconomy). There is easily enough in Haven for a new character to build a nest egg of equipment. After two weeks, they are booted out on their way similar to [young] players currently.

I mean, if UO is going to fling around words like "Persistent State World" maybe it's time they acted the part, hmmm?

Kal Vas Green Span

THEORY INTO PRACTICE: SEERS IN UO
Monday, May 1, 2000 - 9:39 AM [Filed by lum]
The recent story we posted of a Seer on Great Lakes whose imminent transfer provoked a player outcry provoked some thought here at LumCorp.

"Seers" are meant to be the implementors of Ultima Online's ongoing fiction - the Game Masters in a roleplaying sense. Most UO "GMs" are actually more properly described as tech support representatives and make no effort at all to remain "in character". Seers, on the other hand, appear in game as characters in game and are mandated never to "break character".

Now, whether or not you actually ever see one of these characters depends on many things. Among them your political affiliation. You see, there are two major political parties in UO. We'll call them Democrats and Republicans.

The Republicans play UO to crush. They see UO as a Player vs. Player game. An eminently reasonable thought, UO being labelled as a "multi-player game" when you buy it at the store and all. They belong to a well-established PvP guild, which spends their time fighting other well-established PvP guilds, and usually ignore any of UO's fictional backdrop? Why? Well, that's for Democrats. They're Republicans. They have their own game and they're quite happy with it, thank you.

The Democrats, on the other hand, see UO as a roleplaying game. An eminently reasonable thought, UO being labelled as a "roleplaying game" when you buy it at the store and all. They seek like-minded folks to associate with - just like the Republicans. They usually have incredibly convoluted backstories involving elves. Most of them, interestingly enough, are women, and many male Democrats even tend to play female characters. Whereas many female Republicans play male characters. (The gender roles these imply are left as an exercise for the reader.)

Now, there are extreme Republicans. We'll call them the Christian Right. They want to impose their views and order upon everyone, Republicans and Democrats. The method in which this is done usually involves equal proportions of black pearl and nightshade. While some Republicans are disturbed by the Christian Right's zealous imposition of family values, the Democrats are downright furious. They want action. They want action NOW.

Thus we have the extreme Democrats. We'll call them the Politically Correct. They also want to impose their views and order upon everyone, Republicans and Democrats. The method in which this is done usually involves... well, lots of theatre. Democrats are good at theatre. Needless to say, in a straight up fight between the Politically Correct and the Christian Right, the PCs usually end up viewing the world in literal shades of grey they are unaccustomed to in a philisophical sense. So the Politically Correct's response? Take power and change the rules.

Thus we have Seers.

Most seers are self-selected - they are, in practice, chosen by other seers through the application process. Like attracts like. Thus, we have seers that are integral parts of the role-playing community. At first blush, this would seem perfectly natural and normal - until you realize that, in fact, only one political party - and in practice, one extreme wing of one political party - in UO is being represented. The Republicans aren't getting the whole game, you see. This may not bother the vast majority of them, most of whom are now trained via learned response and political infighting to ignore that "RPer crap", but it bothers those of us - call us Libertarians, for lack of a better word - who believe that UO is both multiplayer and a roleplaying game.

Now, if you ask seers or other pillars of the UO roleplayer community why members outside the roleplaying community aren't invited to the party, you may get a story much like the following, which was anonymously submitted:

I've only witnessed a green robed seer 4 times in the past 3 years. The last time was way back when those 4 criminals were being sought for a trial (the little pyramid things about them being arrested still stand in Britain). Anyway the seer appears in downtown Britain on a friday evening, and amid a throng of 15 players, attempts to rally a defense group to help transport a prisoner to Trinsic. There were 3 grief thieves who insisted on doing nothing but fill everyone's screen with snoop reports, there were 3 or 4 players spamming "OSI SUCKS. LAG SUCKS!" and the like.

The situation broke down so badly that the seer actually left character and said, "This is why your shard is the cesspool of UO." I don't know if that seer still exists anymore. It's been a long time, and the churn rate for the volunteer program is faster than the gestation period of a fruit fly. I sure do understand the pressure he/she was probably under and I don't fault the outburst. Naturally, the event never got off the ground that night.

This is a typical "event" which is used by the Politically Correct to justify pitching events to the converted. After all, any time they try to run an event for the unwashed masses, they just go and ruin it. So let's break down exactly why this event failed.

First off, the seer appeared as a green-robed seer and not an RPC (role-played character). Strike one. Seers look like smurfs look like GMs. They are a part and parcel of the Volunteer power structure and just appearing in a robe inspires a visceral reaction from many players.

Second, the seer popped into Britain on a Friday night. Let's just assume for purposes of discussion this was Brit bank. You say the words "Brit bank" to almost any veteran player in UO and you can inspire violent vomiting noises. Brit bank attracts the dregs of UO. Trying to run an event there, failing, and blaming the non-Politically Correct masses for not playing along is like holding a Jimmy Swaggart revival on Castro Street in San Francisco. You can do it, but don't expect to make many converts.

And finally, there was nothing done about the obvious grief players out to ruin the event. The seer in question would have several options available to him or her, ranging from summoning a GM to remove the players to simply going somewhere else. Instead, the seer just gave up, went out of character, cursed the assorted players, and left. Presumably to a player-owned tavern.

So what is the solution, then?

Well, one avenue to consider is the method of storytelling implied. UO is by its very nature non-linear. A linear story line sticks out like a sore, elitist, exclusive thumb. While storylines in the main can be helpful (especially meta-story lines such as the Minax-Britain war) the ground troops - the seers - in general shouldn't be concerned with them. Linear story lines are by their very nature exclusionary. If you don't know the entirety of events from start to finish, you have no clue what role to play - except maybe that of a grief player.

Now, take a small event that was described earlier - an intelligent dragon. Said dragon was by the road, and it was hungry. A player rode by on the horse, and the dragon ate the horse, in character. *sniff sniff* Mmmmm, food.

Earth shaking? No. Involving hundreds of players? Hardly. Making the world a slightly more interactive and vibrant place? Of course. Multiply a thousandfold, and people start roleplaying when you don't expect them to.

And no one asks what party you belong to.
