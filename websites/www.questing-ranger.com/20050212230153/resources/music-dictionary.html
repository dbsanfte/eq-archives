Dictionary of Music Terminology

Aubade (OE-bahd) Early morning concert music performed in open area for a specific individual.

Ballad (BAL-led) A simple narrative song which is often sentimental. Ballads usually have several verses of similar construction that alternate with a refrain. They were original meant to be danced to.

Capriccio (kah-PREE-choe) A quick and light composition that is similar to a fantasia.

Carol (KAIR-ruhl) An English Medieval strophic song that was dramatic, lyrical or narrative in form. Verses were coupled with a refrain [called 'burden'] Currently, any Christmas song is erroneously referred to as being a carol.

Elegy (EL-lih-jee) A funeral song, with a mournful composition.

Etude (AY-tood) A music piece written specifically for the purpose of practicing or showing technique.

Fantastica (fan-tas-TIK-ah) A whimsical composition which follows no particular pattern or form.

Finale (fih-NAH-le) The final movement in a composition that consists of several movements.

Gavotte (gah-VOHT-(te)) A dance from the 16-18th century that was considered a pastoral dance, written in 2 or in cut time and sometimes with a drone suggesting bagpipes.

Hymnus drived from Hymn (him) A song of praise and adoration to a deity.

Lullaby (LUL-luh-bye) A gentle soothing or quiet cradle song used to soothe a child to sleep.

Madrigal (MA-dri-gahl) A vocal song originating in Italy that flourished duringthe Renaissance, which was written for four to six voices. Madrigals are usually about love, but can be about other things. Modern versions of the madrigal are usually presented a cappella.

Mambo (mom-BO) Afro-Cuban dance in 4/4 time, with heavy accents on two and four.

March (march) Processional or military music that is suited towards parades or martial affairs. Marches are usually an ornamentation of regular and repeated drum rhythm and is performed on brass instruments, drums, pipes, and other marital instruments.

Mazurka (ma·zur·ka) A fast triple-time traditional dance from Poland that was original sung and danced. It was especially popular in England during the 18th and 19th centuries where couples would follow a leader in a circular formation around the room.

Minne (MI-ne) A tradition of Medieval courtly music in Germany. The most reoccuring theme in the Minnesang tradition is that of the devoted knight who is loyal to an unattainable lady, with no hope of her love in return.

Minuet (MIN-yoo-et) A slow triple-time dance from the Classical era that originated in France.

Operetta (oe-per-RET-tuh) A less serious form of opera that consists of spoken dialog, songs and dances. The modern version of the operetta is the musical.

Paeon (pay-on) A song of praise.

Pastoral (pas-TOR-rul) A composition that represents rural or country life.

Prelude (PRAY-lood) A short instrumental composition for piano that is intended to introduce a larger compositon or set of compositions that immediately follow.

Requiem(rek-wee-em) A musical composition that honors the dead.

Round (rownd) A part song or instrumental piece which starts off with a single voice or instrument on the tune, followed at intervals by the other voices which enter individually and perform exactly the same melody, thus forming a polyphonic harmony out of a simple melody.

Threnody (thren-O-dee) A composition that expresses lament for the dead.

Virelai (vir-e-lay) A Medieval and Renaissance form of French poetry and song.
