There are many places along life's highways when a person comes to a crossroad... Carpe came to that crossroad a long time ago.  When we were first putting together our ideas and goals for the guild, we decided then and there that Carpe would be like no other guild.  We would strive to become the ideal guild - a family atmosphere where people came before the game... and yet in game we would be able to raid effectively and stealthily.  We knew that we would not have the numbers or class make up that other "uber" guilds have, therefore we had to depend on our mental capabilities - to plan ahead, think of all possibilities, and to execute with precision... all the while without sacrificing our beliefs of the individual at the keyboard - the friendships we form -  being far more important than any loot we can acquire in the game. 

Choosing YOUR path in EverQuest

Verant didn't say it would be easy to become level 60, grab all the best loot in the game, and continue to spend "fun" times with all the people you have met in Norrath.  This page is an attempt to describe the changes you will face when you reach that magical Planar level.  This is not a subtle change, and they don't warn you about this when you start your characters.  It is a change that can either drive you to attain level 60, or make you so frustrated with high level characters that you give up all together.

Leveling from 1-45

Going from level 1 to level 45 is basically a consistent process.  Not much changes with fighting style, how many people you fight with to get experience, the looting policies, etc.  

Some people embrace the change.  They like raids.  They like being around more than one group of people.  They like the chaos and bigger mobs. They understand that to get all of this stuff, they have to work more toward a schedule and not just "show up when I feel like it".  

Some people resist the change.  They like the idea of coming to Norrath whenever it suits them - no schedules to keep or mandatory raiding policies to follow.  They don't understand why they don't get experience in the Planes, or why they have to help other people get items (epics) and they don't get anything of their own.  They get impatient when raid leaders spend time organizing groups or when they don't get in the "experience group".  They have been able to do this for 45 levels, why change game play strategy now?

How you choose to move forward post level 45 will directly reflect the kind of guild you would do best in.  Carpe Diem has been dealing with the issues of "What kind of guild are we" since we first realized our planar capabilities.  There is no middle road here folks, and the explanation for that is listed below.

What changes at level 46?

Basically, the answer is simple.  It no longer requires only ONE group to take down a mob, or to succeed in a zone.  It oftentimes take 2 or 3 or 8 full groups of people to succeed in a certain area of Norrath.  The Planes of Hate, Fear, Growth, Sky, etc. were all designed for RAIDING - meaning multiple groups of people must go in order to succeed. 

What are the complaints that some guild leaders hear from their members at around level 46?

  • Why isn't my group getting experience?

  • Why is it taking so long for us to get started?

  • Why are epic quests so hard?

  • Why do I have to help others with their epic quests?

  • Why didn't I get any loot today?

This brings with it lots of issues.  Experience only goes to one group of people instead of to all that participate in killing a single mob.  The loot that drops here is rare, so if 54 people show up to a raid, it is not likely that 54 pieces of loot are going to drop.  It is VERY VERY common for people to go on a planar raid, and come back with NO loot and with LESS experience than when they left (because you can die a lot in the Planes).  This causes SO MUCH FRUSTRATION - don't blame it on your raid leaders - Verant designed it this way.

So ... it is time to choose your path.

  • You can resist the way Verant has designed the game for post level 45 and continue to level and group and get loot as you have done for the last 45 levels.  You would be more inclined to join a FAMILY GUILD.

  • You can accept that your game strategy has to change - if you change with it, so too will your "monetary" rewards.  You would probably do best in a RAIDING GUILD.

  • You can decide that you don't like being placed in a box.  You are not a mouse in a maze.  You can break the Verant barrier!  THAT'S what Carpe Diem has decided to do.  We will forever be a family oriented guild that can raid... and raid well!  We strive to be the IDEAL GUILD.

 

The Family Guild:

What are the characteristics of a Family Guild?  The people in these guilds have one thing in common - they like to have FUN!  They like to talk a lot!  Guild chat is usually a constant flurry of chatter and jokes.  Impromptu little raids tend to spawn up all over the place.  More often than not, you will see many of the higher-level characters in this guild helping out the lower-levels.  These guilds like to get together for Nekkid Gnome Runs and parties at the Arena.  Weddings are commonplace in guilds such as these.  

The Strengths of the Family Guild lie in it's people.  Guild members are genuinely helpful to their fellow guild members.  Doing things on the spur of the moment is commonplace - they don't live much by schedules.  There generally aren't a lot of rules to follow in the Family Guild.  The focus of these guilds is to enjoy yourself, and rules such as raiding requirements or time you spend in Norrath generally gets in the way of the fun.  In addition, the participation requirements for leadership in a Family Guild are not nearly as strict as they are in a Raiding Guild.

The Weaknesses of the Family Guild lie in its "End-Game Progress".  Due to a lax attitude on raid participation or punctuality, Family Guilds have a consistent problem in making raids work.  Since there are generally no requirements for attendance, larger raids are difficult to schedule appropriately, harder to get started, and even harder to finish successfully.  Members of Family Guilds can go and come as they please, so having the necessary people there to run the raid from start to finish is a seldom occurrence.  

Because of this inability to have consistently successful raids, the turnover rate (or rate at which members leave for other guilds) is relatively high for Family Guilds.  Many high level people are attracted to the family atmosphere of these guilds, but frustrated at their lack of in-game progress, and will leave to find other guilds that can provide the loot and new zones that they are hoping to experience.  Family Guilds can get very put-out with this, claiming that those who leave are "obsessed with loot", and "good riddance to you anyway!".  But the Family Guilds that have this attitude have failed to notice that Verant designed the game for incredible loots at this level, and encourages characters to attain the most they possibly can.  Family Guilds need to understand that if they do not provide their members a way to get their epic weapons or keys to the best zones in the game and the best loot, that THEY WILL LOSE HIGH LEVEL PEOPLE.  It is just the nature of the beast.  

 

The Raiding Guild:

What are the characteristics of a Raiding Guild?  These are the guilds on your sever that people generally refer to as "uber".  They have all the best loot, take on all the best zones, and are the envy of all the 14 year olds who want to check out Veeshan's Peak next week when they hit level 46.  You will constantly see these guilds battling dragons and gods and can wipe out a Plane in less than a few hours.

The Strengths of Raiding Guilds lies in it's efficiency and progress.  Raiding Guilds in general are very structured - lots of rules, with required attendance at raids and punctuality guidelines.  Some go so far as to require a certain number of hours a day that you play on the game, or certain times of the day that you are logged in.  Guild chat for Raiding Guilds is reserved primarily for raid communication, and there is a lot less "fun" chat than you would see in a Family Guild.

Raiding Guilds have a lot to be proud of.  Members of these guilds kill the top mobs with efficiency, obtain the best loot, and make raids work on a consistent basis.  Members of Raiding Guilds are the first to get their epic weapons.  They like to go for the brass ring - NOTHING is too big for them, and they will do anything in their power to win.  

Raiding Guilds are not without their weaknesses though.  Due to their "constantly on" behavior, they oftentimes lose sight of the human experience behind each of the character.  Generally speaking, Raiding Guild members are gruff, to the point, and always down to business.  They don't have the patience for repeated failures, or people who don't know how to play their characters.  And they have no problem saying to your face that you messed up!  This impatience and assertiveness can cause problems in the guild, especially for those members who still focus on fun as the overall goal of the game.  It's no longer "just a game" to many Raiding Guilds.  Real life takes backseat to game obligations.  Shape up or Ship out is the new motto.  

 

The Ideal Guild - Breaking the Verant Barrier:

This is the vision of Carpe Diem.  We are a diverse guild made up of unique individuals who enjoy the GAME of Everquest but do not let it interfere with our personal lives. (Well, we try not to... there are times when our spouse asks us to log and we say "just a moment, dear" and that moment turns into an hour...but we DO try!!)  We rejoice in each others personal and in game triumphs.  We are saddened by each other's personal and in game dilemmas.  We unite behind each other so that we can help out with in game goals (epic mobs, specific armor, etc.), and we encourage each of our member's personal dreams too.

Our strengths are our friendly and playful guild chat, flexibility of events, welcoming of lower levels for the uniqueness the INDIVIDUAL brings to our family - not their skill, raiding success - we plan, practice, and revise our plan constantly so that we can make the most of that we have, are thought of well by our peers, retain high level players because we meet both their emotional and organizational needs - we allow for them to be a part of something bigger than themselves but do not require that they give up their identity to do so.  We often find ourselves consciously or unconsciously asking ourselves NOT what our guild can do for us but WHAT CAN WE DO FOR OUR GUILD!
