    Well, I wrote this up, so I'll keep it posted, although it seems somehow pale after today's events.  First off, let me emphasize again for those who never read beyond the first sentence.  All of the information on this site is still accessable for free.  Every equipment and quest description can be read without having the premium service.  The difference is how you find the information.  Now let me give you some history.

    This site started back in March of 1999 when EQ was still in beta.  It was basically just a one page guidebook I wrote up in my spare time, hosted on a free server.  People really liked it and asked me to write more.  I added in class guides, then quests and then trade skill and equipment lists.  Soon I moved to a server of all game sites which could afford to host the sites by running ads on all the sites.  I kept expanding things, but everything except the forum was still only in html, since that was all I personally knew how to do.  Up until then, I didn't have to pay anything to run the site, and even made a small profit.

    By January, 2000 the site was already one of the most popular EQ sites, and I decided to get my own server where I would have complete control of things.  I also was talking with Illia at that point about putting together a bestiary, which was something nobody else really had.  We launched the bestiary soon thereafter, which was the first true database on the site.  Now anyone who knows anything about websites knows that html pages are generally the least server intensive and least expensive pages you can have.  Databases take processing power and use up more bandwidth.  Still, I loved how the bestiary worked and asked Illia about the possibility of databasing the quests and equipment.  To give perspective, at this time the site ran on a single, low end server, and was using about $600 per month in bandwidth (which was easily paid by the ads I was running).  The quests and equipment were each in a single list broken down by zone.  You went to the zone and scanned down to find what you wanted.  There were no comments and very little cross referencing.

    We then added the quest database in July, 2000 -- about a year ago.  Within a month, we had to add a second server to handle the load and the bandwidth costs started to rise.  Still the versatility and ease of use was pretty obvious.  We added the strategy forums soon thereafter.  Then in December, just in time for the Velious launch, we added the new equipment database.  Suddenly, you no longer had to go to that single list of equipment, sorted solely from best to worst.  You could find the equipment in a whole bunch of ways.  You could even comment on each piece of equipment.  Illia even came up with a way to add in your character information and search for armor upgrades.  We needed two more servers to handle the load.  And our bandwidth costs skyrocket to $6,000 per month.  Yet with the added popularity of the database, our ad revenue still easily covered the costs.  Then in February our ad contract failed and we suddenly had little to no ad revenue, and still had a $6,000 bill coming in each month.  We asked for donations and fortunately enough people gave money so that I didn't have to pay too much out of my own pocket to keep things going.  Nonetheless, this obviously had to stop.

    So I started thinking about it and realized that if the site were still like it was a little more than a year ago, and all of the quests and equipment were still just listed on a series of html pages that could not be sorted or searched, the bandwith costs would probably still be down around $600 and I wouldn't have had to buy 3 new servers.  This is about what I still get in ad revenue.  And then I came up with the premium idea.  That idea is that those who want to use those expensive database functions should pay a small fee to use them.  Otherwise, they should go back to the access that they would have had had we not added in all those expensive upgrades.  I wanted access to remain free, but it only seemed right to me that those who wanted to use all the fancy, expensive features should help cover the costs for them.  So here it is.  You will notice that all of the premium features are in actuality the most processor intensive database functions.  Free users can not only still get the lists you could have gotten before the database, but also get things like the comments, strategy guides, extensive cross-referencing, etc that the database brought to the site.  Premium users get the full benefit of the newly beefed up database functions.
