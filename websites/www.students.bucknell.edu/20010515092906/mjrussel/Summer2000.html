Asexual to Sexual Recruitment Shift in Acropora palmata, St. John, U.S. Virgin Islands.

Russell, M.J., Hoffman, D.

                C-3912 Bucknell University, Biology Department, Lewisburg, PA, 17837, USA.

 

Acropora palmata was one of the most dominant branching corals on many Caribbean fringing reefs.  This corals reliance on asexual reproduction by fragmentation left it susceptible to a combination of events in the late 1970’s early 1980’s.  A string of major Hurricanes, white band disease, the mass mortality of Diadema antillarum, and over-fishing of herbivores left many Acropora stands thinned.  Many of the larger more asexually prolific colonies were destroyed, and with the lack of algae free substrate to recruit to, Acropora palmata populations dwindled.  The past prolific population numbers of this coral can be realized by viewing the large stands of dead Acropora that are found on many fringing reefs of the Caribbean.

Starting in the spring of 1999 to the summer of 2000 the proportion of recruitment by asexual or sexual modes was measured during a photographic survey of the population of Acropora palmata at Peter Bay, St. John U.S.V.I., using 4 random quadrants (100m2 each). The percentage of colonies that were small (<10in2) sexual recruits or fragments of any size were calcualted in the spring, summer, and fall (Fig. 1).  These values show that approximately 50% of all possible recruits come from asexual fragmentation.  This is in contrast to the ~ 69% recruitment by asexual fragmentation noted in similar areas in the late 1970’s and early 1980’s (Highsmith 1982, Bak and Engel 1979). The fall after the Hurricane season was the only season that showed asexual recruitment by fragmentation more prevalent than sexual recruitment.  All of these fragments don’t live to attach and grow into a new colony; so actual recruitment levels from asexual fragments are lower.  Data collected from spring 1999 to summer of 2000 show that only one fragment per 100-meter square per year actually attach at Peter Bay. 

 

 This study was expanded in the summer of 2000 to include the entire reef at Peter Bay as well as the reef at Jombi Bay, St. John U.S.V.I.  All small (<10in2) sexual recruits and all fragments were measured, photographed, and recorded (Fig.2).  There is a significant change in reproductive strategy from 1982 to 2000 (G-test, G=6).  Again it is possible that many of these fragments are not attaching and producing a mature colony, so the percentage of recruitment by fragmentation is probably much less than 50%. 

                 The reliance of Acropora palmata on intermediate disturbances to create fragments and the large disturbances of the late 1970’s and early 1980’s have left the population numbers too low to effectively repopulate using fragmentation from mature colonies.  Sexual recruitment in this species is slow, and is being hampered by the large amount of algal cover that is present on many Caribbean reefs.  It may take a reseeding project to effectively return the Acropora population to numbers where asexual reproduction can again be effective.
