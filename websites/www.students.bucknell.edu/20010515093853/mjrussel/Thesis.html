Abstract

Coral reefs are one of the most diverse and ancient ecosystems on earth.  The current demise of many reefs has brought these areas into the forefront of conservation research and experimentation. Processes that allow the reef to maintain a healthy state revolve around the constant construction of limestone by the colonial cnidarian coral polyps.  Acropora palmata is one of the fastest growing of these corals, and has been the focus of much research and experimentation.  With the current research findings, it seems plausible that this coral could possibly be used to repopulate damaged reef tracts.  This study answers some of the questions about Acropora palmata that needed attending to before a seeding project could be undertaken.  Information on the current population levels, survivorship, and recruitment levels was gathered.  This proposed study serves as a base line study on the current state of the fringing reef at Peter Bay, St. John, U.S. Virgin Islands and can be used in future studies as a reference point to work from.  It also serves to fill in the gaps in the research so that a larger reef conservation project can be undertaken.

 

 

 

 

 

Introduction

Investigation of Caribbean reef morphology and structure dates back to 1959 when Thomas Goreau completed a study on the ecology of Jamaican coral reefs (Goreau, T.F. 1959).  Acroporid corals were found to dominate shallow areas of the reefs seaward slope where the branching form of this coral allows for a buildup of detritus material, which forms the reef flat and ramparts.  This coral is the major building force for healthy coral reefs.  Acropora palmata (Elkhorn coral) comprises up primary structural component in Florida reefs (Ginsburg 1956). Wave forces are dispersed by these branching colonies and then channeled into the canyons of the buttress zone located behind the cervicornis (staghorn) zone.  A zone of Acropora palmata further breaks up the wave force and prevents reef building material from being washed away (Goreau, T.F. 1959).

More recent research includes studies on the fragmentary nature of A. palmata, from data recorded after moderate and severe hurricanes.  The fragmentary reproductive nature of Acroporid corals is well documented (Highsmith et. al. 1980, Highsmith 1982, Rogers et al. 1982, Wallace 1985).  Major storms such as hurricane Greta left 46% of Acropora fragments alive.  It also resulted in an overall increase in the number of Acropora colonies since many of the base branches survived. Data show that two thirds of A. palmata colonies originate asexually (Highsmith 1982, Bak and Engel 1979).  These investigators also noted that the three dimensional growth patterns of Acropora and its dense heavy skeleton may favor this species for asexual propagation through fragmentation.  The heavy skeleton (~2.0 g/cm3) could prevent the fragments from being transported far from their parent colony and the three dimensional nature of these fragments may assure that one part of the fragment will be extended away from the substrate that it settles on (Highsmith 1980,Highsmith 1982).  Hurricanes David and Frederic in 1979 led to another estimate of survivorship of A. palmata fragments after disturbances.  This study concluded that forereefs at Buck Islands and Tague Bay had 66% and 35% live A. palmata fragments, respectively, 11 months after the disturbance (Rogers et. al. 1982).  Recruitment of juvenile Scleractinia corals show that branching forms including Acropora palmata have few juveniles from sexual reproduction relying mainly on fragmentation for propagation (Bak and Engel, 1979). These conclusions show that asexual reproduction by fragmentation is a major part of the life history of Acropora palmata. 

The fragmentary nature of the Acroporid corals has major consequences for the entire reef system.  Fragmentation often leads to monospecific stands covering large areas of the reef.  Fragments from these stands allow for rapid growth around the periphery, where other competitive species are out shaded (Highsmith 1982).  Even corals that are individually dominant in aggression can be overwhelmed (Connell 1973).  Many fragments, by piling up, also consolidate sandy substrates, transforming these soft substrates into areas that allow future colonization. These fast growing coral thickets also provide habitats for a large range of reef fishes, algae, and invertebrates (Talbot, 1965; Glynn, 1973; Grassle, 1973; Patton, 1976; Ogden and Ehrlich, 1977; Brock et al., 1979). Stands of Acropora are responsible for the highest growth rates of reefs (Adey, 1975; Macintyre and Glynn, 1976).   Acroporid corals allow reefs disturbed by tropical storms the chance to return to their pre-storm condition in 5-10 years (Endean, 1973; Shinn, 1976).  This fast recovery is known to be facilitated by fragmentary asexual reproduction because total destruction of Acroporid corals by major storms results in a much longer recovery time (Stoddart, 1974).

The fossil records of coral reefs have also been studied.  Before 1980, Acroporid corals dominated reefs bordering Jamaica.  Hurricane damage, over-fishing, white band disease, and the death of 95% of Diadema antillarum virtually eliminated A. palmata and A. cervicornis from these sites (Connell 1997, Gladfelter 1991, Hughes et al. 1987, Knowlton et al. 1990, Woodley et al. 1981).  It is thought that because of over-fishing the main grazers of macroalgae shifted from herbivorous fish to these urchins.  The Diadema population grew to dense levels and this made them susceptible to a disease that struck in the early 1980's (Hay 1984, Lessios 1988).  This disease wiped out the dense population of urchins and left the reefs without a grazing guild.  This resulted in drastic zonation shifts (Jackson 1991).  Scientists wondered if these zonation shifts were part of the fossil records for these areas, but found that paleoecological descriptions of Holocene and Pleistocene reefs showed that the pre 1980 state was the most common (Adey and Burke 1977, Geister 1980, Mesolella 1967).  It would seem that the current demise of stands of A. palmata might be the result of anthropogenic effects coupled with the natural occurring disturbances from hurricanes.  This combination of effects seems to be keeping these reefs in an unnatural zonation pattern.  Conservation measures, such as reseeding these reefs with A. palmata fragments may allow the natural zonation state to return, and may counter negative effects that humans have on these environments.

The relative interspecific aggressiveness of corals in the genus Acropora has received little documentation, especially in the Western Atlantic.  Sheppard (1979) looked at interspecific aggression between reef corals in the Indian Ocean.  He found that the Acroporid corals, of which there are many more species there than in the Atlantic, had a wide range of aggressiveness.  Some species such as A. palifera were shown to damage almost all species they encountered, but A.humilis was damaged by as many species as it damaged.  These data are interesting, but since the species in the Western Atlantic are genetically removed from their relatives in other areas of the world, it is hard to make similar conclusions about aggressiveness.  It is also important to note that on many Caribbean reefs the coral cover is less than 40%, with the rest of the substrate being covered by turf or macroalgae species (Hughes et al. 1987, Bythell and Bythell 1992, Porter and Meier 1992, Rogers 1992).  Competition or aggressiveness towards these species of algae could be more important than interspecific interactions between Acroporid and other corals in studies in the Caribbean.

Another area of study has looked into the validity of the intermediate disturbance theory as it applies to shallow reef environments (Connell 1978).  Studies have shown evidence that shallow reef zones dominated by branching corals such as Acropora palmata may fit well into the intermediate disturbance model (Rogers, 1993).   More studies provide indirect evidence for areas dominated by Acroporid corals fitting into this model.  Evidence from hurricane recovery observations of coral reefs show that diversity can increase due to intermediate level disturbances, but can decrease drastically when these areas are hit with a severe or long lasting disturbance more akin to man made pressures (Highsmith et al 1980, Rogers et al. 1982).

With all these past studies focusing on Acropora palmata asexual reproduction it seems that this coral may be a good candidate for re-population of damaged reef with corals.  Information is lacking, though, on the current state of the fringing reefs of the Caribbean.  Recruitment rates, survivorship of these recruits and established colonies, location effects, and growth rates need to be studied. Another area that is lacking in documentation is what effect does the shape of A. palmata fragments have on the survivability of fragments.  It has been hypothesized that the three dimensional structure that results from the growth patterns of Acroporid fragments may keep one area of each fragment suspended away from competition or scouring of the substrate it lands on (Highsmith 1982), but data are lacking to validate this.  A close relative to A. palmata, Acropora cervicornis, was shown to have more surviving fragments that had multiple branches than those fragments that were unbranched (Tunnicliffe 1978, 1980).

 This study gathered data on the survivorship and growth of colonies and fragments and also looked at the effect of fragment shape on survivorship.  The study served to answer the following questions: Is the population in this area increasing, decreasing, or stable?  Are new recruits surviving through the hurricane season?  Are fragments or sexually produced recruits more prevalent?  Does the three-dimensional shape of a fragment effect its survivability?  Does the location of a colony or fragment on a fringing reef have any effect on survivability?  Does the number of wounds taken and subsequent fragments produced by colonies differ by location on the reef?  Are there significant differences in the type of substrate that live colonies survive on?  Beyond answering these questions this study also produced a comprehensive photo survey of all colonies at Peters Bay, St. John, and tested to see if the number of branches a colony has is directly correlated with the size of the colony.

 

Materials and Methods

 

An observational study was conducted on the fringing reefs at Peter Bay (West end of Cinnamon Bay), North Coast of St. John in the U.S. Virgin Islands.  Four quadrants were set up at Peter Bay, two in the spring and two more in the summer (Fig 1).  Quadrants were marked on the corners by tagging with a different colored key chain tag per quadrant attached to dead heads of coral with plastic cable ties. Each quadrant was ten meters by ten meters in size and was placed at random spots on the reef.  Each quadrant was measured with a plastic line weighted with large fishing weights to keep it in place.  Within each quadrant the following data were taken for each Acropora palmata: whether it was a fragment or colony, its size (measuring the largest horizontal plane in inches with a tape measure), whether it was attached or detached to the substrate, number of branches, and number of wounds (white colored break points).   Every colony or fragment was also photographed with a reference object for future size determination.  Photographs were made using disposable underwater cameras and the reference object was a piece of measured coral that contrasts with the substrate. Maps of the location of each colony or fragment in the quadrants were made so that they can be found easily in the future.  Maps were drawn on-site with underwater slates.  Data were collected and photographs taken at the time quadrants were set up and then again three months later.  Data and photographs on the spring quadrants were also taken 7 months after set up for a total of three repetitive data sets.

Data analysis was done using the latest version of S.P.S.S. statistical software.  Paired t-tests were used to test for significant changes in mean size of colonies in each quadrant by season, number of wounds per quadrant by location and season, and the number of small recruits per quadrant by season and location. Simple linear regression was used to test if the number of branches was dependent on the size of a colony. Numbers of individual colonies and fragments were counted over all quadrants to see whether the population at Peter Bay was increasing, decreasing, or stable over the study time period which spanned the hurricane season.

The photo documentation was set up digitally with repetitive pictures side by side for each colony or fragment over time.  This showed any visual changes to each representative and now serves as the base line for measuring any future growth.

The conclusions from this study act as a base line for future observational studies in this area, and brings us closer to beginning a study on the feasibility of using A. palmata to "re-seed" damaged reef tracts. 

 

Results

At first glance, the population of A. palmata at Peter Bay seems relatively stable, but a closer look at the data shows that many fluctuations are taking place in different areas of the reef but many balance each other out.  The location of the different quadrants on the reef flat may account for some of these differences (Fig 1).  Quadrant one is positioned on the seaward side of a breaker zone.  It has a medium depth (4-6 feet) relative to the other quadrants.  Quadrant two is in shallower water (2-4 feet) and is closer to shore.  Quadrant three is in deeper water (5-7 feet) and is located at the seaward fringe of the reef.  Quadrant four is located just behind a breaker zone in medium to shallow water (2-5 feet) and is close to shore but also near the side edge of the reef.

Figure 1.  Location of the four quadrants at Peter Bay.

 

 One of the key determinants of change on the reef is the size of the colonies that populate it.  The mean size of each quadrant's colonies was determined and compared to check for changes by quadrant over the year.  No quadrant showed significant changes but all showed trends (See Fig 2).  When taken collectively there was also no significant change in the mean size of colonies at Peter Bay throughout the year (See Fig 3.1-3.2).

Although A. palmata is known for its fragmentary nature of reproduction the majority of new recruits to the reef seem to be sexual (Area <10in2) in nature and not from fragments (Fig 5.1-5.2). In both the spring and the summer the percentage of small sexual recruits was higher than the percentage of fragments.  Also many of these fragments may not live long enough to establish new colonies and so the prevalence of recruitment through fragmentation must be lower than shown.  Only in the fall after the hurricane season do we see a dominance of fragments over sexual recruits.  This may reflect the higher disturbance found in the hurricane season, which produces more fragments and causes the possible death of small sexual recruited colonies.

Fragments that settle into favorable positions on the reef substrate may have the chance to attach themselves and become new colonies.  The number of attached fragments was tracked throughout the year by quadrant to see if unattached fragments were getting the chance to attach themselves (Table 1). Overall the percentage of fragments that were attached went from zero in the spring to 33.33% in the summer and back down to 28.6% in the fall.  Quadrant one and two had their fragments attaching between spring and fall as compared to quadrant three, which had fragments attaching between summer and fall.  Quadrant four had no fragments attach anytime in the year.

The overall numbers of colonies at Peter Bay was tracked from spring to fall in quadrants one and two and from summer to fall for all four quadrants (Fig 6.1-6.3).  The total number of colonies in quadrants one and two changed from 37 in the spring to 44 in the summer and back down to 38 by the end of the hurricane season.  When all four quadrants were looked at from summer to fall we found the number of colonies to be 71 in the summer and lower again at 65 in the fall.

By noting the number of branches on each colony we could check for the validity of the established theory that A. palmata branches at regular intervals of growth (Table 2 and Fig 7).  Our results show a strong correlation between size of colony and number of branches (linear regression p-value of 0.000).  Our results propose that for every 100-inch increase in a colony's size, when viewed from above and measured at its greatest plane, we would expect the colony to have added 2.526 branches.

Almost every colony was resting or attached to substrate covered by mat algae, so substrate didn't have variable effects on the survivorship.  The ability of A. palmata to out-compete the algae is unknown, but the prevalence of the mat algae on the majority of the substrate balances out the algae's effect on the coral over the entire reef flat.

A photographic survey was made of each quadrant in order to have a record of the state of the coral population (Appendix A).  These photographs could also serve as a base line for future observations on these quadrants.

 

 

 

Discussion

The mean size of the colonies in any or all quadrants had not significantly changed over the time course of these observations.  The slow growth of coral along with the error that undoubtedly came into play when measuring the size of the corals while trying to stay stable in the surging water would have negated noting any changes. 

The amount of wounds that the quadrants had sustained over the year remained relatively stable as well.  Variations of a few wounds from season to season probably stem from isolated events such as branches becoming too big for the colony and breaking off in storms.  Wounds that were present in the spring or summer were completely healed by the fall.  This suggests that the healing rate of A. palmata is faster than its normal growth rate.  Some wounds showed more than an inch of growth over bare, wound substrate that had arisen from a branch breaking off.  The slight increase in wounds in the fall and again in the spring may arise from the aftermath of the Hurricane season.  The fall season data is testament to this disturbance, showing a marked increase in A. palmata fragments. 

It was interesting to note that the majority of new recruits to the reef were from sexual means and not from fragmentation.  Only in the fall did the percentage of new recruits from fragments outnumber the percentage of recruits from sexual means.  This is a reversal from a normal A. palmata system in which fragmentation is the dominant form of recruitment.  The importance of sexual recruitment may be a result of the low density of colonies of A. palmata following the diseases and disturbances of the early 1980's when the most dense stands were drastically reduced in population numbers.  In the fall this reef showed the normal dominance of recruitment by fragmentation, or at least showed more percentage of fragments than sexual recruits.  It would be safe to say, however, that many of the fragments seen in the fall do not actually live long enough to attach to the substrate and form a legitimate new colony.  It is also safe to say that many of the new sexual recruits die before they reach the normal branching colony size.  These factors should be looked at in more depth to determine if fragmentation or sexual recruitment is the dominant form of population growth or maintenance.

Attachment of fragments after they settle onto the reef substrate seems to take place at about one fragment per 100-meter square per year at this reef.  Unattached colonies in the spring show attachment by summer but many dont make it through the rough hurricane season and become unattached again or are killed.  The unattached colonies seen in the spring probably originate from branches broken off of mature colonies during the hurricane season of the fall before.

The overall population numbers at Peter Bay remained stable throughout the year.  The actual population numbers only fluctuated by 6 colonies, which includes attached and unattached colonies and fragments.  It seems that the summer is when one would expect to see the most live colonies and fragments with a return to spring numbers in the fall after the hurricane season.  Studies conducted in the future to recount population numbers will show whether or not this area is seeing an increase in population numbers of A. palmata. 

We have shown that the number of branches has a direct relationship with the size of a colony.  Our data suggests that with every increase in size of 100 square inches we would expect to see 2.526 new branches for that colony.  Branching at regular intervals in its growth allows a colony to make many weak points in its structure at the base of its branches.  When these branches get to a particular size they will be easily broken by storm surge, especially during the hurricane season.  These large fragments stand the best chance for attaching to substrate and forming a new daughter colony.  These colonies are in effect clones of the parent colony and would most likely be susceptible to many of the same diseases or stresses that the parent would be. 

The photographic documentation of the four quadrants will be invaluable to future studies of this area.  Comparisons of the size of colonies and the ability to find particular colonies again will be attainable from the photo survey.  We hope that this photo documentation can be added to other studies of Caribbean fringing reefs to complete a picture of the overall health and population densities of these areas.
