Hey Muse,

I'm not a big poster to the boards but this is a thread that I'll weigh in on. I’m going to try to discuss the general topic first and then jump in with my own opinions at the end.

There are a slew of issues that software developers must wrestle with as they try to determine the minimum spec for a product. To name a couple, there are the marketers who want to make the spec as low as possible so they can sell to as big a percentage of the market as possible. You have the developers who want to have the bar as high as possible so they can put in all the neat bells and whistles. Ad to that the testing team who tries to figure out where the bar can reasonably be set. From my years in the testing field, I would have to say that the end result normally works out with the marketers setting a minimum spec below what is useable and the recommended system requirements is what the testers determined to be the minimum acceptable configuration. (Thus if you only hit the recommended spec, consider upgrading your system or not getting that software)

For most game products in the past, this was the entirety of the battle. Once the product was shipped, that was it. Online games like EQ have changed the way this works. Software is no longer static content. Part of the package is regular patches.

One of the big issues that I’m sure Verant was contending with is when they changed the engine from DX 5.3 to DX 7.0a is that the published system requirements on the Basic box for EQ are now wrong. The minimum spec that people committed to having when they purchased the game and entered a contract with Verant is no longer what they are getting. From the standpoint of a consumer this is not a good practice. If I purchase a piece of software for a computer, and it works with that computer, I have a reasonable expectation that that software will continue to perform at the same level of performance with that system.

That said, lets look at some of the other factors here, Within the next year there are several new MMRPG’s slated to launch that have outstanding cutting edge graphics and will begin to erode the existing EQ player base as well as suck up many of the new gamers entering the MMRPG market.

Additionally the old engine was starting to have trouble with compatibility on what is now pretty common technology this was causing an increasing amount of grief for an increasing number of players (As a SBLive user I’m one). Rather than offering up more bubble gum and bailing wire solutions, Verant bit the bullet and decided to go with the upgrade to code that is more compatible with today’s devices.

This will loose some customers for them, but the niche of the computer market that is “Gamer” tends to be people who are on the fast track of upgrading. In my personal observations I would say most Gamers will upgrade at least part if not all of their system every two years or less. Non Gamer users, (web surfer, office apps etc) tend to hang on to the same system for 3-5 years sometimes longer.

So that’s the thumbnail of the dilemma. We all know the direction Vernat is going. I think its reasonable for the most part. If I ran the zoo this is what I would do:

I would put together a small team to do maintenance on the existing code base which Verant says is almost ready to be frozen (apart from content). I would devote the rest of my Dev resources to creating a new, state of the art engine. Use the same world, but all new character models, motion patterns etc, make the game as “kick but” as they can, put it on par with Halo, UO2, and some of the other eye popping games out there. Rather than keep adding servers to the existing EQ, start adding servers for the EQ2, allow server splits, to let the cutting edge customers migrate to the new software, but retain their characters (on a server basis, just like with the current splits) A system like this will help extend the life of Norath by years, without necessarily shutting out players with older systems into the cold.

This is just an idea, a flawed one at that. I could detail a dozen problems with it. But I won’t, it would add nothing to the basic thread, which is, is it fair to require players to upgrade their systems to keep playing the game. There is a kernel of a solution to the upgrade path problem in the above idea, this is why I present it.
I apologize if this post is too long. I hope it sheds a little light into what some of the conversations at Verant may have been leading up to the recent change.

There is no reason whatsoever that users should have to upgrade the client and hardware after only 1-1/2 years of The game being released. This "forced upgrade" is due to 2 reasons: poor design and a lack of foresight. Allow me to explain:

I was pleasantly surprised the other day by some news about a game I had long since given up on: UO. With 3rd dawn, they are adding 3D effects for those who can run a faster system. However, this client will work *in tandem* with a player with an older, 2D client, allowing both to adventure together.

Why can EQ, arguably one of the most succesful games ever, not be able to provide a different engine to those that can use it, while other games are able to get this right?

EQ had a dated engine when it was first released (around the time of release, I think I compared it to the Tomb Raider engine which first came out in '96 ('95? Seems like forever). EQ was released to the public with dated graphics in the same calendar year as games such as UT and Q3, and almost a year after HL. So, it is reasonable to assume that Verant knew they had a dated engine and would have to upgrade eventually. Not being able to allow multiple engines to access the same code (or more importantly, allow original users to continue to access the game with the original advertised sys reqs) is indicative of porr foresight.

Not being able to handle multiple clients smells of poor design. The majority of EQ is serevr side, with the actual maps being help on the clients machine. The client appears to handle both the engine and the networking functions, in a basic Client - Server model. They should be able to separate the 3d-engine and the networking client from each other, allowing a more robust system and more 3d engines to be supported.

In other words, imagine a FPS that could be accesses by someone with a Quake 1 engine, a Quake 3 engine, and even a 2-1/2D engine like the Build-engine or Daggerfall's X-ngine. Sure, it might look lame on the lower end systems, but all the engines use 3D maps. The same maps, with a lower res version of the textures, and maybe even a lower polygon count (simpler objects, maybe even sprites for some of the items like tables and chairs).

Now, don't tell me this would be an incredible drain on their resources. USers in the Quake community ported the original maps from Q1 to Q2 without the resources and income of VI. Hell, you could re-do a Doom map in the Q3 engine for that matter (not sure if this has actually happened or not).

Now, are there other issues with this? Sure. I'm not saying this would solve all the problems. But it would allow for users with lower-end systems (or those who haven't upgraded) to continue plaaying the game they've bought and paid for, and continued to pay for for a year and a half. There is no good reason this couldn't happen, save for a lack of foresight and a poorly thought out initial design.

Ah well, thats enough of a rant for now. I am curious about what the rest of you think of a multi-client system though.

Ok, let me go at this question from a different perspective. I work at a software development company and I have to admit that as we put out new versions of our product we often update the requirements such that the end user needs to have a better machine. As to our product, we also have a client/server architecture just as EQ does.
However, there is one key difference with our upgrade path in that we make our new versions of the client/server backwards compatable with the old versions of the client/server. So, for example, client version 4 can still connect to server version 5. The way we do this is that we program in such a way that the older client still gets everything it needs, it just doesn't get any of the new features sets as it is unaware of them. In addition, our version 4 server can communicate with our version 5 server or version 5 client, it's just that certain features our not available. This would have been one way that Verant could have gone with their EQ client, for instance.

The downside of this approach is that it requires a much higher commitment, since you must maintain multiple versions of your client - read developer and QE resources are greatly increased - and you must code to maintain both API's in the server backend - read again more dev and QE resources. Verant has chosen to take the different path of maintaining one code stream and making all their customers go to "version 5" - there are not many products out there that take that approach. For instance, Microsoft does not make it such that when they release a new Word you have no choice but to upgrade or you cannot read any word docs; Lotus does not make it such that when they release a new version of Notes/Domino you have no choice but to upgrade - in both instances, their customer base (read big business here) would scream their heads off.

So, my 2 cents is that it truly is not acceptable to expect to be forced to upgrade your hardware to work with an existing version of a product - if Verant had released a version 2 of EQ, then it would be ok for the requirements to change to use ver 2, but to only be patching version 1 and be changing the requirements is, in my opinion, the wrong thing (read if this product were in use by big business, it would not be accepted). I have yet to see any other product on the market require you to upgrade your hardware because of a patch...

Of course, tis only my opinion, my 2cents

Everquest is a mass market product. It isn't marketed just for an elite few who have the money and inclination to stay ahead of the curve in equipment and software. It is marketed to people like my son, who plays his games on admittedly older hardware because that is what the family has available for him. It is marketed to anyone who picks up the box, thinks it is neat to hack and slash a little, and has minimum requirements considerably short of the new requirements.

Is it reasonable for Verant to fail to implement new technology in their games? Of course not, and no one would expect them to. But unlike stand alone products, which can be played forever on the machine you had, online games involve a relationship between the purchaser and the seller. I'm not able to simply play Everquest whenever I want to and my machine is free; I have to be able to log on and use the servers. In essence, I've bought not a game, but the ability to play the game.

But it isn't reasonable, let alone intelligent, for the game provider to say to everyone already involved: ok, you've shelled out money for the game, you've invested money in playing the game for x months, now you can either pony up a better system or you can't ever play our game again. My son doesn't have money just lying around, and investing it in making his computer able to play one particular game is nonsense. While there are a few titles out there that he can't play, the vast majority of games are runnable on his system. Having purchased a game that said it only required a certain system to work, where is it fair that he and others like him be forced to lose the value of that purchase? And please note, there isn't anything obvious about the game or its packaging that would make you think as you purchased it that you were going to have to spend money down the road to play it.

What, then, is a reasonable compromise between these two ideas? I think the reasonable compromise would be to dedicate some servers to the older version of the game, and other servers to the newer version. Certainly it would be possible for Verant to have servers service the old gamecode while upgrading the game on most servers to the newer standard? Eventually, as the number of gamers who wanted to limit themselves to the old standards decreased, the number of such servers could be decreased as well. Everyone wins.

As it stands, Verant's approach to the application of DirectX 7.0a is most likely to do one thing: turn off potential buyers of the game and the new expansion pack. Meanwhile, my son is enjoying other experiences, and has cancelled his Everquest account, as will I when they eventually make it impossible for me to play on my machine.

I guess I see a couple different topics in this discussion.

1) Is it okay to increase the requirements for a game after it's release?

In this situation, I think a new standard has to apply. As was aptly pointed out in previous posts, older games were essentially one-shot....you buy it and play it, or upgrade to buy the next version. MMORPG's are a totally new situation, in that it is an on-going, dynamic environment. This makes it INEVITABLE that the requirements will change as the game tries to maintain compatibility with ever-changing "current" standards in the industry. Right or Wrong really don't apply here, this is just an industry dynamic, and really reflects the older model (newer version has higher requirements).

2) Did Verant handle this situation in an appropriate manner?

This is where the Right vs. Wrong judgement comes into play. I have mixed feelings on this topic. For an MMORPG, I feel Verant has noticeably poor technical support. I am a computer consultant, and consider myself knowledgeable about upgrades and compatibility issues. I have struggled with EQ problems almost from Day One of the game, and have always found my contacts with Verant Tech Support to be completely inadequate. I have provided very detailed information on the problems, and rarely even recieve the courtesy of an answer. I did get one reply, which was basically a re-print of the FAQ on the web site (Sorry, I actually mentioned in my original messsages that I had read that and it did not help). I had been considering joining the Guide program until then, and my only reply to my over 15 messages was in response to the head lady at the Guide program going over and asking them to respond (the canned reply, of course).

My point here is that if Verant wants to continually evolve their product, they need to provide at least basic support. I have worked in the industry for over 15 years, and feel comfortable saying this is a definite weak point they seem unable to address.

So for the original purpose of the post: Yes, I feel it is acceptable to upgrade the requirements in a dynamic game (and I felt the split server suggestions were valid), and No, I do not think Verant has handled this issue any better than they have handled past technical issues. A good MMORPG absolutely REQUIRES good technical support!

Nuff said!
