Roleplaying a Shaman - on Race, Class and Attitude by Khronik

The gods had many reasons for putting the races of Norrath upon this soil, each to his or her own design. The Barbarians were born from the gods of Love and Valor, and these ideals are our calling.

Right off, I noticed that Barbarians had a brotherhood few other races shared. There are fewer of us, and the barren, twisting Northlands makes one aware of just how much we depend upon each other for survival. The world is harsh, and the gods are not always kind to us, so we must of necessity be kind to one another.

That I shower my gifts upon my own kind goes without saying. Whenever a Barbarian in need is near, I answer the call without desiring compensation. Often on my trips back to Halas, I will spend time in the newbie areas watching over the young Barbarians. I heal and buff as needed, not partaking in the battle unless dire need presents (aiding is one thing, coddling is another). I will even toss around some pieces of patchwork armor to promising young warriors and Shaman, when the desire to practice my tailoring presents itself.

Races
However, the real test of a Shaman is the width of his love for fellow beings. Once the raw days of youth have past and one makes that crucial journey through BlackBurrow into the outside world, things must change. We are Shaman. We understand the world as no other. We exist in both the Physical and Mystical realms and tie them both together in harmony. That the other inhabitants of Norrath do not share this wisdom is a tragedy, and is the goal of the Shaman to be the light that guides the unlearned.

I do not discriminate by race. The flesh is a shell. A human does no crime being born a human. That he has learned erroneous ways of looking at the world is his curse, but not his fault. In my kindness I will guide him, and should he not see the value of the path I show him, I mourn his loss and bid him good bye.

Elves understand Nature, but their arrogance must be cured if the world is to survive. Halflings are friendly creatures, and brothers of the forest. Their Druids are among the closest allies a Shaman has (although their tendencies to deify nature, rather than harness it as a steed, gets to be a tad foolish). I cannot help but like Dwarves. They are sturdy and forthright, and they drink Ale like a newborn drinks milk. (Besides, I find the sight of a Dwarf running under the power of SoW hysterical).

Half Elves suffer the stigma of any half breed, and I will not add to their pain by discriminating against them. In any case, their existing between cultures makes them ready travelers and explorers, which I admire.

Erudites irk me a bit. They are aloof and effeminate. They withdrew to their island as if they were to good for the rest of us, then proceeded to wage a bloody mage war that threatened the very planet. As individuals, they can be quite pleasant, but as a group, they are dangerous.

I have met few Gnomes, so I have no opinion at this time. I find their living underground to be creepy, though.

The dark races are indeed tragic. Their twisted heritage makes all but a precious few my enemy. But truth be told, I would not have a world without them. Peace is a wonderful goal for old men, but the young need adversity to grow strong. Evil exists to fire us to action, lest we grow soft in a comfortable bed. To the followers of Innoruk I say, "Bring it on."

Ogre and Troll Shaman are perhaps the most troublesome creatures to me. By species, they are my foes, but by trade, they are my brothers. I will deal with a dark Shaman apprehensively, but with professional courtesy. It is odd to make trade with a being who you know some day you may meet in combat to the death, but it is one of the many paradoxes of life a Shaman must struggle to comprehend.

Classes
While a man's birthplace makes little matter to me, his profession greatly affects my perceptions. A man's life path is his own to choose, not some twist of fate.

Warriors are generic to me. I can respect any man who will grapple with the creatures of the world with only 3 feet of steel in his hands. But the warrior varies from the noble fighter to the loathsome mercenary. To me, your cause determines your worth.

Magi of all stripes earn my respect. As a Shaman I know the mental disciplines of wielding mana, and only an egotistical fool would not recognize their superiority in the field. Sadly, their power comes at the expense of harmony with Nature (and with that, the loss of the wisdom and inner peace it gives). Notable exceptions to this are these: Enchanters, to me, are mostly just tricksters. Parlor magicians more suited for gypsy camps than combat. I've met a few that have used their abilities for exploration, and these I tip my helm to, but as a rule, they are just charlatans. Necromancers are dark beings who make my skin crawl. As a Shaman, I have channeled dark mana to sicken and infect, and I feel dirty whenever I do. I've also just never gotten used to the idea of speaking with someone with one of the undead on a leash. (And if there ever comes a day I again run across Tovax Vmar, let him beware my vengeance!)

Bards and Druids are my brothers. They exist to aid all good people and have a certain harmony with the Universe. Plus, they can hold their own in battle. Good people all around.

Clerics and Paladins are typically good, forthright people, who I will welcome so long as they are not over-zealous about their god. I worship the gods of Love and Valor, but I recognize that they are not the only force in the heavens. Intolerance is a bane to harmony.

ShadowKnights are foul to me (the concept of Harm Touch is anathema to everything I hold dear).

Rogues are typically filthy creatures. I've met a few who are more scout than thief, but I always guard my bags in their presence. Besides, as a holy warrior, the notion of stabbing a creature in the back appalls me.

Rangers fall somewhere between Warriors and Druids. Wood Elf Rangers tend to be severely arrogant.

Monks I like, a lot. They are fierce fighters and have impressive self discipline. Their unwillingness to use weaponry seems silly to me, but I wear a bear on my head, so who am I to judge.


Overall Goals and Attitudes
The goal of a Shaman is to protect his children and to become a Man Of Knowledge. How you interpret that is your call. As I said earlier, I count all races my children (though Barbarians are the favored child). Beyond that, I seek to explore Norrath as much as I am capable. SoW makes that dream more of a reality, and Bind Affinity will solidify my ability to wander freely. I may join or start a guild related to helping travelers through dangerous lands. At level 25, I intend to dive full bore into Alchemy.

Often, our spells will be greatly desired in a rough area. SoW and Bind Affinity are always requested, and water is a dire commodity in the Desert of Ro. I will never flat ask for compensation for such gifts, but I will accept donations. As a Barbarian, I don't expect a free ride from anyone, and any attempt to repay the favor, however small, raises you in my esteem. People who demand SoW and other gifts earn my scorn. I am a protector, not a servant. Show respect and you shall get respect in return. Begging merely demeans you, and newbie characters that blindly ask for handouts get flatly ignored. Twinks seriously annoy me, both as a player and as a Barbarian. Handouts are for the weak. Earn your keep.

Khronik of the Red Eye Clan of Veeshan
