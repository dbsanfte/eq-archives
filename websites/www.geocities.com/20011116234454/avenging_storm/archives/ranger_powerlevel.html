PHAELIA FAIRHEART

How to Power-Level as a Ranger

Yes, it can be done. And surprisingly well for that matter. When I first started power-leveling my real life friends who had created new characters in EverQuest, I was told that rangers make awful power-levelers, that we don't have a good enough heal spell or good enough buffs or any number of other facts that, while true, turn out to be less of a hindrance than I had originally feared.

Simply put, I consider myself to be the equal of any paladin of equal level when it comes to power-leveling and certainly superior to a cleric of the same level. What rangers lack in the areas of healing and buffs, they more than make up with in other skills.

There are five areas of power-leveling I consider most important:

  1. Taunting
  2. Healing
  3. Buffing
  4. Pulling
  5. Killing

Each of which will be covered in as great a depth as necessary to help rangers of 22nd season or better make best use of their time spent power-leveling.

1. TAUNTING

Yes, I consider taunting more important than healing (at least, for rangers). Rangers have the benefit of being one of the best taunters in the game (or the handicap, depending on how you look at it). The reason taunting is more important than healing is that, if the person being power-leveled is never hit, he or she will not need healing. This isn't to say you shouldn't keep your heal spell ready, but if you play your cards right, you shouldn't need to exhaust your mana trying to keep up with the MOBs damage.

Note that there's more than one way to taunt, and the taunt skill isn't always the most effective. MOBs hate the ranger spells ensnaring roots and flame lick. If your taunt repeatedly fails, start casting these two (even if they're already ensnared or flame licked, it will get the MOBs attention and hopefully pull them off the power-leveled).

2. HEALING

The person being power-leveled is going to get hit, regardless of how good you are at taunting. There comes a point when the MOB being attacked is going to disregard your presence in favor of the monk doing 20 - 40 points of damage per round no matter how vigorously you stick your tongue out at it. For this reason, rangers really only become viable as power-levelers after level 22, at which point they gain their 25-point heal spell. Trying to power-level without this would be extremely impractical.

I've found that what works best is for the person being-powerleveled to create a social that warns you to HEAL as soon as he finds himself below a certain point of health (which will vary depending upon that person's level and class). You should in turn make a two line social that automatically targets that person and casts a heal. In this way, you can continuously taunt the MOB being attacked and still ensure that the power-leveled doesn't lose all his stuffing while you're vigorously sticking your tongue out. The two line social should look like this:

/target Billybob
/cast #

where # is the spellgem number occupied by your healing spell. You might include a line that automatically states to those around you, "Billybob owes Phaelia his life ... again!" or something similarly irritating to the power-leveled (because if we didn't irritate them, everyone would want to be power-leveled).

3. BUFFING

Another reason a ranger shouldn't try to start power-leveling before level 22 is that, at that point, you gain the skin buff, Skin Like Stone, which is far superior to its earlier counterpart, Skin Like Wood. While this armor and hp buff isn't quite the match for the cleric/paladin line of buffs, your taunt more than makes up for the difference. Another useful buff that's ranger-only is the Feet Like Cat buff, a +15 agi buff that any melee character will appreciate. (If you're power-leveling a caster of any sort, Tunare give you patience. Med times drive me nuts, personally.)

There's not a whole lot to say about buffing the power-leveled other than that you might think to make more socials that automatically buff the power-leveled when he or she alerts you that the buffs you casted earlier are beginning to wear thin. One thing to remember, however, is to keep your damage shield off. Do not cast a damage shield on yourself. The MOB will be hitting you after all, and any damage inflicted by your damage shield counts toward your own damage total, not that of the power-leveled. If you're high enough to be able to cast a damage shield on the person being power-leveled, that's wonderful but not really a necessity.

4. PULLING

With long range fire spells and unsurpassed skill in archery, rangers make some of the best pullers in the game. Add to this the ability to harmony a group of enemies to prevent the power-leveled from being swarmed, and you've got the perfect combination for pulling. Rangers can stand at a distance from a large group of MOBs, harmony the group of them (provided you are fighting outdoors, which you should be since you're a ranger), then pull one or two of them with spells or arrows. Flame Lick works best for this as it serves as both an armor debuff and, while it does minor damage over time, it doesn't compare to the 49 points of damage you might "accidentally" cause with an arrow making a critical hit. I don't recommend using Ensnaring Roots unless the MOB is unnaturally fast or you really don't relish the prospect of being attacked from behind as you run back to the person being power-leveled.

5. KILLING

Always remember that the power-leveled doesn't have to totally obliterate the MOB being attacked. He or she has only to do more than half the damage in order to gain experience. Watch the MOBs life meter, and lay into it as soon as its below half (wait a little longer if your Flame Lick spell has been doing damage over time the entire battle). Eventually the person being power-leveled will do enough damage per round that you can afford to start "jumping in" earlier than halfway through.

Finally, one of the best things that makes rangers such great power-levelers (and such a great class, in my opinion) is their ability to track down the MOBs that would be the most use for power-leveling. And with the new con-color tracking, its even easier to pick the right MOB for the job. I personally recommend the rabid wolf and bear pelts in Qeynos hills to start with as they provide exp for killing and then exp later for turning in the pelts. And at lower levels, the power-leveled won't need to worry about rabies since you can easily refill his or her life meter with one or two casts of your heal spell.

After rabids, move to bandits in West Karana. Again you get the benefit of gaining exp for the kill and later exp for turning in the bandit sashes they loot to Chesguard Sydwen at the paladin guild in Qeynos City. Plus, the money earned from the bronze weapons can be used to purchase the power-leveled new equipment.

Now you know what to do and how to do it! So make a ranger, get him or her to level 22, and start power-leveling!
