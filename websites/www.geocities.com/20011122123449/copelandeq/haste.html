Haste Calculations

Why

There have been so many conflicting reports over haste numbers...even from folks who claim to use the same approach...that I decided to do my own. I have no intention of ramming these numbers down anyone's throat; the main purpose is my personal satisfaction. If you find them useful, that's great.

Methodology

Haste effects were measured by timing 100 swings of a weapon. Swings were counted by watching the flash of the Melee button: a stopwatch was started on a flash and stopped 100 flashes later.  This test was performed on the weapon once without the haste item and once with the item.  The results were compared to determine the percentage speed increase due to the haste effect.  Most tests were repeated several times and the numbers averaged.

The nominal delay (that listed on the property page for the weapon) was not considered in the calculation.  System- and operator-induced timing variances can cause the weapon to behave differently than this nominal value.  In fact, if you look at the timing of unhasted weapons in the raw data below, you will see that my system seems to impose roughly one tenth of a second additional delay on each swing of a weapon.

A relatively slow weapon was used.  This minimizes small differences in pressing the stopwatch button; fractions of a second are much lower percentage variations on slow weapons than on fast weapons.  My standard for testing is now the Jarsath Battle Sword (Delay = 45) as opposed to the previous Rusty Two-hander.  I simply got tired of devoting a bank slot to a rusty weapon! :-)

The haste effect is expressed in two ways below.  These approaches are complementary expressions, not conflicting ones.

Delay Reduction - This approach expresses the haste effect as a percentage decrease in delay of the weapon.  For example, a haste effect that took a Jarsath Battle Sword from Delay = 45 to Delay = 36 would be called a 20% haste effect.  Since weapons are rated by their Delay, this approach has the benefit of working in the terms everyone is familiar without conversion to swings per minute:  put on a 20% haste item and the Amygdalen Tendril goes from 25 to 20 delay.

Swing Increase - This approach expresses the haste effect as a percentage increase in the number of swings of a weapon per unit of time.  For example, a haste effect that allowed you to swing a Jarsath Battle sword 15 times per minute instead of the normal 13 1/3, would be a 12.5% haste effect.  This approach has the benefit of making it easy to consider damage output: put on a 20% haste item and you'll do 20% more damage per minute.

Note that the Swing Increase expressions is not tested by measuring how many swings can be accomplished in a given unit of time.  Since there is no way to measure fractions of swings, that type of measurement would have extremely large error values.  Swing Increase is calculated from the same data as Delay Reduction.

The only thing to keep in mind is to make sure you define which approach you are using when quoting haste rates.  I believe many of the arguments over the haste effect of certain items stems from confusion over these two approaches.

Formulas

Delay Reduction is calculated from the following formula:
    Haste Effectdr = 100 * (NormalTime - HastedTime) / NormalTime
Swing Increase is calculated from the following formula:
    Haste Effectsi = 100 * (NormalTime / HastedTime - 1)
Since a second is 10 haste units, the weapon delay for a hasted weapon can be determined from the 100 swing time by:
    Hasted Delay = HastedTime / 10
