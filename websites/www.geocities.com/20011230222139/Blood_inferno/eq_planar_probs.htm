EverQuest Planar Drop Probabilities (No Planar Loot Rule)

I am writing this column to hopefully clarify how current planar loot is generally distributed on the Brell Serilis server of EverQuest, in hopes of identifying weaknesses in the current player imposed system and proposing a new system.  These loot systems are generally used in the Plane of Fear and the Plane of Hate.

The current player enforced system is generally geared toward allowing all players who are in need of a class specific planar item to roll on said item if it drops for their class.

The system I am going to propose does not follow that same rule, but does encompass, partially, a rule that some players use.  That rule being that if a player is in need of their LAST planar item, they are allowed to have the item without rolling for it.  However, my system goes even one step further, which can not easily be seen as fair on the surface, which is why I have included a few probability tables to illustrate the likelihood, in a percentage, that players have of receiving planar loot.

NEW RULE:  The rule that I propose is two-fold.  First being a semi-common practice of allowing those who are in need of only ONE item be allowed to have that item if it drops.  The second part to my proposal is that any players that have NO planar items, not be allowed to roll on loot until they become the default player for loot.  The default player is "the only person who needs the item", or "the last person who has not had a chance to loot one item during that raid".  At no time will any player be allowed to loot two items before another has had the opportunity to loot, unless they become the default player.  If there are are no players on the raid that have NO planar items, then loot is randomized between those that can use, unless it is a last item piece for a player.  In the event that there are two or more players that have last item needs that are the same, they will random such item only amongst themselves should that item drop.  Likewise, once a player has received their first planar loot they are no longer restricted from future planar loot rolls as the default player.  Now let me clarify the logic behind this rule.

Before I go into the tables it is very important to understand that the loot tables generated in EverQuest are RANDOM.  This  means that it is impossible to predict certainty in any drops.  It also means that drops do not follow a specific pattern, for example, if the first item to drop for the night is a Breastplate there will not be any more Breastplate drops for the night (this statement is FALSE).  It is possible that another Breastplate could drop again, or even two can drop at the same time.  This is, however, unlikely, but not impossible.  For the sake of the tables presented to you, we will ASSUME that loot does follow some normalcy in its drops.  I also want to make it clear that there are many, many different possibilities of combinations that each player may have for loot, and therefore, the four players that I describe basically go from one extreme to the other.

"Yes" means the player has the item for that column, and "No" means that player does not have the item for that column.

Table 2 shows the percentages that each player has to get a chance to roll on an item.  This percentage assumes that it is possible at any time for one of the possible seven loot items to drop and it assumes that an item does drop.  It is understood that loot does not always drop which would, in fact, lower all of the players percentages of receiving a loot item.  However, since that information has not, and will not be disclosed by Verant, my illustration will move to the point in which an item does drop and base the percentages from that point.  Given that and based on the need of the player to receive that drop are derived the percentages for Table 2.

     EXAMPLES: 

          1)  There are a possible 7 items that can drop, assuming that an item DOES drop.  Player 1 needs 1 item.  1 / 7 = 14.3% chance that player 1 will get an opportunity to roll on the item that player 1 needs.

          2)  There are a possible 7 items that can drop, assuming that an item DOES drop.  Player 2 needs 3 items.  3 / 7 = 42.9% chance that player 2 will get an opportunity to roll on an item that player 2 needs.

          3)  There are a possible 7 items that can drop, assuming that an item DOES drop.  Player 3 needs 5 items.  5 / 7 = 71.4% chance that player 3 will get an opportunity to roll on an item that player 3 needs.

          4)  There are a possible 7 items that can drop, assuming that an item DOES drop.  Player 4 needs 7 items.  7 / 7 = 100% chance that player 4 will get an opportunity to roll on an item that player 4 needs.

In these examples, player 1  has a 14.3% chance to roll on an item that s/he needs assuming an item does drop, but  player 4 has 100% guaranteed chance to roll on an item assuming an item does drop.

Table 3 calculates the probability that each player will have in order to WIN the item IF the item that they need drops.  This chart is generally the way most loot is randomized in planar raids and clearly favors the newest player or the player that has NO planar loot.  To most people this system does not seem to give credit to those who have spent countless hours on planar raids, but is continued to be used for lack of a better system.  In this table you can see the probability to win an item is substantially reduced when based on the percentage that the item will drop and then multiplied by the percentage that you will win if others are rolling against you.

     EXAMPLES:

          1)  There are a possible 7 items that can drop, assuming that an item DOES drop.  Player 1 needs 1 item.  1 / 7 = 14.3% chance that player 1 will get an opportunity to roll on the item (Breastplate) that player 1 needs.  There are 3 players total who need that drop (Breastplate).  Each has a 33% chance of winning the roll.  When paired up against each other and against the probability that the item needed will drop, player 1 has an overall 4.7% chance of winning the item.  14.3% x 33% = 4.7% (Rounded up).

          2)  There are a possible 7 items that can drop, assuming that an item DOES drop.  Player 2 needs 3 items.  3 / 7 = 42.9% chance that player 2 will get an opportunity to roll on an item (Boots) that player 2 needs.  There are 3 players total who need that drop (Boots).  Each player has a 33% chance of winning the roll.  When paired up against each other and against the probability that the item needed will drop, player 2 has an overall 14.2% chance of winning the item.  42.9% x 33% = 14.2% (Rounded up).

          3)  There are a possible 7 items that can drop, assuming that an item DOES drop.  Player 3 needs 5 items.  5 / 7 = 71.4% chance that player 3 will get an opportunity to roll on an item (Helm) that player 3 needs.  There are 2 players total who need that drop (Helm).  Each player has a 50% chance of winning the roll.  When paired up against each other and against the probability that the item needed will drop, player 3 has an overall 35.7% chance of winning the item.  71.4% x 50% = 35.7% (Rounded up).

          4)  There are a possible 7 items that can drop, assuming that an item DOES drop.  Player 4 needs 7 items.  7 / 7 = 100% chance that player 4 will get an opportunity to roll on an item (Arms) that player 4 needs.  There are 3 players total who need that drop (Arms).  Each player has a 33% chance of winning the roll.  When paired up against each other and against the probability that the item needed will drop, player 4 has an overall 33% chance of winning the item.  100% x 33% = 33% (Rounded up).

This table clearly shows that a player who has need for only one drop (Player 1) is against the odds of winning that drop when competing against other players for the drop as well as the odds of the item even dropping!  

The table also shows that a player who has no planar loot has a distinct advantage of receiving loot should an item drop.  At a minimum a player who has no planar loot in these examples has a 33% chance of going home with an item.  Where as, the player who has probably spent the most time in the planes only has a 4.7% chance of winning planar loot.

 
Table 4
Chance that item will drop and player will win the roll under new rule.
Helm* Arms* Bracer  BP* Gloves Boots Legs
Player 1 0% 0% 0% 14.3% 0.0% 0% 0%
Player 2 0% 21.5% 0% 0% 21.5% 14.2% 0%
Player 3 71.4% 35.7% 0% 0% 0% 23.6% 35.7%
Player 4 0% 0% 100% 0% 50% 33% 50%

Table 4 calculates the probability, using the new rule, that a player will have of winning the roll on an item IF the item drops.  The * (asterisk) indicates the item that dropped.

     EXAMPLES:

          1)  Assume an item drops (Breastplate).  Player 1 is given the right to loot a Breastplate should that item drop.  As you can see by this rule, player 1's chance of winning the item has increased from 4.7% to 14.3%.  All other players have forfeited their right to loot a Breastplate so their probability of winning one has been dropped to 0%.

          2)  Assume an item drops (Arms).  Three players need the item.  Player 4 is NOT allowed to roll for the item because this player has yet to receive their first planar loot.  This limits the roll to players 2 and 3.  Player 2 has a 42.9% chance to get a roll on an item (Arms) that player 2 needs.  Player 2 also has a 50% chance of winning the roll on the item since player 1 cannot use the item, and player 4 is not allowed in the roll.  42.9% x 50% = 21.5% chance that player 2 will win the roll if the item (Arms) drops.  Player 2 has increased their odds of winning from 14.2% to 21.5%.  In this same example, player 3 has increased their odds of winning from 23.6% to 35.7%.  For the sake of the examples we will assume that player 2 wins the roll for the Arms.

          3)  Assume another item drops (Helm).  Two players need the item.  Player 4 is NOT allowed to roll for the item because this player has yet to receive their first planar loot.  This limits the roll to player 3 since both player 1 and 2 already have the item.  Player 3 has a 71.4% chance to get to roll on an item (Helm) that player 3 needs.  Player 3 also has a 100% chance of winning the roll on the item since player 4 is not eligible to roll and players 1 and 2 can not use the item.  71.4% x 100% = 71.4% chance that player 3 will win the roll if the item (Helm) drops.  Player 3 has increased their odds of winning from 35.7% to 71.4%.  For the sake of the examples we will assume that player 3 wins the roll for the Helm. (duh!)

          4)  Assume another item drops.  At this point it does not matter what item drops because it will automatically go to the default player, which is player 4.  This is because the rule states that "at no time will any player be allowed to loot two items before another has had the opportunity to loot, unless they become the default player".  Player 4 could have become the default player at any time had a bracer dropped, but is now the default player since all other players have looted.  Since all other players have a bracer the item would have "Defaulted" to player 4.  At this point, Player 4 would no longer be restricted to the no planar loot policy.  However, player 4 would not be allowed to roll for loot until all other players had at least received one item for loot.  In our examples all players have now received at least one loot item, so future drops are not restricted to anyone unless they are the default player by virtue of being "the only person who needs the item".

At anytime the order of the drops could have been randomized.  The order of drops is irrelevant at this point and are only used as examples of possible outcomes.  Player 4 could have been the first person to actually receive loot had a bracer dropped even though they appear to be the most restricted.  Table 4 shows that although player 4 has been somewhat restricted on loot items, this player still has the highest probabilities overall to receive loot.   Meanwhile, all other players have had their probability of winning a loot item dramatically increased as well.

By following a looting rule similar to this, we are promoting the distribution of loot to those who have most likely spent the most time trying to get it.  At the same time we are not limiting those who are new to planar raids from achieving their goal of getting loot as well.
