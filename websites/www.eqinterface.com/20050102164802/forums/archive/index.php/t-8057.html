Most of the posters here are making an assumption...namely, that the 3d images are rendered(drawn) "in ratio." By this I mean that a square object for instance, must be drawn 10 pixels by 10 pixels. This is absolutely not the case when rendering 3d objects in a game on screen.

3d objects themselves are defined in their own internal dimensional values (inches, centimeters, etc) which in no way directly corresponds to the pixels on a screen, since the object designer has no way of knowing what resolution your monitor is going to be set to. And in fact, people all use varying resolutions and aspect ratios so there is no sense in designing that way anyway. In our example then, the square object will be defined as 10 "somethings"(inches, centimeters, etc) high by 10 wide.

The rendering engine draws the objects on the screen based on the resolution and aspect ratio the video driver is set to. So, that square object we were talking about might actually be rendered at 10 x 7 pixels, for example, because that ratio appears "square" on screen (depending on the resolution and aspect ratio a particular video driver is set to).

In this specific instance, i.e. 3d games rendering, think of the resolution as merely the size of the window you are looking through.

This is completely different than taking a static image and resizing it to a different aspect ratio because, and this is the important part, the image is NOT being redrawn at the new aspect ratio, it is being stretched/shrunk to the new size.

Also, keep in mind that static images like bitmaps, jpegs, etc, ARE drawn pixel by pixel in direct relation to the resolution and aspect ratio of your video settings. So if they were created by someone at 4:3 and then displayed on a monitor whose video driver is set at a resolution with an aspect ratio of 5:4 it will DEFINITELY look skewed.

Now, a grain of salt: I do not know if the EQ engine actually makes the proper transformations or not, but any game designed to display 3d objects with the correct aspect ratios irregardless of the USER'S video settings, does it this way.

At least, this is my understanding of how this video magic works :p

I use 1280x1024 in game for the same reason that I use it on my desktop. It is the highest resolution my monitor will run at without going all fuzzy. I'm more interested in getting three more lines of text in my editor than in my squares being absolutely perfectly square.

The difference in ratios between 5:4 and 4:3 is about a 6% skew, which isn't really enough to notice. The human visual system is amazing at adaptation, so if something looks like it ought to be square, you'll see it as square and adapt the skew to the rest of the screen without ever being conscious of it.

If the skew really bugs you, you can always shrink the visible area of your display by about half an inch in width.

It is possible to correct the aspect ratio in the graphics pipeline so that a true 4:3 area is displayed on your 5:4 pixels, but I doubt Verant does that. This correction would not add any lag, since it just changes a few numbers in the view projection matrix that is being applied to everything already.
