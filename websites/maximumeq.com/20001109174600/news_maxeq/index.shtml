Gordon comments on the Halloween Events
by Geoff - 11/01/00 18:36 EST
For those of you who don't know, a few events took place last night, to celebrate Halloween.

Stories include a werewolf attack in the West Commons, and a dragon appearing in the Lake of Ill Omen.

I've noticed that there are a bunch of threads on the events last night. Some people enjoyed themselves, others didn't. I think that this is pretty much going to be standard for virtually everything dynamic event that happens in EverQuest.
We cannot have a situation where we provide ample warning for everything that is supposed to be a surprise in game. That surprise is a big portion of what makes these events exciting. If we were to start giving explicit warnings before things happen, the target zones would immediatly be innundated with people ready to take part when an event is launched, and the objective of the event would be overcome in very short order.

Yes, characters die in these types of events. They'll even lose experience if they are killed by an NPC. It's part of the game, and part of what makes EverQuest exciting.

As mentioned by Bill Coyle, one of our game designers, the characters who die in some of these events are the victims who need to be saved. Whether you are a victim, or one heros who overcome the challenge, you are still part of the story.

-Gordon

Those darn warping mobs
by Geoff - 10/30/00 13:36 EST
Gordon answered the age old question about why certain mobs tend to "pop" in and out of view.

>>>Why do MOBS "pop" in and out of view in game. They actually don't from the NPCs point of view. Warping occurs as a consequence of prediction on the client. The client operates in a fashion such that limited bandwidth is used to display NPC position. The client recieves a package from the server that tells the client the position, vector, and speed of the NPC, and any actions that that NPC takes between packets is predicted. When NPCs change their behavior between packets, that behavior is not relayed to the client until the next packet. Currently, updates are sent more often for NPCs and PCs that are in a certain radius from the PC that is observing those NPCs. However, when there is a large number of mobile objects in a close radius, updates cannot be sent instantaneously. We're putting some code in Tuesday that will hopefully drastically reduce warping. Updates for group members, along with those for anyone/anything that you have targeted, will be sent far more often. As such, the chance of what you are observing appearing to "warp" should be reduced. -Gordon

DirectX requirements changing
by Chris - 10/14/00 13:29 EST
This was added to the patch message recently:

October 13th, 7:01pm
------------------------------

*Important System Configuration Announcement*

As many of you know, over the past couple of months we've made numerous enhancements to the EverQuest client. In addition to things such as new /-commands and other changes of that nature, we've enhanced the UI itself to allow moveable and resizable windows, among other things. At the same time we've been working on a brand new sound engine, and have been further concentrating on optimizing and expanding the capabilities of the EverQuest client.

For most applications, the feature set of a version of DirectX is limited to the lowest common denominator between the version that the software is compiled under, and the version running on the client. This means that though you may be running DirectX 7.0a already, and are receiving advantages associated with the more advanced code, the feature-set of DirectX used when in EverQuest is limited to version 5.2.

This limits the type and breadth of enhancements that can be made to the client. Given that we feel that EverQuest players would enjoy continuing to see new enhancements for the client, we will soon begin compiling the client under DirectX 7.0a, and implementing the feature-set available within that version. When we do this, everyone will need DirectX 7.0a (or higher) to play EverQuest.

Here are some of the possible benefits from DirectX 7.0a:

- Better performance (Graphics and Sound)
- Gamma Correction working in D3D
- Proper Mip-Mapping in D3D
- Fixes fogging on some cards. (Some cards have no fogging at all and look pretty bad)
- Allows us to use newer interfaces to support such features as full-screen anti-aliasing, and alternate Z-buffering methods, which tend to look better.

Changing to DX7 does have a potential downside. Those using older or discontinued video or sound cards that do not have DX7 compatible drivers may need to upgrade to continue playing. However, we feel that the benefits that will come from upgrading to this newer version of DirectX outweigh the potential problems.

We plan to begin requiring DirectX7.0a when the EverQuest client is next patched, in two or three weeks. We wanted to be sure to give everyone enough time to check their version of DirectX and install the newest version. You can run DXDIAG.EXE in your WindowsSystem directory to check your DirectX version and get the newest version of DirectX from http://www.microsoft.com/directx if it is needed.

Please keep an eye on the patch message for further information regarding the planned implementation of DX7.

- The EverQuest Team

I have a DSL connection, and a very fast computer/video combination, so I am certain this has nothing to do with the problem. And as further evidence of that, if I were interrupted in combat while casting a spell, the spell timing bar would disappear immediately upon being hit, and I would also receive the message 'your spell has been interrupted.'
Why can't my spells be immediately reset after interruption in that scenario like combat?

While I've never heard of a 2 second pause being insufficient, I have seen this effect before and imagine that it has something to do with the way prediction is implemented. The client and server do not send position data constantly while you are running, but instead update position data either at a regular interval, or when something significant changes. Basically, the server thinks you are still moving even though from your point of view you are stopped.
The trick here is to be sure that the server receives the "I stopped" packet before it gets the "I started casting" packet. The best way around this I've found is to turn slightly upon stopping. I always had a much easier time of getting my spells off once I started doing that.
