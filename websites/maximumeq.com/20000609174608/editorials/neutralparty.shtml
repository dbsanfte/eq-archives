Customers Versus Verant
The article below was written to bring to light some problems I believed could be remedied in EverQuest.  The article was posted to our website.  It was also mailed directly to the PR lead at Verant.

Thank you to Everlore (wwww.everlore.com) and EQ'lizer (eq.gameznet.com) for posting the press release about the article.  They were the only two EQ sites to do so.

I have received many replies from readers.  Links to a couple are below.  I have added my comments in italics.  I will add more letters from readers as time permits.  There are some *very* good ones.

Verant has not responded.

*****

Let me say from the first sentence of this editorial that I am not a Verant basher. Nor am I a VAK (Verant Buttocks Kisser), as those players supporting Verant have come to be called. This editorial is being written to help improve a game that I enjoy immensely. I hope it can help. Perhaps it cannot.

Wood Elves, Ogres, and Barbarians; - Lend me your ear.

I have been a staunch supporter of EverQuest since I began playing in April of 1999. I thoroughly enjoyed the game. I thoroughly enjoyed the people who played it.

The game has evolved since April 1999. The players have also undergone change. As you read this article remind yourself that I support the game. This does not mean that I support either Verant or the players on all counts. It means I support the evolution of the game. That is very important to understand, as it will belay your immediate emotional reactions to some of my points. I am not on your side or against you. I am selfish and wish to play a game that I enjoy.

And so it came to pass that I saw a thread on the official EverQuest message board titled, "Verant VS. Players". Would a company choose a course of action that alienated its customers? It is highly unlikely that any company would. Why then are threads like the one above being posted? Let’s take a look at some of the game issues that are raising the most commotion.

  • Server Overcrowding: The first thread I could find on this topic had 211 posts in it. It is a volatile issue. Here are the facts as I have been informed of them. They may be incorrect. If so, please correct me.

    1800 is the optimal number of players per world according to the people who designed it. I am using a post by Brad McQuaid to come up with this number. I am sure that both gameplay and profitability came into play when choosing this number.

    Let’s look at the gameplay issue first. There are some underlying problems with arbitrarily choosing a number like 1800 and they should be addressed. 1800 players distributed evenly across all of the zones on a server would put approximately 25 players in each zone. We know that in practice this is not the case. Many zones are empty and many have in excess of 70 players using them. Why is this the case? One possible answer is that some zones are simply more enjoyable to play in than others are. They are so much more enjoyable in fact that players are willing to share them with 70+ other players. City zones are relatively boring it seems. Lower Guk is relatively enjoyable, as is Oasis of Marr.

    A secondary problem that many players do not immediately think of is that the design of the game inherently creates logjams of players based on level. It gets increasingly hard to level as you play through the game. Remember how easy levels 1-5 seemed to be when you were level 20? Levels 15-20 look almost as easy when you are level 40. Player progression through the game is slowed by game design as you play deeper into EverQuest. This causes zones like Upper Guk to be crowded one month and relatively empty the next. The pinnacle of the bell curve of players has passed by this once enjoyable zone and on to another. I do not believe that has to be the case.

    The majority of players use a zone to either gain experience or gain items or money. Very few players role-play. The game simply is not built to be a role-play game no matter how many people praise its virtues. You cannot progress through the game by role-playing. Role-playing can enhance the game, but it is not the underlying reason for playing. I personally wish it had more affect on the game.

    Since people are using a zone to either gain experience or items would it not make sense to give them more zones of like quality to do this? The logjam of players was foreseeable. I believe the Verant staff while conducting their Infected Paw changes has even mentioned it. It is not easy to guess what players will enjoy in a zone. However, it becomes brutally evident when they flood to one zone and shun another. Why were more zones changes not made? There may be a very good reason. Profitability is one understandable reason. Perhaps it is simply too expensive to continually change the way zones are setup.

    I believe that overcrowding could actually be fixed, to a certain extent, without new servers by directing the flow of players throughout the current world.

    Older servers like Fennin Ro will in all likelihood always have more players than the new servers. EverQuest is addictive and few people seem to be quitting. Those that started on Fennin Ro are still playing there. Some new players are choosing Fennin Ro. The numbers can only go up until a critical mass is reached and the game is unplayable on that server. Some say that it has already reached that point. How can this be overcome?

    One suggestion was a server cap of X number of players allowed on the server at one time. I do not feel this can work. What happens when my friend who just bought the game cannot play it with me because my server is already at its cap? The social aspect of the game is every bit as important to its success as the hack and slash aspect.

    Verant has stated that they will be splitting the server. This allows the hack and slash aspect of the game to flourish once again, but hurts the social aspect of the game. There is little possibility that all friends will end up on the same server. Circles of friends overlap. One circle may choose Fennin Ro server #1. Another circle may choose Fennin Ro server #2. Which server should I choose? How do I tell who chose server #1 and who chose server #2?

    Can I play on either server if I choose to on any given night? On Mondays I like to play with my circle of guild friends on server #1. On Wednesdays I play with my friends from work on server #2. I do not believe that this is currently possible in EverQuest. I believe that it should be. New servers could be added and players would not be forced to give up hours of gameplay to join them and start over from scratch. I may be wrong, but I believe that Verant’s argument is that the economy would be ruined on the new servers. I argue that there is no true economy on the servers in their current state. Tradeskills were poorly implemented and vendor pricing is unbalanced. The economy is going to be ruined in short order whether or not players are allowed to move to the new server. Postponing the problem is not solving it. Let the players have fun until you can come up with a solution to the economy or lack thereof.

  • Cheating; I am not an expert when it comes to programming or to encryption, but some things seem obvious. Verant has not recognized ShowEQ publicly. It seems probable that choice was made to lessen the number of people using it. Why not just fix the problem? ShowEQ sniffs packets on a local network to produce a map of the zone which the player is in, including all mobs in that zone. It is cheating. It is a bannable offense. I agree with Verant that it should be dealt with swiftly and with extreme prejudice. Verant can never eliminate all cheating or hacking. All they can do is make it incredibly hard and incredibly annoying for the cheaters and hackers. I am not a programmer and I am not a hacker. I do not use ShowEQ. I cannot from experience give a solution to this problem. It is likely that by using a method of encryption the data stream could be made virtually unreadable. Make it so the cheaters cannot get the information passively from the network. They must take it actively from memory. I am confident that is much more difficult to steal the information from memory than it is from the network. It is likely that there are ways to detect attempts to do this. Most of the cheaters would just leave it alone. Make it annoying for these miscreants and they will find something else to do.  

"But encryption will mean more server overhead and more lag!" I can hear the wailing and gnashing of teeth now. I do not believe that is necessarily true. I am confident that there are ways to keep peoples hands out of the cookie jar without breaking the jar.

I have been deliberately vague in this article. I could literally write 50 pages on how the server setup could be more efficient and how game design could be changed to help with overcrowding. Give Verant credit for the fact that there is an incredible depth to gameplay in EverQuest. You cannot simply change one portion of the game without affecting many other balance issues. If this editorial receives a reasonable amount of valuable response I will take it deeper. I fear that I may be wasting my time on deaf ears. 

There are many other problems. One of which is class balancing and how to perfect it. I will wait and see how this editorial works out before I try to comment on those aspects of the game.

I would like to hear from both the players and from Verant. Please only mail me constructive ideas. I do not relish, nor require, a mailbox full of ranting and raving.

This will be sent to Gordon Wrinn at Verant. Though Gordon cannot possibly fix the problems himself, he is the unlucky soul who has been given the PR job. I would love to work with him to help make EverQuest a better game.

John
