At least in my lab we tend to keep things segregated into an acids, bases, oxidizers, and flammable solvents. DI water comes out of the tap. (yay)

Yep water is a good solvent for ionic materials as pointed out with the salt posts it is even pretty good with things that have some polarity or a lot of hydrogen bonding like some cellulose derivatives, starch, sugars....

Water is a lousy solvent for non-polar materials. Just try dissolving something like wax or greasy substances in water - it isn't going to happen (that is why you need soaps and detergents which have a nice polar end and another non-polar end in the molecule - blah blah blah surfactants blah blah micelles blah gibbs surface free energy blah blah blah). You need to find something with similar polar/non-polar character and getting close in cohesive energy density helps a lot too.

So other solvents that may be involved are things like ethanol, hexane, limonene (the terpene in orange oil), benzene, trichlorethane (drying cleaning), THF, NMP, and thousands and thousands of others including probably the best one for a lot of things - supercritical carbon dioxide (although how you contain that in a mixing bowl is beyond me.)

Now how purified water is made is another mystery unless the brew barrel can double as a liquid-liquid extraction vessel and the brewer is skilled with separation processes (I really hated that course).

All I can say is when chemical engineering fails rely on magic!!!
