I find myself in the odd position of agreeing with both the "it's just a game" and the "it's a community" sides in this discussion. :)

One the one hand, it is just a game. If I die in-game and my corpse rots, I'll still have my job IRL, I'll still have a roof over my head, and I'll still have food on the table. EQ is a distraction, an escape from the humdrum boring routine of daily life.

On the other hand, EQ is also a place to meet, chat, and work with others towards common goals. It's irrelevant that those goals aren't real-world ones or life-and-death ones. They're still goals. Missing any goal is a source of disappointment and frustration.

If I agree to meet my best friend for lunch at noon, and I don't show up until 2pm because a monster traffic accident left me stewing in my car for the better part of two hours, I would be very upset. No, I can't do anything about the accident. I didn't cause it, and I couldn't have prevented it from happening. I didn't know about it, so I couldn't avoid it (or maybe it happened after I left). PerhapsI can call my friend if both of us have cell phones, and let him know I'll be late ... but what if one of us doesn't have a cell phone?

Again, I'll still have a job, a roof over my head, and food on the table tomorrow. But I'll still be upset at missing lunch with a friend -- and my friend would likely be upset too.

Same with EQ. If I miss a raid with friends because my ISP conks out at the wrong time, both I and they will be upset. It's not a big thing in the greater scheme of life, but it's also not something to totally ignore.

I guess it all comes down to keeping things in perspective.
