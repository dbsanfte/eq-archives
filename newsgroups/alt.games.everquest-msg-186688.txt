> Win95 evolved from a cooperative effort between Microsoft and IBM.
> 
> In the mid-80's, IBM and Microsoft entered into an agreement to work
> together on a new operating system.  IBM brought the skillset to the
> table to created a true multitasking environment (rather than the
> preemptive multitasking of Windows 3.0) ... Microsoft brought the
> skillset to the table to provide a Windows 3.0-like user interface for
> IBM's new multitasking environment.  The child of this marriage was
> OS/2. 
> 
> OS/2 had it's faults ... mainly the hundreds of setting that had to be
> tinkered with for each DOS app to work.  The refinements continued
> until OS/2 1.3, at which time Microsoft pulled out of the agreement
> ... in essence, they took their ball and went home.
> 
> IBM pressed forward with OS/2, releasing OS/2 2.0 and OS/2 Warp ...
> but IBM couldn't sell water in a desert.  They steadfastly refused to
> listen to the folks who used OS/2 say that there were too many
> settings to be adjusted. Sales suffered, and only a few of the major
> players of the time (Lotus, WordPerfect) made OS/2-specific apps.
> 
> Microsoft, on the other hand, took the knowledge they had gleaned from
> IBM and created an environment that was almost the same as OS/2
> structurally. However, having heard (and listened) to the folks who
> tried to use OS/2 (and I was one of them ... on a screaming i486/50
> with 32 megs of RAM), they removed the necessity for adjusting
> settings (at the expense of declaring that some DOS apps simply would
> not work) and added a few bells and whistles.  This operating system
> was Windows 95. 
> 
> So, you're absolutely correct, DK.  Win95 was not revolutionary at
> all.  It evolved from OS/2 1.3.
> 
> Ben will argue with this, I'm sure.
>
