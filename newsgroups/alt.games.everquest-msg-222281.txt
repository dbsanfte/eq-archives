> In a recent letter to the editor, Kathy McKelvey equated the Ten
> Commandments with morality.  While I could take issue with this
> by pointing to the moral strictures of Hindu's or Wiccans or the
> Ancient Greeks, I'd like instead to defend a more radical idea;
> that morality is not adhering to a set of rules based on
> punishment or reward, but rather comes from within.
>
> Lets imagine that your morality derived from laws instead of from
> religion.  There's someone you despise for some reason, and you
> have the opportunity to kill him, but refrain solely because you
> are afraid you would go to jail.  Is that moral?  How is this
> restraint any different from not killing because you believe
> there's an omnescient observer who will punish you in the
> afterlife?
>
> I believe it would be more moral to refuse to kill because
> killing is illegal (rather than because of fear of consequences),
> to refuse to kill because God said not to (rather than because of
> fear of hell), but most moral of all would be to refuse to kill
> because it is simply wrong to do so, coming to that conclusion by
> philosophical thought rather than having it imposed from outside.
>
> Adhering to an ancient code set up by tribal wanderers thousands
> of years ago in a primative enviroment, rather than rather than
> taking the trouble to rethink morality to fit into our more
> modern civilization, would be nonsensical.  Adhering to those
> tenets because they were passed down by God and shouldn't be
> subject to change would be more understandable, except that God
> does not offer proof of his existance or which of the many
> differing definitions of his laws we should practice, and as a
> natural consequence there are as many differing opinions as to
> what exactly God (or the goddess, or the gods, or the divine
> principle, or... ) might want us to do.
>
> An amusing episode of South Park recently pointed out the folly
> of this game of religious chicken; the scene was the recieving
> line in Hell, and it was filled with people who felt they'd been
> great catholics, babtists, 7th day adventists, so on and so
> forth.  The devil checking them in said that no, they'd guessed
> wrong.  Pressed, he then said that Mormons happened to be the
> ones that got things right.  We on earth have no way of telling
> if that's true, or if it was some other splinter of christianity,
> islam, judaism, hinduism, zorasterism, norse "myth" or so on and
> so forth.
>
> I'd far rather see morality defined as operating within a
> personal and well thought out and defensible philosophical
> framework, than with what any one religious splinter group (and
> they are ALL splinter groups, regardless of your personal guess
> as to which one is the lucky lottery ticket with all the right
> answers) would have you do.
>
