> Debating whether or not historical figures were "terrorists" is
> amusing but nonproductive if people cannot agree on a definition
> of terrorism.  As far as I have seen in this discussion, there
> has been no agreed-upon definition proposed and accepted.
>
> If one merely means "killing with which I disagree," then
> there are terroristic aspects to virtually all wars on
> all sides.
>
> One common and definition is that a requirement for an act to be
> "terrorism" is that it must have as a political goal the intimidation
> of a populace by selective killing, torture, etc. of noncombatants.  In
> other words, a terrorist event must have as a goal the intent to
> instill terror in the victims as an end in iself, and not be a
> byproduct.
>
> The implication of this is interesting because it means that merely the
> killing of noncombatants does not constitute terrorism -- it must be
> part of a strategy of societal intimidation.  Thus, for instance, the
> Battle of Britain was a terror campaign on the part of the Germans
> because the purpose of targeting civilians was a failed attempt to
> break the extremely resilient British spirit.  The bombing of civilians
> was terrorist act because of the *purpose* of the attack. In contrast,
> the Mai Lai massacre would not be a terrorist attack because it was a
> spontaneous acting out of base hatred against civilians and not part of
> a campaign of intimidation.  The Mai Lai massacre was certainly a war
> crime and a crime against humanity; it was not, by this definition, a
> terrorist act.
>
> Using the American Revolution, the British use of the Cayuga and Seneca
> Indians to attack and kill women and children in upper New York was a
> terror campaign because the purpose of the campaign was to break the
> spirit of the settlers on the Northwest frontier through attacks on
> noncombatants.  After the defeat of the British/Loyalist forces at
> Elmira in 1779, the Revolutionaries destroyed tens of Cayuga and Seneca
> villages in retribution, accompanied by the deaths of many Indian women
> and children.  Since this was done as retaliation rather than part of a
> campaign to instill terror, it is technically not a "terrorist" action
> by this definition.  Similarly, the Loyalist Southern campaign of
> terror under Benedict Arnold, which presaged Sherman's March to the
> Sea, was a terrorist act because the purpose was to instill terror in
> noncombatants.
>
> The distinction between the targeting of noncombatants in an effort to
> instill terror and the killing of noncombatants as a byproduct of a
> campaign is an uncomfortable one for supporters of the Palestinian
> attacks on noncombatants, and they thus both adopt a broader definition
> which applies the name "terrorist" to any killing of a noncombatant
> in order to create a moral equivalency.
>
> This redefinition is convenient in the short term, because
> it allows one to use loaded terms in verbal attack.  In the
> long term, however, it is counterproductive because it either
> removes any real meaning to the term or, even worse, it
> justifies attacks on the Palestinians.  If there is no distinction
> between the targeting of civilians for the purpose of instilling
> terror and the killing of civilians as a byproduct of campaign,
> then it would be equally OK for the Israelis to target women and children
> as it is for the Palestinians to do so.  The Palestinian
> campaign of terror is self-contradictory in that it denies
> this distinction when justifying its own acts but clings
> to the distinction when attempting to dissuade the Israelis
> from equivalent tactics.
