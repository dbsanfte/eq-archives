:: The bottom line is that firearm availability has a minor effect on
:: crime.  Prospective studies almost invariably show that increased gun
:: ownership is associated with decreased violent crime in local areas.
:: Broad retrospective studies have been mixed, but show decreasing
:: effect either way as the study groups become more closely matched in
:: demographics.  A classic example of the latter are the studies
:: comparing Canada and the US, where dramatic differences in violent
:: crime between Vancouver, BC and Vancouver, WA essentially disappeared
:: when matched between identical demographic cohorts.  Similarly,
:: studies comparing similar areas on the border between Canada and the
:: US find no relationship between gun ownership and crime rate.
::
:: The reason for this is that gun ownership has little to do with the
:: overall crime rate.  Britain's crime rate fell during the 90s, just
:: as did crime in the US because of age- and income-related demographic
:: effects.  Britain's crime rate has been rising, just as it has and
:: will in the US because of the same effects a mini-spike in young
:: adult demographics and economic woes.
::
:: Gun ownership *does* clearly affect *local* crime rates, as is shown
:: by Lott's work and other experiments of nature.  However, these
:: effects are generally overwhelmed in national statistics by events
:: that have nothing to do with gun ownership.
