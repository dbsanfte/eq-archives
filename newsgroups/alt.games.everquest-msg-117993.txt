> *nix (Linux, Unix, etc) has a utility called "grep". Grep basically takes a 
> text file and displays the lines you want or all but the lines you don't 
> want. So, with an EQ log I ftp to a box on my LAN, I can use "grep "^You 
> say" eqlog.txt" and get an output of lines that start with (thats what ^ 
> means) "You say". Likewise, "grep -v " miss"" will get rid of all the miss 
> messages (yours and others). Regular expressions are a symbolic way of 
> defining patterns to search for, and make grep even more powerful.
> 
> Additionally, you can have fun with grep and "wc" (wordcount). "wc -l" 
> counts the number of lines, so "grep "^You say" eqlog.txt | wc -l" will 
> tell me how many times that character has spoken aloud(the pipe uses the 
> previous commands output for input to the second one... in English it's 
> like "Find all lines beginning with 'You say' and then count them").
> 
> There are many many more powerful ways to do this sort of thing on *nix 
> machines, Perl being high on that list, but grep is pretty easy to get 
> meaningful data from.
