> the prices from www.newegg.com.  I'm picking newegg because they have one

> feedback.  The low prices at pricewatch are often from places that you
have
> to love gambling to buy from (although the place with the lowest price in
> this case looks pretty good, based on the feedback at resellerratings).
>
> All are 533 MHz FSB.
>
>     3.06,   $650    OEM
>     2.8     $383    Retail (e.g., with fan and heatsink)
>     2.66    $300    Retail
>     2.53    $235    Retail
>     2.4     $190    Retail
>
> Here's another way to look at this data:
>
>     step        speed jump      price jump
> 2.8 -> 3.06     9.3%            70%
> 2.66 -> 2.8     5.3%            28%
> 2.53 -> 2.66    5.1%            28%
> 2.4 -> 2.53     5.4%            24%
>
> (Note that this is speed jump of the CPU only.  You won't get an overall
> system performance boost of anywhere near that high, since everything else
> is running at the same speed).
>
> You will come out way ahead in the long run if you buy just enough CPU to
> play the games you are playing now, and will be playing for the next few
> months.  I'm putting together my new gaming system, and I'm going with the
> 2.4.  If a game comes out six months from now that won't play well on a
2.4,
> but would play well on a 3.06, I'll buy a 3.06 and pop it in (the
> motherboard I'm getting can handle the 3.06), and my $190 + whatever the
> 3.06 will be in six months will still come out less than a 3.06 *now*.
>
> So, my recommendation to people putting together gaming systems is:
>
> 1. Best video card you can afford.
>
> 2. Motherboard that can handle the fastest CPU available.
>
> 3. CPU that is fast enough for the current games and the games you expect
to
> come out in the next fix months.
>
> --Tim Smith
