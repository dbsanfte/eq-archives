No offense, but I think that's the problem. The developers seem to have felt
that everyone who plays a warrior is a beginner or someone who doesn't want
to be forced to ever make a decision or use player skill more than measuring
what to attack, when to attack and when and how to try to run and hide (all
of which requires patience and common sense more than anything else; two
qualities I don't necessarily associate with my idea of a fantastic medieval
warrior who wants to kill gnolls, minotaurs, mammoths and dragons but
nevermind that).

I think we should be given far more to work with. Really, how many hours (or
minutes) did it take everyone to master the EQ "melee system"? Given
hundreds of hours to practice, I really don't think that there's too many
levels of complexity out there which all of us couldn't or wouldn't be able
to readily master without having to think too much and assuming we weren't
hit with all of our choices at 1st level (which could be intimidating). I
like playing a warrior in RPGs but not because it's the simplest or easiest
class, although it too-often is. I can't stand EQ warriors primarily because
whether or not they can talk the talk, they can't walk the walk, but
additionally because there's no special player skill involved when playing
one; they have no flexibility in fights and few options other than sit there
and die or not (while swinging for 1/4th - 1/10th the damage of some other
classes). That's just not my definition of "warrior."

BTW, RPing a simpleton or a crude, ignorant savage is another matter entirely
which should have nothing to do with game mechanics or the combat style
requirements of a high ranking swordsman.
