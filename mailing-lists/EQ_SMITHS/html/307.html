Overhead \ o-ver-hed\ adj 1 : business expense not chargeable to a
particular part of the work or product.

I bid, sell and perform engineering services. Overhead is a constant in ANY
business. As Webster's dictionary (see above) states the overhead cost for
a business is not billable to a particular product or part of a product. It
is, however, passed along to the consumer in the form of a generally higher
price. GM, and any other business MUST, as a necessity, pass along the
costs of health insurance, equipment maintenance, downtime, quality
assurance and yes...even employee incompetence to the consumer. Not in the
1st car off the assembly line or the 2nd or the 200th. But as an average of
the products they sell the overhead must be covered or the company simply
fails to make money.
We do not live in an altruistic society. GM is not in business because it
longs to provide a job to all those poor UAW union members. They are in
business to MAKE MONEY.

The trick to handling overhead is to pass along as much of it as possible to
the consumer and still make a profit. When, in the name of selling your
product, you lower your price to the point that the overhead % is higher
than your profit you lose.

As a consulting engineer my corporate overhead rate is 131%. This means
that, as I bid work, I must take my actual hourly rate and multiply it by
131% just to BREAK EVEN.

Bah. This is no place for a lesson in economics.
If some idiot wants to eat all of his failures let him. I certainly wont.
