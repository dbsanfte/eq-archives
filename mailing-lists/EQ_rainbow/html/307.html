>Your wedding story is so saddening. It so affected me, that I immediately
>began writing a response. I explored all these issues involved in this
>situation, from abstracts and absolutes all the way down to specific
>examples from my own experiences. I wrote for pages, meandering through
>this more for myself than as an actual response to your original situation.
>What was the right thing to do in this situation? How can someone wanting
>to play a female character in a game, develop into such a dilema, resulting
>in the deletion of a character you undoubtedly spent a lot of time
>building--not just level-wise, but relationship-wise; all the friends left
>behind....It nearly makes ME cry to think about it.
>
>I decided not to send all the soul searching I had done in the first
>response I worked on, and instead I'll try and focus more on your
>situation.
>
>I, too, play a female character and am a male in real life. One of the
>initial reasons I chose to play a female character was because I liked the
>EQ model's faces better for the females than the males. Kind of vain. But
>that was not the only reason. I was seeking something...something new.
>When I go into a game like this, I'm going in to escape partly, but mostly
>to have a new set of experiences--experiences I can't get in the real
>world,
>with its iron-clad literalism and society's perpetual frowning upon
>dreaming. I can be a male 90% of the rest of my time, but in this game, I
>was going to be a female. I wanted differences and new insights. Oh so
>lofty! Of course I wasn't thinking all of this so concisely when I made my
>character. All this can be summed up in what I was thinking at the
>time--"I
>wonder what it'd be like to play a female in this game."
>
>I did not, and have not, told anyone that I am a man in real life. It
>simply isn't important. I had the situation where I was asked what my real
>name was. I told him, honestly, that I didn't give out my real name in the
>game. How clinical that sounds when I boil the situation down to just
>that.
>It was not that easy. This was a guy I had met in beta, and had played
>with
>for a very long time--from beta on through release. We had become good
>friends. He was young, and he was depressed. He shared a lot with me
>about
>his real life, and I gave him advice as best I could from the things I'd
>experienced. It is amazing to me now when I think on those conversations,
>just how unimportant and easily skipped the idea of my own sex in real life
>really was. I was a friend, and whether I was a male or a female at the
>keyboard did not matter at all. He was very sad at the time he asked me my
>name, very lonely, and he had through his confessions and reliance on my
>sympathy drawn me in close, without me realizing it. Suddenly when
>confronted with this question of my real life name, I did realize what was
>occuring. The young man was basically falling in love with me, as much as
>a
>person can in a situation like this. The lead up to his question is what
>really made me see what was happening. That day, his entire manner was
>somehow different than before, as though he had come to a realization.
>
>Well, needless to say I felt trapped. Immediately I began to berate myself
>for my behavior. What a terrible person I was! How could I have let this
>happen? I should have known this would eventually come up! I had lead him
>on! I should have told him from the very beginning I was a man in real
>life! What should I do? Do I tell him? I couldn't tell him! I didn't
>want to hurt him! I didn't want to hurt me, either. Who knows how he'd
>react? Maybe I could just go linkdead, forget about the whole thing. I
>could start over as someone else and he'd never know, and I wouldn't have
>to
>feel this way....
>
>BZZT. Reality check. Why was I blaming myself, here? If I had played a
>male character in the game, would I have expected myself to immediately
>announce to everyone I met, "Yes, ladies and gentlemen, the organic
>creature
>sitting behind this pixel-composed being you see before you--who from here
>on shall be refered to as "me"-- does infact have protruding genitalia,
>which clinically defines me as a male of the species. Thenceforth, you may
>think of me as such, both in the game and out of the game, and the precious
>societal constructs we've built around the differing sexes will remain the
>sound edifaces to which we so heartily cling for reassurance."
>
>No, I think not.
>
>So why would I suddenly plunge myself into self-chastisement simply because
>someone I had developed an ingame friendship with posed a question like
>this? As I described, the circumstances weren't easy, he was feeling
>attraction toward me, and I cared for him as a friend. But I had nothing
>to
>feel guilty about. I came into the game to escape this real world stuff.
>The fault, if some were to be placed, would fall more solidly upon him for
>putting me into this situation in a place I did not want this sort of
>problem. It was so very much like me being in my office at work, with lots
>of people around, some of whom were friends, others I barely know, and a
>guy
>walking up to me in the middle of a meeting and asking me, "Are you gay?"
>This is a bit of an extreme parallel, but some of the same sentiments and
>forces would be at play. Should I have told everyone the first day of work
>I was gay? Was I being deceitful for not telling everyone? If I tell
>them,
>will they all turn on me? Of course not. It's unimportant, and in the
>setting of work, very much inappropriate.
>
>But! In the game, there's no need to place blame for this. It's supposed
>to be a relaxed environment. It only gets tense when we allow ourselves to
>get tense. I couldn't blame this guy. He was pursuing his feelings. So
>what did I do? I didn't drop link and vanish, though for a moment, that
>seemed the best solution. Instead, I told him simply that I keep the game
>and my real life as separate as possible, and I wouldn't give out my real
>name. He took this well--others might have leapt to the conclusion that I
>didn't want to give out my name because I WAS a man in real life; in this
>situation I would have shrugged off further inquiry and let him decide for
>himself if he wanted to continue being a friend to me or indulge his
>insecurities and break off ties because I MIGHT be a man in real life. At
>which point I'd realize I don't want this guy as a friend anyway. My
>friend
>did ask me once or twice after that in a joking manner whether I might ever
>tell him my real name. I told him honestly, probably not.
>
>I play my female character AS a female. I met someone who played a female,
>but was constantly telling everyone he was a male in real life. Grunt. It
>was actually kind of pathetic and sad. I've no comment on what demons he
>might be dealing with in real life =P For me, nothing I do in the game
>needs a piece of real-life to back it up. Norrath is a completely
>different
>reality, and your presence there is merely a facet of your personality (and
>we all have many many facets to our personalities), not a reflection of
>what
>organs I possess in real-life.
>
>When you play a female, you're going to have people flirt with you. I
>think
>it's fun, as long as they don't drift over into personal things and
>real-life stuff. I do it because I'm playing my character (I have another
>female character who never responds well to flirting; I play her as a
>serious minded dwarven woman who has no time for such frivolous
>shinanigans). My male characters flirt with females occasionally, too.
>All
>within the game. Things only go foul when someone breaches the illusion
>and
>tries to bring real life into it (what's your name in real life? Are you
>really a chick?). Rather like some guy standing up in the middle of a
>really good sci-fi movie and pronouncing, He knows how they did that
>effect!
>Then proceeds to explain to you in detail how all the models, lighting and
>cameras were used. It shatters the illusion, ruins your fun, and is
>totally
>out-of-line. You can, however, have someone in the game you have an
>understanding with already and reveal personal stuff to. I've a friend
>like
>that, and it's nice to talk with him about real life things....
>
>But for everyone else, it's none of their business. You're here to have
>fun.
>
>For you, Tiger, I know what happened to you was a more complicated
>situation. I completely understand why you deleted your character. But do
>not call yourself liar. You did nothing wrong. In this situation, you're
>not obligated to "confess" to your ingame fiance, your guildmates, or
>anyone. You've nothing to confess. Confessions are for wrongdoers. By
>disappearing, you may have made the whole thing much more complicated than
>it actually was, but that's okay. You could actually use this to your
>advantage. If you choose to respond to those people in your guild by
>telling them why you disappeared (you can apologize about not showing up at
>the wedding, but don't apologize for not blurting out that you are really a
>guy in real life to everyone in the guild), you might actually find some of
>your friends suddenly become great friends by understanding. And, too, if
>they react really badly you'll know they suck and being friends with such
>shallow goons would have been a waste anyway. If they want to know why you
>didn't tell them straight up you were a guy in real life, I'd just say it
>wasn't important. Again, why should it be such a big deal? I would never
>pick and choose whom i play with in the game by first interviewing them to
>find out if they're inboard or outboard. And neither do I want to play
>with
>someone who does that.
>
>I have a few characters on Veeshan. If you want help with your new
>character, let me know. None of mine are very high level there, but that
>doesn't matter. I can group with you and show you some nifty places to
>play
>to get back in gear again. Or you could move on over to Brell ;)
