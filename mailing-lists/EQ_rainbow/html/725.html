Joseph asked why I use Dvorak instead of Qwerty. Hehe -- short
question, long answer (sorry, I love trivia).

Qwerty was designed in the days of manual keyboards. The little
bars would jam if someone typed too fast, so some guy designed a
keyboard layout that was as inefficient as possible to slow typists
down & prevent this -- voila, Qwerty. It ended up being the de facto
standard keyboard layout because this same guy was a great salesman
who marketed his typewriters to the U.S. Army during WWII. Of course
the Army bought it in bulk & trained a slew of clerktypists, who then
knew qwerty when they left the army & went to work for offices after
the war. All of these offices then bought Qwerty typewriters, & the
numerous other keyboard layouts that had been in existance pretty
much all died out (at least in the U.S.).

Dvorak, on the other hand, was designed for maximum efficiency. The
Dvorak brothers studied ergonomics (back before the word existed) to
see which patterns of keystrokes were fastest & easiest to make. They
then studied the English language to see which 2-letter combinations
were most common, so that they could set these 2 letters to the
easiest keystroke combos (i.e. th is very common, so t & h are each
in a position that makes it very easy to type one right after the
other). Great engineers, but not very good salesmen, so their
keyboard never really caught on.

All of this boils down to the fact that Dvorak is easier to use,
more efficient, & less tiring for the hands when typing for long
periods. I was at about 45 wpm or so when I was using qwerty
regularly (slower now since I'm rusty). After switching to Dvorak, I
was matching that speed within about 3 weeks, & had surpassed it in
another 2 or 3. Since any Windows PC has Dvorak in the keyboard
settings, you don't have to worry about buying a special keyboard or
being unable to type on someone else's computer. The only problem
comes in DOS or DOS-based programs -- & EQ.
