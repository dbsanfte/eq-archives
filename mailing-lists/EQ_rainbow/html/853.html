I used to use the word 'gay' in the derrogatory sense when I was in high
school. I grew up in a little town in Virginia (my graduating class was
about 70 people), and even here it was in common usage--or should I say,
common misuage (with much emphasis on the vulgar connotations of common).
This was back in--ugh, here I go feeling my age again--the late '80s (I
graduated in '89). I honestly didn't know the real meaning of it at the
beginning of my misuse of it. It was pretty synonymous with 'stupid'.
Saying things like, 'that's so gay!' or 'you're so gay!', you could just as
easily switch out the two words. Toward the end of my high school career,
the real meaning became more clear to me. I knew 'gays' were different and
not to be liked, and you sure didn't want to hang out with any. There was a
kid who was a friend of mine through school that everyone called gay. He
dated a girl, but it just seemed for show. To fit in, I began calling him
gay as an insult behind his back, only this time I used it knowing more
clearly what it meant, making my use of it all the more insidious. This
happened gradually, the transformation of the word's meaning. The initial
erroneous use of it, however, made the newer, hateful, use of it that much
more easy; it was already a part of my vocabulary as an insult. My friend
never discovered my spurious nature. He came out officially after leaving
our hometown. I've since made efforts to reconnect with him.

All the time I was growing up, I fought against an increasing knowledge of
myself, that I, too, was gay. Using it in this hateful manner, in my
peer-pressured and confused mind, separated me from 'gays'. By opposing it
or them, I was placed on the safe side and couldn't be accused. I
understood nothing then, and being in a small town, there was little
opportunity to expand my understanding.

Breaking the habit was hard, and I didn't start until I was in college. I
realized more then, outside of the confines of my hometown's provincial
mindset, particularly about myself. It took a bit to fix my usage. Besides
the misuse being just plain mean, it was also flat-out ignorant of my first
language, and being an English major and a writer, that just was
unnacceptable!

Sometimes, even today, that habitual inclination rises during moments of
upset to insert the word gay as an insult. It's struck down before
utterance, but it goes to show how things that one learns while young can
stick with you throughout your life, and why teaching a child early can
forestall the building of a subconscious prejudice. I had two VERY
compelling reasons to change my ways. There are a lot more people out there
who have neither reason, and probably don't even realize deep-down they have
a forgotten bedrock of ignorance informing on their outlook.

Clayton Kroh
