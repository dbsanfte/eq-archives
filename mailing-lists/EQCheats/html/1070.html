Good points. But again, only partially correct. Nothing... and I stress
NOTHING is absolutely "crack proof" (with one exception that I know of).
It's just a matter of time before the encryption algorithms are cracked.
Better algorithms just ensure that it will take longer to crack. In fact,
there's only one algorithm that's currently absolutely crack proof, and I
doubt that Verant's using it to encrypt server to client/client to server
protocols (it's military grade and is similar to Netscapes 128 bit
encryption key, but on a much more advanced level; hence part of the US
governments reluctance to allow Netscapes encryption technology/algorithms
out of the country - Netscapes encryption key is very hard to crack, but not
impossible, and also because they're afraid that 3rd world countries will
develope their own keys using this technology).

One of the many things that limits Verants use of a good encrypting
algorithm on their game, (or any other company, country, government entity
etc.) is that with highly complex encryption algorithms, the encryption
process is not quick enough to utilize highspeed transmissions at full
bandwith - resulting in wasted bandwith, high packet loss, and lag, making
exploitations (of various forms) easier to use. For the most part, software
capable of homing in on these weaknesses is limited to government agencies
(IE - the FBI, CIA, the Pentagon etc.) which limits the average hacker from
hacking using this type of software.

In short, with extremely few ways to ensure an unbreakable encryption, (only
one instance that I know of with my background and training) and a limited
number of ways to make it absolutely hack proof, you'd have to have a stand
alone only system/server (IE - no LAN, WAN, Intra/Internet access, no modem
etc.) with a limited access login terminal physically located nearby to keep
ALL hackers at bay. Otherwise, someone, somewhere, somehow, will hack into
it and find a way to exploit (heck... even the US Government has hackers
getting into their "secure" computers and they spend millions trying to
protect their systems). It's just a matter of time if you have a hacker
who's deligent enough to stay at it.

On a better note however, once a "hack" or "exploit" is discovered by those
who wish to protect their interests, software etc., they can then find a way
to block out that hack/exploit, making it a little more secure, bringing
their protection methods one step closer to being unhackable/unbreakable.
