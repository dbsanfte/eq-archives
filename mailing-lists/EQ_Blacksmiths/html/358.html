Lets take a good store: say Wal Mart.

If someone steals something, do they raise their prices? Yes. If their
store has items that are not sold very often, do they raise the price of
more commonly sold items to compensate? Yes.

Now, having said that, lets look at another phenomena.

I go to college and learn programming. Joe, he goes to school and doesn't
get more than his High School Diploma. Who makes more money? Who's skill is
more valuable?

Now granted I don't march out and say "hey I want $50,000 up front because
that is what my schooling cost! But you would assume that over the next 3,
5, or 10 years you'd more than exceed that price (of schooling/training) in
your quality of living and/or straight income.

The problem with EQ is that the economy's "bottom" is VERY LOW. In fact it
is CRITICALLY low. Most trade items cannot be sold to NPCs for even equal
to what it cost to make them (materials only). As a result, the player has
no reason to sell lower and lower, as he knows if he makes more than the
NPC gives him he's not totally losing out. Of course granted I've seen
nobody sell for less than materials, but there is no given "skill" cost
because the bottom is so low.

If it wasn't for the fact that the word "economy" is only about how much
money is in the "pool" at any given time, vs. goods measured against money
in that pool (ie. the economy is non-existent), then EQ's economy would
crash horribly and irrepairably.

I agree with the first guy. The only way to ensure a decent profitability
in any amount of time measured less than months is to try and fix the prices.
