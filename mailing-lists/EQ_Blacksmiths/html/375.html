There needs to be a distinction drawn between hobbies and businesses here.
I do not know of many businesses where hobbieists who are willing to sell
for a loss because they do it for fun has any kind of significant impact on
businesses who do something to make money. The markets are too large here.
In EQ, where your market is in the hundreds to thousands, it is quite
possible for a hobbieist to completely ruin other's chances to make a buck
at the trades. How many mom and pop Auto manufacturers are there out there?
There are a few. Vector, Panoz, some others. I'm guessing that the few
dozen to a hundred cars they sell a year aren't putting any kind of dent in
GM's bottom line. The volume is just too small. A couple hundred lost
banded sales to a hobbieist pretty much amounts to the entire banded market.
Comparing the US to EQ doesn't work. Apples and oranges for the above
reasons, and a few others, not the least of whch is that in the US things
wear out and in EQ they are eternal.

The other point where you are wrong is that we have rejected the concepts of
collusion and oligopolies. Anyone with a phone or electricity or Cable TV
or water knows quite well that such concepts still thrive in the US. Not
that that's a good thing, just a fact. The only difference between them,
and any other business in the US is that the government supports them and
discourages others. Which doesn't quite seem fair. I mean, if someone has
the gusto to build that better moustrap, sell it cheaper than anyone else
can, and take the market by storm, driving out all competitors, what does
the government do? They break up the company.
