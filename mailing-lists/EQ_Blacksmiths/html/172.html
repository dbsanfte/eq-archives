> I find this an interesting comment. You state, if not through words
> then through your stated intention of action, that you feel the free
> market should rule; that if one smith is charging five plat less than
> another for a particular item, that the free market will come into
> effect. You state buyers are free to go where they wish with their
> purchasing power.
>
> If he can make purchases from smiths and then resell his purchases at a
> higher price to buyers willing to pay the price, he's FOLLOWING THE
> FREE MARKET THEORY! In fact, he would be what we're all familar with
> in real life, the 'middle man'. Here's an example:
>
> Joe the MiddleMan is a character of high enough level to make a decent
> amount per hour of adventuring. He takes his plat and uses it to
> bankroll his purchasing of smiths' wares in his area; making it his
> goal to try and buy as much as possible from the smiths. He then
> responds to WTB auctions with the product he has 'warehoused', and
> marks it up accordingly. In this fashion, he proceeds forward; buying
> product regularly off of smiths and holding it until a buyer appears.
>
> Now, *anyone*, producer or middleman or
> end-user-reselling-to-another-end-user, *anyone*; who charges an
> excessive amount over the production cost and/or purchase price can
> have qualities such as greed ascribed to them, with a fair amount of
> truth. But if one believes in the Free Market, then it's all fair. If
> one believes in the Free Market, then any price charged will either be
> a successful price (meaning, buyers will pay it), or will not be a
> successful price (no buyers appear).
>
> Further, how fair is it of *you* to *other smiths* to set your prices
> so low they have difficulty making any sort of money. The master smith
> decides, "hey, I have a skill of 150! I can make product with a very
> low failure rate, meaning I don't have much waste. I know, I'll sell
> it for five or ten plat over the materials cost!"
>
> That's all fine and well for the master smiths; but the up and coming
> smiths are faced with an *even longer* road to tred, if they have any
> notions of being able to produce their product for profit. If the
> master smiths are selling at a price designed to allow for only one
> *failure* over two or three or even more attempts, lesser smiths will
> be hard pressed to sell at a profit at similar pricing levels (because
> the lesser smith will have one *success* over two or three or even more
> attempts).
>
> You say you're doing the community a favor by charging a low, low
> price; but what community are you serving, ultimately? The buyer with
> money in hand gets a great deal as smiths begin to lower prices to
> match the lowest; but eventually smiths begin to throw up their hands
> and quit bothering, reducing the availability of armor. So the
> community you're 'doing a favor' is really only a few early buyers, and
> smiths that are happy to sell at near unprofitable levels; because the
> other smiths take it up the backside, and others interested in smithing
> are prevented from doing something they wanted to.
>
> Now, it is a Free Market, as you say. But if you're going to ascribe
> notions of greed and negative emotion to 'middleman resellers', then
> you are not immune to similar labels against you from other smiths.
> How is what the middleman reseller does any more 'greedy' than what the
> master smith does to the lesser smith? I'd say it's not. It's all
> business, and business is war; but if you're going to start throwing
> negative labels around, you'd better be sure you're not engaging in
> hypocracy.
>
> And finally, I'll point out (as others have attempted to do in the
> past), that real life businesses are engaged in the free market of our
> capitalistic society (which, despite some problems, works pretty well);
> but they also have amounts of sanity mixed in amid the incessent
> competitive pricing wars. They don't charge prices designed to drive
> themselves out of business; not without actually going out of business
> sooner or later. Real car manufacturers charge a price per car
> designed to enable them to recoup capital investment costs, raw
> materials costs, costs of training and labor, and the costs of
> unsellable product.
>
> The difference between EQ smiths and real life businesses it that the
> CEO (PC smith) can't take his Board of Directors (group) out to the
> dungeons and kill stuff for a few hours to get more money with which to
> then go back out and sell product at a loss again.
